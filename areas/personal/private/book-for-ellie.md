# A Book For Ellie

> Andrew's book for his daughter Ellie. About people, relationships, and how to move through the world. The single running document — chapters added here as they're written.

**Status**: drafting (chapters 1–6 complete)
**Audience**: Ellie (only). Anything else is a bonus.

---

## Andrew's Voice — Style Notes (for Claude)

When Andrew asks me to "write like him," adopt these patterns. When he doesn't, be myself.

**Voice fingerprints:**
- Direct address. Talks *to* the reader, not at them. Frequent "you" and "I." Names the reader when it matters ("El," "sweetheart").
- Conversational asides and self-interruptions. Parentheticals, "right?…right?", "wanna know something?", "seriously."
- Confessional honesty over polish. Admits when he's still figuring something out ("this is the part I'm still working on myself so don't expect me to be perfect at it"). Doesn't pretend to have it all sorted.
- Repeats key phrases for emphasis, in pairs or threes. ("It was always love. It was only ever love." / "I promise you, you're worth it. I promise you, you're worth it.")
- Short standalone sentences as punches. Often one-line paragraphs. Lands a beat, then moves on.
- Builds an extended metaphor and rides it. (The bike ride to Tom's. The coal trimmers on the steamship. Sunflowers from cracked pavement.) The metaphor does the heavy lifting; the lesson rides on its back.
- Earns the lesson with a story first, then says the lesson plainly. Almost never opens with the thesis.
- Lowercase chapter titles, em-dash structure ("Chapter 4 - Don't Chase People Who Make You Feel Like A Chore").
- Cusses lightly when it lands. Doesn't perform restraint, doesn't perform edginess.
- Comfortable with contradictions and caveats inside the same paragraph. ("I'm not telling you not to chase happiness. I want you to be happy more than almost anything. What I'm telling you is…")
- Concrete over abstract. Tom and Mike, not "a childhood friend." Baltimore, Atlanta, New York — not "I moved around."
- Occasional dad-wisdom one-liners that read like they were earned the hard way: "feelings are weather," "the change in people business is a losing business," "the target for that never moves."
- Ends chapters on a personal direct line to Ellie, not on a tidy summary. Often a promise.

**Things Andrew avoids:**
- Bullet lists and frameworks. The book is prose. (Outside the book, he's fine with structure — but in his voice the prose flows.)
- Hedging and corporate softening. He commits.
- Big vocabulary words for their own sake.
- Quoting other people without making it his own. When he uses Emerson or Nietzsche, he immediately pivots to "but I believe it because I've lived it."

**When in doubt:** tell a small, specific, true-feeling story. Land it with a one-line punch. Trust the reader to do the rest of the work.

## Chapter 1 — Fun Isn't Enough

I am writing this book, or whatever you want to call it, for my daughter.

I'm writing this because I feel like we underestimate our own worldview sometimes and I want my daughter to always feel that, even if they are wrong or misguided, her inner musings are worth sharing.

Seriously. I really feel like most of us, deep down, don't think the way we view the world will matter to anyone else. In a world where there's a share button underneath our thumb at all times, people don't really share anything.

Meanwhile, while a lot of us feel the same things, we're all unique beings with our own subtle nuance about the way we view the world. Why not document that? Why not memorialize that forever?

So like I said, I'm writing this for you, Ellie. Only you. If other people benefit from it then so be it. I want you to be able to come back to this book when life inevitably and relentlessly happens to you and know that this is how your dad thought about this part of life.

The part about people. The part about operating in a way that leads to meaningful interactions and relationships.

Your network is your net worth. The relationships you cultivate, or don't, matter. I promise they do. Building meaningful relationships will create a network that can push you when you need a push, celebrate your accomplishments, and save you when you need a little saving. I promise you El; we all need to be saved at least once.

Your relationships aren't books sitting in neat rows on the shelf waiting for you to dust them off once in a while. They're alive. They're demanding. They require sacrifice. A healthy network thrives on reciprocity and genuine connections.

Whether you like it or not your relationships play a part in how your story starts, unfolds, and ends.

So here we go…

### Chapter 1 — Fun Isn't Enough

Your entire existence as a child is based on fun. You can argue that outside of basic needs like hunger and sleep, fun is the first condition you get addicted to. As a kid, it's simple. You find a thing that's new, experience it for the first time, and then continue to go back to that thing because your brain tells you that you enjoy it. I'm sure there's a scientific explanation for how we process fun, but that's not what this is about.

The reality is that in the beginning stages of life we are very literally spoon-fed fun. We become addicted to it. Maybe it's because our parents have an easier time if we're laughing versus crying. Maybe they feel the deep-seated biological desire to make sure their children have an easier life than they do. Sometimes it feels like parenting is like being one of those coal trimmers at the bottom of a steam ship, constantly shoveling coal into the furnace to keep the ship moving to the next destination, well out of the view of the other passengers. Oddly enough the day usually ends the same way for coal trimmers and parents — covered in dirt, exhausted beyond belief, and thanked by nobody because of course the ship kept moving that day. That's what ships do. Nobody thanks the coal trimmers.

Fun is openly allowed to infiltrate every part of our existence as kids. After all, what's better than having fun?

Not to be the guy who dumps the water on the campfire at the end of a great night, but this condition sets us on a wholly unproductive path as we begin to form our relationships. We begin to base a lot of our decision making on if something is fun, or at the very least, has the potential to be fun. The friendships we make are part of that process.

We become naturally attracted to people who enjoy fun the same way we do. Oh, Rocco likes to play with trucks? So do I. I want to be friends with Rocco. It's annoyingly simple, but the basis on which we form our first friendships, typically, is based solely on fun.

The expectation is that, over time, we correct this condition and begin to form our relationships based on other parameters. Right? …..right?

Most people don't. Most people never remove the fun-filters placed over our four year old eyes (and brains) and go through life continually seeking out people who are fun. We seemingly ignore the list of other parameters we should at least somewhat consider when deciding to form a relationship.

Then what happens? Sometimes, the fun stops. In fact most of the time the fun stops. Life inevitably mutates. The entire foundation on which you built that relationship completely disintegrates. The ship moves to the next destination.

You come to realize that maybe, just maybe, fun isn't enough.

## Chapter 2 — Don't Be So Negative

The "fun is everything" crowd probably hated Chapter 1. Right, El? Let's throw them a little bone to keep them happy. After all, it doesn't take much to mentally satiate someone who thinks fun is the sole basis of all relationships.

Here's the argument in one sentence: Fun is an important part of human interaction, but it's just that, a part of it. It checks a big box. I call it the "shared experience" category. Doing something fun with someone that also enjoys that thing is incredible. We're all dopamine junkies and it's fun to get high together. We'll have the conversation about using drugs another time, but you'll come to find that having a blast with your friends is, in itself, a high that you'll chase forever. That warm dopamine blast that feels like someone dipped your soul in caramel when you're with your friends having fun is unmistakable. It gets more intense as your friend group dwindles and you find the people who you can't live without for the rest of your life. After all, you've been a dopamine user since you were born. I know because I supplied it for you. I taught you to love having fun.

So all this chapter is about recognizing that fun in your interpersonal relationships will present you with some of the strongest feelings you'll ever feel. I want you to have that experience.

I just want you to be conscious that it is only part of the recipe. I want that for you so badly that I wrote a book about it.

## Chapter 3 — Chaos, Adversity

A few pages ago I told you that parents are wired to try and give their children an easier life than they had.

Wanna know something, El?

I don't want you to have an easy life. I really don't. Easy is boring. Incredibly boring. You're going to figure that out one day.

The most beautiful things in your life will be the nuclear fallout of adversity. Nietzsche said "You need chaos in your soul to give birth to a dancing star."

Your relationships aren't any different. Your most meaningful, productive, enriching relationships will be reinforced through adversity — not fun. Fun just isn't enough. You will learn that fighting through something with someone is an irreplaceable experience in terms of building interpersonal foundations.

Your most important relationships will be gigantic sunflowers that bloom from cracked pavement.

We can take a lesson from the business world on this one. There is no better opportunity to build a long standing customer relationship than when they call you and scream at you. The process of resolving whatever the issue is will prove more to that customer that you care than any of your products can.

The same goes for your relationships. When you are at odds with someone, I want you to stare that adversity directly in its face and be completely fearless. Because there is nothing to be afraid of, sweetheart. You're being given a chance to build something better. Use it. Trust me.

## Chapter 4 — Don't Chase People Who Make You Feel Like A Chore

I want to tell you about a kid named Tom.

Tom was my best friend. At least that's what I told myself, and honestly, at least on most days, that's what he told me too. Tom had another best friend named Mike. Mike lived across the street from Tom. I did not live across the street from Tom. I lived far enough away that getting there required a bike ride, and I took that bike ride a lot. A lot.

I don't know if Tom and Mike ever rode their bikes to me. I genuinely can't remember. What I remember is the feeling of showing up, and the particular kind of feeling you get when you realize, somewhere in the back of your brain that you're not quite ready to listen to yet, that you are always the one showing up.

I'm not telling you this so you feel bad for me. I'm telling you this because I think you're going to have your own version of Tom. Everyone does. And I want you to recognize it faster than I did.

Here's the thing about being young. You don't really have the language yet for what you're feeling. I knew something was off. I knew that there was a Mike-shaped gap between me and my best friend that I could never quite close no matter how many times I rode my bike over there. But I didn't have a word for it. So I just kept pedaling.

What I was doing, I'd come to understand much later in life, was auditioning. I was performing. Not in a calculated way. I wasn't some con artist kid running a scheme. I was genuinely funny, genuinely myself in a lot of ways. But underneath all of it was a kid who desperately wanted to be chosen. And when you want to be chosen badly enough, you start doing things to earn it. You become the funniest one in the room. You become the one who brings people together. You become the one who rides the bike.

I spent a lot of my younger years being a connector. I'd take two people who didn't know each other, see something in both of them, and introduce them. I did that over and over. I thought that was my value. I thought that was the thing that made me worth keeping around. And for a while, it worked, or at least it felt like it worked. I had friends across all kinds of different groups. I thought I mattered to all of them.

Then high school ended. And everybody went off into the groups that, looking back, I had largely built, and I wasn't really in any of them. Not in a dramatic way. Nobody told me to get lost. It just sort of happened, the way things do when the foundation was never quite solid enough to hold weight.

I don't have a single close friend from high school. Not one.

I want you to sit with that for a second, not because it's sad (it's actually fine, I promise) but because I think it's instructive. Those relationships weren't bad because the people were bad. They were temporary because they were built on the wrong things. They were built on proximity and performance and the particular kind of fun that evaporates the second everyone scatters.

My real friends, the ones I've had for over twenty years now, the ones who will show up in other chapters of this book because they're woven into everything I believe about relationships, I met them in college. And the difference wasn't some grand awakening I had. I didn't get to college and say, "Alright, new approach, building real foundations now." It was more subtle than that. It just felt different. Natural. Like I didn't have to try out.

That feeling, that naturalness, was the signal. I just didn't know it yet.

I still showed up for those guys, but it didn't feel like a bike ride to Tom's house. It felt like something mutual. Like we were all choosing each other at roughly the same time and with roughly the same energy. That's what reciprocity actually feels like when you're inside of it. It doesn't feel like effort. It feels like exhaling.

Here's what I want you to know, and I want you to really hear this one:

Do not chase people who make you feel like a chore.

You'll know when you're chasing. You'll know the feeling. It's the feeling of sending the message first, always. It's the feeling of making the plan, always. It's the feeling of riding the bike, always. And the person on the other end is perfectly nice, maybe even perfectly wonderful, but they are not meeting you where you are. They are not riding their bike to you.

That is information. Use it.

It doesn't mean those people are bad. It doesn't mean you write them off or blow anything up. It just means you stop organizing your life around someone who isn't organizing theirs around you. Quietly. Without bitterness. You just stop pedaling so hard.

Because here's the other side of this, and this is the part I'm still working on myself so don't expect me to be perfect at it. The right people don't need to be chased. They just show up. Sometimes they show up because you show up for them first and they return it naturally. Sometimes they show up out of nowhere and it clicks immediately. But the common thread is that it doesn't feel like an audition. You're not performing. You're just there, and being there is enough.

My friends in Boston. I go up there a couple times a year. They come down here a couple times a year. We have families now, and jobs, and it's genuinely a little annoying logistically to make it happen. But we make it happen because we want to, not because we're afraid of what happens if we don't. There's a difference between showing up out of love and showing up out of fear of being left behind. I spent too many years doing the second one.

The purpose of your relationships isn't to collect people who like you. It's to find the ones who choose you, and to be someone worth choosing in return.

Stop chasing. Start choosing. And let yourself be chosen, El. You're worth it.

I promise you, you're worth it.

## Chapter 5 — The Target Keeps Moving

There's a quote from Emerson that I keep coming back to. He said the purpose of life is not to be happy, but to be useful, to be honorable, to be compassionate, to have it make some difference that you lived.

I want to be clear about something. I don't believe that because Emerson said it. I believe it because I've lived enough life to feel it in my bones.

Here's the problem with happiness. It's a moving target. The things that made you happy at seven years old are not the things that make you happy at seventeen, and the things that make you happy at seventeen are not the things that make you happy at thirty five. The target keeps moving, and if you spend your whole life aiming at it, you're going to spend your whole life adjusting your aim. That's exhausting. That's also, if I'm being honest, kind of a trap.

Usefulness doesn't move like that. You can ask yourself at any point in your life, "Am I being useful right now?" and get a real answer. Am I showing up for the people who need me? Am I building something that matters? Am I putting more into the world than I'm taking out? Those questions have answers. They don't shift on you the way happiness does.

I'm not telling you not to chase happiness. I want you to be happy more than almost anything. What I'm telling you is don't make it the whole mission, because it will run from you your entire life if you do.

Now. I want to talk to you about time, because this is the part nobody gets right and I'm not sure I have either.

Your formative years don't feel like your formative years when you're in them. They just feel like regular days. They feel like Tuesday. You're not walking around at sixteen thinking, "Wow, I am right in the middle of something that's going to shape the rest of my life." You're thinking about what's for lunch and whether that person you like knows you exist. The weight of it doesn't show up until later, usually much later, when you're looking back and realizing you were standing in the middle of something important the whole time and treating it like a waiting room.

There's that quote, you've probably heard it, "the days are long and the years are short." It's true. It's also incomplete.

The full version is this: the days are long, the years are short, and nobody is coming to remind you. Not your friends, not your teachers, not me. I can write it in this book and you can nod and feel it for a minute and then put it down and go back to whatever you were doing. That's what people do with quotes. They feel true for a moment and then life absorbs them.

The only way this one actually works is if you build the reminder into yourself. Not a phone notification. Not a journal you'll keep for two weeks. Something deeper than that. A way of moving through the world where you occasionally stop and ask, "Am I being present in this? Will I look back on this period and wish I'd paid more attention?"

I moved around a lot in my twenties. Baltimore, Atlanta, New York. I was chasing something, though I couldn't have told you exactly what. Work, money, a version of myself that felt finished. And I remember those years feeling sort of long and uncertain while I was in them. It didn't feel like I was building anything. It felt like I was waiting to start.

But I was building things the whole time. I was building the friendships that are still standing. I was building an understanding of what I actually wanted, mostly by bumping into what I didn't. I was becoming the person who would eventually sit down and write this for you. I just didn't know it yet, because it never feels big when you're in it.

Here's what I want for you. I want you to have ambition. Real ambition, not the kind that's just restlessness dressed up in nice clothes, but the kind that comes from knowing what you're actually for. And I want that ambition to be pointed at something more durable than happiness.

Be useful. To yourself first, because you can't give what you don't have. Then to the people around you. Then to whatever work you decide is worth doing. Not because some nineteenth century poet told you to, but because at the end of a life, the things that stay with you aren't the moments you felt good. They're the moments you mattered to someone. The moments you showed up and it made a difference that you did.

The target for that never moves.

## Chapter 6 — Love Is What People Do

Love is weird. I want to start there because I think we spend a lot of time pretending it isn't, and that pretending gets people hurt.

Here's the thing nobody tells you. A lot of feelings dress up as love. Attention feels like love. Validation feels like love. Someone saying exactly what you've been thinking out loud, someone who makes you feel seen at exactly the right moment, that feels like love too. Your brain, which is a beautiful and wildly unreliable instrument, will take those feelings and file them under love because it doesn't know what else to do with them. And then you'll make decisions based on that filing system, and some of those decisions are going to cost you.

I'm not telling you this to make you guarded. I don't want you to walk through life with your arms crossed waiting for people to prove themselves before you let them in. That's not living. That's just a different kind of lonely.

What I'm telling you is this: learn to be a processor of information.

Watch what people do. Not what they say, not what they promise, not the way they make you feel in the best moments. What do they actually do? Do they show up? Do they follow through? Do they treat you the same way when things are hard as they do when things are easy? That's the data. That's the only data that matters.

Someone can tell you they love you every single day and mean it in the moment every single time. But love without action is just a feeling, and feelings are weather. They change. What doesn't change, what you can actually build a life around, is what people do when it costs them something to show up for you.

I learned this the hard way, the way most people do. I made wrong investments. I gave my time and my energy and my love to people who were very good at saying the right things and not so good at doing them. That's not entirely their fault. People are going through their own stuff, always, and sometimes the gap between what someone means and what someone does is just the weight of their own life pressing down on them. That's worth remembering. It keeps you from becoming bitter.

But here's what else I learned. You are not in the business of changing people. I want you to really hear that one because it will save you years of heartache.

The change in people business is a losing business. Nobody has ever loved someone into becoming a fundamentally different person. You can influence people, you can inspire people, you can be such a steady and generous presence in someone's life that they want to be better. But you cannot change them. And the moment you find yourself in a relationship, romantic or otherwise, where your hope is built on who someone could become rather than who they actually are, you need to stop and look at that honestly.

Don't make people try out. Let them be who they are, and then decide based on that information whether you want to invest. You're not their judge. You're not their project. You're just someone paying attention.

Now. Your love is not unlimited in the way people like to say it is. I know that sounds cold. It isn't. It's just true. Love becomes responsibility the moment you commit to it, and responsibility takes something out of you. It takes emotional energy, time, presence. You have a finite amount of those things on any given day. That means who you love is one of the most important decisions you will make, over and over again, throughout your entire life.

Spend it on people who deserve the responsibility of being loved by you. Spend it on people whose actions match their words. Spend it on people who are trying, even when they're failing, because trying is what matters. And when the evidence tells you that someone isn't meeting you there, trust the evidence. Not with anger. Not with a verdict about who they are as a person. Just with clarity.

The benchmarks are simple even if they're not easy. Ask yourself: am I becoming a better person inside this relationship? Am I growing? Am I more myself, or less? Do I feel more capable of love, or more drained by it? Those questions will tell you everything you need to know.

One more thing, and this one is just for you, and I mean it more than almost anything else in this book.

Nothing I have ever done, no rule I made, no boundary I set, no hard conversation I had with you, nothing, was ever rooted in anything other than love. You may not have always felt that. You may be reading this right now thinking of a moment that didn't feel like love when it was happening. I understand that. I was young once too, and the things my parents did out of love didn't always feel like love in the moment.

But I need you to know. Whatever we've been through, whatever you're going through right now, whatever is still ahead of us. It was always love. It was only ever love.

That's the one thing I will never ask you to take on faith. The evidence is there. I promise you it's there.
