# You're Not Behind. You're Early in Something That Doesn't Have a Name Yet.

Here's what "behind" actually feels like.

You open LinkedIn on a Tuesday morning and someone you vaguely know is announcing their next thing. A newsletter. A cohort. An agency. A course. They have a crisp logo and a clean sentence that explains what they do. They have 11,000 followers and 40 comments that say "this is what I've been waiting for."

And you've been building too. You have a thing. Maybe it's a tool, or a newsletter draft that's been sitting in your notes for three months, or a side project that keeps evolving before it ships. You're doing the work. But you look at that post and something sinks.

You feel behind.

I want to tell you something about that feeling. It's lying to you.

Not because you're secretly amazing and you just don't see it — that's the motivational poster version. I'm talking about something more specific.

You feel behind because your brain is trying to find a map, and the territory you're in doesn't have one yet. You're doing something that doesn't have a job title, a credentialing path, a salary benchmark, or a subreddit with 50,000 members who can tell you what "good" looks like. And so when your brain can't find the map, it decides you're lost.

But you're not lost. You're early.

There's a difference. Lost means you're in the wrong place. Early means the place exists, the map just hasn't been drawn yet.

Think about it this way. In 1994, there wasn't a job called "growth hacker." In 2008, "data scientist" was barely a phrase. In 2018, most companies didn't have a Head of Community. Those roles existed in the form of people doing the work before anyone had figured out what to call it. Some of those people felt behind. Some of them felt like frauds. All of them were just early.

What you're doing right now — this thing that lives at the intersection of two fields you care about, this skill set you've built by accident, this audience you're building before you fully understand why — it probably doesn't have a name yet either.

And here's the problem with not having a name. You can't compare yourself to a benchmark that doesn't exist. The person you saw with the crisp logo and the clean sentence — they found a lane that already had a label. Maybe they're ahead in that lane. They're not ahead of you in yours. You don't share a lane.

I think about this a lot in the context of people building in public right now. There are people documenting their process of building things with AI, writing about the intersection of sales and technology, figuring out what it means to be a creator who is also a builder who is also a practitioner. The rubric doesn't exist. The "successful practitioner of this specific combination of things" profile isn't in the database.

So what does behind even mean here?

It means nothing. You made it up.

What's real is this: you're doing a thing, the thing is developing, and it doesn't look like a finished product yet. That's not being behind. That's being inside the work.

The people who look like they have it figured out are usually either much further from the beginning than you are, or they're doing a fundamentally different thing that already had a map. Neither of those makes you behind. They just make them older in their lane, or cleaner in theirs.

Your lane is being built by you, as you walk it.

Which is uncomfortable. I know. The thing I've noticed about building in undefined spaces is that the uncertainty doesn't feel like freedom. It mostly feels like falling slowly, in a direction you've decided to trust. You're making decisions without a playbook, getting feedback without a rubric, growing without a chart that tells you if you're on track.

And some days you open LinkedIn and someone has a logo and 40 comments and you feel that thing.

When you do, try this. Ask yourself: is this person actually doing the same thing I'm doing, in a more advanced version? Or are they doing a version of a thing that already had a name? Because if it's the second one, they're not your benchmark. They're just more visible.

You're not behind. You're early in something that doesn't have a name yet.

That's not a consolation prize. That's where all the interesting things come from.
