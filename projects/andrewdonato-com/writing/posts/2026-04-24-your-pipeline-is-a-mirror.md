# Your Pipeline Is a Mirror

I used to think I was a prospecting machine. Ask anyone who knew me in my first few years selling — I was on the phone constantly, booking meetings, adding names to the CRM. I had conviction about my number. I had conviction about my effort.

Then my manager pulled up my pipeline on a Monday and just... looked at it. She didn't say anything for a long moment. Then she goes, "Where are your Q3 deals?"

I looked at the screen. The pipeline was loaded. Early stage, mostly. A cluster of opportunities sitting at the same stage they'd been sitting at for six weeks. A graveyard of "following up" notes in the activity log. No movement toward the bottom.

She didn't need to explain it to me. I could see it. Every deal I was chasing was in the same bucket — the comfortable bucket. The bucket where you're technically selling, technically active, technically "working the pipeline." The bucket where nobody is telling you no yet.

I wasn't a prospecting machine. I was an objection-avoidance machine. An efficient one, too.

Here's what I've learned since then: your pipeline doesn't lie. You do. But the pipeline holds still.

A pipeline is a mirror. Not the fun kind you look into before you leave the house, where you make a few adjustments and declare yourself ready. The kind that catches you walking by unexpectedly. The kind that shows you what's actually there.

Look at your pipeline right now — I mean really look at it, stage by stage — and tell me what you see.

If most of your weight is at the top, in the exploratory and discovery stages, you've been spending your time adding names and booking meetings. Good activity. But somewhere between the first call and the proposal, you've been finding reasons to slow down. The uncomfortable part — committing to a timeline, asking for the next step, forcing a decision — you've been postponing that. The pipeline knows.

If the opposite is true, if the bottom half is crammed and the top is empty, you've been running the plays on existing deals but you stopped planting. You've been telling yourself you're busy because you are busy. But busy with what's here isn't the same as building what's next. The pipeline knows that too.

If your pipeline looks like a perfectly even funnel — congratulations, you probably work in a mature territory with steady inbound and you've been doing this long enough that the muscle memory kicked in. Or you're doing the work at every stage and it's clicking. Either way, keep going.

But if it's lumpy — and most pipelines are lumpy — the lump is information. It's an honest accounting of where your effort went and where it didn't. The shape of your pipeline is a record of your decisions. What you chose to work on. What you chose to push off. What you told yourself was "not quite ready" when really you just weren't ready to hear no.

I've done pipeline reviews with dozens of reps over the years. The pattern I see most often isn't laziness. It's avoidance dressed up as activity. Reps who are genuinely working hard, logging calls, sending emails, feeling busy — but clustering their effort in the stage of the process where they feel most comfortable and avoiding the stage that requires them to push.

Top-of-funnel heavy usually means the rep is good at starting conversations but bad at advancing them. They like the energy of the first call. They're uncomfortable asking for commitment.

Middle-heavy usually means they're getting to demo or proposal but stalling on close. They've done the pitch perfectly, they're waiting for the prospect to volunteer a yes. They're not creating urgency. They're hoping.

Bottom-heavy with no new blood means they're managing existing pipeline like it's inventory. But pipeline isn't inventory. It expires. The deals sitting there since Q1 aren't going to suddenly decide to move. They needed to be pushed months ago, and now the window on most of them has closed.

The mirror doesn't tell you why the pipeline looks the way it does. It just shows you what's there. The why is yours to figure out.

Usually it's not a skill problem. Usually it's a comfort zone problem. The parts of the sales process that feel natural, you've been doing. The parts that feel like they could result in rejection, you've been managing around.

Which is human. Completely human. Nobody likes being told no, especially by a deal they've invested real time and energy into. But the avoidance doesn't make the no go away. It just delays it until the deal dies quietly in the CRM and you move it to closed-lost without ever having the conversation that might have saved it.

Here's what I'd actually do with this: open your pipeline this week and look at the distribution. Not at the individual deals — at the shape. Where is the weight? What stage is most represented? And be honest with yourself about why.

Because the pipeline will tell you what happened last quarter. The honest version. Not the story you told your manager on the weekly forecast call. Not the version where you were working hard and the timing was just off. The version where your time actually went.

The mirror doesn't judge. It just reflects.

What it shows you is up to you to deal with.
