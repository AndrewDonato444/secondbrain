### "The moat isn't the tech anymore"
**Pillar:** What I'm Watching

**Hook:** "Everyone's about to be able to build everything. So what actually matters now?"

**Talking points:**
- The answer to "can you build it" is rapidly becoming yes for almost everyone. AI coding tools, no-code platforms, agents — the barrier to building is collapsing
- So the question changes. It's no longer "can you build it?" It's two new questions: should you build it, and can you distribute it?
- The "should you" question matters because just because you can build something doesn't mean anyone needs it. That's a product instinct problem, not a technical one
- But the real game is distribution. Your competitor can build what you build. Accept that. What they can't duplicate is your users. They can't duplicate the data your users are putting into your platform. They can't duplicate the relationships you've built
- Think about it — two identical products, one has 10,000 users generating data and feedback, the other just launched. The tech is the same. The moat is completely different
- This is why sales and marketing people are about to be the most valuable people in the room. The battle isn't who builds the best tech — it's who builds it fastest and gets it into people's hands fastest
- If you're in demand gen, performance marketing, sales — your skills aren't being replaced. They're becoming the bottleneck everyone needs to solve
- End with: "What are you better at — building things, or getting them into people's hands? Because the second one is about to be worth a lot more."

**First comment:**
Here's the shift happening right now:

2020: "Can we build this?" was the hard question. You needed engineers, time, money.

2026: "Can we build this?" — yes, almost always. AI tools collapsed the barrier.

The new hard questions:
1. Should we build it? (Product instinct — does anyone actually need this?)
2. Can we distribute it? (Go-to-market — can you get it into hands faster than the other person who built the same thing?)

Your competitor can match your features. They cannot match:
- Your users
- Your users' data
- Your brand trust
- Your distribution channels
- The speed at which you learn from real usage

The moat moved. It's not technical anymore. It's distribution.

If you're in sales or marketing and you've been worried about AI replacing you — flip the script. The builders are about to need you more than you need them.

What's your distribution advantage? Genuinely curious.

### "Why people don't buy what you're selling (it's not the price)"
**Pillar:** Things I Learned the Hard Way

**Hook:** "I've been selling for 17 years and the biggest lesson I've learned is that people don't buy deliverables. They buy emotions."

**Talking points:**
- When someone says no to what you're selling, your instinct is to add more features, lower the price, or explain harder. None of that works
- There are four emotional states that have to be in place before someone says yes — especially when the price is high
- First is trust. Not "I like you" trust — "this person actually understands my problem" trust. You build it by asking questions nobody else asks
- Second is certainty. They have to believe the outcome is achievable for them specifically. Most people hear a pitch and think "cool, but my situation is different." You have to close that gap
- Third is urgency — but not fake urgency. Real urgency. What does doing nothing for another 6 months actually cost them? Make that number vivid
- Fourth one surprised me: identity alignment. At high price points, buying is an identity statement. "Am I the kind of person who invests at this level?" If your buyers don't see themselves that way, they won't buy no matter how good your pitch is
- End with: "Next time someone says no, don't lower the price. Ask yourself which of those four emotions you missed."

**First comment:**
The four emotional states behind every yes:

1. Trust — "This person actually gets my problem"
2. Certainty — "This outcome is achievable for ME specifically"
3. Urgency — "The cost of waiting is real and specific"
4. Identity — "People like me invest in this"

Most salespeople only work on #1 and #3. They build rapport and create deadlines. But #2 and #4 are where deals actually get won or lost.

Which one is weakest in your current pitch? Genuinely curious.

### "You can just ask"
**Pillar:** How to Think About [X]

**Hook:** "There's a debate right now about whether AI is making people dumber. I think they're asking the wrong question."

**Talking points:**
- There's a real risk that people outsource their thinking to AI. I get that concern. But that's not the only way to use it
- Think about Google. We spent years getting it drilled into our heads: "When you have a question, Google it." Nobody has a problem with that anymore because we're all comfortable with it
- The next version of that is: when you have a question, engage AI as a thought partner. Not to think for you — to think with you
- The difference matters. "Write me an email" is outsourcing. "Here's what I'm trying to say — what am I missing?" is thinking together
- When someone asks me a question now, my first response is usually: "What have you asked?" Not "did you Google it" — "did you engage with this before bringing it to me?"
- The people who are going to get the most out of AI aren't the ones who use it as a shortcut. They're the ones who use it as a sparring partner
- End with: "Next time you're stuck on something, before you ask a colleague, try asking AI first — not for the answer, but for better questions. See what happens."

### "Your first sales hire should sit in customer success"
**Pillar:** Things I Learned the Hard Way

**Hook:** "Every time I've onboarded a new rep, I've wanted to do the same thing: put them on the CS team for two weeks first."

**Talking points:**
- When a new rep starts, we throw them into product training and then onto the phones. They learn features. They don't learn customers
- The best reps I've ever managed all had one thing in common: they deeply understood what happens after the deal closes. They could sell the outcome because they'd seen it
- When a rep sits in CS — handles onboarding calls, listens to renewal conversations, hears what customers complain about — they learn the language of the customer, not the language of the pitch deck
- You know what happens when a rep on a discovery call says "yeah, I've actually heard that from a lot of our customers during onboarding"? The prospect's guard drops immediately. That's not a technique. That's real knowledge
- Most companies silo sales and CS. The result: reps sell things that CS can't deliver, and CS resents sales for overpromising. Two weeks of cross-training fixes that
- End with: "What's one thing your CS team knows about your customers that your sales team has no idea about?"

**First comment:**
Here's what actually happens when a new rep spends 2 weeks in CS before selling:

- They hear the exact words customers use to describe their problems (not the marketing version)
- They see what causes churn — so they stop overselling features that underdeliver
- They build relationships with CS before there's any tension
- On their first discovery call, they sound like someone who's been at the company for a year

The pushback I always get: "We need them selling ASAP, we can't waste 2 weeks."

My answer: a rep who closes their first deal in week 6 with real customer knowledge is worth more than a rep who flails for 3 months with none.

Has anyone actually tried this? Would love to hear how it went.

### "The marginal cost of completeness is near zero"
**Pillar:** How to Think About [X]

**Hook:** "Someone wrote a line that completely changed how I think about doing work with AI. 'The marginal cost of completeness is near zero.'"

**Talking points:**
- I saw this in someone's instructions for how AI should work with them. The full line was: "The marginal cost of completeness is near zero with AI. Do the whole thing. Do it right. Never present a workaround when the real fix exists."
- Think about what that means. When the cost of doing something 100% is basically the same as doing it 80%, why would you ever ship at 80%?
- We've been trained our whole careers to cut scope. "What's the MVP? What can we skip? Where can we save time?" That made sense when every extra feature cost real hours and real money
- But now? Asking AI to also write the documentation, also handle the edge cases, also clean up the code — that costs you almost nothing extra. The effort between "good enough" and "done done" has collapsed
- This doesn't just apply to coding. Writing a proposal? Don't send a rough draft — let AI help you polish it. Building a pitch deck? Don't leave placeholder slides. Prepping for a meeting? Don't wing the last 20% — prep the whole thing
- The new standard isn't "did you finish?" It's "did you finish completely?" Because now everyone can
- The people who still cut corners aren't saving time — they're just choosing to be less thorough than they could be for free
- End with: "What's one thing you've been shipping at 80% that you could easily take to 100%? The cost is probably close to zero. So why haven't you?"

**First comment:**
The full quote that sparked this:

"The marginal cost of completeness is near zero with AI. Do the whole thing. Do it right. Do it with tests. Do it with documentation. Never present a workaround when the real fix exists. The standard isn't 'good enough' — it's 'holy shit, that's done.'"

This reframes the whole "MVP mindset" we've all been taught. When completeness is basically free, incomplete work is a choice — not a constraint.

A few places I've noticed this in my own work:
- Emails I used to send rough, I now let AI polish in 10 seconds
- Docs I used to skip, I now generate alongside the work
- Edge cases I used to punt on, I now handle because it takes 2 more minutes

The bar moved. For everyone. The question is whether you moved with it.

### "You don't need to learn to code. You need a Saturday." (POSTED Apr 18)
**Pillar:** How to Think About [X]
**Scheduled:** LinkedIn Apr 18 (manual) · YouTube Shorts + TikTok Apr 22 9:00am ET (Late.Dev post `69e384951710bfea7bc2b7d5`)

**Hook:** "I've been telling you to build something for two weeks. If you haven't started yet, it's not because you can't. It's because nobody told you where to start. So today I'm going to remove every excuse."

**Talking points:**
- There are two reasons people don't start building. First one: "I don't know how to code." Second one: "I don't know what to build." Let's kill both of them in the next 60 seconds
- Reason one first. In 2026, building something doesn't mean writing code from scratch anymore. It means describing what you want to a tool that writes it for you. That's the whole thing
- Pick one tool. Replit, Lovable, Bolt, v0 — they all work. Stop researching which one is best. The best one is the one you open today. I'd start with Replit if you want something real and hostable on the web, but honestly, the tool barely matters
- You type "build me a page that does X." It builds it. You say "no, make the button blue." It makes it blue. You're not an engineer. You're a director
- Now reason two. "I don't know what to build." This is the one that actually kills people. They sit there trying to come up with The Big Idea. Skip that entire problem
- Build the smallest annoying thing in your week. A page that lists the five things you keep Googling. A dinner rotator so you stop having the same "what are we eating" conversation. A quiz that tells your industry how replaceable they are. A dashboard for the one number you check on your phone twenty times a day. Doesn't matter what it is
- Nobody else has to want it. You're not starting a company. You're learning the loop — "I thought of a thing, I built the thing, the thing exists now." Once you feel that loop once, you can't un-feel it
- Here's the weekend plan. Saturday morning, one hour — open the tool, describe the stupidest version of your idea, watch it build. Saturday afternoon, two hours — iterate. Sunday, one hour — show it to one person. That's it. Four hours
- And if you're looking at all of this and still can't pick what to build — there's a prompt in the comments. Copy it, paste it into Claude or ChatGPT, answer four questions, and it'll tell you exactly what to build and what tool to use. Zero thinking required
- End with: "The hardest part of building isn't the building. It's starting. Everyone who's building right now had one Saturday morning where they opened the tool for the first time and had no idea what they were doing. That's the bar. What's the smallest annoying problem in your week? Because that's your first project. Go."

**First comment:**
I've said "go build something" on every episode for two weeks. I can tell from the replies that people are nodding but not doing. So here's every excuse removed, in one post.

**"I don't know how to code."**
You don't need to. Pick one of these:
- Replit — fastest way to build and host a real app someone can visit
- Lovable — describe the app, it builds it
- Bolt — similar, great for full-stack
- v0 by Vercel — best for UI-heavy stuff
- Claude Code — if you want to build on your own machine

Don't spend a day picking. Pick the first one that loads. The tool matters way less than starting.

**"I don't know what to build."**
Stop trying to have The Big Idea. Build the smallest annoying thing in your own week. Here's a menu — pick the one that makes you go "oh yeah, that annoys me":

*Save yourself time at work*
- Weekly report generator — paste bullets, get a formatted update
- Meeting notes cleaner — dump raw notes, get a summary + action items
- Email template filler for the same emails you write every week
- Client status page you can share instead of writing the update
- Resume tailor — paste a job description, get your resume rewritten for it

*Fix something at home*
- Dinner rotator — what you ate, what's next, what's easy tonight
- Gift log — who you gave what, so you don't repeat
- Chore chart for kids with sticker tracking
- "Days since" counter — last vet visit, last haircut, last oil change
- A shared family notes page that doesn't require an app

*Something fun that might actually go viral*
- "How cooked are you?" quiz for your specific industry
- "What should I watch tonight" randomizer pulling from your own list
- Personality quiz for your niche ("what kind of [X] are you?")
- A roast generator for your friend group
- A decision coin-flipper with your actual options loaded

*Get sharper at content or sales*
- LinkedIn post formatter — paste a rant, get a post
- Video hook generator for your topic
- Cold email personalizer — drop in a prospect, get an angle
- Content calendar with AI-filled slots
- A "what should I post about today" generator pulling from your notes

*A tiny dashboard you'll check once a day*
- Habit tracker that's just a bookmarked page
- One-number dashboard for whatever you refresh your phone for
- Sports, fantasy, or portfolio scorecard
- A "what's on my plate today" view pulling from a few places

Nobody else has to want it. You're learning the loop, not launching a company.

**Still can't pick? Paste this into Claude or ChatGPT:**

> I just watched a video about building my first thing with AI tools this weekend. I don't know where to start. Help me figure out what to build.
>
> Ask me these 4 questions, one at a time, and wait for my answer before asking the next one:
> 1. What do you do for work?
> 2. What's one thing you do every week that annoys you?
> 3. What's one thing in your personal life you wish were easier or automated?
> 4. How comfortable are you with computers on a scale of 1 to 10?
>
> After I answer all four, give me:
> - 3 specific things I could build this weekend, tailored to what I told you
> - For each one, tell me which tool to use (Replit, Lovable, Bolt, v0, or Claude Code) and why
> - Pick the one you think is easiest for me and give me a 4-hour plan (Saturday morning, Saturday afternoon, Sunday)
> - The exact first sentence I should paste into the tool to get started
>
> Be direct. Don't give me ten options. Tell me the one you think I should build and why.

That's it. Four questions, three ideas, one plan. You go from "I don't know what to build" to "I'm building this one specific thing starting Saturday at 9am" in about five minutes.

**The weekend plan:**
- Saturday AM (1 hr) — open the tool, describe the dumbest version of your idea, watch it build
- Saturday PM (2 hrs) — iterate, refine, add the one thing that makes it feel real
- Sunday (1 hr) — show it to one human. Doesn't matter who. Break the seal.

Four hours. That's it. You're not committing to a career change. You're committing to a Saturday.

The first time you describe something and watch it actually appear — that's the moment the game changes for you. You can't un-feel it. And once you've felt it once, "build something" stops being vague internet advice and becomes a thing you just do when something annoys you.

What's the smallest annoying thing in your week right now? Reply with it. I'll tell you what I'd use to build it this weekend.
