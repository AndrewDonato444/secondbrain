### 📍 Mon 2026-04-20 — "The discovery question that makes marketing flinch"
**File:** [posts/calibration-discovery-question.md](posts/calibration-discovery-question.md) · **Archetype:** Operator's Tell

```
I have one question I ask every buyer once we're past the demo and into pricing.

"Where did you actually first hear about us?"

The answer is almost never Google. They'll say Google, because Google was the last click. But push gently and it's a podcast, a Slack DM from a coworker, a LinkedIn post they can't quite place, sometimes a conversation at a conference two years ago. The thing that moved them and the thing that delivered them to the form are almost never the same thing.

Marketing knows this and hates it. Not because they're wrong — because the part they can measure is the small part. Last-click attribution is a flashlight in a dark room. It tells you exactly what's directly in front of it and nothing about the room.

I don't have a clever fix. I just stopped trusting the dashboards over the buyers. Half my budget conversations now start with "I asked the last twelve closed-won how they actually found us, and here's what they said." That's not a methodology. It's just listening to the people who already paid you.

Sales hears what's true. Marketing measures what's measurable. Most companies still pretend those are the same job.
```

### 📍 Wed 2026-04-22 — "The org chart is fiction now"
**File:** [posts/2026-04-22-org-chart-is-fiction.md](posts/2026-04-22-org-chart-is-fiction.md) · **Archetype:** Convergence Take

```
Look at the last week of work and count the times someone did a job that wasn't theirs.

The CSM who shipped in-product copy because the PMM was underwater. The AE who wrote the demand brief because nobody else knew what the prospect actually wanted. The product manager who took a sales call to debug a deal that was really a roadmap problem. The marketer who built a competitive battle card from a Gong clip because that's where the truth lived.

We call these "stretch assignments" or "hybrid skills" or "T-shaped people." We give them performance review credit. We do not redraw the org chart.

I think we should. Not because the chart is wrong on paper — the chart is correct as a map of how budget moves and who reports to whom. But it's wrong as a map of where the work actually happens. Two different documents trying to pretend they're one document.

I don't have a tidy answer here. The companies I see growing fastest aren't the ones with the cleanest functional walls. They're the ones where everyone has stopped pretending the wall is there. Sales does product work. Product does sales work. Whoever's closest to the customer at that moment owns the moment.

The org chart is now a historical document. Every week the work doesn't match it is a week the chart is wrong, not a week the work is wrong.
```

### 📍 Thu 2026-04-23 — "The agent is the new landing page"
**File:** [posts/2026-04-23-agent-is-new-landing-page.md](posts/2026-04-23-agent-is-new-landing-page.md) · **Archetype:** Frontier Translation

```
Marketing teams I talk to are still optimizing landing pages.

The landing page isn't the first interaction anymore. For a real and growing chunk of pipeline, the first interaction is with an agent — a chatbot on someone else's site that recommended you, a sales agent answering a prospect's question before they'd even thought to visit yours, an in-product agent inside a tool the buyer already uses, a "what should I buy for this problem" prompt the buyer typed into Claude or ChatGPT yesterday.

I'm not saying landing pages are dead. I'm saying the conversion event moved upstream of the page, and most marketing budgets haven't moved with it.

Think about who owns the language an agent uses to describe your product. Is it your PMM? Is it whoever wrote your sales decks three years ago? Is it the model's training data, scraped from a Reddit thread about you that nobody on your team has read? Most companies cannot answer this and have not noticed they cannot answer it.

The landing page was a brilliant primitive when the funnel started at search. The agent is the new primitive when the funnel starts at conversation. Whoever owns the prompt that describes you to an agent — and there is one, somewhere, even if no one wrote it on purpose — owns the top of the funnel now.

Most companies still hire for landing page conversion. They have not yet hired for agent conversion. That gap is going to get expensive fast.
```

## Outline notes (for Sunday draft pass)

Brief outlines for each Active 6 angle. Drafts get written in `posts/YYYY-MM-DD-slug.md` when picked.

### #2 — The org chart is fiction now
- **Open:** Look at a recent week and count how many cross-functional handoffs actually happened cleanly.
- **Move:** CSMs are shipping in-product copy. AEs are writing demand briefs. PMMs are running cold outbound. We pretend it's "hybrid" but it's a category collapse.
- **Land:** The org chart is now a historical document. Every week the work doesn't match it is a week the chart is wrong, not a week the work is wrong.
- **Watch:** Don't slip into "here's what to do" — stay descriptive.

### #3 — The most predictive deal signal isn't in your CRM fields
- **Open:** I've stopped trusting MEDDIC fields and started trusting one signal.
- **Move:** When the buyer adds people to the email thread without being asked, the deal closes 80% of the time. That isn't a sales metric — it's product-market-fit showing up in the CRM.
- **Land:** Most leading indicators of revenue aren't sales metrics at all. They're product signals that happened to land in a sales tool.

### #4 — Distribution is the new product strategy
- **Open:** A roadmap meeting and a GTM planning meeting are usually scheduled by different people, in different rooms, with different vocabulary.
- **Move:** That used to make sense. It doesn't anymore. Distribution decisions now constrain what's worth building, and product decisions now constrain what's possible to distribute. They've become one document that we pretend are two.
- **Land:** The companies pulling ahead aren't the ones with better products *or* better distribution. They're the ones who stopped making them separate decisions.
- **Lineage check:** BOL spark says "sales/marketing become more valuable." This post says "the planning artifact itself collapsed." Different claim — passes the no-rip test.

### #5 — The agent is the new landing page
- **Open:** When was the last time you built a landing page expecting it to be the *first* thing a prospect interacts with?
- **Move:** It already isn't. The first interaction is increasingly with a deployed agent — a sales agent, a support agent, an in-product agent answering questions before any human knows the prospect exists. Marketing optimizes the page; the page is the wrong unit now.
- **Land:** Whoever owns the agent's prompt owns the funnel. Most companies have not figured out which function that is.

### #6 — You can't forecast what you don't believe
- **Open:** Forecast accuracy correlates almost perfectly with one thing, and it's not the tool.
- **Move:** It correlates with how willing reps are to call their own deals dead. The technical skill of forecasting is actually the emotional skill of admitting a deal is gone. Most forecasting tools are betting that better dashboards solve a confessional problem.
- **Land:** You don't need a better tool. You need reps who've made peace with calling time of death.
