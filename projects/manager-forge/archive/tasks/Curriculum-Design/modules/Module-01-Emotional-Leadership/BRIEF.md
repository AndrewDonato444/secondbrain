## Executive Overview

The **Emotional Leadership Playbook** is the foundational module in the Emotional Leadership pillar of ManagerForge. It solves the #1 unspoken problem new sales managers face: understanding that managing is **equally about managing emotions AND managing performance**—and that trying to do one without the other results in failure.

This module is the wedge that opens the door to all downstream topics (managing up, difficult conversations, operating rhythm). It establishes the core philosophy: **You cannot change people's emotional archetypes in 6-12 months. Only they can change themselves. Your job is to evaluate, understand, and leverage their existing emotional momentum toward results.**

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

Managing people is as much about managing their emotions as managing their performance. You need to do **both**. If you don't do both, you will struggle—you'll wonder why you're not getting results when really you're probably just not doing one or the other enough.

**More specifically:**

1. You are going to face complex emotional situations constantly—reps venting, dumping personal stuff, sharing obstacles. You are required to have emotional interactions with your team.
2. You have two bad options: Try to make it unemotional (push emotions away, "we don't do that here") or try to change people's emotional archetypes to match your style or what you think they should be.
3. The right option: **Respond to complexity. Evaluate each rep's emotional archetype. Understand their triggers and what they're chasing emotionally. Then use that existing momentum to drive results.**
4. You're also in a unique position as agent for the company (ensuring performance) while being someone who believes in people. This creates tension. Learn to manage both.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Less isolated (this is normal, everyone faces it)
- More capable (there's a framework for understanding this)
- Clear on what they can and can't control (you can't change people; you can understand and leverage them)

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers typically choose one of two paths—both wrong:

**Path 1: The Unemotional Approach**
"We don't do emotional stuff here. This is about sales. Let's drive the bus, get results, and ignore everything else."

**Path 2: The Uniform Structure Approach**
They force everyone into the same structure/cadence because it's easiest and replicates what made them successful as an IC. Same 1:1 format, same deal review format, same team meeting format.

**Result of both:** Roughly 20-30% of the team latches on (these are their "wins"), 30-40% don't really care, and 30-40% actively hate what you're doing and think it's wrong. The manager then concludes they have to convince the 30-40% to "see the light"—which means trying to change them.

### The Real Issue

**You cannot change people's emotional archetypes in 6-12 months.** Only they can change themselves. The fact that you're going to be their manager for maybe one year (if that) before they move to a new role or company means you don't have time to change who they are fundamentally.

Instead: Evaluate their emotional archetype. Understand what triggers them, what they're chasing, how they operate under pressure. Then **use their existing momentum to drive results.**

Example tension:
- Rep is analytical → manager thinks "too slow, I need to speed them up"
- Rep is global/big-picture → manager thinks "too scattered, I need to make them detail-oriented"

**Wrong move:** Spend 6 months trying to change them.  
**Right move:** Use their analytical strength (deep thinking, data rigor) to drive results. Use their global thinking to see market gaps or strategic opportunities.

## Key Frameworks & Concepts

### 1. **The Emotion/Performance Duality**

Managing = Emotion Management + Performance Management

- **Emotion Management:** Understanding what people are feeling, where it's coming from, how it impacts them and the team. Responding to venting, dumps, personal obstacles. Building psychological safety.
- **Performance Management:** Metrics, accountability, results, pipeline, quota, coaching toward targets.

**Most new managers:** Do one really well, neglect the other.
- The IC-turned-manager who crushed their own quota often excels at performance but avoids emotions.
- The empathetic manager often gets great team morale but doesn't push on results.

**The truth:** You need both. Missing either one creates problems.

### 2. **Emotional Archetypes**

People have different emotional "styles"—how they process information, what triggers them, what motivates them, how they act under pressure.

**Some rough categories (not a taxonomy, just examples):**
- **Analytical:** Slows down, digs into data, wants certainty before moving
- **Driving/Urgent:** Wants to move fast, push through, results-focused, can miss nuance
- **Relational:** Cares about people/alignment, needs connection, can get bogged down
- **Global:** Big-picture thinker, sees patterns, can miss details

**Key insight:** People don't change their archetypes. You work with who they are.

**Manager's job:** Evaluate each rep's archetype → understand what works for them → leverage it toward results.

### 3. **You as Agent + Human**

New managers sit in a unique tension:

- **Agent for the Company:** You're responsible for performance, quota, forecasting, ensuring high-velocity execution. You represent company interests.
- **Human Who Believes in People:** You also have a conscience. You know when the company is asking something unreasonable. You see reps struggling and care about their wellbeing.

**The dichotomy:** Sometimes these conflict. Company wants X, you believe Y is right. You have to manage both—hit performance while protecting what you believe in (and keeping your team's trust).

**This is normal and it's hard.** Acknowledging this tension is step one.

### 4. **Emotional Contagion**

Your emotions cascade to your team. This is neuroscience, not feel-good talk.

- Your stress activates their stress responses (mirror neurons, limbic system sync)
- Your visible anxiety makes them anxious
- Your calm makes them capable of being calm

**New managers often don't realize:** The fact that you're stressed about managing, the fact that you're visible to your team in that stress, directly impacts their performance.

**This is why regulation matters:** You can't manage people's emotions until you manage your own.

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: The Identity Crisis (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand the IC → Manager transition is real and disorienting
- Identify the "super salesperson syndrome" (can't let go of selling, can't delegate)
- Introduce the idea that emotional leadership is as important as performance leadership

**Key Teaching Points:**
- You were successful as an IC because of certain things (hustle, individual output, maybe charisma)
- Those things don't work the same way when you manage 5+ people
- The trap: Trying to replicate yourself instead of developing others
- Your team doesn't need another top salesperson; they need a leader
- This transition is an identity crisis for a reason—you're becoming someone different

**Story/Examples:**
- Personal story: How Andrew struggled with this (reference specific failure)
- Real example: Rep who was crushed about manager taking away territory but then realized manager was freeing them up to focus on coaching

**Tension to Address:**
- "If I'm not selling, what's my value?" (Redirect: your value is in developing your team to sell)
- "My team thinks I don't get it anymore." (You need to prove you do—but differently)

### Sub-Module 2: The Archetype Discovery (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Introduce the concept of emotional archetypes
- Help managers understand their own style
- Create the foundational tool for evaluating reps

**Key Teaching Points:**
- People operate from different emotional/processing styles
- These styles are baked in; you don't change them in 6-12 months
- The key is NOT to homogenize; it's to understand and leverage
- Your tendency as a successful manager: Find what works for you, apply it to everyone, wonder why it doesn't work
- Better approach: Understand what works for each person, meet them there

**Self-Assessment Tool:**
- "What's your archetype?" (Managers identify their own style)
- "What archetype(s) do you naturally gravitate toward?" (Managers notice bias)
- "Which of your reps challenges you most because they're different?" (Points to growth area)

**Tension to Address:**
- "But I need consistency." (Consistency is in your values/expectations, not in your method)
- "I don't have time to personalize everything." (You're right—but you're also not doing it efficiently if you're applying uniform methods that only work for 30% of your team)

### Sub-Module 3: The Emotion/Performance Integration (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Establish that both emotion and performance management are required
- Help managers see that ignoring either one creates problems
- Introduce the "20/30/40 split" concept

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Roughly 20-30% of your team will naturally latch onto your style/cadence
- Roughly 30-40% don't care much either way
- Roughly 30-40% will actively resist or dislike what you're doing
- Your assumption: "I have to make the 30-40% agree with me"
- Better assumption: "I have to understand why they're resisting and adapt"
- The 30-40% aren't wrong; they just have a different emotional archetype. Your job is to meet them.

**Business Impact:**
- Ignoring emotions → team disengagement, churn, missed quota
- Ignoring performance → no accountability, lack of rigor, results suffer
- Both → retention, results, sustainable team

**Tension to Address:**
- "Doesn't that mean I'm being manipulative?" (No, understanding people and meeting them is leadership)
- "Won't that make me soft on performance?" (No, you still drive results—just with people intact)

### Sub-Module 4: The Emotional Dump & Boundaries (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Prepare managers for the reality that reps WILL dump emotions on them
- Give them a framework for responding (not accepting everything, not rejecting everything)
- Establish psychological safety without personal boundaries collapse

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Reps are going to come to you with personal stuff, team frustration, venting
- This is a sign of trust—they feel safe with you
- The trap: Becoming the emotional dump site instead of a leader
- The other trap: Shutting down emotion entirely
- The right approach: Listen, acknowledge, validate—then move to action/ownership
- You are not their therapist; you are their leader who cares
- Boundaries are about clarity, not coldness

**Phrases/Scripts:**
- "I hear you, that sucks. Here's what I can do..."
- "I get it. What's one thing we can control here?"
- "That's frustrating. Let's talk about what's in your control vs. what's in mine."

**Tension to Address:**
- "If I set boundaries, will they think I don't care?" (Trust the opposite—clear boundaries show strength and clarity)
- "What if they get upset?" (They might. That's OK. You're still doing your job.)

### Sub-Module 5: The Agent/Human Tension (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Acknowledge the real ethical/emotional tension managers face
- Help them navigate the gray zone where company wants X and they believe Y
- Introduce the concept of "managing both sides"

**Key Teaching Points:**
- You will have moments where company goals and your beliefs about what's right diverge
- This is not a failure of you or the company; it's the reality of the role
- Example tensions: 
  - Company wants to cut compensation; you think it's unfair but won't change
  - Company wants aggressive quota; you think your team is already burnt out
  - Company wants someone fired; you see potential for growth
- Your job: Be transparent with both sides; manage the tension; don't pretend it doesn't exist
- Reps trust you because they sense you care about them. Don't lose that by being purely a company mouthpiece.
- But don't lose company credibility by being purely an advocate against company interests.
- You're the bridge. That's hard. That's also your value.

**Business Impact:**
- Managers who manage this tension well = trusted by both team and leadership
- Managers who pick a side = lose credibility with the other side

**Tension to Address:**
- "Won't I get fired if I challenge the company?" (Sometimes, if you do it wrong. But your credibility is your tool—use it wisely)
- "How much should I be transparent with my team?" (Vulnerable, not reckless. Show you're aware of tension; don't make them your therapist)

## Learner Pack Components

### Worksheets (1–2 per sub-module)

1. **Identity Crisis:**
   - "What made you successful as an IC?" (reflection)
   - "What are you doing now that didn't work as an IC?" (honest assessment)
   - "Where's the biggest gap between your IC skills and your manager skills?" (identify)

2. **Archetype Discovery:**
   - "What's your emotional archetype?" (diagnostic)
   - "What's your team's breakdown?" (map the diversity)
   - "Where are you biased? Who challenges you?" (self-awareness)

3. **Emotion/Performance:**
   - "The 20/30/40 split: Where do your reps fall?" (assess team)
   - "For the 30-40% who resist: Why?" (diagnose—not defend)
   - "How are you showing up on emotion? On performance?" (honest look)

4. **Emotional Dump & Boundaries:**
   - "What's my venting pattern?" (When do I dump, where, on whom?)
   - "How am I currently responding to team venting?" (current state)
   - "What boundaries would make me feel sustainable?" (set them)

5. **Agent/Human Tension:**
   - "Where do I feel the biggest tension?" (identify the gap)
   - "Who do I trust more: my team or my leadership?" (be honest)
   - "How can I be transparent without being reckless?" (navigate)

### Self-Assessment Tools

- **Emotional Regulation Self-Check:** How well are you managing your own stress? (5-question diagnostic + interpretation)
- **Archetype Evaluation for Each Rep:** Template for assessing each rep's style + how to leverage it
- **Boundaries Assessment:** Are your boundaries clear? Are they sustainable? (scoring rubric)

### Role-Play Scenarios

1. **The Venting Rep:** "I hate our leads, I'm not hitting quota, and I don't feel supported."  
   Task: Respond in a way that validates, sets boundaries, and moves to action.

2. **The Resistant Rep:** Refuses to follow your new cadence/structure.  
   Task: Diagnose their archetype, then script how you'd have a conversation to meet them where they are.

3. **The Company Pressure:** Director says "We need 20% cuts or headcount reduction."  
   Task: Script how you'd manage both sides—transparency with team, clear communication upward.

4. **The Mirror:** A rep's anxiety is rising; team energy is dropping.  
   Task: Identify what's contagious from you, then script how you'd reset the dynamic.

### Weekly Zoom Discussion Prompts

1. "What was your biggest identity crisis moment in the first 90 days? How did you move past it?"
2. "What's one archetype on your team that's completely different from you? How are you handling it?"
3. "When did you realize you had to do BOTH emotion and performance? What changed?"
4. "Share a time you set a boundary with a venting rep. What was the outcome?"
5. "What's the biggest tension you feel between company goals and your beliefs? How are you navigating it?"

## Tone & Voice Guidance

- **Personal:** Share real stories, failures, and successes. Don't hide behind theory.
- **Vulnerable:** Acknowledge that this is hard. You've struggled too.
- **Credible:** Ground everything in either your experience or research. No fluff.
- **Direct:** "Here's the problem. Here's why it happens. Here's what to do about it."
- **No jargon:** Avoid corporate-speak. Use language a rep-turned-manager would use.
- **Lightly irreverent:** OK to be a bit self-deprecating or use humor. This isn't a TED talk; it's a leader talking to leaders.
