# EMOTIONAL LEADERSHIP PLAYBOOK — Module Script
## Instructor/Video Scripts for Asynchronous Delivery

**Module:** Emotional Leadership Playbook  
**Target Audience:** First-time SaaS sales managers (0–12 months in role)  
**Delivery:** 5 sub-modules | ~10-15 min each | Asynchronous video + weekly Zoom discussion  
**Tone:** Direct, battle-tested, conversational, vulnerable  
**Core Teaching Goal:** Managing = Emotion Management + Performance Management (both required)

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE IDENTITY CRISIS
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,400 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand the IC → Manager transition is real and disorienting; identify "super salesperson syndrome"; introduce emotional leadership as equally important as performance leadership

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Direct, empathetic]**

So you just got promoted to manager. Congrats. Seriously—you earned it. You crushed your quota. You were the one people came to for advice. You were the top rep, and now you're leading the team.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Here's what nobody told you: The skills that made you successful as a rep are not the same skills that will make you successful as a manager. In fact, some of the things that made you great—your hustle, your ability to close deals on your own, your competitiveness—those things can actually hurt you now.

**[PACING NOTE: Slow down here — this is the identity crisis moment]**

I remember my first 90 days as a manager. I was running around trying to close deals for my reps, jumping into their calls, rewriting their emails. I thought I was helping. What I was actually doing was showing them I didn't trust them. And I was exhausting myself trying to be five people at once.

One of my reps finally pulled me aside and said, "Dude, I don't need you to close my deals. I need you to help me get better at closing my own deals." That hit me hard. Because he was right. I wasn't managing—I was just being a salesperson with extra meetings.

**[TRANSITION — Shift to teaching mode]**

This is what Harvard Business School calls "super salesperson syndrome." It's when you get promoted because you're great at the job, but then you can't let go of doing the job. You keep trying to be the best salesperson on the team instead of being the best manager.

Here's the problem: Your team doesn't need another top salesperson. They need a leader. They need someone who can help them become top salespeople themselves. And that requires a completely different skill set.

**[KEY FRAMEWORK — Deliver clearly, with emphasis]**

When you were a rep, your success was measured by what YOU did:
- YOUR pipeline
- YOUR quota attainment
- YOUR deals closed
- YOUR commission check

As a manager, your success is measured by what YOUR TEAM does:
- Their pipeline health
- Their quota attainment
- Their deals closed
- Their development and retention

You are no longer the star player. You're the coach. And if you can't make that shift, you're going to struggle. Your team will struggle. And you'll burn out.

**[PAUSE — Reflection moment]**

Let me be really honest here. This transition is an identity crisis. You built your entire professional identity around being great at selling. You got promoted because of it. And now you're being asked to stop doing the thing that made you successful and start doing something completely different.

That's disorienting. It's scary. And it's normal.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, real]**

When I made the transition, I had this moment about three months in where I looked at my calendar and realized I hadn't closed a deal in weeks. I felt like I was losing my edge. Like I was becoming irrelevant. I started jumping into deals just to feel useful again.

My director noticed and said, "Andrew, you're a great closer. But if you keep closing deals for your team, you're going to have five mediocre reps instead of five great ones. Your job is to make them better, not to do it for them."

That stung. But it was true.

**[TEACHING POINT — Direct, practical]**

Here's what I learned: Being a manager means letting go of individual heroics and embracing collective success. It means watching your reps struggle through a deal and NOT jumping in to save them—because that struggle is where they learn.

It means celebrating their wins more than your own. It means coaching them through their losses instead of telling them what they should have done. It means building systems and rhythms that help them succeed, even when you're not in the room.

**[TRANSITION TO EMOTIONAL LEADERSHIP]**

And here's the part nobody talks about: Managing people is as much about managing their emotions as it is about managing their performance.

You're going to have reps who are anxious about quota. Reps who are frustrated with leads. Reps who are dealing with personal stuff that's affecting their work. Reps who are burnt out, disengaged, or on the verge of quitting.

Your instinct might be to push all that aside and say, "We don't do emotional stuff here. This is about sales. Let's drive the bus and get results."

But here's the truth: You can't get results without addressing the emotions. Because your reps are human beings, and human beings don't perform well when they're anxious, frustrated, or burnt out.

**[NEUROSCIENCE MOMENT — Ground in research, keep it accessible]**

There's actual neuroscience behind this. Your brain has something called mirror neurons—they're the reason you automatically smile when someone smiles at you, or tense up when someone's anxious around you.

When you walk into a team meeting stressed, your team's mirror neurons pick up on that stress. Their brains start firing in the same pattern as yours. Their cortisol levels—the stress hormone—start to rise. They don't consciously think, "My manager is stressed, so I should be stressed." It happens automatically, brain-to-brain.

This is called emotional contagion. And it means your emotional state directly impacts your team's performance. Not just their mood—their actual cognitive function, their ability to solve problems, their willingness to take risks.

**[PAUSE — Let that sink in]**

So when you ignore emotions and try to just "push through," you're not actually avoiding the emotional stuff. You're just transmitting unregulated stress to your team and wondering why they're not performing.

**[KEY TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core insight]**

Managing is about BOTH emotion and performance. You need to do both. If you only focus on performance and ignore emotions, your team will disengage, burn out, and leave. If you only focus on emotions and ignore performance, you won't hit your numbers and you'll lose credibility.

The best managers I know do both. They care about their people AND they hold them accountable. They create psychological safety AND they push for results. They understand that emotions and performance are not separate—they're deeply connected.

**[TRANSITION TO APPLICATION]**

So what does this look like in practice?

It means when a rep comes to you venting about their leads, you don't just say, "Yeah, leads suck, keep dialing." You also don't let them sit in the venting for 30 minutes and walk away with no action plan.

You say something like: "I hear you, that's frustrating. Let's talk about what's in your control here. What can we do to improve your conversion rate with the leads you do have?"

You validate the emotion. You acknowledge the reality. And then you move to action.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Specific, real]**

I had a rep who was struggling. She was a great closer, but her pipeline was weak. When I dug into it, I realized she was avoiding prospecting because she was anxious about rejection. She'd built up this story in her head that every cold call was a personal failure.

I could have just said, "You need to make more calls. That's the job." But that wouldn't have fixed the problem. Instead, I said, "Let's talk about what's happening when you pick up the phone. What are you feeling?"

She opened up. We talked about the anxiety. I shared my own stories of getting rejected and feeling like a failure. And then we reframed it: Prospecting isn't about you being good enough. It's about finding the people who need what you're selling. Rejection is just filtering.

That conversation shifted something for her. Her prospecting numbers went up 40% over the next month. Not because I told her to make more calls, but because I addressed the emotional block that was keeping her stuck.

**[TEACHING POINT — Tie back to the core insight]**

That's emotional leadership. It's not soft. It's not therapy. It's understanding that emotions drive behavior, and behavior drives results. If you want better results, you have to understand and work with the emotions—not ignore them.

**[CLOSE — Inspiring but grounded]**

So here's your challenge as you move forward: Let go of being the best salesperson on your team. Embrace being the best coach. And understand that coaching means managing both the performance AND the emotions.

You're not a therapist. But you're also not just a quota enforcer. You're a leader. And leaders understand that people are complex, emotional, and capable of great things—when they're led well.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Take a minute and think about this: What skills made you successful as a rep? Write them down. Now ask yourself: Which of those skills are helping you as a manager, and which ones are getting in the way?

Be honest. This is where growth starts.

**[END SUB-MODULE 1]**

## SUB-MODULE 2: THE ARCHETYPE DISCOVERY
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,800 words  
**Learning Objective:** Introduce emotional archetypes; help managers understand their own style; create the foundational tool for evaluating reps; establish that you don't change people—you leverage who they already are

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, conversational]**

Let's talk about why your team resists your leadership style.

**[PAUSE — Let that land, it's provocative]**

I know that's a tough one to hear. But here's the reality: If you've been managing for more than a few months, you've probably noticed that some of your reps love what you're doing, some don't really care, and some actively push back.

You set up a new meeting cadence—some reps show up energized, others show up annoyed. You introduce a new pipeline review process—some reps embrace it, others ignore it. You give feedback—some reps take it and run, others get defensive.

And your instinct is probably to think, "The ones who resist just don't get it yet. I need to convince them. I need to show them why this works."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core mistake]**

Here's the problem: You're assuming everyone processes information, feedback, and structure the same way you do. And they don't.

People have different emotional and cognitive styles—what I call emotional archetypes. These are the patterns that shape how someone thinks, feels, responds to pressure, and motivates themselves.

And here's the kicker: You can't change someone's archetype in 6-12 months. Only they can change themselves, and that takes years of intentional work. You don't have that kind of time. You're going to be their manager for maybe a year before they move to a new role or company.

So trying to change who they are is a losing strategy.

**[PAUSE — Let that sink in]**

The better strategy? Understand who they are. Meet them where they are. And use their existing momentum to drive results.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

I learned this the hard way. I had a rep on my team—let's call him Marcus—who was analytical. He wanted data, context, time to think before making a decision. He was methodical, thorough, and careful.

I'm the opposite. I'm fast, intuitive, and action-oriented. My default is "let's move, we'll figure it out as we go."

So when Marcus would slow down to analyze a deal, I'd get frustrated. I'd say things like, "Stop overthinking it. Just pick up the phone and call them." I thought I was helping him be more decisive.

What I was actually doing was asking him to be someone he's not. And it backfired. He started avoiding me. His confidence dropped. His performance suffered.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The shift]**

One day, my director pulled me aside and said, "Andrew, Marcus is analytical. That's his strength. Stop trying to turn him into you. Instead, figure out how to use his analytical strength to get results."

That flipped everything for me.

I stopped pushing Marcus to be faster. Instead, I gave him time to prepare for calls. I asked him to build data models for our team's pipeline. I let him analyze deal patterns and share insights with the team.

His performance skyrocketed. And the team benefited from his analytical rigor. I wasn't trying to change him anymore—I was leveraging who he already was.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, structured]**

So let's talk about emotional archetypes. I'm going to give you a simple framework—this isn't scientific, but it's useful. Think of it as a map, not a rulebook.

People tend to operate from one of a few core styles:

**1. Analytical**  
Slows down under pressure. Wants data, context, certainty before moving. Thinks deeply, processes thoroughly. Can appear "slow" or "overthinking" to others, but their rigor prevents mistakes.

**2. Driving/Urgent**  
Speeds up under pressure. Wants action, results, forward movement. Pushes through obstacles. Can appear "reckless" or "impatient" to others, but their urgency creates momentum.

**3. Relational**  
Focuses on people and alignment. Wants connection, collaboration, team buy-in. Cares about how decisions impact relationships. Can appear "too soft" or "consensus-driven" to others, but their empathy builds trust.

**4. Global/Strategic**  
Zooms out under pressure. Sees the big picture, patterns, and future implications. Thinks systemically. Can appear "scattered" or "not detail-oriented" to others, but their vision spots opportunities.

**[TEACHING POINT — This is critical]**

None of these is better or worse. They're different. And the key is this: Most new managers assume their archetype is the "right" one because it's what made them successful as a rep.

But here's the truth: Your team is probably a mix of all of these. And if you try to force everyone into your style, you'll only connect with the 20-30% who are naturally similar to you. The rest will resist, disengage, or leave.

**[SELF-ASSESSMENT MOMENT — Interactive, reflective]**

So let's start with you. Think about how you operate under pressure. When things get stressful, what's your default move?

- Do you slow down and analyze? (Analytical)
- Do you push through and keep moving? (Driving)
- Do you check in with people and make sure everyone's aligned? (Relational)
- Do you zoom out and look at the bigger picture? (Global)

Write it down. Be honest. This is your default operating system.

**[PAUSE — Give them time to reflect]**

Now think about your team. For each rep, ask yourself: What's their archetype? When they're under pressure, what do they do?

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the application]**

When I did this exercise, I realized I had:
- Two analytical reps who needed prep time and data
- One driving rep who just wanted me to get out of her way and let her run
- One relational rep who needed frequent check-ins and reassurance
- One global thinker who was always five steps ahead and wanted to talk strategy

And I was treating them all the same. Same 1:1 format. Same feedback style. Same expectations.

No wonder 60% of my team was frustrated with me.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The shift in approach]**

Once I understood their archetypes, I adapted:

- For my analytical reps, I sent 1:1 agendas in advance and gave them time to prepare. I asked for their analysis and used it.
- For my driving rep, I set clear targets and got out of her way. I checked in weekly but didn't micromanage.
- For my relational rep, I built in more frequent touchpoints and made sure to acknowledge their contributions publicly.
- For my global thinker, I gave them bigger projects and asked them to help with strategic planning.

Same team. Different approach. Better results.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Keep it accessible]**

Here's why this works, from a brain perspective: People's emotional archetypes are tied to their personality traits, which are shaped by genetics, early experiences, and neurological patterns. Research on the Big Five personality model shows that traits like conscientiousness, openness, and neuroticism are relatively stable over time.

You can't rewire someone's brain in a year. But you can work WITH their brain instead of against it.

When you meet someone where they are, their brain doesn't have to fight you. Their mirror neurons—those same neurons we talked about in the last module—sync with yours. They feel understood. And when people feel understood, they perform better.

**[TEACHING POINT — The trap to avoid]**

Here's the mistake I see new managers make: They identify their team's archetypes and then try to "fix" the ones who don't match their own style.

"My rep is too analytical—I need to make them faster."  
"My rep is too relational—I need to make them tougher."  
"My rep is too global—I need to make them more detail-oriented."

That's not leadership. That's trying to clone yourself.

**[REFRAME — This is the key insight]**

Better approach: "My rep is analytical—how do I use their analytical strength to drive results?"

Maybe they become the person who builds your pipeline models. Maybe they're the one who digs into why a deal fell apart and finds the pattern. Maybe they're the rep who helps the team understand data trends.

Their analytical nature isn't a weakness to fix. It's a strength to leverage.

**[TRANSITION TO APPLICATION]**

So here's your action step: Evaluate each rep on your team. Write down their archetype. Then ask yourself:

1. How am I currently managing them?
2. Is my approach aligned with their archetype, or am I trying to force them into my style?
3. What would change if I met them where they are?

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the tension]**

I had a rep who was relational. She cared deeply about the team, about collaboration, about making sure everyone was OK. I'm not relational—I'm driving. My instinct was to push her to be more aggressive, more competitive.

But when I stepped back, I realized: She was the glue that held the team together. She was the one people came to when they were struggling. She built trust faster than anyone on the team.

So instead of trying to make her more like me, I gave her space to do what she did best. I asked her to mentor new reps. I involved her in team culture decisions. And her sales numbers improved because she felt valued for who she was, not pressured to be someone else.

**[CLOSE — Practical, grounded]**

Here's the bottom line: You're managing humans, not robots. Humans are diverse. They think differently, feel differently, and respond to leadership differently.

Your job isn't to make them all the same. Your job is to understand who they are and help them succeed as themselves.

That's harder than having a one-size-fits-all approach. But it's also way more effective.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Take a minute and write down: Which of your reps challenges you the most because they're different from you? What's their archetype? What would change if you stopped trying to change them and started leveraging who they already are?

**[END SUB-MODULE 2]**

## SUB-MODULE 3: THE EMOTION/PERFORMANCE INTEGRATION
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,600 words  
**Learning Objective:** Establish that both emotion and performance management are required; help managers see that ignoring either creates problems; introduce the "20/30/40 split" concept; tie emotions to business impact

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, provocative]**

Let me ask you a tough question: Are you avoiding emotions because you think it makes you a better manager?

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Here's what I mean. A lot of new managers—especially former top reps—operate from this belief: "Emotions are distractions. We're here to hit quota. Let's keep it professional, drive the bus, and avoid the messy stuff."

And I get it. You want to be taken seriously. You want to be seen as strong, decisive, and results-oriented. You don't want to be the "soft" manager who lets people vent for 30 minutes and doesn't hold anyone accountable.

But here's the problem: When you try to make your team unemotional, you're not eliminating emotions. You're just forcing them underground. And underground emotions don't go away—they leak out as disengagement, passive aggression, and missed quota.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Core framework]**

Managing people is about two things:

1. **Emotion Management:** Understanding what people are feeling, where it's coming from, and how it impacts their behavior and performance.
2. **Performance Management:** Metrics, accountability, pipeline health, coaching toward targets.

You need both. If you do one without the other, you fail.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, real]**

Early in my career as a manager, I was all performance, no emotion. I ran my team like a machine. Pipeline reviews, deal coaching, quota tracking. I was obsessed with the numbers.

And my numbers were OK. But my team was miserable. I had high turnover. People didn't trust me. I'd walk into the office and the energy would shift—people would tense up, avoid eye contact.

I remember one rep finally saying to me, "Andrew, I don't feel like you actually care about us as people. You just care about our numbers."

That hurt. Because she was right. I thought caring about their performance WAS caring about them. But I was missing half the equation.

**[TEACHING POINT — The flip side]**

On the other hand, I've also seen managers who are all emotion, no performance. They're empathetic, supportive, great listeners. Their teams love them. But they don't hit quota. Reps aren't held accountable. The culture is warm but the results are weak.

And eventually, those managers lose credibility. Leadership stops trusting them. Reps stop respecting them because there's no structure or rigor.

**[THE KEY INSIGHT — This is the aha moment]**

The best managers do BOTH. They care about their people AND they hold them accountable. They create psychological safety AND they push for results.

They understand that emotions and performance are not separate. They're deeply connected.

**[NEUROSCIENCE MOMENT — Ground in research]**

Let's talk about why this is true, from a neuroscience perspective.

When someone is emotionally dysregulated—anxious, stressed, angry, burnt out—their brain shifts into threat mode. The amygdala takes over. The prefrontal cortex—the part of the brain responsible for planning, problem-solving, and strategic thinking—goes offline.

In other words: Anxiety kills performance. Stress kills creativity. Burnout kills motivation.

You can't just push through emotions and expect great results. The brain doesn't work that way.

Research on emotional contagion shows that when managers are stressed, their teams' cortisol levels—the stress hormone—rise in response. Your stress becomes their stress. And stressed reps don't perform well.

So when you ignore emotions and just focus on the numbers, you're actually sabotaging your own performance goals.

**[PAUSE — Let that sink in]**

On the flip side: When people feel psychologically safe—when they trust you, when they feel heard, when they know you care—their brains shift out of threat mode. They can think strategically. They can take risks. They can perform at their best.

This is what Harvard researcher Amy Edmondson calls psychological safety. And her research shows that teams with high psychological safety outperform teams without it.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The 20/30/40 split]**

OK, so let's talk about what happens when you try to apply a one-size-fits-all approach to your team.

You set up a new structure—maybe it's a new 1:1 format, a new pipeline review process, a new team meeting cadence. You roll it out. And here's what typically happens:

- **20-30% of your team loves it.** They latch on, they engage, they thrive. These are your "wins."
- **30-40% of your team doesn't really care.** They show up, they participate, but they're not bought in. They're just going through the motions.
- **30-40% of your team actively resists.** They push back, they complain, they think your approach is wrong.

**[PAUSE — This is the critical question]**

And here's what most new managers do: They look at the 30-40% who resist and think, "I need to convince them. I need to show them why this works. They just don't see it yet."

So they spend all their energy trying to convert the resisters. And it doesn't work. The resisters dig in. The 20-30% who loved it start to feel neglected. And the manager burns out trying to get everyone on the same page.

**[REFRAME — This is the shift]**

Here's the better approach: The 30-40% who resist aren't wrong. They're just different.

They have different archetypes. They need different things. Your uniform structure works great for the 20-30% who are similar to you. But it doesn't work for everyone.

So instead of trying to convince them, ask yourself: "Why are they resisting? What do they need that I'm not providing?"

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the application]**

I had a rep who hated our weekly 1:1s. She'd show up late, give one-word answers, and clearly wanted to be anywhere else.

My first instinct was to think, "She's disengaged. She doesn't respect my time. I need to have a come-to-Jesus conversation with her."

But then I stopped and asked: "What's her archetype? What does she need?"

She was driving. She wanted autonomy, clarity, and then space to execute. She didn't want to sit through a 30-minute check-in every week.

So I adapted. I said, "Let's shift our 1:1s to every other week, and we'll keep them to 15 minutes unless you need more. I'll trust you to come to me if you need help."

Her whole demeanor changed. She started showing up on time, engaged, ready to talk. And her performance improved. Not because I convinced her my way was right, but because I met her where she was.

**[TEACHING POINT — Emotion and performance are linked]**

This is what I mean by emotion/performance integration. When you address the emotional need—in this case, her need for autonomy—you unlock better performance.

You're not being soft. You're being smart.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Let's talk about what this looks like in business terms.

**Ignoring emotions:**
- Team disengagement (people mentally check out)
- High turnover (your best reps leave)
- Missed quota (stressed, burnt-out reps don't hit targets)
- Low trust (reps don't come to you with problems until it's too late)

**Ignoring performance:**
- No accountability (reps coast, underperformers don't improve)
- Lack of rigor (pipeline health suffers, deals slip)
- Lost credibility with leadership (you're seen as too soft)
- Results suffer (you don't hit team quota)

**Doing both:**
- High retention (reps feel supported and challenged)
- Consistent results (reps perform well and sustain it over time)
- Strong trust (reps come to you early when things go sideways)
- Leadership credibility (you're seen as both empathetic and effective)

**[PERSONAL STORY — Show the shift]**

After I learned this lesson, I completely changed how I managed. I started every 1:1 with a simple question: "How are you doing, really?"

Not "How's your pipeline?" Not "What deals closed this week?" Just: "How are you?"

And I'd wait. I'd let them answer honestly. Sometimes they'd say they were stressed about a personal issue. Sometimes they'd say they were frustrated with a deal. Sometimes they'd say they were great.

Then I'd say, "OK, let's talk about your pipeline."

That one shift—starting with emotion, then moving to performance—changed everything. Reps felt heard. They trusted me more. And when we got to the performance conversation, they were more open, more honest, and more willing to be held accountable.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The both/and]**

This is what I call the both/and approach.

You can be empathetic AND hold people accountable.  
You can care about your reps AND push them to hit quota.  
You can create psychological safety AND maintain high standards.

It's not one or the other. It's both.

**[CLOSE — Practical, actionable]**

So here's your challenge: Look at your team right now. Are you leaning too far into performance and ignoring emotions? Or are you leaning too far into emotions and avoiding accountability?

Be honest. Most managers lean one way or the other.

And then ask yourself: What would change if I did both?

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write down: Where's the biggest gap in how you're managing right now? Are you avoiding emotions? Or are you avoiding tough performance conversations? What's one thing you could do this week to integrate both?

**[END SUB-MODULE 3]**

## SUB-MODULE 4: THE EMOTIONAL DUMP & BOUNDARIES
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,400 words  
**Learning Objective:** Prepare managers for the reality that reps WILL dump emotions on them; give them a framework for responding without becoming a dump site; establish psychological safety with healthy boundaries

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Conversational, relatable]**

So it's Monday morning. You walk into the office—or you open Slack—and before you've had your coffee, a rep messages you: "Hey, can we talk? I'm really struggling."

You jump on a call. They spend 20 minutes venting about their leads, their quota, their frustration with the product, their anxiety about missing their number, and maybe even some personal stuff they're dealing with.

You listen. You empathize. You try to help. And when the call ends, you feel emotionally drained. You've got back-to-back meetings the rest of the day, and you're already exhausted.

**[PAUSE]**

Sound familiar?

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is normal, but it's a problem]**

Here's the reality: Your reps are going to come to you with emotional stuff. That's part of being a manager. In fact, it's a sign that they trust you. They feel safe with you. They see you as someone who cares.

But here's the trap: If you're not careful, you'll become the emotional dump site for your entire team. Every rep will come to you to vent. You'll spend half your time absorbing other people's stress, frustration, and anxiety.

And at the end of the day, you'll feel like a therapist, not a leader.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

This happened to me. I had five reps. And every week, it felt like each of them needed to dump on me. One rep was stressed about quota. Another was frustrated with our lead quality. Another was dealing with a personal crisis and couldn't focus.

I thought I was being a good manager by being available, by listening, by absorbing it all.

But I was burning out. I'd go home at the end of the day emotionally exhausted. I started dreading 1:1s because I knew they'd turn into venting sessions. And I wasn't actually helping my reps—I was just letting them dump and then sending them back to work with no plan.

My partner finally said to me, "You're carrying everyone else's stress. When are you going to set a boundary?"

That hit me. Because she was right. I didn't have boundaries. I was a sponge.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The both/and again]**

Here's the key: You can be empathetic AND have boundaries. You can care about your reps AND not absorb all their stress.

In fact, boundaries are what allow you to be empathetic sustainably. Without boundaries, you burn out. And when you burn out, you stop caring. So boundaries aren't selfish—they're necessary.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, actionable]**

Let me give you a simple framework for responding to emotional dumps. I call it: **Listen, Validate, Action, Boundary.**

**1. Listen**  
When a rep comes to you venting or stressed, your first job is to listen. Not to fix, not to jump in with advice, just to listen.

**2. Validate**  
Acknowledge what they're feeling. "That sounds frustrating." "I hear you—that's a lot to deal with." "I get why you're stressed."

This step is crucial. People need to feel heard. If you skip this, they'll keep venting until they feel like you get it.

**3. Action**  
After you've listened and validated, move to action. "OK, so here's what's in your control. Let's talk about what you can do about this."

Shift from venting to problem-solving. This is where you move from therapist to leader.

**4. Boundary**  
If the venting goes on too long, or if it's becoming a pattern, set a boundary. "I hear you. Let's focus on what we can control. I want to help, but I also need us to move to action."

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show how this works]**

I had a rep who would come to me every week to vent about our lead quality. Every. Single. Week.

At first, I'd listen and sympathize. But after a few weeks, I realized: She's not looking for solutions. She's just venting. And I'm enabling it.

So I tried the framework. She started venting, and I said, "I hear you. Lead quality is frustrating. Let's talk about what we can control. What's your plan to improve conversion on the leads you do have?"

She paused. And then we actually had a productive conversation. We built a plan. Her venting dropped. Her performance improved.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Ground in research]**

Here's why this works, from a brain perspective.

When someone is venting, their amygdala—the emotional center of the brain—is activated. They're in threat mode. And when they're in threat mode, their prefrontal cortex (the problem-solving part) is offline.

If you just let them vent without moving to action, they stay in threat mode. They leave the conversation feeling heard, but they don't leave with a plan. So nothing changes.

But when you listen, validate, and then move to action, you're helping their brain shift out of threat mode and back into problem-solving mode. You're activating their prefrontal cortex.

And that's when real change happens.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The empathy/boundary balance]**

Here's what a lot of new managers get wrong: They think empathy means absorbing everything. It doesn't.

Empathy means understanding what someone is feeling and responding with care. But care doesn't mean taking on their stress. It means helping them manage their stress.

You're not their therapist. You're their leader. And leaders help people move forward.

**[PRACTICAL PHRASES — Give them language]**

Here are some phrases you can use when setting boundaries:

- "I hear you. That's tough. Let's talk about what's in your control here."
- "I get it. What's one thing we can do about this today?"
- "That sounds really hard. How can I support you in taking action on this?"
- "I want to help, but I need us to shift from venting to problem-solving. What do you need from me?"

**[PAUSE — Reflection moment]**

Notice what these phrases do: They validate the emotion AND move to action. They don't shut down the emotion, but they also don't let it spiral.

**[PERSONAL STORY — The hardest boundary]**

I had a rep who was dealing with a serious personal issue. It was affecting her work. She came to me in tears, needing to talk.

I listened. I empathized. And then I said, "I care about you, and I want to support you. But I'm not equipped to help you with this in the way you need. Have you thought about talking to a therapist or a counselor?"

She was surprised. But she also seemed relieved. She said, "You're right. I think I need that."

Setting that boundary was hard. I felt like I was being cold. But what I was actually doing was being honest about my limits—and pointing her toward real help.

**[TEACHING POINT — Know your limits]**

You are not a therapist. You are not a counselor. You are not equipped to handle serious mental health issues, trauma, or personal crises.

Your job is to care, to listen, and to point people toward appropriate resources when needed.

If a rep is struggling with something beyond your scope, it's OK to say, "I care about you, but I think you need support that I can't provide. Here's our EAP (Employee Assistance Program) / here's a resource / let's connect you with HR."

That's not cold. That's responsible.

**[CLOSE — Practical, grounded]**

So here's the bottom line: Your reps will come to you with emotions. That's normal. That's good. It means they trust you.

But you need boundaries. Not because you don't care, but because you do. Boundaries allow you to care sustainably.

Listen. Validate. Move to action. And set boundaries when you need to.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Think about this: When was the last time someone dumped on you and you didn't move to action? What happened? What would change if you used the Listen, Validate, Action, Boundary framework next time?

**[END SUB-MODULE 4]**

## SUB-MODULE 5: THE AGENT/HUMAN TENSION
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,800 words  
**Learning Objective:** Acknowledge the real ethical/emotional tension managers face; help them navigate the gray zone where company wants X and they believe Y; introduce "managing both sides" as a skill

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Vulnerable, direct]**

Let's talk about the part of being a manager that nobody warns you about.

You're sitting in a leadership meeting. Your director announces that the company is cutting commission rates. Or changing territories. Or raising quotas without adding headcount. Or laying people off.

You look around the room. Some leaders are nodding. Some are quiet. And you're sitting there thinking, "This is wrong. This is going to crush my team."

But you don't say anything. Because you're worried that if you push back, you'll be seen as not a team player. Not aligned with company goals. Not leadership material.

So you walk out of that meeting, and now you have to go tell your team. And you have to deliver this message like you believe it, even though you don't.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Welcome to the agent/human tension.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Define the tension]**

Here's the reality: As a manager, you sit in a unique position. You're an agent for the company—you represent company interests, you execute company strategy, you're responsible for driving performance toward company goals.

But you're also a human being who has a conscience, who cares about your team, and who sees when the company is asking for something unreasonable or unfair.

And sometimes those two things conflict.

The company wants X. You believe Y is right. And you have to manage both.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, real]**

This happened to me in my second year as a manager. The company decided to change our compensation structure mid-year. Reps who were on track to make great money suddenly saw their potential earnings cut by 30%.

I thought it was wrong. I pushed back in the leadership meeting. I said, "This is going to kill morale. We made a deal with these reps at the beginning of the year. Changing it now is going to break trust."

My director said, "The decision is made. Your job is to help your team understand why this is necessary."

I walked out of that meeting furious. And then I had to go tell my team.

**[PAUSE — This is the hard part]**

I had two choices:

**Option 1:** Be the company mouthpiece. Deliver the message like I believed it. "Hey team, here's the new comp structure. It's aligned with our business goals. Let's move forward."

**Option 2:** Be the team advocate. Vent my frustration with them. "This is BS. I fought for you and lost. I'm as pissed as you are."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Both options are wrong]**

Option 1 makes you look like a robot. Your team knows you don't believe it. They lose trust in you because you're not being real.

Option 2 makes you look like you're not aligned with leadership. Your team loses trust in the company, and you undermine your own ability to execute company strategy.

So what do you do?

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — The both/and approach]**

Here's what I did, and here's what I recommend:

**Be transparent AND be responsible.**

I went to my team and said:

"OK, here's the situation. The company is changing our comp structure. I know this sucks. I pushed back on it in the leadership meeting because I was worried about how it would impact you. But the decision is made, and it's not changing.

So here's what I'm asking: Let's focus on what we can control. I'm going to do everything I can to help you hit your numbers under this new structure. And I need you to keep executing. We can be frustrated AND keep moving forward."

**[PAUSE — Let that sit]**

Notice what I did there:
1. I was honest about my feelings (I pushed back, I was worried)
2. I was transparent about the reality (the decision is made)
3. I didn't undermine the company (I didn't vent or trash-talk leadership)
4. I moved to action (focus on what we can control)

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the skill]**

This is the skill of managing both sides. You acknowledge the tension. You show your team you're human and you care. But you don't lose your credibility with leadership by becoming purely a team advocate.

You're the bridge. You translate company decisions to your team in a way that's honest but constructive. And you translate team concerns to leadership in a way that's clear but not undermining.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Why this works]**

Here's why transparency works, from a brain perspective.

When people sense incongruence—when your words don't match your body language or your tone—their brains pick up on it. Mirror neurons activate, but the signal is confusing. They don't trust you because something feels off.

But when you're transparent and authentic, their brains relax. You're congruent. They might not like the message, but they trust the messenger.

Research on psychological safety shows that teams trust leaders who are honest about challenges and tensions—not leaders who pretend everything is fine.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — When it went wrong]**

I learned this the hard way early on. I once delivered a company decision I didn't believe in, and I tried to act like I was fully on board. I said all the right things. I smiled. I was "positive."

One of my reps pulled me aside afterward and said, "Andrew, I don't buy it. I can tell you don't believe what you just said. Just be real with us."

She was right. I thought I was being a good leader by being "aligned." But I was actually being inauthentic. And my team saw through it.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — When to push back vs. when to execute]**

Here's a question I get a lot: "When should I push back on a company decision, and when should I just execute?"

Here's my framework:

**Push back when:**
- The decision is unethical or illegal
- The decision will cause serious harm to your team (safety, wellbeing, massive attrition)
- You have data or insights leadership doesn't have that would change the decision

**Execute when:**
- The decision is made and won't change
- You've pushed back and been heard, but leadership has decided to move forward
- The decision isn't unethical, just something you disagree with

**[PAUSE]**

And here's the key: When you push back, do it in the room. Don't smile and nod in the meeting and then trash-talk the decision to your team. That's cowardly.

Push back in the leadership meeting. Make your case. And if the decision doesn't change, own it with your team.

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I pushed back successfully]**

I once had a director who wanted to set quotas that were 40% higher than the previous year, with no additional headcount or resources. I thought it was unrealistic and would burn out the team.

In the planning meeting, I said: "I want us to hit aggressive targets. But I'm concerned that a 40% increase with no new resources is setting us up to fail. Can we look at the data on what's realistic?"

We dug into the numbers. We looked at historical performance, market conditions, and capacity. And we landed on a 25% increase—still aggressive, but achievable.

My director respected that I pushed back with data, not just emotion. And my team trusted me because they knew I was fighting for what was realistic.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The trust equation]**

Here's what I learned: Your team trusts you when they know you're fighting for them. Leadership trusts you when they know you're aligned with business goals.

You don't have to choose. You can be both.

**[PRACTICAL PHRASES — Language you can use]**

Here are some phrases for navigating this tension:

**With your team:**
- "I hear you. I pushed back on this, but the decision is made. Let's focus on what we can control."
- "I know this is frustrating. Here's what I'm doing to advocate for you. And here's what I need from you."
- "I don't love this decision either. But here's the reality we're working with."

**With leadership:**
- "I want to make sure we're being realistic here. Let me share what I'm hearing from the team."
- "I'm on board with the goal. I'm concerned about execution. Can we talk through how to make this work?"
- "I'll execute this, but I need to be transparent with my team about the challenges. How do you want me to frame it?"

**[CLOSE — Grounded, real]**

Here's the bottom line: Being a manager means sitting in the tension between company goals and human reality. It's uncomfortable. It's hard. And it's normal.

You don't have to pretend the tension doesn't exist. You can acknowledge it. You can be transparent about it. And you can still execute.

Your team doesn't need you to be perfect. They need you to be real. They need to know you see them, you care about them, and you're doing your best to navigate a complex situation.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Think about this: When was the last time you felt the agent/human tension? How did you handle it? What would you do differently now?

And here's the harder question: Do you trust your team enough to be vulnerable with them about the tensions you face? Or are you trying to appear like you have it all figured out?

Be honest. Growth starts there.
