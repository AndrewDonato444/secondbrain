# Managing Up Without Micromanaging Down — Module Brief

**Module Status:** Ready for Content Development  
**Subagent Target:** Curriculum Design Agent

**Position in Curriculum:** Module 2 (follows Emotional Leadership Playbook)

## Executive Overview

**Managing Up Without Micromanaging Down** is the second wedge module and solves a COMPLETE BLIND SPOT in sales manager training. Neither SaaSy nor Factor8 touches this. Yet it's the #1 unstated fear for new managers: "How do I get what my team needs from senior leadership without looking bad or losing my credibility?"

This module establishes that managing up is INTENTIONAL WORK—not something that happens by accident. New managers must proactively design how they communicate with their manager, then execute that framework. It's the difference between succeeding and failing, even if your team is performing well.

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

Managing up is not the same as being a rep who managed their manager. It's different now. You're a translator between two worlds with completely different needs. What you communicate, how you structure it, and how intentional you are about building trust with your boss could be the difference between you succeeding and you not succeeding.

**More specifically:**

1. You're now responsible for being a go-between: getting resources for your team, translating company strategy to reps, bringing rep reality to leadership.
2. Most new managers over-rotate on their relationship with their reps and ignore the relationship with their manager. They dump negative info unstructured. They don't invest in the framework.
3. Your job is to take the LEAD on proposing how you two communicate about issues, problems, resources, forecasts. Propose the structure. Your manager refines it. You're driving it; they're refining it.
4. You're building TWO things: (a) the actual results/numbers, and (b) personal credibility/trust in you as a person. Both matter. Results matter less if they don't trust you. Trust matters less if results are terrible. But you need both.
5. When you're transparent about problems, you better have a solve. You're not just reporting bad news; you're also bringing the plan to fix it. If you don't, leadership looks at YOU as the problem.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Clear on what their manager actually cares about (not guessing)
- Equipped with a communication framework (Inform, Advocate, Execute)
- Confident in navigating the translator role (rep needs vs. company needs)
- Aware that personal credibility is currency (as important as results)

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers typically do one of two things—both wrong:

**Path 1: The Non-Investor**
They don't prioritize managing up at all. They replicate how they managed "up" as a rep (casual check-ins, focus on results) and don't realize that now they're a manager, the rules have changed.

**Path 2: The Unstructured Dumper**
They communicate, but in an unstructured way. Forecasting calls sound negative. Updates are problem-focused. They dump team issues without context or solution. Result: They erode trust. Leadership thinks, "Does this manager have it under control?"

### The Real Issue

**You are now a translator.** Your manager (VP, Director, CRO, CEO) has completely different concerns than your reps. Your reps need: autonomy, support, resources, clarity. Your manager needs: accurate forecasts, confidence in you, visibility into what's working/not, early warning on problems.

When you over-communicate about problems without structure or solve, you poison the well. Your manager stops trusting your judgment. They start micromanaging you to compensate. You stop being a leader in their eyes; you become a problem.

**The harder truth:** Trust in YOU as a person is as valuable as the results you produce. Andrew's story proves this—doubled revenue in 15 months but didn't build personal credibility with the CEO. When results dipped (circumstances out of control), the CEO looked at HIM first, not at the circumstances. He got laid off because the trust wasn't there.

## Key Frameworks & Concepts

### 1. **The Translator Role**

You sit between two worlds:

**The Company (Manager/Leadership):** Wants
- Accurate forecasts (not sandbagged, not inflated)
- Confidence that you're driving the team
- Early visibility into problems (with context)
- Proof that you're the right person for the job

**The Reps:** Need
- Resources (headcount, territory, tools, compensation)
- Autonomy (not micromanagement)
- Advocacy (you fighting for them)
- Clarity on what's expected

**Your Job:** Navigate both without losing credibility on either side.

**The Tension:** They often want opposite things.
- Reps: "I need higher commission." Leadership: "Cut comp to improve margins."
- Reps: "I need smaller territory." Leadership: "We need coverage."
- Reps: "This quota is unfair." Leadership: "This is the market rate."

You don't choose a side. You manage the tension. You inform leadership of rep reality. You execute company decisions with integrity. You advocate for solutions that work for both.

### 2. **Inform, Advocate, Execute**

A three-part framework for how you show up:

**Inform:** You bring accurate, contextual information to your manager. Not emotions, not venting. Data. "Here's what's happening, here's the context, here's what it means."

**Advocate:** You make the case for what you think is right. You disagree if you disagree, but you do it with data and respect. "I think we should do X because of Y." You don't just accept directives without input.

**Execute:** Once a decision is made, you execute it fully. You don't go back to your team and undermine it. You don't say, "The company made this stupid decision." You own it.

**Key insight:** You can disagree AND commit. Both are leadership.

### 3. **Personal Credibility & Trust Capital**

Results are table stakes. But they're not enough.

**Trust Capital** = How much your manager believes in YOUR judgment, YOUR vision, YOUR ability to navigate complexity.

**You build trust capital by:**
- Being proactive about how you communicate (proposing structure)
- Following through on what you say
- Delivering bad news early with solutions
- Taking ownership (not blaming reps, circumstances, market)
- Being consistent (no surprises)
- Building a relationship (not just transactional)

**You lose trust capital by:**
- Dumping problems unstructured
- Overstating results or sandbagging forecasts
- Appearing to prioritize reps over company
- Making the same mistakes twice
- Disappearing when things get tough
- Not knowing what your manager cares about

**Andrew's story shows this:** He had results (doubled revenue) but no personal trust. When results dipped, the CEO had no trust to fall back on. The CEO became suspicious instead of supportive.

### 4. **The Forecasting Paradox**

Forecasting is THE example of managing up because it has competing pressures:

**What Your Reps Want:** Low forecast so they're not under unrealistic pressure

**What Leadership Wants:** Realistic forecast + confidence that you can hit it

**What You Need to Do:** Call the real number, THEN tell your manager how you'll get there. Not "I'll try." But "Here's the pipeline, here's the conversion, here's the plan."

**The Mistake:** Calling a number that's too low (reps are happy, leadership thinks you've given up) or too high (leadership thinks you're unrealistic).

**The Right Move:** Accurate forecast + high confidence in YOUR ability to execute. Your manager wants to believe in YOU. Give them a reason to.

### 5. **The Underperformer Communication Dilemma**

This is a classic tension: How much do you share about team performance?

**The Trap:** Over-share and you poison your rep's recovery chances. Leadership gives up on Sally. You wanted help, not a severance package.

**The Other Trap:** Don't share and leadership thinks you're hiding problems. Your credibility suffers.

**The Right Move:** Share strategically. You can say "Sally is underperforming AND I have a plan to help her. Here's what we're doing." Now you're not just reporting bad news; you're demonstrating leadership.

**Key insight:** Your reputation is tied to your team's reputation. If you bad-mouth them, you look like you can't manage. If you hide their struggles, you look like you're not paying attention. You have to own it AND have a plan.

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: The Translator Reality (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand that they're now a go-between with conflicting pressures
- Introduce the translator role
- Normalize the tension (this is hard; everyone feels it)

**Key Teaching Points:**
- You were successful as a rep working with YOUR manager (one relationship)
- Now you have TWO relationships: up to your manager and down to your reps
- These relationships have different needs and sometimes opposite asks
- You can't make everyone happy; you can manage the tension
- Being a translator is the value you bring now—not your sales ability

**Real Example:**
- Compensation plan that isn't great for reps but company needs margins
- Forecasting where reps want low numbers, leadership wants aggressive
- Territory changes, quota changes, tool rollouts—always tension

**Personal Story:**
- Andrew's experience managing between co-founders, RevOps person, CEO, and sales team
- How each wanted something different
- How being divided (trying to please everyone) led to failing everyone

**Tension to Address:**
- "I feel like I'm betraying my team." (You're not; you're managing complexity)
- "Won't my manager think I'm weak if I bring problems?" (Only if you bring problems without solutions)

### Sub-Module 2: Know Your Manager (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand what their boss actually cares about
- Create a framework for figuring it out
- Remove the guessing game

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Your manager has their own boss, their own goals, their own metrics
- What they care about flows down from their boss's priorities
- You don't know what they care about unless you ask
- Once you know, you can structure everything around that
- Ask directly: "What are your top 3 priorities? What keeps you up at night?"

**Self-Assessment Tool:**
- "What do I THINK my manager cares about?" (guess)
- "What do I KNOW they care about?" (based on what they say/do)
- "Where's the gap?" (assumption vs. reality)
- "How do I find out for sure?" (ask in 1:1)

**Business Impact:**
- Clarity on what matters saves you from wasting effort
- Your manager becomes less mysterious
- You can tailor communication to what they care about

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they care about the wrong thing?" (Doesn't matter; that's your reality now. Adapt.)
- "What if I don't like my manager's priorities?" (Navigate it, don't fight it)

### Sub-Module 3: Propose Your Communication Framework (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach managers to proactively design how they communicate with their boss
- Introduce "Inform, Advocate, Execute"
- Shift from reactive to proactive

**Key Teaching Points:**
- You can say: "Here's how I think we should communicate about X. You refine it."
- Senior leaders WANT people to take initiative (saves them thinking)
- By proposing structure, you control the relationship dynamic
- Your manager is not your parent; they're your peer working for a common goal
- Structure removes misunderstanding

**Proposed Structures:**
- Weekly forecast call: "Here's the number, here's the plan, here are the risks"
- Problem escalation: "When something goes wrong, here's how I'll bring it to you: problem, context, my recommendation"
- Resource asks: "Here's what I need, here's why, here's the impact"
- Team performance: "How I'll flag concerns and what I'm doing about it"

**Framework: Inform, Advocate, Execute**
- **Inform:** Accurate, contextual data (not emotion)
- **Advocate:** Make your case if you disagree (respectfully, with data)
- **Execute:** Commit fully to decisions, even ones you don't love

**Business Impact:**
- Fewer surprises
- Clearer expectations
- Your manager trusts you more (you're professional)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Won't this make me look like I'm controlling?" (No, you're taking initiative. Leaders like that.)
- "What if they reject my framework?" (Great, now you know their preference. Adapt.)

### Sub-Module 4: The Forecasting Conversation (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach managers how to handle forecasting as a translator
- Navigate accuracy vs. confidence
- Stay credible with both leadership and reps

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Forecasting is the #1 place where company and reps conflict
- Reps want low numbers (less pressure); company wants aggressive numbers (growth)
- Your job is to call the REAL number, then tell your manager how you'll get there
- Confidence in you matters as much as the number itself
- Never sandbag (call too low) or inflate (call too high)

**The Mistake:**
- Calling low to make reps happy → leadership thinks you've given up
- Calling high to impress leadership → leadership thinks you're unrealistic
- Changing the number week-to-week → leadership stops trusting you

**The Right Move:**
- "Here's the forecast. Here's why. Here's my confidence level. Here's what could move it."
- You're not just giving a number; you're demonstrating that you've thought about it
- Reps don't get a vote in the forecast; you do

**Script:**
- "I'm calling $500K this month. We have $400K in advanced/verbal, $150K in qualified opportunity. The risk is X. The upside is Y. My confidence is 75%. Here's what I'm doing to drive it."

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if reps think I'm setting them up to fail?" (You're not setting them; the market is. Your job is to be accurate.)
- "My manager always asks for higher forecasts." (You answer once: "This is the real number. I won't sandbag or inflate.")

### Sub-Module 5: Building Trust Capital (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand that results are not enough
- Teach them how to build personal credibility
- Show them what happens when trust erodes

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Results matter. Trust matters more (over time).
- You can have great results and no trust. (You get laid off.)
- You can have mediocre results and high trust. (You get a chance to improve.)
- Trust is built through consistency, ownership, early visibility into problems
- Trust is lost through surprise, blame-shifting, appearing political

**How You Build Trust:**
- Do what you say you'll do
- Bring bad news early (with solutions)
- Take ownership (never blame reps, market, circumstances)
- Be transparent about what you know and don't know
- Show up when things get tough
- Know your manager's priorities and stay aligned

**How You Lose Trust:**
- Surprise your manager with bad news
- Blame your team or circumstances
- Sandbag forecasts
- Make the same mistake twice
- Disappear during crises
- Go against your manager then complain to your team

**Andrew's Story (The Cautionary Tale):**
- Doubled revenue in 15 months (objectively great results)
- Didn't build personal relationship with CEO
- When results dipped (market conditions), CEO didn't trust his judgment
- CEO became adversarial instead of supportive
- Ended up getting laid off despite the results

**The Lesson:** Results are temporary. Trust is lasting. Build trust.

**Business Impact:**
- Your manager advocates for you (for raises, headcount, resources)
- Your manager gives you autonomy (doesn't micromanage)
- Your manager believes your judgment (even when results are messy)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't this political?" (No, it's professional.)
- "What if my manager doesn't like me?" (Don't confuse liking with trusting. You need trust.)

## Learner Pack Components

### Worksheets (2 per sub-module, 10 total)

1. **Translator Reality:**
   - "What are my team's top 3 needs right now?" (get specific)
   - "What are my manager's top 3 priorities?" (be honest)
   - "Where do these conflict?" (name the tension)

2. **Know Your Manager:**
   - "What do I THINK my manager cares about?" (guess)
   - "What do I KNOW they care about?" (evidence)
   - "What are their top 3 metrics/goals?" (write it out)
   - "My action: Ask them directly in 1:1"

3. **Communication Framework:**
   - "How do I currently communicate about problems?" (self-assess)
   - "Propose a new structure: Problem escalation, forecasting, resource asks" (draft it)
   - "My first conversation with my manager about this" (script it)

4. **Forecasting:**
   - "What's my real pipeline?" (by stage)
   - "What's my conversion rate?" (historical)
   - "What's my true forecast?" (calculate it)
   - "What could move it? Risks? Upside?" (identify variables)
   - "How do I present this with confidence?" (script it)

5. **Trust Capital:**
   - "Where's my trust account with my manager?" (assess)
   - "What am I doing well?" (inventory)
   - "What am I losing trust on?" (honest look)
   - "What's my plan to rebuild?" (action)

### Self-Assessment Tools

- **Manager Relationship Health Check:** How strong is your relationship with your boss? (diagnostic + interpretation)
- **Communication Clarity:** Are you being clear when you bring problems? (scoring + feedback)
- **Trust Capital Audit:** How much trust do you have? (based on behaviors)
- **Translator Effectiveness:** How well are you managing up vs. down? (balance assessment)

### Role-Play Scenarios

1. **The Bad News:** Reps are tracking 10% below quota. Your manager asks why. Script your response.
2. **The Compensation Pushback:** Reps hate the new comp plan. You need to roll it out. Your manager wants consensus. Navigate it.
3. **The Territory Change:** Company needs to restructure. Reps are upset. Your manager is watching. Handle the conversation.
4. **The Forecast Question:** Your manager asks for higher forecast. You disagree. Conversation.
5. **The Sally Situation:** Sally's underperforming. Your manager asks if you can fix her. You have a plan. Present it.
6. **The Resource Ask:** You need headcount. Your manager says budget's frozen. You make the case.

### Weekly Zoom Discussion Prompts

1. "What's one tension you're managing between your team and leadership right now?"
2. "How well do you know what your manager cares about? How could you find out?"
3. "When have you successfully navigated bringing bad news? What worked?"
4. "What does trust capital mean to you? Where do you have it? Where do you need it?"
5. "Share a time you proposed a communication framework. How did it go?"
