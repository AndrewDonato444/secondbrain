# MANAGING UP WITHOUT MICROMANAGING DOWN — Learner Pack
**Production-Ready Assets for Student Use**

**Module:** Managing Up Without Micromanaging Down  
**Target Audience:** First-time SaaS sales managers (0–12 months in role)  
**Core Framework:** Inform, Advocate, Execute  
**Sub-Modules:** Translator Reality | Know Your Manager | Propose Framework | Forecasting | Trust Capital

---

## HOW TO USE THIS PACK

This pack contains everything you need to apply what you're learning:

- **Worksheets:** Fill these out as you watch the videos. They're designed to solve real problems, not just check boxes.
- **Self-Assessments:** Use these to diagnose where you are and where you need to improve. Be honest.
- **Role-Play Scenarios:** Practice these before you need them. Write your responses. Discuss them in your weekly Zoom.
- **Discussion Prompts:** Bring these to your weekly peer discussion. Real learning happens when you share what's working and what's not.
- **Conversation Scripts:** Use these word-for-word or adapt them to your style. They're tested in real manager situations.

**Golden Rule:** Don't just read this. Do the work. Managing up is a skill. Skills require practice.

- Maintains your authority as the leader
- Moves toward action

_______________________________________________

### Debrief

**What just happened:**

You were tested. Your team wanted to see if you'd side with them against leadership. If you did, you lose credibility up. If you just defended leadership, you lose credibility down. The right move: acknowledge the reality, own the decision, move to action.

**Example Response (Andrew's approach):**

"I hear you. This is hard. Losing accounts you've built hurts. I get it. I pushed back on this in the leadership meeting because I was worried about exactly this—how it would impact you. But the decision is final, and it's not changing. So here's where we are: We can spend the next two weeks being pissed off, or we can spend it figuring out how to crush it in our new territories. I'm asking you to do the second thing. I'll help you ramp fast in your new accounts. But I need you to commit."

# BONUS SECTION: CONVERSATION SCRIPTS

These scripts are tested in real manager situations. Use them word-for-word or adapt them.

---

## SCRIPT 1: Asking Your Manager About Their Priorities

**When to use:** When you realize you don't know what your manager actually cares about.

**The Script:**

"Hey [Manager Name], I want to make sure I'm focused on what matters most to you. Can I ask—what are your top 3 priorities this quarter? What keeps you up at night? And what do you need from me to feel confident?"

**Why it works:** You're being direct, you're framing it around their needs, and you're giving them space to answer honestly.

---

## SCRIPT 2: Proposing a Communication Framework

**When to use:** When you want to proactively design how you communicate with your manager.

**The Script:**

"I want to make sure I'm giving you what you need to feel confident in me and the team. I'd like to propose a communication structure—feel free to change it. Here's what I'm thinking:

- Weekly 1:1s where I lead with forecast, pipeline, and team performance
- When problems come up, I bring them to you within 24 hours with context and a recommendation
- Monthly summary of wins, challenges, and actions

Does this work for you? Is there anything you'd add or change?"

**Why it works:** You're taking initiative, framing it around their needs, and giving them space to refine it.

---

## SCRIPT 3: Delivering a Forecast with Confidence

**When to use:** In your weekly forecast call.

**The Script:**

"I'm calling $500K this month. My confidence is 80%.

Here's the breakdown: $400K in committed or verbal, $150K in advanced stage.

The risks: One deal ($75K) is moving slower than expected. Another deal ($50K) is dependent on a budget approval.

Here's my plan: I'm reviewing both deals with the reps this week, bringing in our VP to close the big one, and pushing three other deals forward as backup.

If everything goes right, we could hit $550K. If one slips, we'll land around $450K. But $500K is the real number."

**Why it works:** You're being clear, transparent about risks, and demonstrating you have a plan.

---

## SCRIPT 4: Delivering Bad News with a Solution

**When to use:** When something goes wrong and you need to tell your manager.

**The Script:**

**Inform:** "I need to update you on [problem]. Here's what happened: [context]."

**Advocate (if needed):** "I think we should [recommendation] because [reason]."

**Execute:** "Here's what I'm doing: [action 1], [action 2], [action 3]. I'll have an update for you by [date]."

**Why it works:** You're not just dumping a problem. You're giving context and a plan.

---

## SCRIPT 5: Advocating Against a Decision You Disagree With

**When to use:** When your manager proposes something you think is wrong.

**The Script:**

"I want to talk through the implications of [decision]. Here's my concern: [data-backed reason]. Can we look at the data together and talk through what's realistic? If the decision is final, I'll execute it fully. But I want to make sure we're thinking through the trade-offs."

**Why it works:** You're advocating respectfully, with data, and making it clear you'll execute if the decision doesn't change.

---

## SCRIPT 6: Handling an Underperformer Conversation with Your Manager

**When to use:** When your manager asks about a struggling rep.

**The Script:**

"[Rep name] is underperforming. They're at [X%] of quota. Here's what I'm seeing: [specific issue]. Here's what I'm doing: [action plan]. If they can get back to [target] in the next [timeframe], I think they're recoverable. If not, we'll need to make a change. I'll keep you updated."

**Why it works:** You're transparent about the problem, but you're also showing you have a plan. You're not hiding it, and you're not giving up on the rep prematurely.

---

## SCRIPT 7: Asking for Resources (The ROI Case)

**When to use:** When you need budget, headcount, or tools.

**The Script:**

"I want to make the case for [resource]. Here's why: [business problem]. Here's the ROI: [data showing impact]. I know budget is tight, but I think the cost of NOT doing this is higher than the cost of doing it. If the decision is final, I'll figure out how to hit the number without it. But I wanted to make the case."

**Why it works:** You're making a business case, acknowledging constraints, and showing you'll execute either way.

---

## SCRIPT 8: Taking Ownership When You Screw Up

**When to use:** When you drop the ball.

**The Script:**

"I didn't deliver [thing you committed to]. That's on me—I [reason, not excuse]. I should have [what you should have done]. I'll have it to you by [new deadline]. Is that OK, or do you need it sooner?"

**Why it works:** You're taking ownership, not making excuses, and committing to a fix.

---

## SCRIPT 9: Rebuilding Trust After a Miss

**When to use:** When you've lost credibility and need to rebuild.

**The Script:**

"I want to talk about how I'm communicating with you. I know I've [missed forecast / surprised you / dropped the ball]. I'm taking ownership of that. Here's what I'm going to do differently: [specific behavior change]. I'd appreciate your feedback along the way—let me know if I'm improving or if you need something different from me."

**Why it works:** You're acknowledging the gap, taking ownership, and asking for feedback. This shows maturity.

---

## SCRIPT 10: Executing a Decision You Disagree With (To Your Team)

**When to use:** When you need to roll out a company decision you don't love.

**The Script:**

"Here's the situation: [company decision]. I know this isn't what we wanted. I pushed back on it in the leadership meeting because I was worried about [your concern]. But the decision is final, and it's not changing. So here's where we are: We can spend the next two weeks being frustrated, or we can figure out how to execute this well. I'm asking you to do the second thing. I'll support you. But I need you to commit."

**Why it works:** You're being honest that you advocated, but you're not undermining the decision. You're moving to action.
