# MANAGING UP WITHOUT MICROMANAGING DOWN — Module Script
## Instructor/Video Scripts for Asynchronous Delivery

**Module:** Managing Up Without Micromanaging Down  
**Target Audience:** First-time SaaS sales managers (0–12 months in role)  
**Delivery:** 5 sub-modules | ~10-15 min each | Asynchronous video + weekly Zoom discussion  
**Tone:** Direct, strategic, battle-tested, vulnerable  
**Core Teaching Goal:** Managing up is intentional work. You're a translator between two worlds. Trust capital matters as much as results.

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE TRANSLATOR REALITY
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,600 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand they're now a go-between with conflicting pressures; introduce the translator role; normalize the tension

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, relatable]**

So you just became a manager. And if you're like most people, you spent your first few weeks figuring out how to manage DOWN—how to run 1:1s, how to give feedback, how to coach your reps.

But here's what nobody told you: Managing down is only half the job. The other half—the part that will actually determine whether you succeed or fail—is managing UP.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

And here's the thing: You probably think you already know how to manage up because you managed your boss when you were a rep. You had a good relationship with them. You knew what they cared about. You gave them updates. You asked for help when you needed it.

But now that you're a manager, the rules have changed. And if you don't adapt, you're going to struggle—even if your team is performing well.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

Let me tell you what happened to me.

I got promoted to manager in my early 30s. I was a top rep. I had a great relationship with my director. I thought I understood the game.

But as a manager, I made a critical mistake: I didn't invest in managing my relationship with the CEO. I thought my results would speak for themselves. And they did—for a while.

In my first 15 months as a manager, I doubled our revenue. We went from $3 million to over $6 million. I was proud. My team was proud. I thought I was crushing it.

But behind the scenes, I was losing the CEO's trust.

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional anchor]**

I didn't realize it at the time, but I was doing everything wrong when it came to managing up. I'd dump bad news on him with no context. I'd forecast one number, then change it a week later. I'd push back on his ideas in front of other people. I'd complain about resources without bringing solutions.

I thought I was being honest and direct. What I was actually doing was eroding his confidence in me.

And when our growth slowed—not because of anything I did, but because of market conditions—the CEO didn't have any trust to fall back on. He looked at me as the problem.

Six months later, I was laid off.

**[PAUSE — Let that sink in]**

Doubled revenue in 15 months. Laid off anyway.

Why? Because I built results but I didn't build trust. And in the long run, trust matters more.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core insight]**

Here's what I learned the hard way: As a manager, you're no longer just responsible for your own performance. You're now a translator between two completely different worlds.

**World One: Your Team**  
Your reps need:
- Resources (headcount, territory, tools, compensation)
- Autonomy (not micromanagement)
- Advocacy (you fighting for them)
- Clarity on what's expected
- Protection from chaos

**World Two: Leadership**  
Your manager (VP, Director, CRO, CEO) needs:
- Accurate forecasts (not sandbagged, not inflated)
- Confidence that you're driving the team
- Early visibility into problems (with context and solutions)
- Proof that you're the right person for the job
- Results

**[PAUSE]**

And here's the tension: These two worlds often want opposite things.

**[REAL EXAMPLES — Make it tangible]**

- Your reps want higher commission. Leadership wants to cut comp to improve margins.
- Your reps want smaller territories so they can go deeper. Leadership wants broader coverage.
- Your reps think the quota is unfair. Leadership thinks it's the market rate.
- Your reps want you to fight against a decision. Leadership wants you to execute it with commitment.

**[TEACHING POINT — This is the shift]**

When you were a rep, you only had to manage one relationship—the one with your manager. And when there was tension, you could just focus on your quota and let leadership figure out the rest.

But now? You're in the middle. And both sides are watching you.

Your reps are watching to see if you'll advocate for them. Your manager is watching to see if you're aligned with company goals. And you can't just pick a side.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, simple]**

This is what I call the **Translator Role**. You sit between two worlds with different needs, and your job is to navigate both without losing credibility on either side.

You're not a messenger. You're not just passing information up and down. You're translating. You're taking what your team needs and framing it in a way leadership can hear. You're taking what leadership decides and delivering it in a way your team can understand and execute.

And when those two worlds conflict—which they will—you don't choose a side. You manage the tension.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the complexity]**

I once had to roll out a comp plan change that I knew my team would hate. Leadership needed to adjust margins, and comp was the lever.

I had two choices:

**Option 1:** Be the company mouthpiece. "Hey team, new comp plan. It's aligned with our goals. Let's execute."

**Option 2:** Be the team advocate. "This is BS. I fought for you and lost. I'm as mad as you are."

**[PAUSE]**

Both are wrong.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The right approach]**

Here's what I did:

I went to my team and said: "Here's the situation. The company is changing our comp structure. I know this sucks. I pushed back in the leadership meeting because I was worried about how it would impact you. But the decision is made, and it's not changing. So here's what I'm asking: Let's focus on what we can control. I'm going to do everything I can to help you hit your numbers under this new structure. And I need you to keep executing."

**[PAUSE]**

Notice what I did there:
1. I was transparent about what happened
2. I showed them I advocated for them
3. I didn't undermine the company
4. I moved to action

That's translation. I took a company decision, added context, and delivered it in a way that kept trust on both sides.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Ground in research]**

Here's why this matters from a brain perspective.

When you're caught between two competing demands, your brain experiences what psychologists call "role conflict." It's stressful. Your amygdala—the threat-detection part of your brain—lights up. You feel anxious, overwhelmed, like you're failing both sides.

But here's the thing: The stress isn't the problem. The problem is not having a framework for how to navigate the conflict.

Once you understand that your job IS to manage the tension—that you're not supposed to eliminate it, just translate between the two sides—your brain can relax. You're not failing. You're doing the job.

**[TEACHING POINT — Normalize the difficulty]**

I'm going to be really honest with you: This is hard. Being a translator is one of the hardest parts of being a manager.

You're going to feel divided. You're going to feel like you're letting someone down no matter what you do. You're going to feel like you're being political when you're just trying to navigate complexity.

That's normal. Every manager feels this. The ones who succeed are the ones who learn to manage the tension instead of avoiding it.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Show the stakes]**

When I lost my job, one of the things that hit me hardest was realizing that I'd let both sides down.

My team felt like I didn't protect them from bad decisions. And leadership felt like I wasn't aligned with company goals.

I thought I was being a good leader by being "real" with my team about the things I disagreed with. What I was actually doing was undermining my own ability to lead.

**[PAUSE]**

I don't want you to make the same mistake.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The mindset shift]**

Here's the mindset shift you need to make: **You are not choosing sides. You are managing both sides.**

When your team comes to you frustrated with a company decision, your job is not to agree with them and trash-talk leadership. Your job is to help them understand the context and execute.

When leadership makes a decision you disagree with, your job is not to smile and nod and then complain to your team. Your job is to push back in the room, and if the decision doesn't change, own it and execute.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — This is the through-line]**

Over the next few modules, I'm going to teach you a framework for managing up that I wish I'd had when I started. It's called **Inform, Advocate, Execute.**

**Inform:** You bring accurate, contextual information to your manager. Not emotions. Not venting. Data and reality.

**Advocate:** You make the case for what you think is right. You disagree if you disagree. But you do it with data, respect, and in the room—not behind closed doors.

**Execute:** Once a decision is made, you execute it fully. You don't undermine it. You don't go back to your team and say, "The company made this stupid decision." You own it and you lead through it.

**[PAUSE — Let that framework land]**

Inform. Advocate. Execute.

That's how you manage up without losing credibility. That's how you stay a translator without picking a side.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you don't do this:

- Leadership stops trusting you. They think you're not aligned. They start micromanaging you to compensate.
- Your team stops respecting you. They think you're just a mouthpiece. They disengage.
- You burn out. You feel like you're failing everyone. You start looking for a new job.

Here's what happens when you do this well:

- Leadership trusts you. They give you autonomy. They advocate for you when you need resources.
- Your team respects you. They know you're fighting for them AND executing with integrity.
- You succeed. You hit your numbers. You build credibility. You get promoted.

**[CLOSE — Grounded, direct]**

So here's the bottom line: You're a translator now. That's the job.

It's not what you signed up for. It's harder than you thought. And it's the difference between succeeding and failing as a manager.

Over the next few modules, I'm going to teach you how to do it well. How to understand what your manager actually cares about. How to propose a communication framework. How to navigate forecasting when your team and leadership want different things. And how to build trust capital—because results alone aren't enough.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Take a minute and write this down:

1. What are your team's top 3 needs right now?
2. What are your manager's top 3 priorities?
3. Where do those conflict?

Be honest. That tension is where your real work begins.

**[END SUB-MODULE 1]**

## SUB-MODULE 2: KNOW YOUR MANAGER
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,400 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand what their boss actually cares about; create a framework for figuring it out; remove the guessing game

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Provocative, direct]**

Quick question: What are your manager's top three priorities?

**[PAUSE — Let them think]**

Can you name them? Right now, without guessing?

If you can't, you're not alone. Most new managers have no idea what their boss actually cares about. They're guessing. They're assuming. And they're wasting time and energy on things that don't matter.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the problem]**

Here's the reality: Your manager has their own boss. They have their own metrics. They have their own pressures. And what they care about flows down from what their boss cares about.

When you don't know what your manager cares about, you end up doing one of two things:

**Option 1:** You assume they care about the same things you care about. (They don't.)

**Option 2:** You try to cover all your bases and update them on everything, hoping something sticks. (This overwhelms them and wastes your time.)

Both are bad strategies.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

When I was a new manager, I thought my CEO cared about revenue growth. That's what I cared about. That's what I thought mattered.

So every time we talked, I'd lead with revenue numbers. "We're up 20% this quarter. Pipeline looks strong. Deals are closing."

And he'd say, "That's great. What about forecast accuracy?"

I'd brush past it. "Yeah, forecast is solid. Anyway, let me tell you about this big deal we just closed—"

And he'd interrupt. "Andrew, I need you to focus on forecast accuracy. I need to know what's coming so I can plan."

But I didn't really hear him. I kept leading with what I thought mattered.

**[PAUSE — This is the mistake]**

Six months later, I realized: He didn't care about my big deals. He cared about predictability. He needed to forecast to the board. He needed confidence that I could call a number and hit it.

But I was giving him what I wanted to talk about, not what he needed to hear.

**[TEACHING POINT — The insight]**

Here's what I learned: **Your manager's priorities are not the same as your priorities. And your job is to figure out what they actually care about—not what you think they should care about.**

This sounds obvious. But most managers don't do it. They assume. They guess. Or they never ask.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Simple, actionable]**

So here's what you do: **You ask.**

Not in a casual way. Not in a "Hey, what are your goals?" way that they'll brush off. You ask directly and specifically.

Here's the script:

"I want to make sure I'm focused on what matters most to you. What are your top three priorities for this quarter? What keeps you up at night? What do you need from me to feel confident?"

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

And then you shut up and listen. Don't fill the silence. Don't interrupt. Just listen.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the application]**

After I learned this lesson, I scheduled a 1:1 with my CEO and I said exactly that: "I want to make sure I'm focused on what matters most to you. What are your top three priorities right now?"

He paused. And then he said:

1. Forecast accuracy. I need to know you can call a number and hit it.
2. Pipeline health. I need to see that we have enough in the pipe to sustain growth.
3. Team retention. I can't have you burning through reps.

**[PAUSE]**

Notice what's NOT on that list? Big deals. Revenue growth. Closing logos.

I'd been spending 80% of my energy on things he didn't care about and 20% on the things he did.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The shift]**

Once I knew his priorities, everything changed.

I restructured how I communicated with him. Every update started with forecast accuracy. I showed him pipeline health data. I talked about what I was doing to support and retain my team.

And suddenly, he started trusting me more. Not because my results got better—they were already good. But because I was giving him what he needed to feel confident in me.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you know your manager's priorities:

- You stop wasting time. You focus your energy on what actually matters to them.
- You build trust faster. They feel understood. They feel like you "get" what they need.
- You get more autonomy. When they trust that you're focused on the right things, they micromanage less.
- You get better at managing up. You can structure every conversation, every update, every ask around what they care about.

**[TEACHING POINT — The common mistake]**

Here's the mistake I see new managers make: They ask about priorities once, in an onboarding meeting, and then never ask again.

But priorities change. The board shifts focus. The market changes. A new competitor emerges. What your manager cared about in Q1 might not be what they care about in Q3.

So you need to ask regularly. Every quarter, at minimum. Anytime there's a major shift in the business, ask again.

**[SELF-ASSESSMENT TOOL — Make it interactive]**

Here's what I want you to do right now:

**Step 1:** Write down what you THINK your manager cares about. What are their top 3 priorities? Guess.

**Step 2:** Now be honest—do you actually KNOW, or are you guessing?

**Step 3:** Look at how you've been communicating with them. Are you talking about what THEY care about, or what YOU care about?

**[PAUSE — Reflection moment]**

Most managers realize at this point: "Oh. I've been guessing. And I've probably been guessing wrong."

That's OK. Now you know. And now you can fix it.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The meta-lesson]**

Here's the bigger lesson: **Your manager is not a mystery. They're a person with goals, pressures, and priorities. And most of them will tell you exactly what they care about—if you ask.**

But you have to ask. And you have to listen. And you have to believe them when they tell you.

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I got it wrong]**

I once had a director who kept telling me he cared about "team morale." And I kept brushing it off. I thought he was just being soft. I thought what he REALLY cared about was quota attainment.

So I drove my team hard. We hit our numbers. And he kept saying, "Andrew, I'm worried about your team. Are they OK? How's morale?"

And I'd say, "They're fine. We're hitting quota. That's what matters, right?"

Six months later, three of my top reps quit. And my director said, "I told you I was worried about morale. You didn't listen."

He was right. He told me what he cared about. I didn't believe him. I paid the price.

**[TEACHING POINT — Believe what they tell you]**

So here's the rule: When your manager tells you what they care about, believe them. Even if it doesn't make sense to you. Even if you think something else matters more.

Their priorities are the reality you're working in. Adapt.

**[FRAMEWORK RECAP — Tie it together]**

Let's go back to **Inform, Advocate, Execute.**

**Inform:** Once you know what your manager cares about, you can structure how you inform them around those priorities. You lead with what matters to them, not what matters to you.

**Advocate:** When you advocate for something—a resource, a decision, a change—you frame it in terms of their priorities. "I know forecast accuracy is your top priority. Here's how adding headcount will improve it."

**Execute:** When they make a decision, you execute it—because you understand the context of what they're trying to achieve.

**[PRACTICAL APPLICATION — Give them a tool]**

Here's a simple exercise you can do this week:

**Step 1:** Schedule a 1:1 with your manager. Don't wait for your next scheduled one—do it now.

**Step 2:** Ask them: "What are your top 3 priorities this quarter? What keeps you up at night? What do you need from me to feel confident?"

**Step 3:** Write down what they say. Don't interpret it. Don't filter it. Just write it down.

**Step 4:** Look at your last three updates or conversations with them. Were you talking about their priorities, or yours?

**Step 5:** Adjust. Restructure how you communicate around what they care about.

**[PAUSE]**

That's it. Simple. But most managers never do it.

**[CLOSE — Direct, motivating]**

Here's the bottom line: You can't manage up effectively if you don't know what your manager actually cares about.

Stop guessing. Stop assuming. Ask. Listen. Believe them. And then structure everything you do around those priorities.

This is one of the highest-leverage things you can do as a manager. It takes 30 minutes. And it will change your entire relationship with your boss.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. What do I THINK my manager cares about?
2. What do I KNOW they care about?
3. What's the gap?
4. When will I ask them directly?

Don't wait. Do this this week.

**[END SUB-MODULE 2]**

## SUB-MODULE 3: PROPOSE YOUR COMMUNICATION FRAMEWORK
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,800 words  
**Learning Objective:** Teach managers to proactively design how they communicate with their boss; introduce "Inform, Advocate, Execute" in depth; shift from reactive to proactive

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, strategic]**

Let me ask you something: Who's responsible for designing how you and your manager communicate?

**[PAUSE]**

Most new managers think the answer is: "My manager. They set the structure. They tell me what they want."

And you're wrong.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the shift]**

Here's the truth: **You're responsible for proposing how you communicate with your manager. Not them. You.**

Your manager is busy. They're managing multiple people. They're dealing with their own boss. They're in back-to-back meetings. And they probably don't have time to think deeply about how communication should work between the two of you.

So if you wait for them to design it, one of two things happens:

**Option 1:** They default to whatever they're already doing with everyone else, whether it works for you or not.

**Option 2:** There's no structure at all, and you just have ad hoc conversations that leave both of you frustrated.

**[PAUSE]**

Neither of these is good.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

When I became a manager, I waited for my CEO to tell me how he wanted to communicate. I figured he'd set the cadence, tell me what he needed, give me the structure.

He didn't. He was too busy. So we just had random check-ins. Sometimes we'd go two weeks without talking. Other times, he'd pull me into a hallway conversation and grill me about forecast with no warning.

I felt reactive. I felt like I was always on my heels. And I'm sure he felt frustrated because he wasn't getting what he needed from me.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The insight]**

One day, a mentor said to me: "Andrew, you need to propose how you two communicate. Don't wait for him to design it. You design it, and let him refine it."

I was skeptical. I thought, "Isn't that presumptuous? He's the CEO. Shouldn't he set the structure?"

But my mentor said, "Senior leaders love people who take initiative. It saves them thinking. Propose the structure. If he doesn't like it, he'll tell you. But at least you're driving the relationship."

**[PAUSE]**

So I did.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the application]**

I scheduled a meeting with my CEO and I said:

"I want to make sure I'm giving you what you need to feel confident in me and in the team. Here's what I'm proposing:

- **Weekly 30-minute 1:1:** I'll come prepared with forecast, pipeline health, and any issues that need your input. You can ask me anything.
- **Problem escalation:** When something goes wrong, I'll bring it to you within 24 hours with three things: the problem, the context, and my recommended solution.
- **Monthly performance review:** I'll send you a written summary of team performance, key wins, key challenges, and what I'm doing about them.

Does that work for you? Is there anything you'd add or change?"

**[PAUSE]**

He looked at me and said, "This is great. Let's do it. Can we add a quarterly planning conversation where we talk about headcount and territory?"

I said, "Absolutely."

And just like that, we had a structure.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — What just happened]**

Notice what happened there:

1. I proposed a structure. I didn't ask him to design it. I came with a plan.
2. I framed it around HIS needs ("what you need to feel confident"), not mine.
3. I gave him space to refine it. I didn't present it as final. I presented it as a starting point.
4. He appreciated it. Senior leaders want people to take initiative.

**[FRAMEWORK DEEP DIVE — This is the core]**

Now let's talk about the framework I want you to use for managing up. I introduced it briefly in the first module, but now we're going to go deep.

It's called **Inform, Advocate, Execute.**

This is how you show up in every interaction with your manager. This is your operating system.

**[PAUSE — Set up the framework clearly]**

### **1. INFORM**

**Inform means:** You bring accurate, contextual information to your manager. Not emotions. Not venting. Not spin. Just reality.

**What this looks like:**
- "Here's the forecast: $500K. Here's why I'm confident: we have $400K in advanced stages. Here's the risk: one deal might slip."
- "Sally's underperforming. She's at 60% of quota. Here's what I'm seeing: she's struggling with discovery. Here's what I'm doing: I'm coaching her on qualification and giving her a 30-day plan."
- "The team is frustrated with the new territory structure. Here's what I'm hearing: they feel like their books got smaller without input. Here's what I told them: the decision is final, and we're going to make it work."

**[TEACHING POINT — Why this matters]**

When you inform well, you give your manager the data they need to make decisions and to trust you. You're not dumping problems. You're not hiding bad news. You're giving them reality with context.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't vent: "The leads suck, the reps are struggling, everything's a mess."
- Don't spin: "Everything's great!" when it's not.
- Don't surprise: Bringing up a major problem for the first time when it's already a crisis.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the contrast]**

Early in my career, I'd go to my CEO and say, "We're going to miss forecast this month."

He'd say, "Why?"

And I'd say, "Deals slipped."

That's not informing. That's just stating a fact with no context.

**Here's how I learned to do it better:**

"We're going to miss forecast by $50K. Here's why: Two deals slipped because the buyers went dark after verbal commitments. Here's what I'm doing: I'm working with the reps to get these back on track for next month, and I'm tightening our qualification process so we don't count verbals as committed unless we have a signed LOI."

**[PAUSE]**

See the difference? Same bad news. But one version leaves him with more questions. The other version gives him context and confidence that I'm on it.

**[FRAMEWORK CONTINUED]**

### **2. ADVOCATE**

**Advocate means:** You make the case for what you think is right. You disagree if you disagree. But you do it with data, respect, and in the room—not behind closed doors.

**What this looks like:**
- "I know you want to raise quotas by 30%, but I'm concerned that's not realistic given our current pipeline and capacity. Can we look at the data together?"
- "I think we should add headcount before we expand territory. Here's why: our current reps are at capacity, and if we expand without adding people, I'm worried about retention."
- "I disagree with cutting this rep. I think she's recoverable. Here's my plan to get her back on track. Can you give me 60 days to prove it?"

**[TEACHING POINT — Why this matters]**

When you advocate well, you earn respect. Your manager sees that you have a point of view. You're not just a yes-person. You're thinking strategically.

But—and this is critical—you have to advocate in the right way.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't argue emotionally: "That's a terrible idea."
- Don't undermine in public: Disagreeing with your manager in front of other people without warning.
- Don't advocate without data: "I just feel like this is wrong."

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I got it right and wrong]**

I once disagreed with my CEO about territory design. He wanted to split territories to create more coverage. I thought it would hurt rep productivity.

**Wrong way (what I did the first time):**

In a leadership meeting, he presented the plan. I said, "I don't think this is going to work. It's going to piss off the reps and hurt productivity."

He got defensive. I looked like I was undermining him in front of the team. It damaged our relationship.

**Right way (what I learned to do):**

Before the meeting, I went to him privately and said: "I want to talk about the territory plan. I'm concerned about rep productivity. Can I show you some data on how territory size impacts close rates? I want to make sure we're thinking through the trade-offs."

We had a real conversation. I advocated with data. He adjusted the plan. And in the meeting, I supported it.

**[PAUSE]**

Same disagreement. Completely different outcome.

**[FRAMEWORK CONTINUED]**

### **3. EXECUTE**

**Execute means:** Once a decision is made, you execute it fully. You don't undermine it. You don't go back to your team and say, "The company made this stupid decision." You own it and you lead through it.

**What this looks like:**
- After advocating against a decision and losing, you go to your team and say: "Here's the new plan. I know it's not what we wanted, but this is the direction we're going. Here's how we're going to make it work."
- You deliver company decisions with integrity, even when you don't fully agree with them.
- You commit fully, not halfway.

**[TEACHING POINT — Why this matters]**

When you execute well, you build trust with both sides. Leadership trusts that you're aligned. Your team trusts that you're not political—you're just navigating reality.

But when you execute poorly, you lose credibility everywhere.

**[PERSONAL STORY — The comp plan example]**

I told this story in the first module, but it's worth repeating here because it shows execution in action.

Leadership changed our comp plan mid-year. I advocated against it. I lost. The decision was made.

I could have gone to my team and said, "This is BS. I tried to stop it. Sorry." That would have been undermining.

Instead, I said: "Here's the situation. The company changed our comp plan. I pushed back on it because I was worried about how it would impact you. But the decision is final. So here's what I'm asking: Let's focus on what we can control. I'm going to help you hit your numbers under this new structure. And I need you to keep executing."

**[PAUSE]**

That's execution. I was honest about advocating. But I didn't undermine the decision.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The both/and]**

This is the both/and of managing up:

- You can disagree AND commit.
- You can advocate for your team AND execute company decisions.
- You can be transparent with your team AND not undermine leadership.

**Inform, Advocate, Execute** is how you do all three.

**[PRACTICAL APPLICATION — Give them structure]**

Now let's talk about how to actually propose a communication framework with your manager.

Here's a template:

---

**Email/Meeting Script:**

"Hey [Manager Name], I want to make sure I'm giving you what you need to feel confident in me and the team. I'd like to propose a communication structure between us. Here's what I'm thinking:

**Weekly 1:1 (30 min):**
- Forecast update (number + confidence level + risks/upside)
- Pipeline health (what's moving, what's stuck, what I'm doing about it)
- Team performance (any concerns, any wins)
- Your questions/input

**Problem Escalation:**
- When something goes wrong, I'll bring it to you within 24 hours with: problem, context, my recommendation

**Monthly Summary:**
- Written summary of performance, key wins, key challenges, and actions

Does this work for you? Anything you'd add or change?"

---

**[PAUSE]**

That's it. Simple. Clear. Actionable.

**[TEACHING POINT — What makes this work]**

Notice what this does:

1. You're taking initiative (you're proposing the structure)
2. You're framing it around their needs ("what you need to feel confident")
3. You're giving them space to refine it (not presenting it as final)
4. You're covering all the key elements: forecast, pipeline, team, problems

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you propose a communication framework:

- **Clarity:** Both of you know what to expect. No more guessing.
- **Trust:** Your manager sees that you're proactive and strategic.
- **Efficiency:** You're not wasting time on ad hoc conversations.
- **Autonomy:** When your manager trusts that you're communicating well, they micromanage less.

**[CLOSE — Motivating, direct]**

Here's the bottom line: You can't wait for your manager to design how you communicate. You have to do it.

Propose the structure. Let them refine it. And then execute it consistently.

This is one of the most high-leverage things you can do as a manager. It takes one conversation. And it changes the entire relationship.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. How do I currently communicate with my manager? (Be honest—is there a structure, or is it ad hoc?)
2. What do I think they need from me? (Forecast accuracy? Pipeline visibility? Team updates?)
3. What structure will I propose?

Do it this week. Don't wait.

## SUB-MODULE 4: THE FORECASTING CONVERSATION
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,600 words  
**Learning Objective:** Teach managers how to handle forecasting as a translator; navigate accuracy vs. confidence; stay credible with both leadership and reps

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Provocative, high-stakes]**

Let's talk about the conversation that will make or break your credibility as a manager: forecasting.

**[PAUSE]**

Forecasting is where managing up gets real. It's where you're most exposed. It's where your manager is watching you most closely. And it's where most new managers fail.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why forecasting matters]**

Here's why forecasting is so important:

Your manager needs to forecast up to their boss. Their boss needs to forecast to the board. The board needs to forecast to investors. And if you're wrong—if you sandbag, if you inflate, if you change your number every week—you break the chain.

Your manager stops trusting you. They start questioning every number you give them. They start micromanaging your pipeline. And your credibility erodes.

**[PAUSE]**

So let's talk about how to do this well.

**[THE TENSION — Make it real]**

Here's the problem: Forecasting puts you in the middle of two competing pressures.

**Your reps want:** Low forecast (less pressure, more breathing room, higher chance of beating the number)

**Your manager wants:** Realistic forecast + confidence that you can hit it

**You want:** To call a number that's accurate, that your team can hit, and that your manager believes in

**[PAUSE]**

And here's the trap: If you try to make everyone happy, you make no one happy.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

In my first year as a manager, I tried to thread the needle on forecasting. I'd call a number that was higher than my reps were comfortable with but lower than I thought we'd actually hit. I thought I was being smart—giving my team a buffer while still showing leadership ambition.

It backfired.

My reps thought I was setting them up to fail. And my CEO thought I was sandbagging. He'd say, "Andrew, you called $400K and we hit $500K. Did you not see that coming?"

I'd say, "I wanted to under-promise and over-deliver."

He said, "I don't need you to play games with the forecast. I need you to call the real number so I can plan."

**[PAUSE — This is the lesson]**

He was right. I was trying to be clever. What I should have been doing was being accurate.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The principle]**

Here's the principle: **Your job is to call the REAL number. Not a sandbagged number. Not an inflated number. The real number.**

And then—and this is key—you tell your manager HOW you're going to get there.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, actionable]**

Here's the structure for a forecasting conversation:

### **1. THE NUMBER**

"I'm calling $500K this month."

State it clearly. Don't hedge. Don't say "I think maybe around $450K to $500K." Call the number.

### **2. THE CONFIDENCE LEVEL**

"My confidence is 80%."

Tell them how confident you are. If you're 90% confident, say that. If you're 60% confident, say that. Don't fake it.

### **3. THE BREAKDOWN**

"Here's how I'm getting there:
- $350K in committed/advanced stage (verbal or signed)
- $150K in qualified opportunity that I expect to close
- $50K in upside if two deals accelerate"

Show your work. Don't just give them a number. Show them the pipeline.

### **4. THE RISKS**

"The risks: One deal ($75K) is dependent on a committee decision that might slip. Another deal ($50K) is with a new buyer who's taking longer to evaluate than expected."

Be honest about what could go wrong. Don't hide it.

### **5. THE PLAN**

"Here's what I'm doing to drive this:
- I'm personally reviewing the $75K deal with the rep this week
- I'm pulling in our VP of Sales to close the $50K deal
- I'm pushing the team to advance three more deals into committed stage as backup"

Show them you have a plan. You're not just guessing. You're executing.

**[PAUSE — Let that structure land]**

Number. Confidence. Breakdown. Risks. Plan.

That's the forecasting framework.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show it in action]**

Here's how I learned to do this after my CEO called me out:

"I'm calling $500K this month. My confidence is 75%.

Here's the breakdown: $400K in committed or verbal, $150K in qualified opportunity.

The risks: One deal is $100K and it's with a new buyer who's moving slower than expected. Another deal is $75K and it's dependent on a board approval that might not happen until next month.

Here's my plan: I'm reviewing both deals with the reps this week. I'm bringing in our CEO to close the $100K deal. And I'm pushing three other deals forward as backup in case one of these slips.

If everything goes right, we could hit $550K. If one of those big deals slips, we'll land around $450K. But $500K is the real number."

**[PAUSE]**

My CEO looked at me and said, "That's exactly what I need. Thank you."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why this works]**

Here's why this works:

1. **You're being accurate.** You're not playing games. You're calling the real number.
2. **You're being transparent.** You're showing your work. They can see your thinking.
3. **You're demonstrating confidence.** Not in the outcome, but in your ability to execute.
4. **You're managing risk.** You're not pretending everything's perfect. You're naming what could go wrong and what you're doing about it.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Why transparency builds trust]**

Here's the brain science behind why this works:

When you're transparent and honest, your manager's mirror neurons sync with yours. They feel like you're on the same team. Their amygdala—the threat-detection center—relaxes. They don't have to wonder if you're hiding something.

But when you hedge, when you sandbag, when you change your forecast every week, their brain picks up on the incongruence. They start to distrust you. And once that trust is broken, it's hard to rebuild.

**[TEACHING POINT — The rep conversation]**

Now let's talk about the hard part: How do you navigate forecasting with your reps?

Because here's the tension: Your reps are going to try to influence your forecast.

**What they'll say:**
- "I don't think that deal's going to close. You shouldn't count it."
- "Can we call a lower number? I don't want to be under pressure."
- "If we sandbag, we can beat the number and look like heroes."

**[PAUSE]**

And here's what you need to say:

**[SCRIPT — Give them language]**

"I hear you. But my job is to call the real number, not to manage expectations. I'm not going to sandbag the forecast because that breaks trust with leadership. And I'm not going to inflate it because that sets us up to fail.

I'm going to call what I think we can actually hit. And then we're going to execute to get there.

Your job is to close deals. My job is to forecast accurately. We both have to do our jobs."

**[PAUSE]**

That's the line. You're not being mean. You're being clear.

**[TEACHING POINT — The both/and again]**

Here's the both/and of forecasting:

- You can be transparent with your manager AND not throw your reps under the bus.
- You can call a realistic number AND support your team in hitting it.
- You can be honest about risks AND still demonstrate confidence.

**[PERSONAL STORY — The cost of getting it wrong]**

I told you earlier about losing my job. One of the things that contributed to losing my CEO's trust was inconsistent forecasting.

I'd call a number on Monday, then change it on Friday. I'd say we were tracking to $500K, then we'd hit $400K with no warning.

My CEO started to think I didn't know my business. And he was right—I didn't have a good handle on my pipeline. I was guessing instead of analyzing.

Once he lost confidence in my forecast, he lost confidence in me. And when results dipped, he didn't give me the benefit of the doubt.

**[PAUSE — Let the stakes land]**

That's how important this is. Forecasting isn't just a number. It's a credibility test.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you forecast well:

- **Trust:** Your manager believes you when you say a number. They stop questioning you.
- **Autonomy:** They don't micromanage your pipeline because they trust you have a handle on it.
- **Credibility:** When you call a number and hit it consistently, you build a reputation for reliability.
- **Support:** When you need resources (headcount, tools, budget), they're more likely to give them to you because they trust your judgment.

Here's what happens when you forecast poorly:

- **Micromanagement:** Your manager starts questioning every deal, every stage, every assumption.
- **Lost trust:** They stop believing you. They start triangulating with your reps or RevOps to verify what you're saying.
- **No autonomy:** They take control because they don't trust you to have control.
- **No resources:** When you ask for headcount or budget, they say no because they don't trust you'll use it well.

**[CLOSE — Direct, high-stakes]**

Here's the bottom line: Forecasting is where you prove you can do this job.

It's not about being perfect. It's not about hitting every number. It's about being accurate, transparent, and confident in your ability to execute.

Call the real number. Show your work. Name the risks. Show your plan.

Do that consistently, and your manager will trust you. Do it inconsistently, and you'll lose credibility fast.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. How do I currently forecast? (Am I sandbagging? Inflating? Being accurate?)
2. How confident is my manager in my forecast? (Do they trust my numbers, or do they question them?)
3. What's one thing I can change about how I forecast to build more trust?

Be honest. This is where your credibility is built or broken.

**[END SUB-MODULE 4]**

## SUB-MODULE 5: BUILDING TRUST CAPITAL
**Runtime:** ~15 minutes | ~3,000 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand that results are not enough; teach them how to build personal credibility; show them what happens when trust erodes

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Vulnerable, high-stakes]**

I'm going to tell you the story of how I lost my job.

Not because I want sympathy. But because I don't want you to make the same mistake I did.

**[PAUSE]**

I got promoted to sales manager when I was 32. I was a top rep. I knew how to sell. I knew how to close deals. I thought I was ready.

And in a lot of ways, I was. In my first 15 months as a manager, I doubled our revenue. We went from $3 million ARR to over $6 million. My team was performing. Our pipeline was strong. By every metric, I was crushing it.

**[PAUSE]**

And then I got laid off.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The setup]**

So what happened?

Here's what I thought at the time: "This is unfair. I delivered results. I hit my numbers. I grew the team. Why am I the one getting cut?"

But here's what I learned later: **Results are not enough. You also need trust.**

And I didn't have it.

**[PERSONAL STORY — The unraveling]**

Let me tell you what I did wrong.

I didn't invest in my relationship with the CEO. I thought my results would speak for themselves. I thought if I hit my numbers, everything else would take care of itself.

So I didn't prioritize 1:1s with him. I'd skip them if something more urgent came up. I didn't ask him what he cared about. I didn't propose a communication structure. I just... assumed he knew I was doing a good job because the numbers were good.

And when I did communicate with him, I did it wrong.

I'd dump bad news on him with no context. "Hey, we're going to miss this month." No explanation. No plan.

I'd forecast one number, then change it a week later. He'd say, "You said $500K." I'd say, "Yeah, but a deal slipped. We're going to land at $450K."

I'd push back on his ideas in leadership meetings—in front of other people—without having a conversation with him first. I thought I was being honest. What I was actually doing was making him look bad.

I'd go back to my team and vent about decisions I disagreed with. I thought I was being real with them. What I was actually doing was undermining my own ability to lead.

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional core]**

And slowly, over months, I eroded his trust in me.

He started questioning my judgment. He started asking for more data. He started pulling other people into conversations to verify what I was saying.

And when our growth slowed—not because I was failing, but because the market shifted—he didn't have any trust to fall back on.

He looked at me and thought: "Is Andrew the right person for this job?"

And his answer was no.

**[PAUSE — Let that sink in]**

Six months after we hit $6 million ARR, I was laid off.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The lesson]**

Here's what I learned: **You need TWO things to succeed as a manager: Results AND Trust Capital.**

**Results** = The numbers. Quota attainment. Pipeline health. Team performance.

**Trust Capital** = How much your manager believes in YOU as a person, as a leader, as someone who can navigate complexity and make good decisions.

**[PAUSE]**

Results are table stakes. They're necessary. But they're not sufficient.

If you have great results but no trust, you're fragile. The moment results dip—and they will, because markets change, reps leave, deals slip—your manager will look at YOU as the problem.

But if you have results AND trust, your manager will give you room to navigate challenges. They'll believe in you. They'll support you. They'll advocate for you.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — What is trust capital?]**

So what is trust capital?

Trust capital is the currency you build with your manager over time. It's how much they believe in your judgment, your integrity, your ability to lead.

**You build trust capital by:**

1. **Doing what you say you'll do.** Consistently. No surprises.
2. **Bringing bad news early.** With context. With a plan.
3. **Taking ownership.** Not blaming your team, the market, or circumstances.
4. **Being transparent.** About what you know and what you don't know.
5. **Proposing solutions.** Not just highlighting problems.
6. **Following through.** On commitments, on execution, on communication.
7. **Being consistent.** In forecasting, in updates, in how you show up.

**You lose trust capital by:**

1. **Surprising your manager.** Bringing up problems at the last minute.
2. **Blame-shifting.** "It's the reps' fault. It's the market. It's the product."
3. **Sandbagging or inflating.** Playing games with forecasts.
4. **Being inconsistent.** Changing your story. Changing your forecast.
5. **Undermining leadership.** Venting to your team about company decisions.
6. **Not knowing your business.** Not having answers. Not having data.
7. **Making the same mistake twice.** Not learning. Not adapting.

**[PAUSE — Let those lists land]**

Read that list again. How many of those did I do? Almost all of them.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — A specific moment]**

I remember one moment that crystallized this for me—after I'd already been laid off.

I ran into a former colleague who'd stayed at the company. We got coffee. And I said, "I don't get it. I doubled revenue. Why did I lose my job?"

And he said, "Andrew, you delivered results. But nobody trusted you. The CEO didn't trust your judgment. The board didn't trust your forecast. The team didn't trust that you had their back because you kept changing your story."

**[PAUSE]**

That hurt. But it was true.

I'd built results but I hadn't built trust. And when results dipped, I had nothing to fall back on.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The both/and again]**

Here's the both/and:

- You need results AND trust.
- You need to hit your numbers AND build credibility.
- You need to execute AND invest in the relationship.

**Most new managers focus on results and ignore trust. That's the mistake.**

**[FRAMEWORK CALLBACK — Inform, Advocate, Execute]**

Let's go back to **Inform, Advocate, Execute.** Because this framework is how you build trust capital.

**Inform:** When you inform your manager accurately and consistently, you build trust. They know you're not hiding things. They know you have a handle on your business.

**Advocate:** When you advocate respectfully and with data, you build trust. They see that you're thinking strategically. They see that you're not a yes-person.

**Execute:** When you execute fully—even on decisions you don't love—you build trust. They see that you're aligned. They see that you're a leader.

**[PAUSE]**

This is how trust is built. Not through big gestures. Through consistent, everyday behaviors.

**[TEACHING POINT — Trust is slow to build, fast to lose]**

Here's the hard truth: Trust capital takes months to build and days to lose.

You can do everything right for six months, and then one big mistake—one surprise, one blame-shift, one moment of undermining—can set you back.

So you have to be intentional. You have to protect it. You have to invest in it every day.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — The underperformer situation]**

Let me give you an example of how trust capital plays out in a real situation.

I had a rep—let's call her Sarah—who was struggling. She was at 50% of quota. She'd been underperforming for two quarters.

I had two choices:

**Option 1:** Hide it from my CEO. Coach her quietly. Hope she turns it around before he notices.

**Option 2:** Bring it up proactively. Tell him what's happening. Tell him my plan.

**[PAUSE]**

Early in my career, I would have chosen Option 1. I would have tried to hide it because I didn't want him to think I couldn't manage my team.

But by this point, I'd learned. So I chose Option 2.

I went to my CEO and said: "I need to tell you about Sarah. She's underperforming—50% of quota for two quarters. Here's what I'm seeing: she's struggling with discovery and qualification. Here's my plan: I'm putting her on a 60-day improvement plan. I'm coaching her personally on every deal. And I'm going to make a call in 60 days about whether she can recover.

I'm not asking you to fix this. I'm telling you what I'm doing. And I'll update you in 30 days."

**[PAUSE]**

You know what he said?

"Thank you for telling me. Let me know if you need anything. And keep me posted."

That's it. No panic. No micromanaging. Just trust.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why this worked]**

Here's why that worked:

1. I brought it up proactively. He didn't find out from someone else.
2. I brought context. I didn't just say "Sarah's struggling." I said WHY.
3. I brought a plan. I wasn't asking him to solve it. I was telling him what I was doing.
4. I demonstrated ownership. I wasn't blaming Sarah. I was owning the situation.

That ONE conversation built trust capital. He saw that I was on top of my business. He saw that I wasn't hiding problems. He saw that I was leading.

**[CONTRAST — What losing trust looks like]**

Now let me show you the opposite.

Later in my tenure, I had another underperformer. And this time, I made a different choice.

I didn't tell my CEO. I thought I could fix it quietly. I didn't want him to lose confidence in me.

But three months later, he saw the rep's numbers in a board report and said, "Andrew, why is this rep at 30% of quota? How long has this been going on?"

I said, "A few months. I've been working on it."

He said, "Why didn't you tell me?"

**[PAUSE]**

I didn't have a good answer. And in that moment, I lost trust capital.

He started wondering: What else is Andrew not telling me? Does he have a handle on his team? Can I trust his updates?

And once that doubt creeps in, it's hard to shake.

**[TEACHING POINT — Bad news ages poorly]**

Here's the rule: **Bad news ages poorly. Bring it up early.**

If you have a problem, your instinct might be to hide it and try to fix it before your manager finds out. Don't. That strategy always backfires.

Bring it up early. With context. With a plan. And you'll build trust instead of losing it.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Let's talk about what trust capital actually gets you.

**When you have trust capital:**

- **Autonomy:** Your manager doesn't micromanage you. They trust you to handle your business.
- **Support:** When you need resources—headcount, budget, tools—they're more likely to say yes because they trust you'll use them well.
- **Advocacy:** Your manager advocates for you with THEIR boss. They talk you up. They fight for your promotion, your raise, your visibility.
- **Room for mistakes:** When something goes wrong, they give you the benefit of the doubt. They assume you're handling it.
- **Resilience:** When results dip, they don't panic. They trust that you'll navigate it.

**When you don't have trust capital:**

- **Micromanagement:** Your manager questions everything. They triangulate with other people to verify what you're saying.
- **No resources:** When you ask for headcount or budget, they say no. They don't trust you'll use it effectively.
- **No advocacy:** Your manager doesn't fight for you. They don't talk you up. You're on your own.
- **Zero margin for error:** When something goes wrong, they assume it's your fault. They start looking for a replacement.
- **Fragility:** When results dip, you're in danger. You don't have trust to fall back on.

**[PAUSE — Let the stakes land]**

This is why trust capital matters. It's the difference between thriving and surviving. Between getting promoted and getting laid off.

**[TEACHING POINT — How to assess your trust capital]**

So how do you know if you have trust capital with your manager?

Here are some questions to ask yourself:

1. **Does my manager give me autonomy, or do they micromanage me?**
2. **When I bring up a problem, do they trust that I'm handling it, or do they take over?**
3. **When I ask for resources, do they say yes, or do they push back?**
4. **Do they advocate for me with their boss, or am I invisible?**
5. **When results dip, do they support me, or do they panic?**

**[PAUSE]**

Be honest. If the answer to most of those questions is negative, you don't have trust capital. And you need to start building it.

**[PRACTICAL TOOL — Give them a checklist]**

Here's a simple checklist for building trust capital:

**Daily:**
- Do what you say you'll do
- Communicate proactively (don't wait to be asked)

**Weekly:**
- Bring updates with context (not just data)
- Ask your manager what they need from you

**Monthly:**
- Reflect on your relationship: Is trust growing or eroding?
- Bring bad news early (with a plan)

**Quarterly:**
- Re-confirm your manager's priorities
- Assess: Am I building trust or losing it?

**[CLOSE — Vulnerable, motivating]**

Here's the bottom line: I lost my job because I didn't build trust capital.

I delivered results. I hit my numbers. I grew the team. But I didn't invest in the relationship. I didn't manage up intentionally. And when things got hard, I didn't have trust to fall back on.

**[PAUSE]**

Don't make the same mistake.

Results are necessary. But trust is what makes you resilient. Trust is what gives you autonomy. Trust is what gets you promoted.

Build it intentionally. Protect it fiercely. And understand that it's as important as the numbers.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. Do I have trust capital with my manager? (Be honest.)
2. What am I doing well? (Where am I building trust?)
3. What am I losing trust on? (Where am I making mistakes?)
4. What's one thing I can do this week to build more trust?

Don't wait until it's too late. Start building trust capital now.
