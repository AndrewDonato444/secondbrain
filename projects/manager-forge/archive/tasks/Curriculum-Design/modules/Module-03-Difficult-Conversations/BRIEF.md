# Difficult Conversation Choreography — Module Brief

## Executive Overview

**Difficult Conversation Choreography** addresses the emotional labor that kills new managers: firing, performance plans, critical feedback, comp plan changes, underperformer conversations. This module doesn't teach "nice" conversation skills. It teaches how to have truthful, structured conversations that respect people's adulthood and build credibility.

The core insight: **Conversations aren't isolated. They're cumulative. Every conversation is a net product of all previous interactions. If you build trust and framework early, conversations are positioned to succeed. If you don't, every conversation fails—no matter how well you execute it.**

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

Every conversation you have with a rep is a net product of all the conversations and interactions you've had with them up until this point. You don't get an opportunity to have a good conversation one time and a bad conversation another time.

**More specifically:**

1. **Trust is cumulative, not situational.** If you don't establish trust early, every conversation will be hard, no matter how well you handle it. If you do establish trust + framework early, every conversation is positioned to succeed.
2. **Don't underestimate adulthood.** Your reps are adults. They've lived. They understand what the truth is. When you try to mask hard truth or soften it, you erode trust. When you're real, they respect you.
3. **No surprises ever.** The systems you set up and the interaction points you have should never lead to surprises. The underperformer should know they're underperforming before the formal conversation. The comp plan should be explained before the rollout. The person getting fired should have had multiple warnings.
4. **Being liked is not the goal.** Respect is. Be the one person who tells the truth. Be real. Check exaggeration. Deliver hard feedback without flinching. That's where respect comes from.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Clear on how to structure difficult conversations (not winging it)
- Confident that adults can handle hard truth
- Equipped to avoid surprises
- Understanding that consistency in small interactions builds the foundation for big conversations

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers avoid difficult conversations because they want to be liked. They think softening bad news will protect the relationship. They try to motivate underperformers without setting clear expectations for what happens if they don't improve. They hype new comp plans without doing the math. They surprise people with formal consequences because they were hoping to fix things quietly.

**Result:** Trust erodes. When the hard conversation finally happens, it's a shock. The rep feels blindsided. They lose respect. The manager looks weak.

### The Real Issue

**You don't need to be liked. You need to be respected.** And respect comes from being real.

Most new managers think they're being kind by softening truth. What they're actually doing is treating their reps like children who can't handle reality. Adults can handle reality. What they can't handle is surprise, inconsistency, or being treated like they're too fragile for the truth.

The managers who succeed are the ones who say: "Here's what the data shows. Here's what I think about it. Here's what needs to happen next. I'm always going to tell you the truth, whether it's good news or bad news."

That's respect.

## Key Frameworks & Concepts

### 1. **Conversations Are Cumulative**

Every conversation with a rep exists in context. Context comes from:
- How consistently you've followed through on what you said
- How predictable you are (do they know what to expect from you?)
- Whether you've been real or been a politician
- How much they trust you to have their back while also being honest

**New managers make the mistake of thinking a difficult conversation can be "pulled off" if they just nail the execution in that moment.** Wrong. The conversation's outcome was largely determined in the 50 conversations before it.

**This is why:**
- An underperformer who knows they're struggling before the formal conversation takes it better than one who's blindsided
- A comp plan change is digestible if you've been doing the math with the team all along
- Firing someone is clean if there were multiple warnings, not a surprise

**The implication:** Your job as a manager isn't to "nail" difficult conversations. Your job is to build trust and framework so that when difficult conversations happen, they're not surprising or relationship-damaging.

### 2. **Don't Underestimate Adulthood**

Your reps are adults. They've lived. They've been through ups and downs. They understand what truth is.

When you try to mask bad news or make it sound better than it is, two things happen:

1. **They know you're spinning.** Adults can smell patronization.
2. **They lose respect for you.** Because now they know you're not real with them.

**Better approach:** Be direct. Be factual. Be honest about what the situation is and what needs to happen. Respect their ability to handle it.

**Examples of underestimating adulthood:**
- "The comp plan is actually better!" (when you haven't done the math and they know it)
- "You're doing great, just need a little push!" (when the data shows they're underperforming)
- "We're making some changes to territory." (surprise shift without warning)

**Example of respecting adulthood:**
- "I did the math on the comp plan. Here's where you make more, here's where you make less. Here's how to maximize it." (truth + tools)
- "Your numbers are 30% below what we need. Here's the data. Here's the expectation. Here's my plan to help you. Here's what happens if you don't improve." (no surprise, clear expectations)

### 3. **The Data-First Framework**

This is the structure that makes difficult conversations possible without surprises or damaged relationships.

**Step 1: The Data**
Here's what happened. Numbers, metrics, observable facts.
- "Your close rate is 15%. The expectation is 25%. That's a gap."
- "You've missed quota for two consecutive quarters."
- "Your forecast accuracy is 60%. We need 85%."

**Step 2: The Story**
What does the data tell us? What's the pattern?
- "The data shows you're struggling with discovery. You're qualifying too many early-stage deals that never close."
- "The pattern is: you get busy and stop prospecting, which starves the pipeline."
- "The comp plan shift means you make more on X but less on Y. That's the trade."

**Step 3: Agreement on Truth**
Do we agree? Is there something we're missing? Get on the same page about what's actually happening.
- "Do you see the same thing in the data?"
- "Is there context I'm missing?"
- "Do you agree that this needs to change?"

Once you have agreement on truth, emotions are manageable. Because you're not debating what's real.

**Step 4: The Outcome**
What are we chasing? What's the goal?
- "We need you to hit 25% close rate."
- "We need you back to quota."
- "We need you to maximize this new comp plan."

**Step 5: The Expectations**
Given the data and the goal, what's expected moving forward? What's the path? What are the milestones?
- "Here's the 60-day improvement plan."
- "Here's the comp plan roadmap."
- "Here's what success looks like."

**Step 6: Managing Emotions**
Once you have data, story, truth agreement, outcome, and expectations, emotions are something you can navigate, not something that derails the conversation.

If someone gets upset, you can say: "I know this is frustrating. And the data is the data. Let's figure out what we can control."

### 4. **No Surprises Ever**

This is the fundamental principle that prevents difficult conversations from becoming crises.

**No surprises means:**
- Underperformers know they're underperforming before the formal conversation
- People know comp is changing before the announcement
- Someone being fired has had multiple warnings
- Performance improvements have been discussed informally before a plan is formalized

**How you prevent surprises:**
- **Consistency in small interactions.** Regular 1:1s where you're real about performance. "You're at 60% of quota. Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what we need to do."
- **Framework from day one.** "Here's how we work together. Here's how I give feedback. Here's what I expect."
- **Transparency.** "I'm going to be straight with you. Always. Good news and bad news."

**What surprises look like (and why they're dangerous):**
- Manager avoids saying anything about underperformance for 3 months, then suddenly: "You're on a PIP."
- Team hears about comp plan change the day it's announced, with no warning.
- Someone who thought they were doing fine gets fired.

All of these destroy trust because they feel like betrayal.

### 5. **Being Real > Being Liked**

This is the mindset shift that unlocks difficult conversations.

New managers think their job is to keep people happy. Wrong. Your job is to help them succeed and to be truthful about what that requires.

**What "being real" means:**
- Tell people when they're off track, not just when they do well
- Do the math instead of hyping (comp plans, quotas, expectations)
- Check exaggeration ("You're saying the comp plan is terrible, but based on the math, here's why it's actually decent for you")
- Call out patterns ("You keep saying you'll prospect more, but the pipeline data shows you haven't. Let's talk about why")
- Be the one person they can trust for truth

**Why this builds more respect than being nice:**
- People know you're not bullshitting them
- They can rely on you (predictability)
- They see you're thinking, not just managing up
- They feel like you actually care about their success (because you're honest about obstacles)

**The contrast:**
- Nice manager: "You're doing great! Just keep pushing!" (rep doesn't trust them, thinks they're not paying attention)
- Real manager: "You're at 60% of quota. Here's what I'm seeing as the problem. Here's how we fix it." (rep knows where they stand, trusts the feedback)

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: The Cumulative Conversation (14 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand that conversations don't happen in a vacuum
- Show them that trust is built over time, not in a moment
- Introduce the concept that small interactions set up big conversations

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Every conversation with a rep exists in context
- That context is built from 50 previous conversations
- The outcome of a difficult conversation was largely determined before it happened
- Your job isn't to "nail" a difficult conversation; your job is to build foundation so conversations succeed
- Consistency in small things = trust in big things

**Real Examples:**
- An underperformer who knows they're struggling vs. one who's blindsided
- A comp plan change that's been talked about vs. one that's a surprise
- Firing someone who had warnings vs. someone who had no signal
- A 1:1 pattern where manager is always real vs. one where manager sometimes is, sometimes isn't

**Personal Story:**
- Time Andrew avoided difficult conversation because he wanted to be liked; delayed problem, made it worse
- Time Andrew was consistent in small interactions; big conversation was easy

**Tension to Address:**
- "But I don't want to have hard conversations every week." (Then the one hard conversation will be harder)
- "Won't I look like I'm constantly criticizing?" (No, you're being real, which is respectable)

### Sub-Module 2: Don't Underestimate Adulthood (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers stop treating reps like fragile children
- Show them that adults respect truth more than kindness
- Build the case for directness

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Your reps are adults. They understand what truth is.
- When you soften bad news, they know you're spinning
- Spinning erodes trust more than honesty
- Respect comes from being real, not from being nice
- Be the one person who tells the truth

**Phrases/Scripts:**
- "Here's the data. Here's what it means. Here's what we need to do." (vs. "You're doing great, just need to push a little harder!")
- "I know you want to hear X, but the math shows Y. Here's how to maximize this." (vs. hyping a bad comp plan)
- "You're not where we need you to be. Here's the gap. Here's the plan." (vs. avoiding it)

**Business Impact:**
- Respect > Likability
- People trust you more when you're real
- They perform better when they know where they stand
- Retention improves (people leave managers who aren't real; they stay with managers who are)

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they get upset with the truth?" (Better upset and on track than happy and failing)
- "Isn't that harsh?" (No, it's respectful. Harsh is surprising them after hiding the truth)

### Sub-Module 3: The Data-First Framework (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach the step-by-step structure for difficult conversations
- Show how data prevents emotion from derailing things
- Give them a framework to lean on

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Start with data (observable facts)
- Tell the story (what does the data mean?)
- Get agreement on truth (do we agree?)
- State the outcome (what are we chasing?)
- Set expectations (what's required moving forward?)
- Then manage emotions (now they're manageable)

**The Framework In Depth:**

**DATA:** "Your close rate is 15%. The expectation is 25%."

**STORY:** "The data shows you're struggling with discovery. Early-stage deals that should go nowhere are in your pipeline taking your time."

**AGREEMENT:** "Do you see it that way? Is there context I'm missing?"

**OUTCOME:** "We need to get your close rate to 25%."

**EXPECTATIONS:** "Here's how we get there: [60-day plan with milestones, coaching structure, support]"

**EMOTIONS:** "I know this is tough to hear. And the data is the data. Let's focus on what we can control."

**Real Examples:**
- Underperformer conversation (data-first vs. emotional-first)
- Comp plan explanation (data-first vs. hype-first)
- Performance plan (data-first vs. surprise-first)

**Why This Works:**
- Once you agree on truth, there's no debate
- Emotions become something you manage, not something that derails
- Rep understands it's not personal; it's about the data
- Manager has a roadmap so it doesn't feel chaotic

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they don't agree with the data?" (Then you figure out why. Is the data wrong? Is there context? But you work from truth.)
- "Does this feel cold?" (No, it feels professional. It respects their adulthood.)

### Sub-Module 4: The Real Conversations (16 min)
**Objectives:**
- Walk through the 4 hardest conversations managers face
- Show how to apply the framework to each
- Give specific language for each scenario

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Not all difficult conversations are the same
- But they all follow the same structure (data → story → agreement → outcome → expectations)
- Specificity matters (comp plan is different from underperformer)
- Preparation matters more than winging it

**The Four Hardest Conversations:**

**1. Underperformer Before the Plan**
- What it is: Realizing someone isn't where they need to be before you formalize a plan
- Why it's hard: You want to give them a chance privately; you don't want to make it "official"
- The mistake: Avoiding the conversation, hoping they'll improve; then surprising them with a plan
- The right approach: Data-first. "Here's the gap. Here's what I see. Here's what needs to change. If it doesn't, we'll move to a formal plan."
- Why it matters: If you have this conversation early and consistently, the formal plan isn't a surprise

**2. Firing Someone**
- What it is: Letting someone go
- Why it's hard: Emotionally difficult; you feel bad for them; you're ending their paycheck
- The mistake: Agonizing over it; trying to make it less painful; making it longer than it needs to be
- The right approach: Have had multiple warnings before this point. Be direct. Be brief. HR takes over after 2 minutes.
- Script: "This isn't working out. Your last day is [date]. HR will explain benefits and logistics. You can take personal items from your desk."
- Why it matters: If you've been real and consistent leading up to this, it's not a shock. It's sad but expected.

**3. New Comp Plan**
- What it is: Explaining a compensation change
- Why it's hard: People worry about money; if the plan is worse for some, they'll resist
- The mistake: Hyping it ("This is great!") without doing the math; not giving people tools to maximize it
- The right approach: Do the math first. Show comparisons. Show the hooks (how to maximize it). Be honest about trade-offs.
- Script: "Here's the old plan. Here's the new plan. You make more on X, less on Y. Here's how to maximize: [specific tactics]."
- Why it matters: People don't care if the plan is "better" or "worse" in the abstract. They care if they can make money. Show them.

**4. Performance Plan**
- What it is: Formalizing an improvement plan
- Why it's hard: It feels official; rep knows there are stakes; you're documenting failure
- The mistake: Delivering it as a surprise because you were hoping they'd improve on their own
- The right approach: They know they're underperforming because you've been having conversations. The plan is just formalizing what you've been saying.
- Script: "We've talked about the gap. Now we're formalizing it. Here's what success looks like. Here's how I'll help. Here's what happens if we don't get there."
- Why it matters: No surprises. No shock. Just formalization of what was already clear.

**Business Impact:**
- Clear conversations lead to better outcomes
- People respect you for being specific and prepared
- Retention improves (people leave vague, avoidant managers)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't firing someone more humane if I ease them into it?" (No. Be direct. Be kind. But be quick.)
- "What if they fight the comp plan?" (They will. But if you've done the math, you can counter with specifics.)

### Sub-Module 5: Never Break Trust (14 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand that trust is fragile once built
- Show them how small inconsistencies destroy trust
- Give them a framework for protecting trust

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Trust is slow to build, fast to lose
- One broken promise erodes months of foundation
- Consistency matters more than perfection
- The moment you stop preparing, stop following through, stop being real—people notice
- Protecting trust means staying committed to the framework even when it's inconvenient

**What Breaks Trust:**
- Canceling a scheduled 1:1 repeatedly
- Saying you'll do something and not doing it
- Being real one time, politician the next
- Not following up on a concern someone raised
- Showing favoritism
- Making decisions without explanation
- Being inconsistent with different people

**What Builds Trust (and protects it):**
- Always prepare for interactions (even when you're busy)
- Follow through on what you say
- Be consistent (same person to everyone)
- Address concerns when raised (don't let them fester)
- Be equally real with everyone
- Explain your decisions
- Stick to the framework even when it's hard

**Personal Story:**
- Time Andrew let standards slip; people noticed immediately; trust eroded in weeks
- Time Andrew stayed consistent even when it was inconvenient; trust deepened

**Business Impact:**
- Consistent managers get autonomy (leadership trusts them)
- Inconsistent managers get micromanagement (leadership doesn't trust them)
- Consistent managers get better performance (reps trust them)

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if I'm too busy to prepare for 1:1?" (Then you've already broken trust)
- "Do I really need to be the same with everyone?" (Yes. Consistency is part of trustworthiness)
