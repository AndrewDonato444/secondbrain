# DIFFICULT CONVERSATION CHOREOGRAPHY — Module Script
## Instructor/Video Scripts for Asynchronous Delivery

**Module:** Difficult Conversation Choreography  
**Target Audience:** First-time SaaS sales managers (0–12 months in role)  
**Delivery:** 5 sub-modules | ~10-15 min each | Asynchronous video + weekly Zoom discussion  
**Tone:** Direct, honest, battle-tested, respectful  
**Core Teaching Goal:** Conversations are cumulative. Foundation matters more than execution. Adults can handle truth.

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE CUMULATIVE CONVERSATION
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,800 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand that conversations don't happen in a vacuum; trust is built over time; small interactions set up big conversations

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, relatable]**

So you need to fire someone. Or tell a rep they're underperforming. Or explain a comp plan change that's going to piss people off.

And you're sitting there thinking: "How do I have this conversation? What do I say? How do I make this go well?"

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Here's what I need you to understand: That's the wrong question.

The real question isn't "How do I have this one conversation well?" The real question is: "What have I been doing for the last six months that will make this conversation succeed or fail?"

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core insight]**

Because here's the truth: **Every conversation you have with a rep is a net product of all the conversations and interactions you've had with them up until this point.**

You don't get to have a good conversation one time and a bad conversation another time. You don't get to wing it. You don't get to start fresh.

Every difficult conversation you have exists in context. And that context was built—or not built—by you, over months, through dozens of small interactions.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

Let me tell you about two firing conversations I had. Same situation. Two completely different outcomes.

**The first one:** I had a rep—let's call him Tom—who wasn't performing. He'd been at 60% of quota for three months. I knew he was struggling. But I kept hoping he'd turn it around. I didn't want to have the hard conversation. I wanted to be liked. So I avoided it.

I'd say things like, "You're doing good work, Tom. Just keep pushing." I'd soften the feedback. I'd avoid talking about the numbers because I didn't want to make him feel bad.

Three months went by. No improvement. Finally, leadership told me: "You need to let him go."

So I scheduled a meeting. I told him we were letting him go. And he looked at me like I'd just punched him in the stomach.

He said, "What? I thought I was doing fine. You never told me this was coming."

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional anchor]**

And he was right. I didn't tell him. I avoided it. I wanted to be nice. And in doing that, I destroyed his trust in me.

**The second one:** I had another rep—let's call her Maria—who was also struggling. Same situation. 60% of quota. Three months.

But this time, I did it differently.

In our first 1:1 after I noticed the pattern, I said: "Maria, we need to talk about your numbers. You're at 60% of quota. That's below where we need you to be. Here's what I'm seeing in the data. Here's what I think is happening. Here's what we need to fix. If we can't get you back on track in the next 60 days, we'll need to talk about whether this role is working out."

**[PAUSE]**

No surprise. No softening. Just truth.

Over the next 60 days, I coached her. We worked the plan. But she didn't improve. And when I had to let her go, she wasn't blindsided. She said, "I know. I tried. It didn't work. I appreciate you being honest with me from the start."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The contrast]**

Same situation. Two different outcomes.

**Tom felt betrayed.** Because I avoided the truth for months and then surprised him with the consequence.

**Maria felt respected.** Because I told her the truth early, gave her a clear plan, and didn't surprise her when it didn't work out.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

That's what I mean by cumulative conversation. The firing conversation with Tom was determined in the 20 conversations before it where I wasn't real with him. The firing conversation with Maria was positioned to succeed because I built trust and clarity from day one.

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, simple]**

Here's the principle: **Trust is cumulative, not situational.**

If you don't establish trust early—through consistent, honest, real interactions—every conversation will be hard, no matter how well you execute it in the moment.

But if you do establish trust and framework early, every conversation is positioned to succeed. Even the hard ones.

**[TEACHING POINT — What builds cumulative trust]**

So how do you build cumulative trust? What are the small interactions that set up the big conversations?

**1. Consistency in 1:1s**

Are you having regular 1:1s with every rep? Or are you canceling them when you're busy? Because here's what happens when you cancel: Your reps learn that you're not reliable. And when you finally show up for the hard conversation, they don't trust that you've been paying attention.

**2. Honest feedback in the moment**

Are you giving real feedback in real time? Or are you saving it all up for a formal review? Because if you wait, everything feels like a surprise. But if you're consistently saying, "Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what needs to change," then the formal conversation is just a continuation of what you've already been saying.

**3. Transparency about expectations**

Do your reps know what success looks like? Do they know what happens if they don't hit it? Or are you keeping that vague because you don't want to create pressure?

Vague expectations don't protect people. They set them up to fail.

**4. Following through on what you say**

If you say you're going to do something, do you do it? Or do you let things slide? Because every time you don't follow through, you erode trust. And when you show up for the difficult conversation, they're thinking, "Why should I believe you this time?"

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Show the application]**

When I started managing a new team, I made a commitment to myself: I'm going to be real from day one. No surprises. No softening. Just truth.

So in my first 1:1 with each rep, I said:

"Here's how I work. I'm going to be honest with you. Always. Good news and bad news. If you're on track, I'll tell you. If you're off track, I'll tell you. You will never be surprised by feedback from me. And in return, I need you to be honest with me. If something's not working, tell me. If you're struggling, tell me. We'll figure it out together."

**[PAUSE]**

That was the foundation. And over the next six months, I lived it. When someone was struggling, I said so. When someone was crushing it, I said that too. When I disagreed with a company decision, I explained the context but I didn't undermine it.

And when I finally had to have a hard conversation—putting someone on a performance plan—it wasn't a shock. Because we'd been having real conversations all along.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Ground in research]**

Here's why this works from a brain perspective.

Your brain is constantly scanning for patterns. It's asking: "Can I trust this person? Are they predictable? Are they safe?"

When you're inconsistent—when you're nice one day and critical the next, when you avoid feedback and then drop a bomb—your rep's amygdala lights up. They feel unsafe. They stop trusting you.

But when you're consistent—when you're always real, always honest, always following the same framework—their brain relaxes. They know what to expect. And even when the news is bad, they can handle it because it's not a surprise.

**[TEACHING POINT — The mistake new managers make]**

Here's the mistake I see new managers make over and over: They think they can save up feedback and deliver it all at once in a "big conversation."

They avoid the small, real conversations because they don't want to be negative. They don't want to create tension. They want to be liked.

So they wait. And wait. And wait.

And then when they finally have the conversation—the performance plan, the firing, the critical feedback—the rep is blindsided. They feel betrayed. And they lose respect for the manager.

**[PAUSE]**

You can't avoid the small conversations and expect the big conversation to go well. It doesn't work that way.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The both/and]**

Here's the both/and:

- You can be honest AND kind.
- You can give critical feedback AND maintain trust.
- You can hold people accountable AND be someone they respect.

But you have to do it consistently. In every conversation. Not just the formal ones.

**[REAL EXAMPLE — Make it tangible]**

Let me give you another example. Comp plan changes.

I once had to roll out a comp plan that was going to be worse for some reps and better for others. Leadership needed to shift incentives. It was final.

I had a choice: Drop it on the team in an all-hands meeting and hope for the best? Or prepare them in advance?

I chose advance. Two weeks before the announcement, I started talking about it in 1:1s. I said:

"The company is looking at adjusting the comp plan. I don't have all the details yet, but I wanted you to know it's coming. When I have the numbers, I'll walk you through them and we'll do the math together. No surprises."

**[PAUSE]**

When the plan was announced, my team wasn't happy. But they weren't shocked. Because I'd been preparing them.

**Contrast that with another team:** Their manager didn't say anything. Just dropped the news in an all-hands. The team felt blindsided. They lost trust in him. Three reps quit.

**[TEACHING POINT — The compounding effect]**

Here's what I want you to understand: Small interactions compound. They build on each other. They create the foundation.

When you're consistent in small things—regular 1:1s, real feedback, honest updates, following through on commitments—you build a foundation of trust. And when the big conversation happens, that foundation holds.

But when you're inconsistent—canceling meetings, avoiding feedback, surprising people with decisions—you erode trust. And when the big conversation happens, you have nothing to stand on.

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I got it wrong]**

I told you about the two firing conversations. Let me tell you about the bigger lesson.

After I fired Tom—the one I blindsided—I sat with my own manager and said, "That was brutal. I don't know how I could have made that go better."

And he said, "Andrew, that conversation was never going to go well. Because you didn't build the foundation. You avoided the truth for three months. You can't make up for that in one conversation."

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional core]**

He was right. I thought I could nail the execution of that one conversation and it would be fine. But the outcome of that conversation was determined weeks earlier, in all the conversations I didn't have.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The mindset shift]**

Here's the mindset shift: **Stop thinking about difficult conversations as isolated events. Start thinking about them as the culmination of everything that came before.**

Your job as a manager isn't to "nail" the difficult conversation. Your job is to build trust and framework so that when the difficult conversation happens, it's not surprising or relationship-damaging.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you get this right:

- **Underperformers know they're underperforming** before you put them on a plan. The plan is just formalizing what they already know.
- **Comp changes are digestible** because you've been transparent about the possibility and you help people understand the math.
- **Firing someone is sad but not shocking** because they've had multiple warnings and clear expectations.
- **Critical feedback lands well** because it's part of an ongoing dialogue, not a surprise attack.

Here's what happens when you get it wrong:

- **People feel blindsided.** They lose trust in you.
- **Every conversation is a crisis** because there's no foundation.
- **You burn out** because every difficult conversation is emotionally exhausting.
- **Your team disengages** because they don't know where they stand.

**[CLOSE — Grounded, direct]**

So here's the bottom line: The difficult conversation you're worried about? It's already happening. It's happening in every 1:1. In every piece of feedback. In every interaction.

The question isn't "How do I have that conversation?" The question is: "What foundation am I building right now?"

Be consistent. Be honest. Be real. Follow through. Don't avoid the small conversations because you want to be liked.

Because in six months, when you need to have the big conversation, the outcome is going to be determined by what you did today.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Take a minute and write this down:

1. Think about one rep on your team. What foundation have you built with them over the last few months? Have you been consistent? Honest? Real?

2. Is there a difficult conversation you've been avoiding? Why?

3. What small conversation do you need to have this week to start building the foundation?

Be honest. That's where your work begins.

**[END SUB-MODULE 1]**

## SUB-MODULE 2: DON'T UNDERESTIMATE ADULTHOOD
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,600 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers stop treating reps like fragile children; show them that adults respect truth more than kindness; build the case for directness

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Provocative, direct]**

Let me ask you something: How old are your reps?

**[PAUSE]**

I'm serious. What are their ages? Mid-20s? Early 30s? 40s?

Here's my follow-up question: Do you treat them like adults?

**[PAUSE — Let them think about it]**

Because here's what I see all the time with new managers: They treat their reps like children who can't handle the truth.

They soften bad news. They spin negative situations into "opportunities." They avoid being direct because they think it's mean. They try to motivate people by hyping things instead of being honest about reality.

And you know what happens? **Their reps lose respect for them.**

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core insight]**

Here's the truth: **Your reps are adults. They've lived. They've been through ups and downs. They understand what truth is. And when you try to mask it or make it sound better than it is, they know. And they lose respect for you.**

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

Let me tell you about a mistake I made.

I was managing a team when the company rolled out a new comp plan. And to be honest, it was worse for most of the team. The commission rates went down. The accelerators were harder to hit. The math didn't lie.

But in the leadership meeting where they announced it, they kept saying, "This is actually better! You'll make more money if you execute well! This is a great opportunity!"

And I knew it was BS. I'd done the math. For 60% of my team, this was a pay cut unless they significantly overperformed.

But I went back to my team and I repeated the company line. I said, "Hey, this new comp plan is actually really good. If you hit these targets, you'll make more. Let's focus on execution."

**[PAUSE — This is the mistake]**

And you know what happened?

One of my top reps—let's call her Sarah—came to my office an hour later and said, "Andrew, I did the math. This comp plan is worse for me. I'd have to hit 130% of quota to make what I made last year at 100%. Why are you telling me it's good when it's not?"

**[PAUSE]**

I didn't have an answer. Because she was right. I was spinning.

And in that moment, I lost her respect.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The insight]**

Here's what I learned: **Adults can smell patronization. And when you spin, when you soften, when you try to make bad news sound good, they know. And they stop trusting you.**

Sarah didn't need me to tell her the comp plan was good. She needed me to tell her the truth and help her figure out how to navigate it.

**[TEACHING POINT — What "Don't Underestimate Adulthood" means]**

So what does it mean to not underestimate adulthood?

It means:

**1. Tell the truth about what's happening**

Don't spin. Don't soften. Don't make bad news sound good. Just tell them what's real.

**2. Respect their ability to handle it**

Your reps have been through tough stuff before. They've lost jobs. They've been passed over for promotions. They've had bad quarters. They can handle reality. What they can't handle is being lied to.

**3. Be the one person who's real**

Most managers try to be politicians. They say what they think people want to hear. You can be different. You can be the one person who tells the truth. And that's where respect comes from.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Getting it right]**

Let me tell you how I handled a similar situation later.

We had another comp plan change. This one was also worse for some reps. But this time, I didn't spin.

I went to my team and I said:

"The company is changing the comp plan. I'm going to be straight with you: For some of you, this is going to be worse. For others, it'll be better. Here's the new structure. Let's do the math together. I'll show you where you make more and where you make less. And then we'll talk about how to maximize it."

**[PAUSE]**

I opened a spreadsheet. We ran scenarios. I showed them: "If you hit 100% of quota, here's what you make under the old plan. Here's what you make under the new plan. Here's the delta. Here's how to close that gap."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The contrast]**

Notice what I didn't do: I didn't hype it. I didn't say it was good when it wasn't. I didn't try to motivate them with fake positivity.

I just told them the truth and gave them tools.

And you know what happened? They respected me for it.

One rep said, "I appreciate you being honest. Most managers would have tried to sell us on this. You just told us what it is and helped us figure it out."

**[PAUSE]**

That's the difference between treating people like children and treating them like adults.

**[TEACHING POINT — The phrases that underestimate adulthood]**

Here are some phrases I hear new managers use all the time that signal they're underestimating adulthood:

**"This is actually a great opportunity!"** (when it's not)

**"You're doing great! Just keep pushing!"** (when the data shows they're struggling)

**"The company really cares about you!"** (generic, no substance)

**"Everything's going to be fine!"** (when you don't actually know that)

**"We're making some changes to territory."** (surprise announcement with no context)

**[PAUSE]**

These phrases feel safe. They feel nice. But they erode trust.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The better way]**

Here's how to reframe those same situations with honesty:

**Instead of:** "This is actually a great opportunity!"  
**Say:** "This situation is tough. Here's what's real. Here's what we can control. Here's what we're going to do."

**Instead of:** "You're doing great! Just keep pushing!"  
**Say:** "Your numbers are at 70% of quota. That's below where we need you. Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what we need to fix."

**Instead of:** "The company really cares about you!"  
**Say:** "Here's what I'm doing to advocate for you. Here's what I can control. Here's what I can't."

**Instead of:** "Everything's going to be fine!"  
**Say:** "I don't know if this will work out, but here's what we're going to try. Here's what success looks like."

**Instead of:** "We're making some changes to territory."  
**Say:** "Here's what's changing, here's why, here's how it impacts you. I know this sucks. Here's what I can do to help."

**[PAUSE — Let those contrasts land]**

See the difference? Same situations. But one version treats people like children who need to be protected from reality. The other version treats them like adults who can handle truth and want to know where they stand.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Why honesty builds trust]**

Here's the brain science behind this:

When you're honest, your brain and your rep's brain sync up. There's neural coherence. They feel like you're on the same team looking at the same reality.

But when you spin, when you soften, when you say one thing but your body language says another, their brain picks up on the incongruence. Mirror neurons detect the mismatch. And their amygdala—the threat-detection center—lights up. They start to distrust you.

**Adults are hardwired to detect dishonesty.** You can't fake it. You can't spin your way into trust.

**[TEACHING POINT — Respect > Likability]**

Here's the mindset shift I need you to make: **Your job is not to be liked. Your job is to be respected.**

And respect comes from being real.

When you're honest about what's hard, when you don't sugarcoat, when you tell people where they stand—even when it's uncomfortable—you earn respect.

When you try to be liked by softening everything, by spinning, by avoiding hard truths, you might get temporary comfort. But you lose long-term respect.

**[PERSONAL STORY — The cost of being liked]**

I spent my first year as a manager trying to be liked. I wanted my team to think I was a good guy. I wanted them to feel good after our 1:1s.

So I'd avoid critical feedback. I'd hype situations that weren't great. I'd tell people they were doing fine when they weren't.

And you know what happened? They stopped respecting me.

I remember one rep saying to a colleague—and it got back to me—"Andrew's nice, but I don't trust his feedback. He tells everyone they're doing great. I don't actually know where I stand."

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional anchor]**

That hurt. Because I thought I was being kind. What I was actually doing was being useless.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The shift]**

After that, I made a decision: I'm going to be the one person who tells the truth. Even when it's uncomfortable. Even when it creates tension. Because that's what people actually need.

And here's what I found: **People didn't like me more. But they respected me more. And respect is what actually matters.**

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Being real in action]**

Let me give you an example of what this looks like in practice.

I had a rep—let's call him Mark—who kept complaining about lead quality. Every 1:1, same story: "The leads are garbage. I can't close garbage leads. This isn't my fault."

Old Andrew would have said, "I hear you. I'll talk to marketing. Keep doing your best."

New Andrew said: "Mark, let's look at the data. Your close rate is 10%. The team average is 20%. If the leads were garbage, everyone's close rate would be low. But it's just yours. So the problem isn't the leads. The problem is your qualification or your close process. Let's figure out which one and fix it."

**[PAUSE]**

That wasn't comfortable. Mark got defensive. He didn't like hearing that.

But two weeks later, he came back and said, "You were right. I was qualifying too loosely. I fixed it. My close rate is up to 18%."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Truth creates respect]**

He didn't like what I said in the moment. But he respected me for saying it. Because I didn't let him off the hook with a comfortable lie. I told him the truth and helped him fix it.

**That's what adults actually want.** They don't want to be coddled. They want to be told the truth so they can do something about it.

**[TEACHING POINT — When to be direct, when to be gentle]**

Now, here's the nuance: Being real doesn't mean being brutal. It doesn't mean being harsh. It doesn't mean you ignore emotions.

You can be direct AND kind. You can tell the truth AND be empathetic.

**The framework:**

**1. Start with the data** (observable facts, no interpretation)  
"Your close rate is 10%. The team average is 20%."

**2. Tell the story** (what does the data mean?)  
"This tells me you're struggling with qualification or close process."

**3. Validate their experience** (acknowledge the emotion)  
"I know it feels like the leads are bad. That's frustrating."

**4. Redirect to what's controllable** (move to action)  
"But we can't control lead quality. We can control your qualification. Let's work on that."

**[PAUSE]**

That's how you be real AND respectful. You don't ignore their feelings. You just don't let feelings replace facts.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you respect adulthood and tell the truth:

- **People trust you.** They know you're not spinning. They know you're giving them reality.
- **They perform better.** Because they know where they stand and what needs to change.
- **They stay longer.** People leave managers who aren't real. They stay with managers they respect.
- **You build credibility.** With your team and with leadership. Everyone sees you as someone who tells the truth.

Here's what happens when you underestimate adulthood and spin:

- **People lose respect for you.** They know you're not being real.
- **They stop trusting your feedback.** If you spin the bad stuff, they don't believe the good stuff either.
- **They disengage.** Why work hard if the manager's just going to sugarcoat everything?
- **You lose credibility.** Your team sees you as weak. Leadership sees you as someone who can't handle hard conversations.

**[CLOSE — Direct, motivating]**

Here's the bottom line: Your reps are adults. Treat them like it.

Tell them the truth. Don't soften it. Don't spin it. Don't protect them from reality.

Respect their intelligence. Respect their experience. Respect their ability to handle hard things.

Be the one person who's real. That's where trust comes from. That's where respect comes from.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. Think about the last time you had to deliver bad news. Did you soften it? Did you spin it? Or did you tell the truth?

2. Is there a situation right now where you're avoiding being direct because you want to be liked?

3. What would it sound like to tell the truth in that situation—honestly, but respectfully?

Write out the words. Practice saying them out loud. Get comfortable with being real.

**[END SUB-MODULE 2]**

## SUB-MODULE 3: THE DATA-FIRST FRAMEWORK
**Runtime:** ~15 minutes | ~3,000 words  
**Learning Objective:** Teach the step-by-step structure for difficult conversations; show how data prevents emotion from derailing things; give them a framework to lean on

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, practical]**

Okay. So you understand that conversations are cumulative. You understand that you need to treat people like adults. You're ready to be real.

But here's the question you're probably asking: **"Okay, but HOW? What do I actually SAY in these difficult conversations?"**

**[PAUSE]**

That's what this module is about. I'm going to give you a framework—a step-by-step structure you can use for any difficult conversation.

This framework has saved me more times than I can count. It's kept conversations from spiraling into emotional chaos. It's helped me deliver hard news without destroying relationships. It's helped me hold people accountable without being an asshole.

It's called the **Data-First Framework.**

**[FRAMEWORK INTRODUCTION — Clear, simple]**

Here's the structure:

**1. DATA** — Start with observable facts  
**2. STORY** — Explain what the data means  
**3. AGREEMENT** — Get on the same page about truth  
**4. OUTCOME** — State what we're trying to achieve  
**5. EXPECTATIONS** — Set clear next steps  
**6. EMOTIONS** — Acknowledge and manage feelings

**[PAUSE — Let that structure land]**

Data. Story. Agreement. Outcome. Expectations. Emotions.

That's your roadmap. Every difficult conversation you have, you follow this structure. And if you do, the conversation will be hard—but it won't be chaotic.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why this order matters]**

Now, here's why the order matters:

If you start with emotions—"I know you're frustrated, let's talk about your performance"—the conversation gets emotional before you've even established facts. And once emotions take over, it's really hard to get back to reality.

If you start with outcome—"We need you to hit quota"—without establishing the data first, the person feels attacked. They get defensive. They push back.

But if you start with data—objective, observable facts—you anchor the conversation in reality. And once you've agreed on what's true, emotions become something you can manage instead of something that derails you.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

Let me tell you about a time I did this wrong and then learned to do it right.

I had a rep—let's call her Lisa—who was underperforming. She was at 60% of quota. She'd missed her number for two straight quarters.

The first time I tried to address it, I started with outcome: "Lisa, we need to talk about your performance. You need to get back to quota."

She immediately got defensive. "I'm working as hard as I can. The leads are bad. The territory is tough. I don't know what else you want from me."

And we spent 30 minutes arguing about leads and territory and effort. We didn't solve anything. She left frustrated. I left frustrated.

**[PAUSE — This is the mistake]**

I started in the wrong place. I jumped to outcome before we agreed on the data. So she had no context. She just felt attacked.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Getting it right]**

A month later, I tried again. This time, I used the Data-First Framework.

**DATA:** "Lisa, I want to talk about your performance. You're at 60% of quota. You've been below target for two quarters. Your close rate is 12%, and the team average is 22%. Your pipeline coverage is 2x, and we need 3x to consistently hit quota."

**[PAUSE]**

Just the facts. No interpretation yet. No emotion. Just data.

She didn't argue with the data. She couldn't. It was true.

**STORY:** "Here's what the data is telling me: You're getting enough at-bats, but you're not closing at the rate you need to. And your pipeline isn't deep enough to compensate. So we have a close rate problem and a pipeline problem."

**[PAUSE]**

Now I'm interpreting the data. I'm telling her what it means. But it's still grounded in facts, not feelings.

**AGREEMENT:** "Do you see it that way? Is there something in the data I'm missing? Is there context that would change this picture?"

**[PAUSE]**

This is critical. I'm giving her space to push back, to add context, to make sure we're looking at the same reality.

She thought for a second and said, "No. You're right. I know I'm struggling with close rate. I think I'm qualifying too loosely."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — What just happened]**

Notice what just happened: **We agreed on the truth.** 

She wasn't defensive anymore because I didn't attack her. I just showed her the data and asked if we were seeing the same thing. And we were.

Once you have agreement on truth, the rest of the conversation is about solving the problem, not debating whether there is one.

**[FRAMEWORK CONTINUED — Outcome]**

**OUTCOME:** "Okay. So here's what we're chasing: We need to get you to quota. That means we need your close rate up to at least 20%, and we need your pipeline coverage at 3x. That's the goal."

**[PAUSE]**

Now I'm stating what success looks like. Clear. Measurable. Not vague.

**[FRAMEWORK CONTINUED — Expectations]**

**EXPECTATIONS:** "Here's how we're going to get there. For the next 60 days, we're going to focus on two things: qualification and pipeline generation. I'm going to coach you on discovery every week. We're going to role-play your qualification questions. And you're going to commit to 20 prospecting calls a week to build pipeline. At the end of 60 days, we'll look at the data again. If we're making progress, great. If not, we'll need to talk about whether this role is the right fit."

**[PAUSE]**

Clear expectations. Clear timeline. Clear consequences if it doesn't improve.

**[FRAMEWORK CONTINUED — Emotions]**

**EMOTIONS:** "I know this is hard to hear. I know you're frustrated. And the data is the data. I'm here to help you. But you have to own the work. I can coach you, but I can't do it for you. How are you feeling about this?"

**[PAUSE]**

Now we're acknowledging emotions. But emotions are the last step, not the first. Because we've already agreed on the truth. So emotions are something we're managing, not something that's controlling the conversation.

Lisa said, "I'm frustrated with myself. But I appreciate you being clear. I know what I need to do now."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The contrast]**

Compare that to the first conversation, where I started with outcome and we spent 30 minutes arguing.

In the second conversation, we spent 15 minutes. We agreed on the problem. We made a plan. We moved forward.

**That's the power of the Data-First Framework.**

**[TEACHING POINT — Let's break down each step]**

Now let's go deeper into each step so you know exactly how to apply it.

### **STEP 1: DATA**

**What it is:** Observable facts. Numbers. Metrics. Things you can point to that are objectively true.

**What it sounds like:**
- "Your close rate is 15%. The expectation is 25%."
- "You've missed quota for three consecutive months."
- "Your forecast accuracy has been 60% for the last two quarters. We need 85%."
- "You have four deals that have been in 'verbal commit' stage for more than 30 days."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't interpret yet: "You're struggling" is interpretation. "Your close rate is 15%" is data.
- Don't generalize: "You're not performing well" is vague. Give specific numbers.
- Don't bring emotions into it: "I'm worried about you" is emotion. Save that for later.

**[PAUSE]**

Start with data. Always. It's your anchor.

### **STEP 2: STORY**

**What it is:** Your interpretation of what the data means. The pattern you're seeing. The diagnosis.

**What it sounds like:**
- "The data shows you're getting enough meetings, but you're not qualifying them well. You're spending time on deals that were never going to close."
- "The pattern I'm seeing is: you get busy, you stop prospecting, and three months later your pipeline is dry."
- "This tells me there's a discovery problem. You're not uncovering pain clearly enough to get to a commit."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't make it personal: "You're not trying hard enough" is an attack. "The data shows a qualification issue" is a diagnosis.
- Don't make assumptions: "You don't care about this job" is an assumption. Stick to what the data shows.

**[PAUSE]**

The story is your hypothesis about what's happening. It's not a judgment. It's a diagnosis.

### **STEP 3: AGREEMENT**

**What it is:** Checking to make sure you're both looking at the same reality. Giving them space to add context or push back.

**What it sounds like:**
- "Do you see it that way?"
- "Is there context I'm missing?"
- "Am I reading this right, or is there something else going on?"
- "Do you agree that this is the problem, or do you see it differently?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't skip this step. If you don't get agreement on the truth, the rest of the conversation won't work.
- Don't fake the question: If they push back, actually listen. Maybe there IS context you're missing.

**[PAUSE]**

Agreement is the hinge. Once you have it, the conversation moves forward. If you don't have it, you need to stay here until you do.

### **STEP 4: OUTCOME**

**What it is:** Stating clearly what success looks like. What you're trying to achieve together.

**What it sounds like:**
- "We need to get your close rate to 25%."
- "We need you back at quota."
- "We need your forecast accuracy at 85%."
- "We need you to maximize this new comp plan."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't be vague: "We need you to do better" doesn't mean anything. Be specific.
- Don't sugarcoat: Don't say "I'd love to see improvement" when what you mean is "You need to hit quota or we'll have a different conversation."

**[PAUSE]**

Clear outcome. Measurable. No ambiguity.

### **STEP 5: EXPECTATIONS**

**What it is:** The plan. The roadmap. What happens next. What they need to do. What you'll do to help. What the timeline is. What happens if it doesn't improve.

**What it sounds like:**
- "Here's the 60-day plan. Week 1-2: We're going to role-play discovery calls. Week 3-4: You're going to apply the new framework to live calls and we'll review. Week 5-6: We'll check the data and see if your close rate is improving."
- "I'm going to coach you every week. You're going to commit to 20 prospecting calls per week. At the end of 60 days, if your pipeline coverage isn't at 3x, we'll move to a formal performance plan."
- "Here's what I need from you: [specific actions]. Here's what I'll do: [specific support]. Here's the timeline: [specific dates]. Here's what happens if we don't see improvement: [clear consequence]."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't leave it vague: "Let's see how it goes" is not a plan.
- Don't skip the consequence: They need to know what happens if it doesn't improve.
- Don't overpromise: Only commit to things you can actually do.

**[PAUSE]**

Expectations are the action plan. Clear. Specific. With milestones and consequences.

### **STEP 6: EMOTIONS**

**What it is:** Acknowledging how they're feeling. Validating that this is hard. But not letting emotions derail the plan.

**What it sounds like:**
- "I know this is hard to hear."
- "I know you're frustrated. The data is what it is. Let's focus on what we can control."
- "I get that you're upset. And we still need to do the work."
- "This is tough. I'm here to help you. But you have to own the execution."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't let emotions override the data: "I'm working so hard!" doesn't change the close rate numbers.
- Don't rescue them from discomfort: It's okay for them to feel bad. That's part of accountability.
- Don't make it about your emotions: "I'm so worried about you" centers you, not them.

**[PAUSE]**

Emotions are real. Acknowledge them. But they're the last step, not the first. And they don't change the plan.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why this framework works]**

Here's why this framework is so powerful:

**1. It prevents defensiveness.** When you start with data instead of judgment, people can't argue with it. It's just facts.

**2. It creates agreement.** Once you both see the same reality, you're on the same team solving a problem, not opponents debating.

**3. It keeps emotions manageable.** By the time you get to emotions, you've already established truth and a plan. So emotions don't derail things.

**4. It gives you a roadmap.** You're not winging it. You're following a structure. That keeps you grounded even when the conversation gets tense.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Using it for different scenarios]**

Let me show you how this framework applies to different types of difficult conversations.

**Example 1: Comp Plan Change**

**DATA:** "Here's the old comp plan. Here's the new comp plan. Here's how your commission changes on deals under $50K and over $50K."

**STORY:** "What this means: You'll make less on small deals, more on big deals. If you keep your current deal mix, you'll make about 10% less. If you shift to bigger deals, you could make 15% more."

**AGREEMENT:** "Does that math track with what you're seeing?"

**OUTCOME:** "The goal is to help you maximize this new plan so you make as much or more than you did before."

**EXPECTATIONS:** "Here's how we're going to do that: We're going to focus on larger opportunities. I'm going to help you get introduced to bigger accounts. You're going to shift your prospecting to focus on deals over $50K. We'll check progress monthly."

**EMOTIONS:** "I know this feels like a pay cut. I get it. Let's focus on what we can control—deal size and strategy."

**[PAUSE]**

Same framework. Different content.

**Example 2: Firing Someone**

**DATA:** "We've had three conversations about your performance over the last four months. You've been below quota every month. We put you on a 60-day improvement plan. At the end of that plan, your close rate was 13%, below the 20% target."

**STORY:** "What this tells me: The role isn't working out. We've tried coaching, we've tried a structured plan, and the numbers haven't improved."

**AGREEMENT:** "Do you disagree with that assessment?"

**OUTCOME:** "We're going to move in a different direction."

**EXPECTATIONS:** "Your last day will be [date]. HR will handle the logistics. You can take your personal items from your desk today."

**EMOTIONS:** "I know this is hard. I'm sorry it didn't work out. HR will explain benefits and next steps."

**[PAUSE]**

Direct. Clear. Kind. But not drawn out.

**[BUSINESS IMPACT — Make it tangible]**

Here's what happens when you use this framework:

- **Conversations stay grounded.** You don't spiral into emotion or blame.
- **People respect you.** They see that you're being fair and data-driven.
- **You feel more confident.** You're not winging it. You have a structure.
- **Outcomes are clearer.** People know what's expected and what happens next.

Here's what happens when you don't use a framework:

- **Conversations get messy.** Emotions take over. People get defensive.
- **Nothing gets resolved.** You talk in circles and nothing changes.
- **You lose credibility.** People think you're unprepared or unfair.
- **Relationships get damaged.** Because the conversation felt chaotic or personal.

**[CLOSE — Practical, empowering]**

Here's the bottom line: Difficult conversations don't have to be chaotic. They don't have to be relationships-ending. They don't have to feel like you're winging it.

You have a framework now. **Data. Story. Agreement. Outcome. Expectations. Emotions.**

Use it every time. Practice it. Get comfortable with it.

And when you walk into that hard conversation, you'll know exactly what to say and exactly how to structure it.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Write this down:

1. Think about a difficult conversation you need to have. Write out each step:
   - DATA: What are the facts?
   - STORY: What do the facts mean?
   - AGREEMENT: What question will you ask to get alignment?
   - OUTCOME: What does success look like?
   - EXPECTATIONS: What's the plan?
   - EMOTIONS: How will you acknowledge feelings?

2. Practice saying it out loud. Word for word. Get comfortable with the structure.

Don't wait. Do this before your next difficult conversation.

### **CONVERSATION 1: THE UNDERPERFORMER (BEFORE THE PLAN)**

**[TEACHING POINT — What this is]**

**The mistake:** Waiting. Hoping they'll figure it out. Avoiding the conversation because you don't want to make it "official" yet.

**The cost:** By the time you formalize a plan, they're blindsided. They had no idea you were worried. Trust is broken.

**The right move:** Have the conversation early. Before it's formal. Before it's a crisis.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

I had a rep—let's call him Jake—who started missing quota. Month one: 80%. Month two: 75%. Month three: 70%.

I noticed. But I didn't say anything because I thought, "Maybe he's just having a rough patch. I don't want to put pressure on him."

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional anchor]**

**[FRAMEWORK APPLICATION — Step by step]**

Here's how I learned to do it differently.

**SETUP:** Schedule a 1:1. Make it clear this is an important conversation. Don't ambush them.

**DATA:**  
"Jake, I want to talk about your performance. You've been below quota for three months. Month one: 80%. Month two: 75%. Month three: 70%. Your close rate is 12%, and the team average is 22%. Your pipeline coverage is 2x, and we need 3x to hit quota consistently."

**[PAUSE]**

Just the facts. No judgment. No emotion.

**STORY:**  
"Here's what the data is telling me: You're getting at-bats—you're having enough conversations. But you're not closing at the rate you need to. And your pipeline isn't deep enough to make up for it. So we have a close rate problem and a pipeline problem."

**[PAUSE]**

Now I'm diagnosing what the data means. Still factual. Still grounded.

**AGREEMENT:**  
"Do you see it that way? Is there something I'm missing? Is there context that would change this picture?"

**[PAUSE]**

Give them space to respond. Maybe they have context. Maybe they agree. Either way, you need alignment before moving forward.

**OUTCOME:**  
"Here's what we're chasing: We need to get you back to quota. That means close rate up to at least 20%, pipeline coverage at 3x. That's where we need you."

**[PAUSE]**

Clear target. Measurable.

**EXPECTATIONS:**  
"Here's how we're going to get there. For the next 60 days, we're going to focus on qualification and pipeline. I'm going to coach you on discovery every week. We'll role-play your qualification framework. You're going to commit to 20 prospecting touches per week to build pipeline. We'll check the metrics every two weeks. If we see improvement, great. If not, we'll move to a formal plan. I want to be clear: this is serious. But we're not there yet. I'm invested in helping you turn this around."

**[PAUSE]**

Clear plan. Clear support. Clear consequence.

**[PAUSE]**

Acknowledge the discomfort. But don't rescue them from it.

2. **Clear expectations.** They know exactly what needs to change.

4. **Respect.** You're treating them like an adult who can handle truth.

### **CONVERSATION 2: FIRING SOMEONE**

**[TEACHING POINT — What this is]**

This is the hardest one. You're letting someone go. You're ending their paycheck. It's emotional.

**The mistake:** Agonizing over it. Trying to make it "nicer" by drawing it out. Explaining too much. Apologizing for things that aren't your fault.

**The cost:** You make it worse. The person leaves confused or hurt in a different way. The conversation drags and becomes more painful.

**The right move:** Be direct. Be brief. Be kind. Hand off to HR.

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I got it wrong]**

The first time I fired someone, I tried to soften it. I spent 20 minutes explaining all the reasons. I said things like, "This was really hard for me." I apologized for things that weren't my fault.

And the person left angry and confused. They said later, "I wish he'd just told me straight instead of dragging it out."

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The insight]**

Here's what I learned: **When you're firing someone, they want clarity and speed. Not softness. Not explanation. Just clarity.**

You're not doing them a favor by making it longer or nicer. You're just making it harder.

**[FRAMEWORK APPLICATION — Step by step]**

Here's the structure for a firing conversation:

**SETUP:** Schedule a meeting. Have HR on standby (either in the room or immediately after). Know what you're going to say beforehand. Don't wing this.

**DATA:**  
"We've had three conversations over the last four months about your performance. We put you on a 60-day improvement plan with clear metrics. At the end of that plan, you were at 65% of quota with a close rate of 13%, below the 20% target we set."

**[PAUSE]**

You're anchoring this in history. This isn't a surprise.

**STORY:**  
"What this tells me: The role isn't working out. We've tried coaching, we've tried a structured plan, and the numbers haven't improved to where we need them."

**[PAUSE]**

You're not blaming them. You're not saying they're a bad person. You're saying the fit isn't right.

**AGREEMENT:**  
"I don't think you'll disagree with that assessment." (or "Do you see it differently?")

**[PAUSE]**

You're giving them a moment to respond, but you're not debating.

**OUTCOME:**  
"We're going to move in a different direction. Your last day is [date]."

**[PAUSE]**

Direct. Clear. No ambiguity.

**EXPECTATIONS:**  
"HR is going to meet with you after this to go over benefits, final paycheck, and next steps. You can take your personal items from your desk today. If there's anything you need from me logistically, let me know, and I'll make sure it gets handled."

**[PAUSE]**

You're handing off to HR. Your part of the conversation is done in 2 minutes.

**EMOTIONS:**  
"I know this is hard. I'm sorry it didn't work out."

**[PAUSE]**

Short. Kind. But not drawn out.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — What NOT to say]**

Here's what you DON'T say:

- "This was really hard for me." (Centers you, not them.)
- "I fought for you but leadership overruled me." (Undermines the decision and makes you look weak.)
- "You're a great person, this just isn't the right fit." (Feels patronizing when someone just lost their job.)
- "I'm sure you'll land on your feet." (Minimizes their reality.)
- Long explanations about why this happened. (They don't need a dissertation. They need clarity.)

**[TEACHING POINT — Why short and direct is kinder]**

I know it feels harsh to keep it to 2 minutes. But here's the truth: **When you're firing someone, they're not listening after the first sentence anyway.** 

As soon as you say "Your last day is [date]," their brain goes into fight-or-flight. They're not processing your reasons. They're not hearing your apologies. They're just trying to survive the moment.

So keep it short. Let HR handle the logistics. Let them process the emotion later.

**That's kinder than dragging it out.**

### **CONVERSATION 3: COMP PLAN CHANGE**

**[TEACHING POINT — What this is]**

Leadership changes the comp plan. Maybe it's better for some, worse for others. Maybe it's just different. Your team is anxious. Some are pissed. You have to explain it.

**The cost:** Your team knows you're full of shit. They lose trust. They revolt or disengage.

**The right move:** Do the math. Show them the numbers. Give them tools to maximize it. Be honest about trade-offs.

**[PERSONAL STORY — When I spun it]**

**[FRAMEWORK APPLICATION — Step by step]**

**SETUP:** Schedule a team meeting and individual 1:1s. Do the math BEFORE you talk to them. Know the numbers.

**DATA:**

**[PAUSE]**

Just the facts. No spin.

**STORY:**  
"Here's what this means: If you keep your current deal mix, you'll make less. If you shift to bigger deals and hit accelerators, you'll make more. It depends on your execution."

**[PAUSE]**

Honest interpretation. No hype.

**AGREEMENT:**  
"I've done the math for each of you individually. Let's go through your numbers together and make sure we're on the same page about how this impacts you."

**[PAUSE]**

You're inviting them to verify. This builds trust.

**OUTCOME:**  
"The goal is to help you maximize this new plan so you make as much or more than you did before."

**[PAUSE]**

Clear outcome. You're on their side.

**EXPECTATIONS:**  
"Here's how we're going to do that. One: We're going to shift prospecting to focus on larger accounts. I'll help you get introductions. Two: We're going to adjust your deal qualification so you're not wasting time on small deals. Three: We're going to track your progress monthly and adjust as needed. Here's a spreadsheet with scenarios—play with it and see what you need to do to hit your income target."

**[PAUSE]**

You're giving them tools. Not just telling them to "work harder."

**EMOTIONS:**

**[PAUSE]**

Honest about your advocacy. Honest about the reality. Focused on action.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — Why this works]**

When you do the math and show them how to maximize the plan, you're giving them agency. They're not helpless victims of a bad decision. They're strategic operators who can adapt.

**That's respect.**

## SUB-MODULE 5: NEVER BREAK TRUST
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,800 words  
**Learning Objective:** Help managers understand that trust is fragile once built; show them how small inconsistencies destroy trust; give them a framework for protecting trust

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Direct, high-stakes]**

We've spent four modules talking about how to build trust through cumulative interactions, how to respect adulthood, how to structure difficult conversations, how to handle the hard moments.

But here's the thing: **All of that work can be destroyed in a week. In a day. In one moment.**

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Because trust is slow to build and fast to lose.

You can do everything right for six months—consistent 1:1s, honest feedback, clear expectations, transparent communication. And then you make one mistake. You break one promise. You let one standard slip.

And people notice. And they start to question everything.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — This is the core insight]**

So this module is about how to protect the trust you've built. How to stay consistent even when it's hard. How to avoid the small mistakes that erode credibility.

Because if you don't protect trust, all the work you've done to build it doesn't matter.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, specific]**

Let me tell you about a time I broke trust.

I was managing a team. I'd built strong relationships with my reps. We had regular 1:1s. I gave honest feedback. I followed through on commitments. They trusted me.

But then I got busy. We were in the middle of a big quarter. I had a lot of pressure from leadership. And I started letting things slip.

I canceled a 1:1 with one of my reps. Then another. I said I'd follow up on something a rep asked about—and I forgot. I said I'd advocate for a territory change—and I didn't bring it up in the leadership meeting because I didn't want to create friction.

**[PAUSE]**

Small things. Nothing catastrophic. But they added up.

And one day, one of my top reps came to my office and said, "Andrew, I don't trust you anymore."

**[PAUSE — This is the emotional anchor]**

That hit me like a punch. I said, "What? Why?"

She said, "You used to show up for me. You used to follow through. But for the last month, you've been flaky. You canceled our 1:1 twice. You said you'd push for my territory change and I don't think you did. I don't feel like you have my back anymore."

**[PAUSE]**

She was right. I'd been letting standards slip. I thought I had a good reason—I was busy. But from her perspective, I'd stopped being consistent. And consistency is what trust is built on.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — The lesson]**

Here's what I learned: **Trust isn't a one-time thing. It's not something you build once and then you have it forever. Trust is maintained through consistent behavior. And the moment you stop being consistent, trust starts to erode.**

**[TEACHING POINT — What breaks trust]**

So what breaks trust? What are the things that new managers do—often without realizing it—that destroy the credibility they've built?

Here's the list:

**1. Canceling 1:1s repeatedly**

When you cancel a 1:1, you're sending a message: "You're not a priority." Do it once, okay. Do it repeatedly, and people stop trusting that you're paying attention to them.

**2. Not following through on what you say**

If you say you're going to do something—follow up on a lead, advocate for a resource, check on a deal—and you don't do it, people stop believing you. Every unkept promise is a withdrawal from the trust account.

**3. Being real one time, politician the next**

If you're honest in one conversation and then spin in the next, people don't know which version of you is real. Inconsistency breeds distrust.

**4. Not addressing concerns when raised**

If a rep brings up a problem and you say "I'll look into it" and then never mention it again, they feel dismissed. Even if you can't fix the problem, you need to close the loop.

**5. Showing favoritism**

If you're more available to some reps than others, if you give some people more grace than others, people notice. And they stop trusting that you're fair.

**6. Making decisions without explanation**

If you make a call—change a territory, shift a quota, reassign a deal—without explaining why, people fill in the blanks. And they usually fill them in negatively.

**7. Being inconsistent with different people**

If you hold one person accountable for missing forecast but let another person slide, people see the double standard. And they lose respect.

**[PAUSE — Let those sink in]**

Look at that list. How many of those have you done? Be honest.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — The cost]**

After my rep told me she didn't trust me anymore, I had to do some hard reflection. And I realized: I'd done almost all of those things in the last month.

I'd canceled 1:1s. I'd said I'd follow up on things and didn't. I'd been inconsistent. And I'd lost her trust in a matter of weeks—trust that had taken months to build.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — How to rebuild trust]**

I went back to her and I said:

"You're right. I've been flaky. I've been letting things slip because I've been under pressure, but that's not an excuse. Here's what I'm committing to: I'm not canceling another 1:1 with you unless it's an emergency, and if I do, we'll reschedule immediately. I'm going to follow through on what I said I'd do. And if I can't do something, I'll tell you instead of letting it drop. I need to earn your trust back. Will you give me a chance?"

**[PAUSE]**

She said yes. And over the next two months, I kept that commitment. I showed up. I followed through. I stayed consistent.

And slowly, the trust came back.

**But here's the thing: It took two months to rebuild what I broke in two weeks.**

**[TEACHING POINT — Trust is slow to build, fast to lose, slow to rebuild]**

That's the harsh reality. Trust is:

- **Slow to build** — It takes months of consistent behavior.
- **Fast to lose** — One or two big mistakes, or a pattern of small ones, and it's gone.
- **Slow to rebuild** — If you break it, you're starting over. And people are watching more carefully the second time.

**[TEACHING MOMENT — What builds and protects trust]**

So how do you protect the trust you've built? How do you stay consistent even when it's hard?

Here are the behaviors that build and maintain trust:

**1. Always prepare for 1:1s**

Even when you're busy. Even when you don't feel like it. Show up prepared. Have an agenda. Show them they matter.

**2. Follow through on commitments**

If you say you'll do something, do it. If you can't, go back and say, "I said I'd do this, but I can't. Here's why. Here's what I can do instead."

**3. Be consistent in how you show up**

Same person to everyone. Same standards. Same level of honesty. Same level of follow-through.

**4. Address concerns when raised**

Even if you can't solve the problem, close the loop. "You asked about this. Here's what I found out. Here's what I can and can't do."

**5. Explain your decisions**

Don't just make calls and expect people to accept them. Tell them why. Give them context. Help them understand.

**6. Treat everyone the same**

Hold everyone to the same standards. Be available to everyone. Don't play favorites.

**7. Stay committed to the framework even when it's inconvenient**

When you're under pressure, when you're busy, when it's hard—that's when consistency matters most. That's when people are watching.

**[PERSONAL EXAMPLE — Staying consistent under pressure]**

After I rebuilt trust with my team, I hit another high-pressure quarter. Same situation—leadership was on me, deals were shaky, I was stressed.

But this time, I didn't let standards slip. I kept my 1:1s. I followed through on commitments. I stayed real with my team even when I didn't have good news.

And you know what happened? My team trusted me more. Because they saw that I stayed consistent even when it was hard. And that's when they knew they could rely on me.

**[NEUROSCIENCE TIE-IN — Why consistency matters]**

Here's the brain science behind why consistency builds trust:

Your brain is constantly scanning for patterns. It's asking: "Is this person predictable? Can I rely on them?"

When someone is consistent—when they show up the same way every time, when they follow through, when they keep their word—your brain relaxes. You feel safe. You trust them.

But when someone is inconsistent—when they're reliable one day and flaky the next—your brain goes into threat mode. You can't predict them. You stop trusting them.

**Consistency is the foundation of trust.**

**[TEACHING POINT — The hard truth]**

Here's the hard truth: **Being consistent is exhausting. It's easier to let things slide when you're busy. It's easier to cancel a meeting when something urgent comes up. It's easier to not follow through when you're overwhelmed.**

But easy doesn't build trust. Easy doesn't earn respect. Easy doesn't make you a good manager.

**Consistency when it's hard—that's what separates good managers from mediocre ones.**

I remember one week where I had three big deals closing, a board meeting to prepare for, and a personal emergency at home. I was stretched thin.

I had a 1:1 scheduled with one of my reps. And I thought about canceling it. I thought, "He'll understand. I'm busy. I'll reschedule."

**[PAUSE]**

That's when I knew: The moments when it's hardest to stay consistent are the moments that build the most trust.

**[TEACHING POINT — The both/and]**

Here's the both/and:

- You can be stressed AND show up for your team.

It's hard. But it's doable. And it's what the job requires.

- **You damage your reputation.** People know you as flaky. That limits your career.

Don't be most managers.
