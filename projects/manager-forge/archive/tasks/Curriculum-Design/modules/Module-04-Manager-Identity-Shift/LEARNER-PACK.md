### SUB-MODULE 3: THE GRIEF CYCLE

#### Worksheet 3.1: Where Am I in the Cycle?
**Purpose:** Self-identify your current grief stage so you can stop blaming yourself for your emotions and understand what's normal.

**Instructions:** Read the descriptions of each stage. Then check the box that describes where you are RIGHT NOW. (You can only pick one. If you're between stages, pick the one that resonates most.)

☐ **Denial (Weeks 1–4):** I'm thinking I can do both—manage and close deals. Everything feels manageable. I'm telling myself this is temporary.

☐ **Anger (Weeks 4–12):** I'm frustrated. My team isn't as good as me. Why won't they listen? Why did I say yes to this? I'm irritated more often than not.

☐ **Bargaining (Weeks 12–20):** I'm looking for middle-ground solutions. Maybe a hybrid role? Maybe I freelance? Maybe I keep my top accounts? I'm trying to find a way to have both.

☐ **Depression (Weeks 20–24):** I'm questioning whether I'm good at this. I miss closing deals. I feel like I've lost who I am. I'm quiet. Introspective. Sometimes unmotivated.

☐ **Acceptance (Week 24+):** I'm building something new. I'm not grieving anymore. I can see wins in my team's growth. I'm starting to like being a manager. The old identity feels like the past now.

**Honesty Question:** What stage would your team say you're in? (Sometimes we think we're further along than we actually are.)

**What Comes Next:** Based on your current stage, what's the most important thing you need to do?
- If Denial: Stop pretending. Name that you're grieving.
- If Anger: Let yourself feel it. Don't blame your team. Find a peer to talk to.
- If Bargaining: Stop looking for a middle ground. Accept the choice.
- If Depression: Sit with it. It will pass. You're integrating the loss.
- If Acceptance: Keep building. You're on the right track.

## SECTION C: ROLE-PLAY SCENARIOS (5 detailed scenarios)

### Scenario 1: The Rep Wants Your Advice on a Deal
**Context:** One of your reps, Michael, comes to you with a deal that's stuck at the 30-yard line. The client (a VP) likes the property but is concerned about the timeline. Wants to know what you'd do.

**Setup:** 
- Deal size: $4M (one of the biggest of the year)
- Risk: If you jump in, Michael feels like you don't trust him. If you don't, he's stuck and might lose the deal.
- Timeline: Client wants an answer by Wednesday (2 days).

**Stakeholder Roles (if running as a group exercise):**
- Michael (rep, feeling insecure)
- The Client (VP, worried about timelines)
- You (manager, deciding whether to jump in or coach)

**What Happens:** Michael comes into your office. "This deal is huge and I'm stuck. The client keeps saying 'I like it but the timing doesn't work for us.' What would you do?"

**Decision Point:** Do you:
a) Jump in and handle the client conversation?
b) Tell Michael what to say and have him execute?
c) Ask Michael what HE thinks is really going on and coach him through it?
d) Tell him to figure it out himself?

**Debrief Questions:**
1. What did you choose and why?
2. If you chose A or B, what happens to Michael's confidence long-term?
3. If you chose C, what questions would you actually ask him?
4. What would Michael learn from each approach?
5. Which outcome is better for your team's sustainability?

**Best Practice Answer:** C. Ask Michael questions:
- "What do you think is really going on here? Is it actually timing, or something else?"
- "What would you try if I wasn't here?"
- "What does the client actually need to move forward?"

Coach him to think. Let him own the solution.

### Scenario 2: Big Opportunity Comes Your Way
**Context:** A luxury development opportunity lands on your desk. $15M project. Multiple units. Huge commission. Your broker mentions it and immediately asks: "Are you going to take this one or delegate it?"

**Setup:**
- Opportunity: $15M project, 8-10 units, high-touch client
- Tension: Your best rep (Sarah) is ready for something this big, but she's never done it
- Risk: If you take it, you're back in the rep game and your team sees you as the closer. If Sarah takes it and struggles, that's on you.
- Timeline: 6-month project.

**What's Really Being Tested:**
- Can you resist the temptation to be the hero?
- Can you trust your team with something that matters?
- Do you believe in your development of Sarah?

**Decision Point:** Do you:
a) Take the deal yourself. You're the best person for it.
b) Take it as a co-listing with Sarah so you can control it.
c) Delegate it entirely to Sarah and coach her through it.
d) Tell the broker to find someone else, it's not your deal to take anymore.

**Debrief Questions:**
1. What did you choose and why?
2. If you chose A, what message does that send your team?
3. If you chose C, what support does Sarah need from you to succeed?
4. What would you be choosing (short-term win vs. long-term team development)?
5. In 2 years, which choice creates a better team?

**Best Practice Answer:** C with heavy coaching. Delegate to Sarah. Give her:
- Clear outcome: Close this project by [date].
- Context: This is a big deal. Your biggest yet. You're ready.
- Constraints: Get client legal review, loop me in before major negotiation points.
- Your support: Weekly check-ins, not daily. You own this.

### Scenario 3: Your Team Does Something Differently Than You'd Do It
**Context:** Your rep, Jennifer, closes a deal using a strategy you would have never used. It worked. The client is happy. The deal is closed.

**Setup:**
- The deal: $5M property
- Jennifer's approach: She partnered the property with a complementary listing (yours, from your rep days, but you kept it for some reason). Created a package. Client loved it. Bigger deal than either property alone.
- Your approach: You would have approached each property separately, maybe suggested the pairing, but not made it the primary strategy.
- The tension: You feel a little weird that you didn't think of it. Also, she used your property without really asking. Also, it worked and you didn't predict it.

**What's Being Tested:**
- Can you celebrate a win that's not your way?
- Can you let your team innovate without controlling their method?
- Can you feel good about their success even if you didn't predict it?

**Decision Point:** Do you:
a) Redirect her: Great job, but next time let me know before you bundle my listings.
b) Celebrate the win but explain why your way would have been better for long-term strategy.
c) Be genuinely excited about her innovation and ask her to teach the team her approach.
d) Leave it alone. She won. That's enough.

**Debrief Questions:**
1. What did you choose and why?
2. If you chose A or B, what message does that send about innovation?
3. If you chose C, what does that build in your team?
4. When's the last time someone did something better than you expected?
5. How did you feel about it?

**Best Practice Answer:** C. Pull Jennifer aside and say:
"That bundling strategy was brilliant. I would have never thought to do it that way. Walk me through your thinking. And then I want you to teach the team this approach in our next huddle. You found something that works."

### Scenario 4: Coaching Moment—Rep Struggling with Prospecting
**Context:** One of your reps, David, has been struggling with prospecting for 3 months. His pipeline is drying up. He's getting frustrated. You see him in the office one morning looking defeated.

**Setup:**
- David's track record: He's a great closer, but not a natural prospector
- The problem: Without prospecting, there's nothing to close
- The temptation: You're an incredible prospector. You could prospect FOR him, get him deals, and he'd close them. Problem solved.
- The reality: If you do that, he never learns to prospect. He becomes dependent on you. Your team doesn't scale.

**What's Being Tested:**
- Can you resist the urge to solve the problem?
- Can you coach someone through struggle instead of taking over?
- Can you be patient while someone learns something that doesn't come naturally?

**The Conversation:** David says, "I'm not getting anywhere with prospecting. I'm not good at this. Can you help me?"

**Decision Point:** Do you:
a) Offer to prospect with him / for him / in his place for a while.
b) Tell him what he's doing wrong and show him the right way.
c) Ask what he's tried, what's worked before, what he thinks is blocking him.
d) Tell him he needs to figure it out himself.

**Debrief Questions:**
1. What did you choose and why?
2. If you chose A, what are you teaching David about capability?
3. If you chose C, what would you actually ask him?
4. What's the difference between helping and enabling?
5. How long are you willing to coach someone through a struggle?

**Best Practice Answer:** C. Ask questions:
- "Walk me through what you're doing to prospect. What are you actually doing?"
- "Of all the activities you do, which ones generate the most interest?"
- "What's one prospect you got interested, and what did you do differently?"
- "What would you try if you knew you couldn't fail?"
- "What support do you need from me to make this work?"

Then coach him based on what he learns about himself, not what you'd do.

### Scenario 5: Delegating a Key Project—Can You Let Go of How?
**Context:** Your team is creating a new client onboarding process. This matters—it'll improve client satisfaction and set the tone for your firm's service. You have a very specific way you'd do this (based on your rep experience).

**Setup:**
- Project scope: Design a new onboarding process for new clients
- Timeline: 6 weeks
- Team: 2 reps you trust (Maria and Alex)
- The tension: You know EXACTLY how this should be done. You can see the whole thing. But if you tell them step-by-step, they're just executing your vision, not building theirs.
- Your fear: What if they do it wrong? What if it takes longer? What if the client experience isn't as good as it would be if you did it?

**What's Being Tested:**
- Can you stay out of the method even though you have a good one?
- Can you trust your team to find a different (maybe better) way?
- Can you let go of control for the sake of ownership?

**How You Brief Them:** You have a choice in how you frame this:

**Method-Based Brief:**
"Here's what I want: First, we interview 5 current clients about their onboarding experience. Then we map the current journey. Then we identify 3 pain points. Then we prototype a new process. Timeline: 2 weeks per phase. I want to review at each stage."

**Outcome-Based Brief:**
"We need a new client onboarding process that improves satisfaction and sets us apart. Clients are telling us the current experience feels disjointed. I want you to design something better. Deadline: 6 weeks. Context: This matters because happy onboarded clients become repeat clients. Constraints: Budget is $X, we can't change anything without legal approval, I need to see it before we implement."

**Decision Point:** Do you:
a) Give the method-based brief. It'll get done right and on time.
b) Give a hybrid. Some structure + some freedom.
c) Give the outcome-based brief. Trust them to figure out the how.
d) Do it yourself. It's too important to delegate.

**Debrief Questions:**
1. What did you choose and why?
2. If you chose A, who grows from this project? (You or them?)
3. If you chose C, are you comfortable NOT knowing how they'll do it?
4. What's the trade-off between control and ownership?
5. What would their process look like? Would it be different from yours?

**Best Practice Answer:** C. Give outcome-based brief. Then:
- Let them research and explore their own way
- Check in at milestones (2 weeks, 4 weeks), not at every step
- Ask about their process: "Walk me through how you're thinking about this." Not: "Here's what I would do."
- If they're going off a cliff, redirect the outcome, not the method: "I'm seeing us go in a direction that might not work. Let's talk about what you're trying to solve."

## SECTION D: WEEKLY ZOOM DISCUSSION PROMPTS (5 prompts, one per sub-module)

### Week 1 Discussion: The Death of the IC
**"What IC skill are you having the hardest time letting go of?"**

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Go around the room. Have people name ONE skill they miss most.
- Don't let them gloss over it. If they say "I miss closing," ask: "What specifically about closing?"
- Look for patterns. If most people are struggling with the same skill, that's the team's growth area.
- Normalize: "Everyone here is grieving something. That's the point."

**Follow-up Questions (if conversation is slow):**
- "If you could keep one rep skill and drop everything else, what would it be?"
- "What does that skill give you that managing doesn't?"
- "Is there a way to get that need met as a manager?"

**Close with:** "This week, every time you miss that skill, name it. Don't suppress it. Grieve it. That's how you move forward."

---

### Week 2 Discussion: The Skill Translation
**"Where are you in the grief cycle right now? What does that stage feel like?"**

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Have people identify their current stage (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance).
- Ask them to describe what that stage FEELS like, not just what they're doing.
- Normalize all stages. "Anger is healthy. Bargaining is understandable. Depression is part of the process."
- If someone is in an early stage, don't push them to acceptance. Just name where they are.

**Follow-up Questions:**
- "How long have you been in this stage?"
- "What's keeping you here?" (If they seem stuck.)
- "What would the next stage look like for you?"

**Close with:** "Notice where you are. There's no rush to move. The stages move at their own pace."

---

### Week 3 Discussion: The Grief Cycle
**"What was a win for your team this week? How did it feel different than a personal win?"**

**Facilitator Notes:**
- This is about shifting their felt sense of "winning."
- Have people describe a recent team win in detail.
- Ask: "How did you feel when that happened?"
- Compare to: "How would you have felt if you'd done it yourself?"
- Help them recognize the difference (slower but deeper, sustainable, builds legacy vs. immediate but self-contained).

**Follow-up Questions:**
- "Did your team know you were proud of them?"
- "How did it feel to celebrate someone else's work?"
- "What would it feel like if you had wins like that every week?"

**Close with:** "That feeling—that's your new success metric. Keep noticing it."

---

### Week 4 Discussion: Delegation & Ownership
**"Where are you over-specifying method instead of trusting outcome?"**

**Facilitator Notes:**
- This is a vulnerability question. People might not want to admit they're micromanaging.
- Ask people to share one area where they're prescribing method too much.
- Normalize it: "We all do this. The question is whether we're willing to change it."
- Brainstorm as a group: How could they delegate outcome instead?

**Follow-up Questions:**
- "What's your fear if you DON'T specify method?"
- "What would happen if you let them try?"
- "Has your team ever surprised you with a good solution?"

**Close with:** "Pick one area. One area where you'll shift from method to outcome this week. Just one. Report back."

---

### Week 5 Discussion: The New Value
**"What's one way you're treating your team like extensions of you? How could you shift that?"**

**Facilitator Notes:**
- This is about recognizing when you're unconsciously expecting your team to think/work like you.
- Have people identify one area: "I expect them to work early / hustle like I do / solve it the way I would / have my work ethic."
- Ask: "What if they don't work that way? Is that a problem?"
- Help them distinguish between "values we all share" and "methods that are specific to you."

**Follow-up Questions:**
- "What if your team member brings a different strength than you have?"
- "How could you leverage that instead of trying to change them?"
- "What would it be like to build a team of different people, not mini-yous?"

**Close with:** "Your new job is not to replicate yourself. It's to develop people who are better than you in their own ways. How does that shift your management?"
