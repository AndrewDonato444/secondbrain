## OPENING FRAMING (1 min)

**[0:00–1:00]**

"You're managing by crisis. Your reps text you at 6 PM about deals they should have told you about in the 1:1. Your team meeting gets canceled because of an 'emergency.' You miss the fact that your best rep is burning out because you haven't had a real conversation with her in three weeks.

This is not your fault. No one teaches you how to structure time. But it's destroying your effectiveness.

Here's the truth: rhythm beats heroics. A predictable 1:1 every Thursday at 2 PM will surface problems faster than a crisis firefight. A consistent team meeting will build trust faster than a perfect all-hands. And a monthly review will reveal patterns—about your team, about the market, about yourself—that you can't see day-to-day.

This module is about building that rhythm. Not perfectly. Just consistently. And we'll show you exactly how."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Pause after "This is not your fault." Let it land.
- Use calm, steady tone. This should feel reassuring, not urgent.
- Emphasize: This is about structure, not perfection.

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE WEEKLY 1:1 STRUCTURE
**[1:00–15:00] (14 minutes)**

### Opening Hook (1–2 min)

**[1:00–2:45]**

"Let's start with the weekly 1:1. For some of you, this is sacred. You guard it. For others, it's the first thing that dies when the calendar gets messy. And for a lot of you, it exists, but it's awkward. You sit down, the rep has nothing to say, you have nothing to ask except 'How's your pipeline?' and 20 minutes later you're both relieved it's over.

The 1:1 should be the opposite of that. It should be the place where your rep actually tells you what's real. And it should be the place where you actually get to know them—not as a salesperson, but as a person.

Here's why it matters: your rep will only surface problems in a 1:1 if they trust you. And they only trust you if they see you regularly, in the same way, at the same time. That consistency is what builds trust."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- This is relatable. Most new managers have awkward 1:1s.
- Key insight: Consistency breeds trust. Trust enables honesty.
- Tone: sympathetic, then practical.

### Key Teaching Points (10–11 min)

**[2:45–13:15]**

#### The Purpose of the 1:1

"The 1:1 has one job: **create a safe space for your rep to tell you what's real.** Not what's safe to say. What's real.

In a team meeting, your rep can't say 'I'm struggling with confidence on calls.' In a Slack message, they can't say 'I don't understand the market right now.' In a group setting, they'll never admit they're dealing with personal stuff that's affecting their focus.

But in a 1:1? That's your only chance to hear it.

And if you miss those signals? They fester. A small confidence issue becomes a bigger problem in three weeks. A personal crisis becomes a performance miss in a month. A pipeline stall becomes a deal loss in six weeks.

Your job in the 1:1 is to create conditions where your rep can be honest."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- The 1:1 is diagnosis + coaching, not performance review (that's the monthly review).
- Safe space = predictable, regular, focused on the rep (not you).
- Fester = that's the real cost of skipped 1:1s.

#### The 1:1 Format (Wins → Performance → Coaching → Personal → Next Week)

"Here's the structure we recommend. It's not sacred. But it works because it moves from low-stakes to higher-stakes, builds connection, and ends with clarity.

**[5 min] WINS**
Start here. Ask your rep: What are you proud of this week? What went well? Did you close a deal? Learn something? Get positive feedback? Make a hard call? Help a team member?

This is not filler. This is where you learn what your rep values. It also primes their brain to talk to you in a positive frame—not defensive, not worried.

*Template:*
- "What's one win you had this week?"
- "How did that feel?"
- "What did you do well there?"

**[15 min] PERFORMANCE & PIPELINE**
Now zoom in on the numbers. Where's the rep against their goal? How many conversations? Pipeline movement? Closing rate? Stuck deals?

This is conversational, not interrogatory. You're trying to understand their current reality and their thinking.

*Template:*
- "Walk me through your pipeline. What's happening?"
- "That deal stuck on Thursday—what's your read on it?"
- "You're 3 conversations short of goal. What's in the way?"

**[15 min] COACHING**
This is the heart. One area where the rep can improve. Not everything. One thing. Is it prospecting? Presentation? Qualification? Follow-up? Mindset?

Pick the thing that, if they improved it, would change their results most. Then coach it. Don't tell them the answer. Ask them questions that help them see it.

*Template:*
- "I noticed in that call, you talked a lot. How did the prospect respond?"
- "What do you think they heard?"
- "What would change if you asked more questions before pitching?"

**[10 min] PERSONAL & REAL**
Here's where you ask: How are you? Not 'how's work.' How are you? Are you stressed? Excited? Dealing with something? Bored? 

This is the trust-building moment. Your rep doesn't share personal stuff unless they believe you care about them as a human, not as a sales unit.

*Template:*
- "How are you outside of work?"
- "Is there anything I should know that's affecting your focus right now?"
- "What would help you feel more supported?"

**[5 min] NEXT WEEK**
End with clarity. What's the rep's focus next week? One thing to work on? A deal to push? A conversation to have about that coaching point?

*Template:*
- "What's your focus for next week?"
- "How will you approach that coaching area?"
- "What do you need from me?"

**Total Time: 45–60 minutes per rep, once per week, same time if possible (Thursday afternoons are classic), back-to-back if you have multiple reps.**"

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Wins first = psychological safety.
- One coaching point per week = it's digestible.
- Personal conversation = relationship depth.
- Next week clarity = accountability + autonomy.
- Consistency: "Same time" matters more than "perfect time."

#### Why Consistency Matters

"You might think: 'This is a lot of time. Can't I do this every other week?' No. Here's why:

A rep only builds trust when they know you'll show up. If you skip a 1:1 because you're busy, the message they hear is: 'You're not important to me.' Even if that's not what you mean, that's what they hear.

One skipped 1:1 breaks the pattern. Two skipped 1:1s in a row tells the rep that the 1:1 is optional. Three in a row tells them that problems are their responsibility to surface, not yours to find. And then guess what? They don't surface them.

We've seen this play out. Andrew (Manager-Forge director) canceled three 1:1s in month two of his tenure because of 'urgent stuff.' That same rep had a major issue brewing—a lost prospect network, confidence collapse, family stuff affecting focus. The rep didn't tell him. Why? Because the 1:1 was clearly optional. When Andrew reset in month three and protected the rhythm, the same rep immediately surfaced the issue in the first protected 1:1.

Consistency. That's the currency you spend to buy honesty."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Skipped 1:1s send a signal. And reps read that signal.
- Real example: Use Andrew's story as a gut check.
- Protect rhythm = get honesty.

### Real Examples & Stories (1–2 min)

**[13:15–14:30]**

**Example 1: The Rep with No News**

"You sit down. You ask 'How'd your week go?' Silence. 'I dunno, pretty normal.' You ask about their pipeline. 'Yep, still working those three deals.' Then you're staring at each other.

Here's what most managers do: they fill the silence. They talk. 'Well, you know what I think you should do...' And now the rep is in listening mode, not talking mode. You got less, not more.

Here's what to do instead: ask smaller questions. 'Tell me about the first call you had this week.' 'What was hard about it?' 'What would you change?' Let them talk. They do have news. You're just asking for it wrong.

The rep's job in a 1:1 is to talk. Your job is to listen and ask good questions. If your rep has 'no news,' you're asking the wrong questions."

**Example 2: The Over-Prepared Manager**

"You walk in with a three-page doc. 'Okay, let's talk about these gaps in your calls.' Now the rep is defensive. You're running a performance audit, not a conversation.

The 1:1 should feel like a conversation. You might have notes. But the rep should do 70% of the talking. If you're talking more than 30% of the time, you're doing it wrong.

The frame is: 'I want to understand how you see it. What's real for you?' Not: 'Here's what I think.' Your job is to help them see it themselves."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Both examples are about listening vs. talking.
- Manager's job: ask good questions, listen, help them see.
- Rep's job: talk, be honest, reflect.

### Tension Point Addressed (1 min)

**[14:30–15:00]**

"Okay, I can hear you: 'This is 45–60 minutes per rep. If I have six reps, that's five hours a week. I don't have five hours.'

First: yes, you do. It's just hidden. You spend that time in crisis management, back-channel Slack conversations, and rework. Rhythm replaces crisis. It's not additional.

Second: start with three reps if you're new. Build the habit. Then expand.

Third: this is the highest-ROI time you spend. One early signal caught in a 1:1 is worth ten hours of crisis management later.

You have the time. You're choosing what to do with it."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Rhythm replaces crisis (it's a reallocation, not addition).
- ROI is massive. Believe it.
- Direct: you're choosing what to prioritize.

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**[15:00–15:15]**

"The weekly 1:1 is your diagnostic tool. It's where you find out what's real. It's where your rep learns that you care about them as a person. And it's where trust is built.

The format is simple: wins, performance, coaching, personal, next week. The time is sacred: 45–60 min, once a week, same time.

Why it works: consistency builds trust. Trust enables honesty. Honesty surfaces problems early. Early detection prevents crises.

Protect this meeting. Everything else will be better because of it."

## SUB-MODULE 2: THE WEEKLY TEAM MEETING
**[15:15–28:00] (13 minutes)**

### Opening Hook (1–2 min)

**[15:15–16:45]**

"Most team meetings are either a waste of time or a funeral. You go around the room and everyone reports their numbers. Nobody connects. Nobody learns. And the rep who's struggling gets more anxious, not less.

Here's what a good team meeting does instead: it creates momentum. It builds connection. It surfaces best practices. It catches early signals. And it does all that in 60 minutes.

The difference isn't the amount of time. It's the structure."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Relatable: most team meetings do feel like a waste.
- We're going to fix that.
- Structure matters.

### Key Teaching Points (10–11 min)

**[16:45–27:15]**

#### The Purpose

"Your weekly team meeting has three jobs:

1. **Alignment.** Everyone knows the week's priority and how it affects them.
2. **Connection.** The team sees each other as humans, not competitors.
3. **Learning.** Someone shares a win or technique that the team can use.

It is NOT a performance review. It is NOT a one-on-one in public. It is NOT a place to coach weakness. (That's the 1:1.)

If you use team meeting time to coach someone's gap in front of the group, you've just broken trust. That rep will shut down. And the rest of the team will get anxious too, because now the team meeting feels like a performance audit."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Alignment, connection, learning.
- What it's NOT is equally important.
- Public coaching kills trust.

#### The Team Meeting Format

**[10 am Monday is the classic. 60 minutes. Here's the structure:]**

**[5 min] OPENING & ANNOUNCEMENTS**
"You come in. 'Hey team. Here's what we're focused on this week. Here's what happened in the market. Here's what leadership cares about.' You're setting the frame. Then you pass it to the team.

Not 30 minutes of you talking. Five minutes. You set the context and step back.

*Template:*
- 'This week we're focused on [priority].'
- 'Market news: [one thing from the week].'
- 'Leadership is watching [metric].'
- 'Let's go.'

**[20 min] PIPELINE REVIEW**
'Who wants to start?' Go around the room (not forced). Each rep does a 2–3 minute update: 'Here's where I am against goal. Here's what I'm working on. Here's what's stuck.' That's it. No judgment. No commentary from you unless asked.

Why this matters: reps see each other's pipelines. They notice if someone's short. And that rep knows they're short—no hiding. It also helps reps learn from each other. 'Oh, she's using three-step qualification. I should try that.'

*Template for rep:*
- 'I'm at [number] conversations against [goal]. [Deal names] are moving. [Deal name] is stuck because [reason].'

*Your job:* listen. One clarifying question if needed. Move on.

**[15 min] WINS & LEARNING**
'Who had a win this week? A closed deal? A tough call that went well? A technique you tried that worked?' 

Pick 2–3. Have them talk for a minute. Then ask: 'What did you do?' 'How did you approach that?' Let the team learn from it.

This is where you celebrate. This is where you build culture. And it's where best practices spread.

*Template:*
- 'Sarah, you closed the [property]. How'd you land that?'
- 'I qualified three times before presenting.'
- 'Team, hear that? Qualification. It matters. Who else is going to try that this week?'

**[15 min] TACTICAL TOPIC**
One topic, every week. Could be: cold calling technique, market update, how to write a strong proposal, objection handling, how to follow up with a tough prospect, how to ask for referrals, etc.

You pick the topic based on: what's the bottleneck right now? What if everyone got 1% better at this, what would change?

Then you teach it in 15 minutes. Or bring in someone from your team. Or have a rep teach it.

The point: learning happens in public. And everyone gets the same information. No rep feels left out. No 'I didn't know that was important.'

*Template:*
- 'This week we're all practicing [technique].'
- 'Here's how: [3-step process].'
- 'I want to see it in your calls this week. We'll talk about how it went next Monday.'

**[5 min] CLOSE & NEXT WEEK**
'Here's what I'm expecting this week. Stay focused. Let's have a great week. See you next Monday.'

You're done. You moved fast. You built connection. You taught something. And everyone knows what matters.

**Total Time: 60 minutes, same time every week (Monday 10 am is standard), everyone attends.**"

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Fast pace. No long-winded you talking.
- Reps do most of the talking.
- Learning is public. Wins are public. Both build culture.
- Consistency: same time, every week, non-negotiable.

#### Why This Structure Works

"Here's what this does differently:

**It prevents invisibility.** If a rep is struggling, everyone sees it in pipeline review. You see it. The rep sees it. Their peers see it. That rep now knows they're on your radar, which changes behavior (in a good way).

**It builds connection.** You're not talking at them. You're facilitating them talking to each other. Suddenly they're not competitors. They're teammates learning together.

**It surfaces early signals.** Rep goes quiet in pipeline review? Rep's usual chattiness is gone? You notice. That's a signal to follow up in the 1:1.

**It normalizes learning.** You're teaching one skill every week. That tells the team: we expect to get better. There's no judgment. We're all learning. And that creates psychological safety."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Visibility prevents invisibility.
- Team connection changes culture.
- Early signals become obvious.
- Learning culture reduces shame.

### Real Examples & Stories (1–2 min)

**[27:15–28:00]**

**Example: The Over-Structured Manager**

"A manager we worked with did daily standups (15 min) plus twice-weekly forecasts (30 min each) plus a weekly team meeting (60 min). That's 3.5 hours a week for a team of four.

And the team hated it. They felt micromanaged. One of their top performers left in month three. Why? Because she felt like she was being monitored, not led.

When she left, the manager said: 'But I was so connected to the team!' No. You were so connected to their activity. You lost the connection with the people.

This manager reset. Daily standups gone. Twice-weekly forecasts gone. Just: one weekly team meeting (Monday 60 min) + individual 1:1s.

Team engagement went up. The remaining reps felt trusted. And the team meeting became the place where real conversation happened.

More meetings doesn't mean better leadership. Consistent, well-designed meetings means better leadership.

The counter-example is the no-structure manager: no 1:1s, no team meeting, just Slack and crisis. That manager missed that one rep was underperforming for three months. By the time they found out (because leadership asked), the rep was already checked out. Too late.

**Rhythm beats no rhythm. And excessive structure beats no structure but less beats more.**"

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Over-structure breeds micromanagement.
- No structure breeds crisis management.
- Lean rhythm is the sweet spot.

### Tension Point Addressed (1 min)

**[28:00–28:15]**

"'This feels like a lot of talking, not a lot of doing.' Yes. Talking is the doing. If your reps don't feel seen, heard, and connected to the team, they won't do the work. But if they do? They'll move mountains.

This 60 minutes of conversation every week pays for itself ten times over in reduced turnover, increased motivation, and better team performance."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Talking = leading.
- Trust + clarity = better execution.
- Direct ROI link.

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**[28:15–28:30]**

"The weekly team meeting is your leadership moment. You set the week. You celebrate wins. You teach skill. You build culture.

60 minutes. Monday 10 am (or your equivalent). Consistent. Reps do the talking. You facilitate and teach.

Why it works: visibility + connection + learning = a team that moves together."

## SUB-MODULE 3: THE MONTHLY BUSINESS REVIEW
**[28:30–40:30] (12 minutes)**

### Opening Hook (1–2 min)

**[28:30–29:45]**

"Once a month, you need to zoom out. Not daily deals. Not this week's activity. The whole picture.

How is the team performing against goal? What patterns are emerging? Who's struggling? Who's on fire? What did you miss? What's the forecast? And what's one thing you're going to focus on coaching as a team next month?

This is the monthly business review. And it's where you shift from manager to leader."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Zooming out = strategic thinking.
- This is different than weekly meetings.
- This is where you coach trends, not activities.

### Key Teaching Points (10–11 min)

**[29:45–40:00]**

#### The Purpose

"The monthly business review (MBR) is the place where you:

1. **Recap the month.** What happened? Against what? Why?
2. **Forecast next month.** What are we building toward?
3. **Identify coaching needs.** What patterns need to change?
4. **Assess team health.** How's morale? Who's at risk? Who's energized?

This is not a Zoom call with your team. This is your moment to think and be intentional. Then you bring insights back to the team (not as blame, but as learning)."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- This is solo reflection, then team conversation.
- Different than daily/weekly cadence.
- Strategic + human.

#### The MBR Framework (45–60 min, solo prep + 60 min team conversation)

**[SOLO PREP: 30 min, the day before team MBR]**

Grab your metrics. Get them in front of you.

**1. Team Performance Recap (5 min)**
- Total team revenue. Against goal. Why up? Why down?
- Individual rep ranking. Who's ahead? Who's behind?
- Key deals closed this month. Key deals lost.
- Conversion rate. Close rate. Average deal size. Cycle time.
- Any new problems (like pipeline cliff at month three)?

*Questions to ask yourself:*
- Are we on track for the quarter?
- Is anyone dramatically off?
- What changed from last month?

**2. Rep-by-Rep Snapshot (10 min)**
Not a judgment. An observation. For each rep:
- Rank (1 is best, etc.)
- Trend (up, stable, down?)
- Biggest win this month.
- Biggest struggle this month.
- Risk level (green, yellow, red).

Risk level is key. Red = you need to intervene (coaching, support, or possibly a harder conversation). Yellow = watch it, coach it in 1:1. Green = great, keep doing it.

**3. Team Health Snapshot (5 min)**
- Morale. Is the team energized or depleted?
- Turnover risk. Who might be thinking about leaving?
- Connection. Do people feel like a team or solo contributors?
- Alignment. Do people know what matters and why?

**4. Forecast Next Month (5 min)**
- What's the pipeline tell you? Will next month be better or worse?
- What initiatives should the team focus on?
- What's one thing you want the whole team to improve?

**5. Coaching Plan (5 min)**
- One area the team should collectively improve (skill, mindset, behavior).
- One rep who needs focused coaching.
- One rep who's ready to develop further (new responsibility, stretch).

---

**[TEAM CONVERSATION: 60 min, once per month, end of month]**

You've prepped. Now you meet with the team. This is a conversation, not a report.

**[10 min] RECAP**
'Hey team, here's where we landed this month. [Headline number]. We were targeting [goal]. So we [hit, missed, exceeded] by [X]. Here's why: [the story]. This is not about blame. This is about learning.'

Factual. No judgment. Just the reality.

**[15 min] WINS & LOSSES**
'These five deals closed. Here's what was great about each one. [Tell the story of one win and what the team can learn.] These two deals we missed. Here's why. Not blame. Just what we learned.'

You're teaching. You're celebrating. You're normalizing 'we win and lose together.'

**[15 min] FORECAST**
'Here's what I see in next month's pipeline. Good news: [X]. Concern: [Y]. Here's what that means for us. Here's what each of you needs to focus on so we hit [goal].'

You're connecting activity to outcome. You're making the future concrete.

**[15 min] COACHING PRIORITIES**
'This month, our focus is [skill/topic]. I noticed [specific pattern]. Going forward, I want to see [different behavior]. Here's how we'll work on it. And [rep name], I'm going to give you extra coaching on [area].'

Direct but supportive. You're not saying 'you suck.' You're saying 'here's what's working, here's what's not, here's how we improve.'

**[5 min] CLOSE**
'Great month, team. Let's bring that energy next month. We've got [goal] to hit. And I believe we can do it. See you next week.'

**Total Time: 60 min team conversation, once per month, ideally the last Thursday or Friday of the month.**"

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Prep solo. Then lead the conversation.
- Data + narrative. Not just numbers.
- Coaching + celebration. Not just criticism.
- Forecast creates forward momentum.

#### Why This Structure Works

"The monthly review does something 1:1s and team meetings can't: it shows patterns.

In a 1:1, you see individual rep. In a team meeting, you see current week. But in the MBR, you see: 'Oh, every rep's closing rate dropped. That's not individual. That's market or messaging.' Or: 'That one rep has now been yellow for two months. This is a trend, not a blip.'

Patterns change how you coach. If closing rate drops across the board, you teach a skill. If one rep's closing rate dropped, you coach that person. Two completely different responses.

The MBR also creates accountability in a healthy way. Every rep knows: once a month, we're all standing in front of the results. No hiding. But also: we're doing this together. You're not being called out. We're reflecting together.

And the forecast? That's the leadership move. You're saying: 'I can see forward. I see what's coming. And here's what we need to do about it.' That builds confidence."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Patterns reveal insight.
- Collective accountability (not public shaming).
- Forecast = leadership.

### Real Examples & Stories (1–2 min)

**[40:00–40:20]**

**Example: The MBR That Caught a Cliff**

"A manager was doing weekly 1:1s and team meetings but skipped the MBR for month two and three because 'things were going well.'

At the end of month three, she looked at the annual forecast and saw: the pipeline for month four was half of month three. A cliff.

She looked back at month three's team meeting notes. Same activity levels. Same calls. But deal size had gotten smaller. Closing rate hadn't changed.

So why the cliff?

If she'd done the MBR in month three, she would have seen it then. Not at the end. She could have coached her team on deal size. She could have shifted strategy. Instead, she was surprised.

She did the MBR in month four. Found the issue. Coached it. Month five came back. But month four was missed.

One missed MBR cost the team a month of revenue.

The MBR is not optional. It's where you see what's coming."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Patterns emerge in monthly data.
- Missing the monthly view = missing the forecast.
- Early warning system.

### Tension Point Addressed (1 min)

**[40:20–40:30]**

"'I barely have time for weekly meetings. How do I add a monthly review?' 

The MBR takes an hour. It prevents ten hours of crisis. And it gives you strategic insight you can't get any other way.

Plus, the monthly review is mostly solo work. You're reading a spreadsheet. You're thinking. Then you have a 60-min conversation. That's actually less time than an extra day of meetings.

Do it."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Prevention > reaction.
- Strategic insight is different value.
- Direct: do it.

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**[40:30–40:45]**

"The monthly business review is where you zoom out. You see patterns. You forecast. You coach. You build team health.

Prep solo. Lead one conversation. 60 minutes.

Why it works: patterns reveal what daily meetings can't. And forecasting changes strategy."

## SUB-MODULE 4: THE EARLY SIGNAL SYSTEM
**[40:45–53:30] (13 minutes)**

### Opening Hook (1–2 min)

**[40:45–42:15]**

"Your rep who closed the most deals last quarter just had her worst week ever. She's in your Monday team meeting, and she's quiet. Doesn't volunteer. Doesn't laugh at the normal jokes.

Your instinct is: 'She's burned out. Or she's looking for a new job. Or something happened.'

You're right to notice. That's your early warning system firing. But most managers ignore it. They think: 'Maybe she just had a bad week. Don't overreact.'

And then she is burned out. Or she gets an offer. Or she quits. And you're shocked.

The early signal system is the skill of noticing small changes—and then being curious, not accusatory."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Noticing is the skill.
- Small changes = early warnings.
- Curiosity > accusation.

### Key Teaching Points (10–11 min)

**[42:15–52:45]**

#### What to Watch For

"There are three categories of signals. Learn to see them.

**BEHAVIORAL CHANGES**
Your rep is usually chatty. Now she's quiet.
Your rep usually volunteers. Now she's silent.
Your rep usually asks questions. Now she just nods.
Your rep usually jokes. Now she's serious.

You're looking for: **what's different from normal?**

The exact behavior doesn't matter. What matters is: this person is operating outside their baseline.

Why? Because behavior change indicates something internal has changed. A change in confidence. A change in energy. A change in stress. A change in commitment.

*Questions to ask yourself:*
- What did I notice that was different?
- How long has this been going on?
- Is it situational (like, after a lost deal) or sustained?

**PIPELINE CHANGES**
Your rep usually has 20 conversations a week. Now she has 12.
Your rep usually has three stage-4 deals. Now she has none.
Your rep usually closes 40% of stage-3 deals. Now it's 20%.

You're looking for: **what changed in their engine?**

This is harder to spot because it happens in the spreadsheet, not in a meeting. But if you're reading your metrics weekly, you'll see it.

Why? Because pipeline changes reveal a lot: lost confidence (fewer dials), lost strategy (fewer stage-4 deals), lost skills (lower close rate), lost motivation (just going through motions).

*Questions to ask yourself:*
- What metric changed?
- When did it change?
- Is it one deal that's missing or a system problem?

**ENERGY DROPS**
Your rep always has energy. Now she's flat. Tired. Like she's going through motions.
Your rep usually makes jokes in 1:1s. Now she gives one-word answers.
Your rep usually pitches ideas. Now she just executes what you ask.

You're looking for: **what changed in their emotional state?**

This is the hardest to see because it's subtle. But it's the biggest early warning sign.

Why? Because energy drop is almost always the first sign of: burnout, personal crisis, loss of confidence, or disengagement.

*Questions to ask yourself:*
- What's the gap between their normal energy and right now?
- Did something happen (in work or their life)?
- How long has this been going on?

---

#### How to Intervene (Curiosity, Not Accusation)

**[45:00–52:00]**

"You noticed a signal. Now what?

Most managers do one of two things:
1. **Ignore it.** 'Maybe she'll snap out of it.'
2. **Confront it.** 'You seem off. What's wrong?' (Feels like accusation.)

Both are wrong.

Here's the right approach: **Gentle curiosity.**

In your next 1:1, after you've covered wins/performance, go here:

'I noticed something. You've been quieter in team meetings the last couple weeks. Is everything okay? Like, outside of work, too?'

Notice what you did:
- You named the specific behavior. (Not 'you seem off,' but 'you've been quieter in team meetings.')
- You gave them an out. (You didn't say 'Something's definitely wrong.' You asked 'Is everything okay?')
- You opened the door to personal stuff. (Not 'What's wrong with your prospecting?' but 'outside of work, too?')

Now they can tell you, or they can say 'No, I'm fine, just a lot on my plate.'

If they open up: **Listen. Don't fix.** You're not trying to solve their problem. You're trying to understand it.

'Tell me more.'
'How long has that been going on?'
'What would help?'
'Do you need anything from me?'

If they say they're fine: **Believe them, but watch.** You'll know in two weeks if they're fine. If the behavior continues, you follow up again.

---

'I noticed [specific change]. Help me understand what's going on.'

That's it. You're creating space. You're saying: 'I see you. I care. I'm here if you need support.'

And that's when reps tell you what's real."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Notice specific behaviors, not vibes.
- Gentle curiosity, not accusation.
- Create space. Then listen.
- Follow up if it continues.

#### Why This Changes Everything

"Here's the real impact of the early signal system:

A rep is dealing with a personal crisis (family stuff, health, whatever). You notice the behavior change in week one. You ask. She tells you. And now you know: 'Okay, her performance is going to dip, and that's okay. I'm going to support her through this.'

Versus: You don't notice. Week three, she's now really struggling. She hasn't told you. She hasn't told anyone. And she's spiraling. And now you're dealing with a performance issue when what you're really dealing with is someone who needed support.

Or: A rep is losing confidence. You notice her close rate dropping. You ask in the 1:1. She says 'Yeah, I feel like I'm not connecting with prospects.' You coach her. You build her confidence back. Week five, she's back to baseline.

Versus: You don't notice. Month two, her confidence is gone. She's now looking for a new job. And you've just lost a rep.

Early signals are the difference between early intervention (easy) and crisis management (hard).

And most of it happens in the regular 1:1s and team meetings. You just have to be paying attention."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Early signals = early intervention.
- Early intervention prevents crises.
- It's hidden in the regular cadence. You just have to notice.

### Real Examples & Stories (1–2 min)

**[52:00–52:45]**

**Example: The Signal Sandwich**

"A rep started acting different. She was in a team meeting, and she was quiet (behavioral change). Her pipeline was down 40% (pipeline change). And she was flat in the 1:1, giving one-word answers (energy drop).

All three signals pointing the same direction: something is wrong.

Her manager noticed. Asked in the 1:1. Found out: her kid had just been diagnosed with a learning disability. She was stressed. She hadn't told anyone. She was just trying to push through.

Her manager said: 'This is not something you push through alone. Here's what we're going to do. You're going to take two weeks to get support set up. Your goal for those weeks is zero. Just go figure out what your family needs. I've got your back. When you come back, we'll look at your goal again.'

That rep came back. Told everyone on the team (in her own way). Got support from the team. And actually ended up with her best quarter because she knew the team had her back.

If the manager had ignored the signals, that rep would have quit. Or her performance would have tanked. Instead, she became the team's best performer because the manager cared enough to notice and ask."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Multiple signals = definitely something.
- Caring > fixing.
- Trust and support change outcomes.

## SUB-MODULE 5: CONSISTENCY OVER PERFECTION
**[53:15–60:00] (12 minutes)**

### Opening Hook (1–2 min)

**[53:15–54:30]**

"You have a crisis. A big one. A deal falls through. A rep quits. A market shift. And your calendar says: team meeting at 10 am.

What do you do?

Some of you cancel. 'We have bigger fish to fry right now.'

But here's what that does to your team: it tells them that the rhythm is optional. And if the rhythm is optional to you, it's optional to them.

That rep who's struggling? They don't show up to your 1:1 because they think 'Hey, the manager cancels when things get busy, so I don't have to take this seriously either.'

And then the rhythm is dead. And you're back to managing by crisis.

This module is about protecting your rhythm when everything in you wants to abandon it."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Relatable. Everyone has crises.
- Consistency is a signal. Reps read that signal.
- Protecting rhythm = protecting trust.

### Key Teaching Points (10–11 min)

**[54:30–59:45]**

#### Why Consistency Matters More Than Perfection

"Here's the thing about rhythm: it only works if it's consistent.

A perfect 1:1 that gets canceled is worse than a mediocre 1:1 that happens every week.

Why? Because consistency is the signal that matters. When you protect the 1:1, your rep hears: 'You matter to me. This is not optional.' When you cancel it, they hear: 'You're optional.'

Same with team meetings. A mediocre 45-minute team meeting every Monday beats a perfect 90-minute team meeting that happens sometimes.

Why? Because if your team meeting is every Monday, they can count on it. They know what to expect. They prepare. They show up with energy. It becomes a rhythm.

But if it's 'whenever you can fit it in,' it's not a rhythm. It's a meeting. And when it happens, nobody's fully committed because it could disappear.

**Consistency creates trust. Consistency creates culture. Consistency creates momentum.**

Perfection does none of those things if it's sporadic."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Consistency > perfection.
- This is about the message you send.
- Reps read consistency as commitment.

#### How to Protect Your Rhythm

"Here's the practical framework for protecting your rhythm:

**1. CALENDAR BLOCK**
Monday 10 am: Team meeting (recurring, locked, no exceptions).
Thursday 2 pm: 1:1s (recurring, locked, no exceptions).
Last Thursday of the month: MBR (recurring, locked).

Block them in your calendar. Not as soft meetings. As events. Non-negotiable.

**2. COMMUNICATE THE RHYTHM TO YOUR TEAM**
'Here's my rhythm. Monday 10 am, team meeting. Thursday 2 pm, 1:1s. Last Thursday of the month, MBR. These are not meetings. These are the foundation of how we lead together. They will not get canceled unless there's a literal emergency.'

A literal emergency is: the building is on fire, someone is in the hospital, we lost a major client.

A literal emergency is NOT: a deal is falling through, we have a lot going on, a fire drill came up, leadership asked for something urgent.

You've seen this. You have the capacity to do both. You just have to say no to the thing that matters less.

**3. SAY NO TO COMPETING PRIORITIES**
Someone asks you to a meeting Thursday at 2 pm. You say: 'I can't. I have 1:1s. But I can do Wednesday at 4 pm.'

Leadership wants you for an all-hands Thursday afternoon. You say: 'My team 1:1s are then, but I can give you the first 30 minutes Thursday morning.'

This does two things: it protects your rhythm, and it models that priorities are real. You're not just saying yes to everything. You're protecting what matters.

And guess what? People respect that. They don't resent it. Because they see: 'This leader has a clear rhythm, and she protects it.' And that makes people feel secure.

**4. RESCHEDULE INSTEAD OF CANCEL**
1:1 needs to move because of a genuine conflict? Don't cancel. Reschedule to Tuesday or Friday.

Team meeting needs to move? Don't cancel. Move it to Tuesday 10 am (if you can't do Monday).

The point is: the meeting still happens. The rhythm is protected. You're just shifting the day.

**5. COMMUNICATE WHEN YOU DO SLIP**
Sometimes you'll miss a 1:1. Or you'll have to move the team meeting. It happens.

When you do, tell your rep: 'Hey, I know I moved our 1:1. I want to protect this time for us. Let's nail Wednesday at 4 pm instead.'

You're showing: 'I care enough about this to acknowledge that I moved it and reschedule it.' That's different than ghosting.

---

#### What Will Try to Derail Your Rhythm

"These things will happen. Anticipate them. Plan for them.

**The Crisis**
A deal falls through. A rep quits. You're in firefight mode.

Here's what to do: 'I'm in the middle of something, but team meeting is at 10 am on Monday. Let's meet there. We'll all deal with this together.' 

Or: 'I have a conflict. Let's move to Tuesday 10 am. I'll explain then.'

The rhythm doesn't stop for crisis. The crisis gets dealt with in the rhythm.

**The Email from Leadership**
'I need you in a call Thursday at 2 pm. It's urgent.'

Here's what to say: 'I have 1:1s then. Can it be 4 pm? Or Wednesday at 3 pm?'

If it truly can't move: 'Okay, I can do 30 minutes from 2–2:30 pm Thursday, then I have to step into my 1:1s.' You've just protected most of your rhythm.

**The Guilt**
'I should be doing forecasts. I should be closer to the reps. I should be doing more training. I should be in more leadership meetings.'

Here's the truth: 4–6 hours a week (three 1:1s, one team meeting, one MBR) is your leadership foundation. Everything else you add is gravy. If you're trying to add stuff without protecting the foundation, the foundation will crumble.

**The New Manager Trap**
You're new. You want to prove yourself. So you say yes to every meeting, every project, every ask.

And then your team meeting gets moved. Your 1:1s get canceled. And suddenly the foundation is gone.

Don't do that. Say no. Protect the foundation. Prove yourself by having the best team culture, the best team performance, the best retention. You prove yourself by protecting rhythm.

---

#### The Real Cost of Skipped Meetings

"Let me be specific about what happens when you abandon rhythm:

**Missed 1:1s:**
- Rep doesn't surface problems.
- Small issues become big issues.
- Rep feels unsupported.
- Trust erodes.
- Rep starts looking.

**Missed Team Meetings:**
- Team loses momentum.
- No celebration of wins.
- No public learning.
- Team members feel solo, not connected.
- Culture flattens.

**Missed MBRs:**
- You lose the pattern.
- You miss forecast shifts.
- You can't coach strategically (only react).
- Team surprises you month after month.

**The Cascade:**
One missed meeting is a one-off. Two missed meetings is a pattern. Three missed meetings is a new reality: 'Our manager doesn't protect this.' And once the reps believe that, rebuilding takes months. Not weeks. Months.

We've seen it. Manager cancels 1:1s for two weeks because of a project. Rep stops showing up with real issues. Manager notices in week four and tries to rebuild trust. It takes three months of consistent 1:1s to get back to where you were.

Protect rhythm from the beginning. Don't let it erode. Because rebuilding is so much harder than protecting."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Specific consequences for each skipped meeting.
- Rebuilding trust is slower than protecting it.
- Frame this as practical, not emotional.

### Real Examples & Stories (1–2 min)

**[59:00–59:30]**

Month two: a client situation came up. Big urgency. Andrew canceled his 1:1s two weeks in a row. 'It's temporary. The team will understand.'

And they did understand. But they also stopped bringing real issues.

Same rep, same situation in month three, it got surfaced in week one of the 1:1.

The difference wasn't the rep. The difference was consistency."

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Real story = credible.
- Shows the cost of breaking rhythm.
- Shows the benefit of restoring rhythm.

Then you deal with the crisis. And you still do the 1:1s and team meeting. You have 168 hours a week. Your team meeting is 1 hour. Your 1:1s are 3-4 hours. A crisis doesn't remove those hours. It just means you're busier. And guess what? You need your rhythm more, not less, when things are chaotic.

The crisis will be there after the 1:1. But the rep will be stronger because you showed up."

## MEETING HYGIENE TIPS FOR INSTRUCTORS

1. **Start on time. End on time.** Respect schedules. Builds trust.
2. **One person talks at a time.** No side conversations. Reps do 70% of talking, you do 30%.
3. **No phones.** Model focus. You're here for them.
4. **One topic, not 47.** Depth > breadth.
5. **Celebrate wins publicly.** Shame privately (if at all). Never in group.
6. **Recap the 1:1 in writing.** Not a transcript. One-liner on next week's focus.
7. **Same time, every time.** Even if you're moving it, it's moving to a new fixed time.
8. **Agenda in advance.** Team meeting agenda goes out Monday morning (or Friday before).
9. **No side meetings.** Don't coach someone one-on-one right before/after the team meeting. Feeds the fear that the team meeting is where you're critiquing them.
10. **Show up fully.** If you're checking email or stressed, they feel it. Be present.
