# Effective 1:1s — Module Brief

**Module Status:** Ready for Content Development  
**Module #:** 6 of 12  
**Position in Curriculum:** Deep skill (follows Operating Rhythm; precedes Pipeline Review)

---

## Executive Overview

**Effective 1:1s** teaches the most underestimated tool in a manager's arsenal: the weekly one-on-one conversation. This module goes deep on how to structure, facilitate, and coach in 1:1s so that they actually build trust, surface problems, and drive performance—instead of becoming status update sessions where nothing real gets said.

The core insight: **A 1:1 is only effective if the rep comes willing to be vulnerable. If they're defensive, guarded, or surface-level, you're wasting both your time.** This module teaches how to build psychological safety so reps actually bring their real concerns, obstacles, and wins.

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

The 1:1 is not a status update meeting. It's a coaching and trust-building conversation. If your reps are only telling you what's going well and avoiding what's hard, your 1:1 is failing. Your job is to create enough safety that they bring the real stuff: obstacles, frustrations, confusion, personal challenges affecting work, and honest assessment of their performance.

**More specifically:**

1. **Safety comes first, coaching comes second.** You can't coach someone who doesn't trust you. If your 1:1s feel like performance interrogations, reps will perform the surface-level answer instead of being real. Start by building safety.

2. **Coaching is asking, not telling.** The rep should leave your 1:1 thinking they figured something out, not that you told them what to do. Your job is to ask questions that help them see what they already know.

3. **You're listening for what's NOT being said.** A rep goes quiet when they usually talk. A rep's energy is different. A rep who usually complains about leads suddenly stops. These are signals. Your job is to notice and ask about them.

4. **Accountability happens in 1:1s, but not through scolding.** "You're at 60% of quota" is data. "Here's why that matters and what we're doing about it" is accountability. Accountability without shame builds ownership.

5. **Emotional dumps belong in 1:1s.** Reps are people. They have personal stuff affecting their work. Your job is not to be their therapist but to acknowledge that life happens and help them navigate it while staying focused on work.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Confident in the 1:1 structure (they have a roadmap)
- Equipped to ask coaching questions (not just give answers)
- Clear on how to build safety (so reps bring the real stuff)
- Understanding that vulnerability from them requires some from you too

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers typically do one of two things in 1:1s—both counterproductive:

**Path 1: The Status Report Manager**
"Give me an update on your deals. What's your forecast? Any blockers?"

What happens: Reps come prepared to give you the sanitized version. They don't surface real obstacles. They don't ask for help because they think you want them to be independent. They don't mention personal stuff affecting performance. The 1:1 becomes a box-check where everyone knows it's not a real conversation.

**Path 2: The Interrogation Manager**
"You're at 60% of quota. What's going wrong? Why is your close rate down? Are you doing the activities?"

What happens: Reps get defensive. They make excuses or blame external factors. They stop bringing problems to you because they know they'll get questioned. They don't trust you. The 1:1 feels adversarial.

### The Real Issue

**Reps only bring their real obstacles and honest assessment if they feel safe.** Safety means:
- You won't use what they say against them
- You actually want to help, not interrogate
- You're human enough to acknowledge complexity
- You're not judging them for struggling

Most new managers skip the safety-building and jump straight to problem-solving. They wonder why their reps aren't bringing real issues.

The answer is: **Reps don't trust that it's safe.**

## Key Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. **The Safety Pyramid**

Safety is built in layers. You can't skip to coaching until foundation is solid.

**Layer 1: You are consistent**
- Same time every week (shows priority)
- You prep (shows respect)
- You follow through on what you say (builds trust)
- Reps know what to expect

**Layer 2: You listen more than you talk**
- You ask questions (shows curiosity)
- You don't interrupt (shows respect)
- You don't jump to solutions (shows patience)
- Reps feel heard

**Layer 3: You acknowledge the human stuff**
- Personal challenges affect work (that's real)
- You don't pretend to understand if you don't
- You're vulnerable sometimes too (shows humanity)
- Reps know you see them as people

**Layer 4: You give feedback respectfully**
- Data-first (not emotions)
- No shame or judgment
- "Here's what I see, here's what I think, what's your take?" (vs. "You're wrong")
- Reps feel evaluated, not demolished

**Layer 5: You coach, not direct**
- "What do you think?" (not "Here's what you should do")
- Help them see their own blind spot
- Give them autonomy in the solution
- Reps feel capable

Once layers 1-3 are solid, you can do 4-5. Skip layers 1-3 and layers 4-5 fail.

### 2. **The Listening Framework**

Most new managers listen for what they want to hear. Better managers listen for what's being said AND what's not being said.

**Listen For:**
- What they're saying (the words)
- How they're saying it (the tone, energy, confidence)
- What they're not saying (the silence, the gaps)
- What changed from last week (different energy, different focus, different emotional tone)

**The Questions You Ask Based on Listening:**
- "You usually talk about X; I didn't hear about it today. What's up?" (Notice the gap)
- "Your energy feels different. Everything OK?" (Notice the tone)
- "You said the lead is 'somewhere in the process.' What does that actually mean?" (Listen for vagueness = confusion)
- "You've never mentioned this before. What's changed?" (Notice the new signal)

**Why It Matters:**
- Reps are experts at surface-level answers
- Real coaching requires understanding what's underneath
- Listening catches problems early (before they become crises)

### 3. **The Coaching Question Framework**

Coaching is not advice. Coaching is questions that help someone see what they already know.

**The Coaching Sequence:**

**Situation:** "You're at 18% close rate; you were at 22% last month. What's different?"

**Follow-Up:** "Walk me through a deal that didn't close last week. What happened?"

**Reflection:** "What do you see when you look at that conversation? What could have gone differently?"

**Ownership:** "If you were coaching someone else through that, what would you tell them?"

**Action:** "What's one thing you could do differently on your next call?"

**Follow-Through:** "Let's revisit this next week and see if that shift helped."

**Why It Works:**
- You're not telling them they're doing it wrong
- They're seeing it themselves
- They own the solution (not you)
- They're more likely to implement it

### 4. **The Emotional Dump Boundary**

Reps will dump personal stuff on you. It's a sign of trust. But you're not their therapist.

**Healthy Dump:**
- "I'm dealing with some personal stuff that's affecting my energy right now."
- "I've been anxious about my close rate."
- "I'm not sure I'm good at this; I'm frustrated with myself."
- You: Listen. Validate. Offer support. Redirect to what's in their control.

**Unhealthy Dump:**
- "My wife and I are fighting; I don't know what to do with my life."
- "I think I have depression; I can't function."
- "I'm being abused at home."
- You: Listen. Validate. Suggest professional help. Support them. But this is beyond your scope.

**Your Boundary:**
- "I'm here to help you succeed in this role and work through professional obstacles. For the personal stuff that's really heavy, I think talking to someone trained in that is the move. I support you doing that, and I'm also here for the work side of this."

**Why It Matters:**
- You're a leader, not a therapist
- Clear boundaries actually deepen trust (you're honest about limitations)
- It frees reps to get real help
- It protects you from emotional burnout

### 5. **The Accountability Framework**

Accountability without shame = reps owning performance.

**What Accountability Sounds Like:**

"You're at 60% of quota. Here's where we expected you to be. Here's what the data shows. I think the issue is discovery—you're qualifying too early, so deals die in the follow-up. Here's what I'm seeing. What's your take? Do you agree? If you do, here's how we fix it..."

**What Shame Looks Like:**

"You're 40% behind quota. I don't know what's going on. You should be crushing this. Are you even trying?"

**The Difference:**
- Accountability: Data + diagnosis + problem-solve together
- Shame: Blame + judgment + you-figure-it-out

**Why It Matters:**
- Accountability builds ownership
- Shame builds resentment and defensiveness
- Reps want to improve; they don't want to be made to feel small

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: The Safety Foundation (14 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand why safety matters in 1:1s
- Teach them how to build it
- Show the cost of skipping it

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Reps will only be honest if they feel safe
- Safety is built through consistency, listening, and respect
- You can't coach someone who doesn't trust you
- Vulnerability requires some vulnerability from you too

**The Safety Checklist:**
- Same time every week? ✓
- You prepare for it? ✓
- You follow through on what you say? ✓
- You listen more than talk? ✓
- You're curious, not judgmental? ✓

**Real Example:**
- Andrew's 1:1 with Sally: Month 1, Sally gave surface answers, didn't bring real obstacles
- Andrew realized he was interrogating, not listening
- He shifted: "I noticed you didn't mention the leads we talked about last week. What's going on?"
- Sally opened up: "I'm not sure I'm good at this. I feel lost."
- Once he created safety, real coaching happened

**Business Impact:**
- Reps bring real obstacles early
- You catch problems before they're crises
- Team retention improves

**Tension to Address:**
- "Doesn't too much safety mean no accountability?" (No, accountability happens in safe space.)
- "How do I create safety if I'm their boss?" (You create it by being consistent, curious, and humble.)

### Sub-Module 2: Listening Like It Matters (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach managers to listen for what's said AND unsaid
- Show how listening reveals problems
- Help them spot early signals

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Most new managers listen for status, not for signals
- The gap between "I'm good" and "I'm struggling" is in listening
- Tone, energy, and silence are data
- Changes from last week are signals
- Your job is to notice and ask

**Listening Signals to Watch:**
- Rep who usually talks about pipeline doesn't mention it (something's wrong)
- Rep's energy flat (morale issue, or personal stuff, or losing confidence)
- Rep complaining less (trying to hide, or genuinely improving—ask to know)
- Rep's close rate narrative shifting (gaining clarity or losing it?)

**The Listening Question:**
"You seem different today. What's up?" (This simple question opens doors if you ask with genuine curiosity.)

**Real Example:**
- Marcus usually complained about leads; one week he didn't
- Andrew noticed and asked: "The leads haven't been mentioned. What's changed?"
- Marcus revealed: "I realized I was qualifying wrong. I'm not going to blame the leads anymore."
- Without noticing the gap, Andrew would have missed the growth moment

**Business Impact:**
- Early signal detection
- Reps feel seen
- Problems surface before they're crises

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't asking about everything intrusive?" (No, genuine curiosity is care. Trust the intent.)
- "What if they don't want to talk?" (That's data too. "OK, I'm here if you need me." Respect the boundary.)

### Sub-Module 3: The Coaching Architecture (15 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach the sequence of coaching questions
- Show how to move from situation to action
- Help managers get comfortable asking instead of telling

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Coaching is a sequence, not a single conversation
- You start by understanding the situation
- You help them see what they're doing
- You help them own the solution
- You follow up

**The Coaching Conversation:**

1. **Understand:** "Walk me through what happened with that deal."
2. **Reflect:** "What do you see when you look at that conversation?"
3. **Connect:** "What's the pattern? Is this the first time or have you seen this before?"
4. **Own:** "What would you do differently if you were coaching someone else through this?"
5. **Action:** "What's your play?"
6. **Commit:** "When will we check on this?"

**Common Mistakes:**
- Jumping to advice (rep doesn't own it)
- Asking rhetorical questions (reps feel manipulated)
- Coaching everything (pick ONE thing per 1:1)
- Not following up (reps don't believe you care)

**Real Example:**
- Sarah's close rate dropped; Andrew could have said, "You need to ask discovery questions."
- Instead: "Tell me about a call where the prospect said no." She walked through it. "What happened in that conversation?" She saw she didn't dig on their goals. "What would you do differently?" She said, "I'd ask more about what success looks like for them." "Try that on your next call." Next week, close rate improved.
- She figured it out; she owned it; she implemented it

**Business Impact:**
- Reps feel capable
- Solutions stick because they own them
- You're building their coaching muscle, not creating dependency

**Tension to Address:**
- "Doesn't coaching take longer than just telling them?" (Yes, but they keep the insight. Telling doesn't stick.)
- "What if they don't see the problem?" (Then you have more work to do. But they will eventually see it if you ask patiently.)

### Sub-Module 4: Emotional Dumps & Boundaries (12 min)
**Objectives:**
- Prepare managers for personal stuff coming up in 1:1s
- Teach them how to respond with empathy and boundaries
- Help them stay in their lane

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Reps are people; stuff happens
- Acknowledging it builds trust
- Getting tangled in it burns you out
- Clear boundaries actually deepen respect

**The Boundary Conversation:**
"I'm here to support you. And I also want to be honest: for the deeper personal stuff, talking to a therapist or counselor is probably the move. I can support you doing that, and I'm also here for the work side of things."

**What to Do:**
- Listen without judgment
- Validate their experience
- Ask: "Is this affecting your work right now? How?"
- Offer practical work support
- Suggest professional support if needed
- Follow up on how they're doing

**What NOT to Do:**
- Pretend to be a therapist
- Try to solve their personal problems
- Get overly involved in their personal drama
- Use it against them later ("Remember when you said...")

**Real Examples:**
- Rep dealing with divorce; affecting focus; you listen, validate, offer flexibility, suggest counselor
- Rep with anxiety; affecting confidence; you listen, validate, coach them on what they can control, suggest professional help
- Rep grieving a loss; affecting energy; you listen, give them space, check in

**Business Impact:**
- Reps feel supported
- You stay in your lane
- Trust deepens without you burning out

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they think I don't care if I set a boundary?" (Trust that clear boundaries show strength, not coldness.)
- "What if it's serious?" (Validate it, suggest help, follow up. You're not responsible for fixing it.)

### Sub-Module 5: Accountability Without Shame (12 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach how to have hard conversations in 1:1s
- Show the difference between accountability and shame
- Help managers address performance without damaging trust

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Performance gaps are data, not moral failures
- Accountability is shared problem-solving
- Shame shuts people down
- Being direct about the gap is kind; softening it is not

**The Accountability Conversation:**

"You're at 60% of quota. Here's where we need to be. I think the issue is discovery—your calls are focused on product, but they should be about their business. That's why deals go silent. Here's what I see. What's your take?"

**Why It Works:**
- You're stating the data (not emotional)
- You're diagnosing (showing you're thinking)
- You're inviting their perspective (showing respect)
- You're not shaming (showing humanity)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Softening the message ("You're doing OK, but...")
- Being vague ("You need to do better")
- Blaming ("You're not hustling enough")
- Lecturing ("Here's what you should do...")

**Real Example:**
- Jamie's close rate 18%, supposed to be 25%
- Andrew: "Your close rate is 18%. I need it at 25%. I think it's because you're moving deals too fast without understanding their process. Here's what I see. What's your take?"
- Jamie: "Yeah, I'm pushing too hard because I'm nervous."
- Andrew: "OK, so nerves are making you move faster. What if we slowed down the discovery phase? What would that look like?"
- Jamie: "I'd ask more questions and listen more."
- Andrew: "Try that on your next 3 calls and tell me how it goes."
- Result: Jamie closed 28% next month

**Business Impact:**
- Reps own performance gaps
- Solutions are sustainable
- Trust stays intact even during hard conversations

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't being direct harsh?" (No, being vague is worse. Clear is kind.)
- "What if they get defensive?" (That's their reaction to the data. You stay calm and data-focused.)

## Key Tensions to Address

- **"How much safety is too much safety?"** → Safety enables accountability; they're not opposites.
- **"Isn't listening for what's unsaid kind of reading their mind?"** → No, it's noticing patterns and asking. That's respect.
- **"Does coaching take forever?"** → It's slower than telling, but it sticks. Time investment is worth it.
- **"What if they never open up?"** → That's a trust issue. Keep building safety. It takes time.

---

## Tone & Voice Guidance

- **Warm but Direct:** 1:1s are intimate; they matter. Be real.
- **Practical:** Give them scripts and sequences they can use.
- **Vulnerable:** Share times you got this wrong; times you fixed it.
- **Respectful:** Honor the difficulty of being real in a power dynamic.
