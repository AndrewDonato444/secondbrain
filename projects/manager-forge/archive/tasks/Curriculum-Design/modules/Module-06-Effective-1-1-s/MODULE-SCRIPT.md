# MODULE 6: EFFECTIVE 1:1s — INSTRUCTOR SCRIPT
**Duration:** 66 minutes across 5 sub-modules  
**Audience:** New managers in luxury real estate  
**Core Thesis:** 1:1s are not status meetings. They are the foundation of trust, coaching, and performance. When done right, reps open up. When done wrong, managers miss everything.

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE SAFETY FOUNDATION (14 minutes)

### OPENING HOOK (0:00–1:30)

**[SCRIPT]**

"I want to start with a story. Andrew runs a team of six agents. He was frustrated because his people weren't bringing him real problems. They'd come to 1:1s, say 'Everything's fine,' give surface answers to his questions, and leave. He thought the problem was his reps. He thought they weren't coachable.

He was wrong.

The real problem was that his reps didn't feel safe.

One day, Andrew had a 1:1 with Sally, one of his top agents. She'd been slipping the last month—close rate dropping, fewer conversations booked. Andrew asked her what was going on. Sally said, 'I'm fine. Just a slow month.' But Andrew noticed something in her voice. A flatness. So he did something different. He stopped asking questions. He just listened. After about 30 seconds of silence, Sally's eyes got wet. And then she told him the truth: She was scared. Her mom had been diagnosed with cancer. She wasn't sleeping. She felt like she was failing her family and her team.

That conversation changed everything. Not because Andrew fixed her cancer or her family situation. But because Sally felt seen. Safe. Respected. And once she felt that, she became coachable again.

Your reps won't bring you their real obstacles until they believe it's safe to do so. And safety isn't about being their friend. It's about consistency, listening, and respect."

**[TALKING POINTS]**

- **Safety is the foundation of coaching.** Without it, reps give surface answers. With it, they open up.
- **Safety isn't about being liked.** It's about being predictable, respectful, and genuinely curious.
- **The Safety Pyramid** has five layers: consistency, listening, humanity, respectful feedback, coaching.
- **Most new managers skip layer 1** and go straight to fixing. Then wonder why reps hold back.

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (1:30–12:30)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Here's what most new managers miss: Building safety takes time. It's not a conversation. It's a pattern. Let me walk you through the five layers of the Safety Pyramid, because each one matters, and they build on each other.

**Layer 1: Consistency**

Consistency means your reps know when and where to find you, and they know you show up as the same person every week. If you schedule 1:1s and cancel half of them, your reps learn: This isn't safe. The manager doesn't really care. They stop bringing real stuff.

Consistency looks like:
- Same day, same time every week. Write it in your calendar. Treat it like a client meeting.
- Same location. Consistent environment reduces friction.
- You're actually present. Phone away. Calendar blocked. Eyes up.
- You show up with the same emotional tone. Not manic on Monday, withdrawn on Wednesday.

One manager I worked with was canceling 1:1s for 'urgent things' every other week. Her team thought the urgent things were always more important than them. So they stopped bringing important stuff to 1:1s. They just brought status. The moment she committed to protecting that time, things shifted.

**Layer 2: Listening**

Listening means your reps experience you hearing them. Not planning your response. Not waiting for your turn to talk. Actually hearing them.

Most managers think they listen. But they're actually interrogating. They ask questions, wait for answers, prepare the next question. That's interrogation. It feels like a court.

Real listening sounds like:
- 'Tell me more about that.'
- 'What happened next?'
- 'How did that land with you?'
- Long, comfortable silences while they think.
- You reflecting back what you heard: 'So it sounds like you felt blindsided in that listing meeting.'
- You noticing what's NOT being said: 'You usually mention the lead flow. You haven't this week. What's that about?'

Andrew realized he was interrogating Sally. So he stopped asking questions and just said: 'Talk me through how this month has felt.' And then he shut up. Sat with the silence. Sally filled it. And the truth came out.

**Layer 3: Humanity**

Humanity means your reps see you as a whole person, not just a manager. It doesn't mean oversharing. It means being real.

When was the last time you said in a 1:1, 'I had no idea how to do that when I started. I struggled for months'? Or 'I made a mistake on that deal. Here's what I learned'?

Vulnerability—the willingness to be real about your own learning curve and mistakes—opens the door. It says, 'It's safe to not be perfect here. It's safe to be human.'

One manager told her team: 'I realized I was so focused on hits, I wasn't coaching you. I was managing activity. That was my blind spot. I'm working on it.' Her team felt safer after that. Because she was honest. She wasn't pretending to be infallible.

**Layer 4: Respectful Feedback**

Respect means you give feedback in a way that dignifies the person and focuses on the behavior, not the character.

Bad feedback: 'You're lazy. You didn't prep for that listing.'

Good feedback: 'I noticed you didn't have comps pulled for that showing. What happened there? Help me understand.'

The difference is you're curious about the gap, not judgmental. You're giving them a chance to explain. You're treating them like a capable person who had reasons, not a lazy person who doesn't care.

And here's the thing: You deliver hard feedback to someone who feels safe, and they hear it as coaching. You deliver the same feedback to someone who doesn't feel safe, and they hear it as threat.

**Layer 5: Coaching**

Coaching is only possible once layers 1-4 are solid. Coaching means you ask questions that help them see what they need to see and solve their own problems.

We'll go deep on this in sub-module 3. But the principle is: You're not telling them what to do. You're helping them think. 'What do you think you could have done differently? What's one thing you'll try next week?'

This only works if they've felt the first four layers. Otherwise they think you're testing them or setting them up."

**[TALKING POINTS—Instructor Notes]**

- Pause after each layer. Ask learners: "Which of these layers is strongest for you? Which is your gap?"
- Safety isn't warm fuzziness. It's structural. Consistency + listening + humanity + respectful feedback + coaching.
- The pyramid is a sequence. You can't skip layer 2 and be strong on layer 4. You have to build.
- Consistency is the non-negotiable. If you don't protect the 1:1 time, nothing else matters.

### REAL EXAMPLE (12:30–14:00)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Let me tell you what this looked like for Marcus, one of Andrew's reps. Marcus had been with the team for eight months. He was decent on activity—probably 12-15 conversations a week—but his close rate was low, and he was always frustrated with the lead quality.

Every 1:1, Marcus complained. 'The leads are garbage. Nobody from this area actually buys.' Andrew had been responding by problem-solving. 'Well, maybe you need to shift your initial call approach' or 'Have you thought about working the past client list?'

But Marcus wasn't really listening. He was just venting. And Andrew realized: Marcus doesn't feel safe bringing me real stuff. He's just complaining because that's the safe play.

So Andrew shifted. He committed to showing up at the same time every week. He stopped taking notes and gave Marcus his full attention. And one week, Marcus came in and didn't mention leads for the first time in months. Andrew just noticed. 'Hey, you haven't mentioned leads today. What's different?'

Marcus paused. Then said, 'I figured something out.' And he explained: He'd been calling people with the wrong pre-qualification question. He was asking if they were thinking of selling in the next year. But the leads were investors. They didn't want to sell—they wanted to build. He was disqualifying his own buyer base.

This was Marcus's insight. Not Andrew's. But Andrew only heard it because Marcus felt safe to bring it. And it took consistency, listening, and respect to get there. That's the safety foundation."

## SUB-MODULE 2: LISTENING LIKE IT MATTERS (13 minutes)

### OPENING HOOK (15:00–16:15)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Most managers think they listen. But they're listening for information they can act on. They're listening for problems they can solve. They're not listening for what's actually happening underneath.

Real listening is different. It's listening for the signals. What's said. How it's said. What's not said. What changed from last week.

Sarah runs a team. One of her reps, Jamie, came to a 1:1 seeming fine—smiling, engaged, talking about his deals. But Sarah noticed something: He usually asks her about her deals at the end of the 1:1. This week, he didn't. So she noticed the gap and asked, 'Hey, what's going on with you outside of work?'

Jamie hesitated. Then admitted: His wife was pregnant. They weren't ready. He was scared. He hadn't told anyone on the team yet. But that gap—the missing question—was the signal. Sarah listened for what wasn't there. And because she did, she could actually help."

**[TALKING POINTS]**

- **Listening is a skill, not a personality trait.** Even people who think they're bad listeners can get better.
- **You listen for four things:** What's said + how it's said + what's NOT said + what changed from last week.
- **Most managers listen for the problem, not the person.** Flip that.
- **Listening changes the entire conversation.** When reps feel heard, they open up more.

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (16:15–27:15)

**[SCRIPT]**

"The Listening Framework has four channels. You need to develop all four. Most managers only use one.

**Channel 1: What's Said (The Content)**

This is the easiest channel. It's the actual words coming out of their mouth. They tell you about a deal that fell through, a client who ghosted, a frustrating conversation they had.

Most managers stop here. They hear the content and jump to solution mode. 'Well, here's what you should have done different.'

But that's too fast. Content without context is just information. You need the other channels.

**Channel 2: How It's Said (The Tone)**

This is where listening gets interesting. The same words said in different tones mean completely different things.

'This deal fell through' said with shrug and laugh? That's different than 'This deal fell through' said flat and defeated.

Jamie said, 'Work's been fine, really busy,' but his tone was dull. Dead. Sarah noticed. She didn't just hear the content 'I'm busy.' She heard the how: 'I'm overwhelmed and discouraged.' Those are different problems.

Most managers miss tone because they're looking at their notes or thinking about the next question. So here's the practice: Put the pen down. Look at them. Actually watch their face while they talk. Notice:

- Is their energy higher or lower than normal?
- Are they avoiding eye contact?
- Is their voice tight or relaxed?
- Are they fidgeting?
- Is their pace faster or slower?

Tone tells you what's really happening. The content is the story they're telling. The tone is the truth underneath.

**Channel 3: What's Not Said (The Gaps)**

This is the most powerful channel for coaches. What's missing tells you what's really on their mind.

Marcus usually complained about leads. When he didn't mention leads, that was data. Something was different. That gap was the signal.

With Jamie, the missing question was the signal. With Sally, the absence of her normal energy was the signal.

Here's how you listen for gaps: You build a baseline. You know how your rep normally shows up. What they normally talk about. Their normal pace and energy. Then you notice when that shifts.

You do this by paying attention week to week. 'Last week you mentioned the lead follow-up system. This week you haven't. What's that about?'

'You usually ask me how my family's doing. You didn't today. Everything okay?'

'Your close rate is 28%. That's great. But it's down from your normal 31%. I noticed. Help me understand what's different.'

Gaps are whispers. They're signals that something shifted. And most managers miss them because they're not paying attention to the pattern. They're just listening to this week's content in isolation.

**Channel 4: What Changed from Last Week (The Trajectory)**

This is listening for narrative. It's listening for how someone's been trending over time.

Sarah noticed with Jamie that week over week, he was gradually becoming quieter. His energy was steady but lowering. He was less engaged. That trajectory—the downward slope—was more important than any single thing he said.

Same with Sally. Month 1 with Andrew: calm, steady, normal. Week 3: still normal. Week 4: slight flatness in tone. Week 5: missing some energy. By week 6 she was clearly struggling. The trajectory was gradual. But if you're listening week to week, you see it.

This is why consistency matters. If you only see your rep every other week, or if you're not paying attention, you miss the trajectory. You miss 'something is slowly changing in this person.'

So put it together:

What's said: 'Things are fine. I'm busy.'
How it's said: Flat, discouraged tone.
What's not said: Missing the usual questions back to me. Missing his normal energy.
What changed: Week over week, becoming quieter.

All four channels say: Something is going on. This person needs to be asked. And they're probably going to say 'I'm fine' the first time. But now you know to listen harder."

**[TALKING POINTS—Instructor Notes]**

- Pause and ask learners: "Tell me about a rep who's acting different lately. What are the signals you're seeing? Check all four channels."
- This is about pattern recognition. Build a baseline for each person. Then notice shifts.
- The most important signal is usually what's missing, not what's there.
- Listening like this feels weird at first. You'll feel like you're eavesdropping. You're not. You're coaching.

---

### REAL EXAMPLE (27:15–28:45)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Sarah's rep, Jamie, is a good example of listening to trajectory. Jamie started strong with the team. High energy, asks good questions, always thinking ahead. After a few months, Sarah noticed a slow shift. Nothing dramatic. Just:

- His questions got shorter.
- He stopped staying late.
- His jokes became less frequent.
- He was hitting activity numbers but with less enthusiasm.
- His deal flow was steady, but he wasn't as excited about his pipeline.

These are small signals. Individually, they could mean anything. But together, they painted a picture: Jamie was disengaging. Not screaming it. Just quietly fading.

When Sarah asked him about it, Jamie said, 'I'm fine.' But she listened for all four channels:

Content: 'I'm fine, just refocusing.'
Tone: Uncertain, like he was testing her.
What's not said: Didn't ask about her family or upcoming events, which he normally does.
Trajectory: Continuing the slow fade from the last three weeks.

So Sarah didn't drop it. She said, 'I've noticed over the last few weeks, your energy feels different. I'm wondering what's happening. And I want to know if it's something I'm doing, something with the job, or something personal.'

That question—asked with genuine curiosity, not judgment—opened the door. Jamie told her: He'd gotten his wife pregnant and they were figuring out if they could afford for her to stay home. He was terrified. He'd been withdrawn because he was trying to figure out finances, not because anything was wrong with work.

Sarah only knew to ask because she was listening on all four channels. And because she did, she could actually support him. They mapped out a financial plan. He'd stay on the team. He'd be fine. But if Sarah had just heard content ('I'm fine'), she'd have missed all of it."

---

### TENSION POINT (28:45–29:35)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Someone's probably thinking: 'Isn't this a lot of pressure? I'm supposed to notice everything about every person?'

No. You're not supposed to be psychic. You're supposed to pay attention. These are your people. You meet with them every week. You know them. You just need to listen across all four channels instead of just hearing content.

And here's what's useful to know: The signals are usually loud to someone who's listening. You don't need to be a therapist to notice that Jamie's energy dropped. You don't need to be psychic to notice Marcus didn't mention leads.

The signals are there. Most managers just aren't listening for them.

Also: This doesn't mean you solve everything you hear. You listen, you ask, you understand. Then you help where you can—usually that's at the work level. Personal stuff? You listen, validate, offer work support, and suggest professional help if that's what they need. But that's sub-module 4. Stay tuned."

---

### CLOSING SUMMARY (29:35–29:50)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Listen for what's said. Listen for how it's said. Listen for what's not said. Notice what changed from last week. This is the Listening Framework. This is how you catch signals. And signals are how you know what someone actually needs.

Next, we're going to learn the structure for how to respond to those signals. That's the Coaching Architecture. Let's go."

## SUB-MODULE 3: THE COACHING ARCHITECTURE (15 minutes)

### OPENING HOOK (29:50–31:15)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Andrew had a rep named Sarah. Great energy, good work ethic, but her close rate was dropping. She'd been at 38% close rate. Now she was at 32%. It was a real dip.

Andrew's instinct was to fix it. To tell her what to do. 'I think you need to focus on asking about their goals earlier in the conversation. That'll help you get more commitments.'

But Sarah shut down. She said, 'I know I should be better. I know I'm slipping. I don't need you to tell me what to do. I need to figure it out.'

Sarah was right. Andrew told her the answer. She didn't own it. So she didn't implement it.

Here's what Andrew did instead: He asked her to think through what happened on her recent showings. He asked her what she thought was different. He asked her what she noticed. And Sarah—through asking and reflection—realized: She wasn't asking buyers about their actual goals. She was assuming. That was the blind spot.

Once Sarah identified it, she owned it. She changed her approach. Her close rate went back to 38%.

The difference? Coaching isn't giving advice. Coaching is asking questions that help someone think. And when they do the thinking, they do the changing.

Here's the Coaching Architecture. It's a six-step sequence. This is how you coach someone who's identified a gap."

**[TALKING POINTS]**

- **Coaching is about helping them think, not telling them what to do.**
- **There are six steps in the sequence, and they matter.** Skip one and the coaching falls apart.
- **The sequence is: Understand → Reflect → Connect → Own → Action → Follow Up.**
- **This is how you turn a performance gap into owned change.**

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (31:15–44:00)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Here's the six-step Coaching Sequence. I'm going to walk through each one, then we'll look at a full conversation example.

**Step 1: Understand the Situation**

Before you can coach, you have to understand what actually happened. Not your interpretation. The actual situation.

This sounds like:
- 'Walk me through that showing. What happened?'
- 'Tell me about that conversation. Start to finish.'
- 'Help me understand what was going on in that listing appointment.'

You're asking them to give you the play-by-play. Not the headline. The full story.

Most managers skip this. They think they already know what happened. They heard the headline—'My close rate dropped'—and they jump to the fix. But you can't coach what you don't understand.

With Sarah, Andrew said, 'Tell me about your last three showings. Walk me through them.' Sarah described each one. And as she was describing, she started noticing her own pattern. She was assuming buyer goals instead of asking. Andrew didn't tell her that. She saw it in the telling.

**Step 2: Reflect What You Heard**

Once you've understood, you feed back what you heard. You reflect it. This does two things:

1. It confirms you actually understand (instead of projecting your interpretation onto their situation).
2. It gives them a chance to hear their own story from the outside. That's often where insight lives.

This sounds like:
- 'So it sounds like you went into those three showings with a pretty specific idea of what the buyers wanted, and you didn't actually confirm that with them first. Is that right?'
- 'What I'm hearing is that you have a really clear vision for the property, but the buyers had a different vision. And you didn't surface their vision early. That fair?'

You're not being clever or implying criticism. You're just reflecting back the pattern you're hearing.

With Sarah, Andrew said, 'I'm hearing that in all three of those showings, you had an idea about what they wanted—rental income, or primary residence—and you were leading the conversation toward that instead of asking them first. Does that land?'

Sarah said, 'Oh my god. Yeah. That's exactly what I'm doing.' She saw it when she heard it reflected.

**Step 3: Connect to Pattern**

Now you help them see this isn't an accident. It's a pattern. This is where the real insight lives. Because if someone thinks it's a one-time mistake, they won't change. But if they see it's a pattern in how they approach things, that's different.

This sounds like:
- 'I'm noticing this is a pattern for you. When is this pattern showing up? Where else are you doing this?'
- 'This isn't just these three showings, is it? When else do you catch yourself assuming instead of asking?'
- 'I wonder if this pattern shows up in your follow-up too. Do you follow up with your assumptions, or do you follow up with questions?'

You're helping them see: This isn't a mistake. This is a habit. That's important to see, because habits require different work than accidents.

Andrew asked Sarah, 'Is this something you do in other parts of your business too? Follow-ups, qualification calls, listing presentations?' Sarah thought about it. 'Yeah. I'm pretty opinionated. I probably lead people toward my ideas instead of understanding theirs first.' That's a pattern. That's a blind spot she actually has.

**Step 4: Own the Problem**

This is crucial. Before you talk about the fix, they have to own the problem. They have to say, 'This is my thing. This is the gap I need to close.'

Because if you own it for them, they won't change it. They'll think you've identified their problem and assigned them the fix. That's not ownership. That's assignment.

This sounds like:
- 'What do you think this blind spot is costing you? What does this pattern cost?'
- 'How do you feel about this? What do you want to do about it?'
- 'If you keep doing this, where does that take you? What's the downside?'

You're asking them to connect the dots between their behavior and their results. Once they feel the consequence, ownership follows.

Andrew asked Sarah, 'What do you think this is costing you? Like, if you keep assuming what buyers want instead of asking them, what happens to your business?' Sarah said, 'I lose deals. I'm literally leaving money on the table because I'm not understanding what people actually need.' There's ownership. She feels the cost.

**Step 5: Action and Commitment**

Once someone owns the problem, they're ready to commit to action. Real action. Specific action. Not vague, but behavioral.

This sounds like:
- 'What's one thing you're going to do differently in your next showing?'
- 'How will you catch yourself when you're assuming? What's your trigger?'
- 'Tell me specifically what you'll do in that first 10 minutes with the next buyer.'

The action needs to be something they can do this week. Not a big overhaul. One thing. One shift. Because small, owned changes are more powerful than big, assigned ones.

Andrew said, 'In your next showing, what's one thing you're going to do differently?' Sarah said, 'I'm going to ask them about their timeline, their budget, and their goals before I show them anything. I'm going to understand them first.'

That's specific. That's testable. That's something she can do Monday.

**Step 6: Follow Up**

This is the step most managers skip. And it's crucial. Because follow-up is where you demonstrate: This matters. I care about your growth. I'm tracking what you committed to.

Follow-up looks like:
- 'How did that go? How did it feel to ask them first instead of assuming?'
- 'Did you catch yourself when you were about to assume? What happened?'
- 'What did you learn from doing it that way?'

Follow-up is also where you celebrate. Because if Sarah changed her approach and it worked, you want her to feel that win. She earned it. Coaching is honoring earned wins.

The week after her commitment, Andrew asked Sarah, 'How did it go with those showings?' Sarah said, 'I did it. I asked them first. And three out of four said yes to a second showing. That's way higher than normal.' Andrew said, 'That's real. You changed your approach and you got a different result. What did you learn?'

That cycle—commit, do it, see the result, reflect on the learning—is what makes someone own change."

**[TALKING POINTS—Instructor Notes]**

- Go through the six-step sequence slowly. This is foundational.
- The sequence is: Understand → Reflect → Connect → Own → Action → Follow Up. Don't skip any.
- Most managers shortcut this. They skip Understand, go straight to Fix. That's why it doesn't work.
- Practice this sequence with one rep this week. Feel how it works.

### FULL CONVERSATION EXAMPLE (44:00–45:30)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Let me give you a full conversation so you see how this flows. This is Andrew coaching Jamie on performance.

**Context:** Jamie is at 60% quota, close rate 18%. That's significantly below where he needs to be.

**Andrew:** 'Jamie, I want to talk about your numbers. You're at 60% of quota, close rate 18%. I've noticed this trend. Walk me through what you think is happening.'

**Jamie:** 'I know, I know. I'm trying hard. The market's slow.'

**Andrew:** 'I believe you're trying. Help me understand: What's your typical deal progression like right now? Give me an example.'

**Jamie:** 'Well, I'll get a buyer inquiry. I schedule a showing for the next day. We show them properties. If they like something, I send them info and follow up.'

**Andrew:** 'How long is the follow-up usually? Like, how many conversations?'

**Jamie:** 'Usually just a couple. I send them stuff and then I follow up by phone. If they're not ready, they're not ready.'

*[Andrew reflects what he heard]*

**Andrew:** 'Okay, so I'm hearing: You get the inquiry, you move pretty quickly to showings, and then you do a follow-up or two. But it sounds like the time between the showing and the follow-up might be short. And the number of conversations after the showing might be short. Is that fair?'

**Jamie:** 'Yeah, I guess. I don't want to be pushy.'

*[Andrew connects to pattern]*

**Andrew:** 'I wonder if this is a pattern for you. You mentioned not wanting to be pushy. How often are you not following up enough because you're worried about being pushy?'

**Jamie:** 'Probably a lot, to be honest. I'd rather lose a deal than bug somebody.'

*[Andrew helps Jamie own it]*

**Andrew:** 'I get that. Nobody wants to feel like a pushy salesman. But here's what I'm seeing: Your close rate is 18%. That means 82 out of every 100 conversations don't close. That's a lot of people who could have been clients but they're not. What do you think that's costing you?'

**Jamie:** 'Yeah. It's definitely costing me. I mean, my paycheck's smaller. And I'm frustrated because I know these people could have bought.'

**Andrew:** 'Right. So if you keep being afraid to follow up, what happens?'

**Jamie:** 'I keep missing deals. I keep being at 60% of quota.'

*[Andrew gets commitment to action]*

**Andrew:** 'What if we reframe this. Following up isn't pushy if you're adding value. You're not following up to be annoying. You're following up because you found something that matches what they said they wanted. Or you have new info about the market. That's you being their agent, not being pushy. What would it look like if you followed up two more times, but you had a reason each time?'

**Jamie:** 'So like, not just 'Are you interested?' but like 'I found another property that matches what you said you wanted'?'

**Andrew:** 'Exactly. What's one reason you could follow up this week?'

**Jamie:** 'I have a buyer who wanted a property with a view and under $800k. I found something this morning. I could send it to them and suggest a call.'

**Andrew:** 'Do it. That's your action. Send them that property, tell them why you think it matches, and set a specific time for a call. Not 'call me when you want.' Specific time.'

**Jamie:** 'Okay. I'll do it tomorrow.'

*[Andrew commits to follow up]*

**Andrew:** 'Perfect. Let's talk about this next week. I want to hear how it went. How many of those targeted follow-ups you did, and what the response was.'

---

That's the full sequence. Notice: Andrew didn't tell Jamie 'You're not following up enough.' Jamie figured it out. Andrew asked questions that helped Jamie think. And then Jamie committed to specific action. That's coaching."

### TENSION POINT (45:30–46:15)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Here's what people ask: 'This seems slow. Isn't there a faster way?'

There might be faster ways. You could tell Jamie what to do and he'd probably do some of it. But he wouldn't own it. He'd see it as your idea. The moment something gets hard, he'd stop.

When someone owns the solution—when they've thought it through and committed—they implement it even when it's hard.

Also: This six-step sequence is what you do when someone has a real coaching gap. Not every 1:1. Some 1:1s are check-ins. Some are celebrations. Some are status updates. The Coaching Architecture is for when there's a real gap to close or a blind spot to uncover.

And here's the last thing: Coaching takes time. Real coaching. But the alternative is managing activity forever and never actually developing people. I'd rather spend 20 minutes doing real coaching than 20 hours managing around a gap."

### CLOSING SUMMARY (46:15–46:30)

**[SCRIPT]**

## SUB-MODULE 4: EMOTIONAL DUMPS & BOUNDARIES (12 minutes)

### OPENING HOOK (46:30–47:45)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Andrew's rep, Tommy, came to a 1:1 on a Tuesday. About five minutes in, Tommy started talking about his marriage. How it wasn't working. How his wife felt like he didn't care about her anymore. How he was thinking about therapy.

Andrew didn't know what to do. He's a manager. He's not a therapist. He listened for a bit, then said something like, 'I'm sorry you're going through that. That's tough.'

Tommy kept talking. Twenty minutes later, Andrew was still sitting there, listening to marriage problems, and he felt completely out of his lane.

Here's what Andrew did wrong: He didn't set a boundary. He didn't validate and redirect. He just listened until the time ran out.

Here's what he should have done: Listened, validated Tommy's feelings, offered work support, and suggested professional help.

Boundaries aren't cruel. They're respectful. Boundaries say: I care about you as a person. And also, I'm not the right person for this. Let me help you find the right person.

This is sub-module 4. This is about emotional dumps and how to handle them with grace and clarity."

**[TALKING POINTS]**

- **Emotional stuff will come up in 1:1s.** That's normal. You're building safety.
- **Your job is to listen, validate, and redirect.** Not to fix or become their therapist.
- **Boundaries are about knowing your lane.** You're their manager. You can support their work performance. You can't fix their personal life.
- **Setting a boundary is an act of respect.** It says, 'You deserve better than what I can offer on this.'

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (47:45–57:00)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Here's the reality: When you build safety, personal stuff will come up. That's not a sign you did something wrong. That's a sign you did something right. People feel safe. So they bring their real stuff.

Your job is to navigate that with grace. And that takes a boundary.

**The Boundary Sequence has four parts:**

1. **Listen** (really listen)
2. **Validate** (let them know you heard them)
3. **Offer work support** (if there's a work angle)
4. **Suggest professional help** (if it's personal)

Let me walk through each one.

**Part 1: Listen**

When someone brings personal stuff, your first job is just to listen. Don't interrupt. Don't try to fix it. Don't problem-solve. Just listen.

Tommy told Andrew about his marriage. Andrew should have just listened. Not offered advice. Not said, 'Have you tried couples therapy?' Just listened.

Listening sounds like:
- Silence. Comfortable silence while they talk.
- 'Tell me more.'
- 'How long has this been going on?'
- 'What's that been like for you?'

The listening part usually takes 5-10 minutes. Let it. This is the person feeling heard. That matters.

**Part 2: Validate**

After you listen, you validate. You acknowledge what they're experiencing. You don't have to agree with their choices or fix their problem. You just acknowledge their experience is real and it's hard.

Validation sounds like:
- 'That sounds really hard.'
- 'I can see why that would be stressful.'
- 'That's a lot to carry.'
- 'I appreciate you trusting me with that.'

What validation does NOT sound like:
- 'Well, have you tried...?' (This is advice.)
- 'That's not so bad. You'll figure it out.' (This is dismissal.)
- 'I know exactly how you feel. Let me tell you my story.' (This is making it about you.)

With Tommy, Andrew should have said: 'That sounds really hard. Feeling disconnected from your spouse while you're also working hard here—that's a lot to carry.'

That's validation. Andrew heard. Andrew understood. It's hard. Next.

**Part 3: Offer Work Support**

Now, here's where you can actually help: Are there work things that would make this person's life easier?

- Can they adjust their schedule?
- Do they need flexibility for counseling appointments?
- Can they take a personal day?
- Do they need anything work-wise to reduce stress?

This is the lane where you can help. You're not fixing their marriage. You're not going to couple's counseling with them. But you can say: 'Is there anything work-wise that would make this less hard?'

With Tommy, Andrew could have said: 'I can't help fix the marriage stuff, but I can help with work. Do you need any flexibility? A day off? Anything that would reduce stress on this side?'

Maybe Tommy needs to adjust his schedule for a couple weeks. Maybe he needs to know his job is secure while he works through this. That's work support.

**Part 4: Suggest Professional Help**

Here's where you set the boundary. And here's the language:

'Tommy, I'm glad you felt comfortable bringing this to me. I want to be honest: What you're dealing with is bigger than what I can help with as your manager. You need someone who specializes in this. I'd really encourage you to get support from a therapist or counselor. That's the kind of help this deserves.'

Notice what this does:
- It honors that he trusted you.
- It's honest about your limits.
- It validates that this deserves real support.
- It redirects him to the right help.

This is not cold. This is not dismissive. This is respectful.

And here's what Tommy gets: He knows Andrew cares. He knows Andrew has limits. And he knows he needs real help. That's all true.

**Putting It Together**

With Tommy, it looks like this:

**Tommy:** 'My marriage is falling apart. I don't know what to do.'

**Andrew:** [Listens for a few minutes. Lets Tommy talk.]

**Andrew:** 'Tommy, I can see this is really hard. Feeling like your marriage isn't working—that's painful.'

**Tommy:** 'Yeah. I don't know how to fix it.'

**Andrew:** 'I want to be straight with you: What you're dealing with is too big for me to help with. You need a professional—a therapist or counselor. Someone who can actually help you work through this. I'd really encourage you to find someone. And from a work side, is there anything I can do to make things easier while you're dealing with this?'

**Tommy:** 'I might need a couple afternoons a week for appointments.'

**Andrew:** 'Okay, let's figure that out. But the real work here—getting help for your marriage—that's on you. And it's worth doing. Does that make sense?'

**Tommy:** 'Yeah. I know I need help. I've just been putting it off.'

**Andrew:** 'Then this is the sign. Go get help. I'm here for you on the work side. But this deserves more than I can give.'

That's the full boundary. Andrew heard Tommy. Andrew cared. Andrew also respected his limits. And Andrew pointed Tommy toward real help. That's how you handle emotional dumps with grace."

**[TALKING POINTS—Instructor Notes]**

- The boundary isn't 'Don't tell me personal stuff.' The boundary is 'I listen and I care, and I'm also honest about what I can help with.'
- Most managers either become the therapist (which is unsustainable) or shut people down (which breaks safety). The boundary sequence finds the middle.
- Boundaries are not meant to make someone feel unheard. They're meant to make sure they get real help.
- Practice saying: 'That's beyond my lane. I want you to get real support.'

## SUB-MODULE 5: ACCOUNTABILITY WITHOUT SHAME (12 minutes)

### OPENING HOOK (59:30–60:45)

**[SCRIPT]**

"Andrew had to have a hard conversation with Jamie. Jamie's numbers: 60% of quota, close rate 18%. That's not sustainable. Something has to change.

Andrew's instinct was to either:

A) Soften it. 'Hey, I know it's been slow. Market's tough.'
B) Shame it. 'Jamie, your numbers are terrible. This is unacceptable.'

Neither one works. Softening it means Jamie doesn't feel the urgency. Shaming it means Jamie gets defensive or demoralized.

Here's what Andrew did instead: He came with data, a diagnosis, and a problem-solving frame. Not blame. Not shame. Not softening. Clarity.

'Jamie, let's look at your numbers. You're at 60% of quota, close rate 18%. Here's what I'm seeing: You're getting conversations, but you're not following up enough. I think you might be afraid of being pushy, and that fear is costing you deals. I think we can fix this. But we have to fix it together. What do you think?'

That's accountability without shame. That's how you have the hard conversation."

**[TALKING POINTS]**

- **Hard conversations need three things: data, diagnosis, and collaboration.**
- **Data is objective.** It's not an opinion. It's 'You're at 18% close rate.'
- **Diagnosis is your analysis.** It's not judgment. It's 'I think this is what's happening.'
- **Collaboration is the frame.** It's 'Let's solve this together,' not 'You need to fix this.'

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (60:45–71:00)

**[SCRIPT]**

"The Accountability Framework has three parts. I'm going to walk through each one because this is where most managers either go too soft or too hard.

**Part 1: Data**

You lead with facts. Not interpretations. Not feelings. Facts.

Facts look like:
- 'Your close rate is 18%. Your target is 28%.'
- 'You've had 12 conversations this month. Your target is 15.'
- 'Your average deal size is $280k. Your average in prior months was $340k.'

These are objective. They're not up for debate. You're not saying 'You're lazy' or 'You're not trying hard enough.' You're saying 'Here's what the number is.'

Most managers skip this step or bury it. They say, 'I've noticed you're struggling' instead of 'Your close rate is 18%.' Vague is where people hide. Data is where clarity lives.

With Jamie, Andrew said, 'You're at 60% of quota, close rate 18%.' That's data. Jamie can't argue with the data. It's either true or it's not. And it's true.

**Part 2: Diagnosis**

Once you've stated the data, you offer your diagnosis. Your analysis of what's happening.

Your diagnosis is your perspective. Not the truth. Your perspective. So you offer it with some humility.

Diagnosis sounds like:
- 'I think what's happening is...'
- 'My observation is...'
- 'Here's what I'm seeing...'
- 'I suspect...'

The reason you use this language is it invites conversation. It doesn't close it down.

Bad diagnosis: 'You're not following up enough and that's why your close rate is low.'

That's stated like fact. It's not fact. It's your observation.

Good diagnosis: 'I think what might be happening is that you're not following up enough times with buyers. I wonder if that's a pattern for you.'

That opens the door. It says 'Here's what I'm seeing. What do you see?'

With Jamie, Andrew said, 'Here's what I'm observing: You get conversations. You show properties. And then you follow up once or twice, but if they're not ready, you move on. I think you might be uncomfortable with follow-up, and that's costing you close rate. Does that resonate?'

Notice: Andrew stated his observation. He invited Jamie to either confirm or correct it. That's diagnosis.

**Part 3: Collaboration**

Now here's the key to accountability without shame: You frame this as a problem you're solving together.

Not 'You need to fix your close rate.' But 'We need to figure this out. What are we going to do?'

Collaboration sounds like:
- 'Here's what I'm seeing. How do you see it?'
- 'I have a thought, but I want to hear yours first.'
- 'This is a gap we need to close. Let's figure it out together.'
- 'What would it take for you to get your close rate from 18% to 25%?'

The collaboration frame does something important: It removes shame. Because shame lives in 'You're broken and you need to fix yourself.' Collaboration lives in 'We're looking at this together and we're going to solve it.'

Now, here's important: Collaboration doesn't mean Jamie gets to opt out. You're not saying 'Let's do whatever you want.' You're saying 'Let's figure out what needs to change and commit to it.'

With Jamie, Andrew said, 'Look, here's where we are. You're at 18% close rate. Your target is 28%. That's a real gap. I think it's because you're not following up enough. But I could be wrong. How do you see it?'

Jamie said, 'Yeah, I think you're right. I don't like feeling pushy.'

Andrew said, 'Okay, so we know what the problem is. We know you're uncomfortable with follow-up. Now let's figure out how to change that. What would make you feel less pushy about following up?'

That's collaboration. They're solving it together.

**Putting It Together: The Full Hard Conversation**

Here's what this looks like in a full conversation:

**Andrew:** 'Jamie, I want to talk about your numbers. You're at 60% of quota, close rate 18%. I want to understand what's happening.'

**Jamie:** 'I know. I've been struggling.'

**Andrew:** 'I want to share what I'm seeing, and I want to hear what you're seeing. Here's my observation: I think you're getting good conversations, but you're not following up consistently. I wonder if you're uncomfortable with follow-up, and that's costing you close rate. Does that land?'

**Jamie:** 'Yeah. I hate feeling like I'm bugging people.'

**Andrew:** 'I get that. Nobody wants to feel pushy. But here's the reality: If you don't follow up, you miss deals. And you're missing a lot of deals right now. So we've got to figure out how to get you comfortable with follow-up, because your current approach isn't working. You with me?'

**Jamie:** 'Yeah, I know. I don't want to stay at 18%.'

**Andrew:** 'Right. So here's what I think we need to do. We need to reframe follow-up. Following up isn't pushy if you have a good reason. You're not following up to annoy people. You're following up because you found something that matches what they want, or you have new info that helps them. That's you being their agent. That's not pushy. What do you think?'

**Jamie:** 'I mean, yeah. When you put it that way, it makes sense.'

**Andrew:** 'Okay, so here's my ask: I want you to commit to following up at least three times with every buyer, and I want each follow-up to have a specific reason. Not 'Just checking in.' But 'I found another property' or 'I have new info about the market' or 'I want to make sure you're still on track.' Can you commit to that?'

**Jamie:** 'Yeah. I think I can do that.'

**Andrew:** 'Awesome. And I'm going to check in next week on how it went. I want to hear how many follow-ups you did, what the reasons were, and what the response was. This is going to move your close rate. You're going to feel better. Let's do this.'

Notice what happened:
- Data: 'You're at 60% of quota, close rate 18%.'
- Diagnosis: 'I think you're not following up enough and you're uncomfortable with it.'
- Collaboration: 'We need to figure this out together. Here's what I think. What do you think?'

And notice the frame: Not blame ('You're not trying hard enough'). Not shame ('Your numbers are embarrassing'). Clarity and collaboration ('Here's what I see. Here's what we're going to do. I'm with you.').

That's accountability without shame."

**[TALKING POINTS—Instructor Notes]**

- Hard conversations are not comfortable. But they're necessary. And they're more humane than avoiding the issue.
- Most managers either go too soft (and the person doesn't change) or too harsh (and the person gets defensive). Data + diagnosis + collaboration finds the middle.
- Your tone matters. You can say the exact right words but if your tone is condescending or frustrated, it won't land. Lead with respect and genuine curiosity.
- After the hard conversation, you have to follow up. Because follow-up is what makes the conversation stick.
