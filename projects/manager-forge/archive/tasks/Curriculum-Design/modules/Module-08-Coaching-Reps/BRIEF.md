# Coaching Reps to Results — Module Brief

**Module Status:** Ready for Content Development  
**Module #:** 8 of 12  
**Position in Curriculum:** Advanced coaching (follows Pipeline Review; precedes Hiring & Team Building)

---

## Executive Overview

**Coaching Reps to Results** addresses the hardest part of management: helping underperformers improve without burning yourself out trying to save everyone. This module teaches structured coaching that drives real behavior change—and when to recognize that coaching isn't working and harder decisions are needed.

The core insight: **Coaching works only when the rep wants to improve. Your job is to create clear expectations, provide structured support, and track improvement. If they're not improving after 60-90 days of good coaching, the problem isn't your coaching—it's fit.**

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

You cannot want someone's success more than they want it. Your job is to create the conditions for improvement (clear expectations, structured coaching, consistent follow-up), and then let the rep own their results. If they're not improving, it's not a failure of your coaching—it's data about fit.

**More specifically:**

1. **Coaching is a structured process, not random advice.** Most new managers give vague feedback ("Be better at discovery") and hope it works. Real coaching is: "Here's the gap. Here's the specific behavior to change. Here's how we'll measure it. Here's the timeline."

2. **You're coaching behavior, not personality.** You can't coach someone to "be more confident" or "be a hunter." You can coach them to "ask three discovery questions before presenting product" or "make 25 calls per day." Behavior is observable and measurable. Personality is not.

3. **Improvement timelines are real.** 30 days is too short. 6 months is too long. 60-90 days is the sweet spot. If a rep isn't showing improvement in 60-90 days of structured coaching, they're not going to improve. It's time for a different conversation.

4. **Underperformance is expensive.** A rep at 50% of quota for 6 months costs you territory, team morale, and leadership trust. Don't let underperformers linger because you're hoping they'll turn it around. Coach them hard for 60-90 days, then make a decision.

5. **Some people aren't coachable.** Not everyone wants to improve. Some people are defensive, blame external factors, or just don't have the capability. Your job is to identify this early and act, not spend 9 months trying to save someone who doesn't want to be saved.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Equipped with a structured coaching process (they have a roadmap)
- Clear on timelines (60-90 days, not forever)
- Confident identifying coachability (who wants to improve vs. who doesn't)
- Understanding that letting someone go is sometimes the most compassionate thing

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers typically do one of two things with underperformers—both waste time and energy:

**Path 1: The Endless Hope Manager**
"I believe in them. They just need more time. I'll keep coaching."

What happens: 6 months later, the rep is still underperforming. You've invested hours of coaching. The team resents you for not addressing it. Leadership questions your judgment. The rep has false hope and then gets blindsided when you finally let them go. Everyone loses.

**Path 2: The Give-Up-Too-Fast Manager**
"They're not hitting quota after 30 days. They're not a fit. Time to move on."

What happens: You develop a reputation for not investing in people. High performers worry they'll be fired if they have one bad month. Retention suffers. Leadership sees you as too quick to pull the trigger.

### The Real Issue

**Coaching takes structure and time—but not infinite time.**

Most new managers don't have a structured coaching process, so they give vague feedback, hope for the best, and wonder why nothing changes. When nothing changes, they either give up too fast or hold on too long.

The right approach: **Structured coaching for 60-90 days. Track improvement. If it's happening, keep coaching. If it's not, make a change.**

## Key Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. **The 60-90 Day Coaching Window**

This is the timeline that works. Not 30 days (too short for behavior change). Not 6 months (too long to let underperformance drag).

**Week 1-2: Diagnosis**
- Identify the specific performance gap
- Understand why it's happening (skill gap? motivation? fit?)
- Set clear expectations

**Week 3-4: Structured Coaching Begins**
- Weekly coaching conversations
- Specific behavior changes
- Measurement and tracking

**Week 5-8: Early Signals**
- Is behavior changing?
- Is performance improving?
- Are they engaged in the process?

**Week 9-12 (End of Month 3 / Start of Month 4): Decision Point**
- If improving: Keep coaching
- If not improving: Time for a harder conversation (PIP or exit)

**Why 60-90 Days:**
- Long enough for real behavior change
- Short enough to not waste months on someone who won't improve
- Clear timeline for everyone involved

### 2. **The Coachability Assessment**

Not everyone is coachable. Here's how to tell the difference.

**Signs of Coachability:**
- They admit they're struggling (ownership)
- They ask for help (openness)
- They implement feedback (action)
- They show incremental improvement (progress)
- They're frustrated with themselves, not external factors (accountability)

**Signs of Uncoachability:**
- They blame leads, territory, product, market, economy (external blame)
- They don't implement feedback (resistance)
- They make the same mistakes repeatedly (no learning)
- They're defensive when you give feedback (closed)
- They don't improve despite clear coaching (no progress)

**Why It Matters:**
- Coachable people improve with structure
- Uncoachable people waste your time
- Identifying this early saves everyone months of frustration

### 3. **The Behavior-Focused Coaching Framework**

You can't coach personality. You can only coach behavior.

**Wrong Coaching:**
- "Be more confident."
- "Be a hunter."
- "Have better energy."
- "Be more strategic."

**Right Coaching:**
- "Ask three discovery questions before showing product."
- "Make 25 calls per day, tracked in CRM."
- "Prepare for each call by researching the prospect's LinkedIn."
- "Send follow-up emails within 24 hours of the call."

**Why Behavior Matters:**
- Observable (you can see if they're doing it)
- Measurable (you can track it)
- Actionable (they know what to do)
- Improvable (they can practice it)

**The Shift:**
- From: "You need to be better at discovery."
- To: "In your next 5 calls, I want you to ask these three questions: [list them]. We'll review the calls and see how prospects respond."

### 4. **The Structured Coaching Conversation**

Coaching isn't a pep talk. It's a process.

**Step 1: Data**
"Your close rate is 15%. The expectation is 25%. That's a 10-point gap."

**Step 2: Diagnosis**
"I think the issue is discovery. Your calls focus on product too early. You're not understanding their goals first."

**Step 3: Agreement**
"Do you see it that way? What's your take?"

**Step 4: Specific Behavior Change**
"Here's what I want you to try: On your next 5 calls, ask [these questions] before you mention our product. Focus on understanding their goals."

**Step 5: Measurement**
"We'll review your calls next week and see if this changes how prospects engage."

**Step 6: Follow-Up**
"Next week, same time. We'll look at the data and see if we're moving in the right direction."

**Why It Works:**
- Clear expectations
- Specific actions
- Measurable outcomes
- Accountability built in

### 5. **The Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) Framework**

When coaching isn't working, a PIP formalizes expectations and timelines.

**What a PIP Is:**
- A formal document stating performance gaps, expectations, timeline, and consequences
- Usually 30-60 days
- Clear metrics for success
- Clear consequences if not met

**What a PIP Is Not:**
- A surprise (they should already know they're struggling)
- A guarantee of employment (it's a final chance, not a promise)
- A punishment (it's a structure to help them improve or exit cleanly)

**When to Use a PIP:**
- Rep has been coached for 60-90 days with no improvement
- Behavior gaps are clear and documented
- You've given feedback consistently
- They're not improving

**Why PIPs Matter:**
- Legal protection (you documented the process)
- Clarity for the rep (they know exactly what's expected)
- Timeline for you (you're not extending indefinitely)
- Transparency for leadership (they see you're managing it)

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: The Coachability Assessment (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers identify who's coachable vs. who's not
- Teach the signals of coachability
- Help them make faster decisions about where to invest time

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Not everyone wants to improve
- Coachable people take ownership and implement feedback
- Uncoachable people blame external factors and resist
- Your job is to identify this early

**The Assessment:**
- Do they admit they're struggling? (ownership)
- Do they ask for help? (openness)
- Do they try what you suggest? (action)
- Are they improving, even incrementally? (progress)
- Do they own their results or blame externals? (accountability)

**Real Example:**
- Rep A (coachable): "I know my close rate is low. I think I'm moving too fast through discovery. Can you help me slow down?"
- Rep B (uncoachable): "My close rate is low because the leads are terrible. There's nothing I can do until we fix the lead quality."

**Coaching Decision:**
- Rep A: Invest time. They want to improve.
- Rep B: Give one more chance to shift mindset. If they don't, move on.

**Business Impact:**
- You invest coaching time where it matters
- You don't waste months on people who won't change
- Team morale improves (they see you address underperformance)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't giving up on someone harsh?" (No, wasting their time on a role they can't succeed in is harsher.)
- "What if they become coachable later?" (Great, but you can't wait forever.)

### Sub-Module 2: The 60-90 Day Coaching Plan (14 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach the structured timeline for coaching
- Show how to diagnose, coach, measure, and decide
- Help managers commit to a timeline (not indefinite coaching)

**Key Teaching Points:**
- 60-90 days is the right window
- Week 1-2: Diagnose the gap
- Week 3-8: Structured coaching with weekly check-ins
- Week 9-12: Decision point (improving or not)
- If not improving after 90 days, it's time for a harder conversation

**The Plan Structure:**

**Week 1-2: Diagnosis**
- What's the performance gap? (data)
- Why is it happening? (skill? motivation? fit?)
- Set clear expectations (what success looks like)

**Week 3-8: Coaching**
- Weekly 1:1 coaching conversations
- Specific behavior changes each week
- Track metrics (calls, close rate, pipeline, etc.)
- Celebrate small wins

**Week 9-12: Assessment**
- Is performance improving?
- Are behaviors changing?
- Are they engaged?
- If yes: Keep coaching
- If no: Time for PIP or exit conversation

**Real Example:**
- Sarah's close rate: 18% (should be 25%)
- Week 1-2: Andrew diagnosed discovery issue (moving too fast to product)
- Week 3-8: Coached her to ask 3 discovery questions before presenting; tracked her calls
- Week 9: Close rate moved to 22% (improvement)
- Decision: Keep coaching; she's improving
- Month 4: Close rate hit 26% (success)

**Business Impact:**
- Clear timeline prevents endless coaching
- Reps know what's expected and when
- Leadership sees you're managing it
- You're not burning out on someone who won't improve

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they need more than 90 days?" (They might. But if there's no improvement in 90 days, more time won't help.)
- "Isn't 90 days too fast?" (For behavior change, no. For skill acquisition, maybe—but then they weren't ready for the role.)

### Sub-Module 3: Behavior-Focused Coaching (14 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach managers to coach specific behaviors, not vague traits
- Show how to make coaching actionable
- Help them measure behavior change

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Personality is not coachable; behavior is
- Vague feedback doesn't work ("Be better at discovery")
- Specific feedback works ("Ask these 3 questions before presenting")
- Behavior change is observable and measurable

**The Behavior Shift:**

**Vague:** "You need to be more strategic."
**Specific:** "Before each call, research the prospect's company on LinkedIn and reference something specific in your opening."

**Vague:** "Be more confident."
**Specific:** "When a prospect pushes back, don't apologize. Instead, say: 'Tell me more about that concern.'"

**Vague:** "Improve your close rate."
**Specific:** "On your next 10 calls, ask these 3 discovery questions: [list]. We'll review how prospects respond."

**Why It Works:**
- Reps know exactly what to do
- You can observe whether they're doing it
- You can measure the impact
- Improvement is clear

**Real Example:**
- Marcus's problem: "Not confident in calls"
- Andrew's coaching: "In your next 5 calls, when a prospect says 'I need to think about it,' say: 'What specifically do you need to think about?' Don't rush to close; slow down and listen."
- Result: Marcus asked the question; prospects opened up; Marcus felt more confident because he had a specific tool

**Business Impact:**
- Coaching sticks (because it's actionable)
- Reps improve faster
- You're not wasting time on vague advice

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't telling them exactly what to say micromanaging?" (No, it's giving them a tool. Once they learn it, they own it.)
- "What if they don't like the specific behavior?" (Try it first. Measure. If it doesn't work, adjust.)

### Sub-Module 4: The Performance Improvement Plan (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach when and how to use a PIP
- Show the structure of an effective PIP
- Help managers use it as a tool, not a punishment

**Key Teaching Points:**
- A PIP is a formal structure for final-chance improvement
- It's not a surprise (rep already knows they're struggling)
- It's clear: expectations, timeline, metrics, consequences
- It protects you legally and gives the rep clarity

**When to Use a PIP:**
- 60-90 days of coaching with no improvement
- Rep is still underperforming
- You've documented feedback
- You need a formal timeline to decide

**The PIP Structure:**

**Section 1: Performance Gaps**
"Your close rate is 18%. The expectation is 25%. Your activity is 15 calls/week. The expectation is 25 calls/week."

**Section 2: Expectations**
"Over the next 60 days, you need to:
- Hit 25 calls per week (tracked in CRM)
- Improve close rate to 22% by end of month 1, 25% by end of month 2
- Complete discovery training and implement [specific behaviors]"

**Section 3: Support**
"I will:
- Meet with you weekly to review progress
- Provide call coaching and feedback
- Give you access to [resources]"

**Section 4: Timeline**
"This PIP runs from [start date] to [end date]. We'll check progress weekly. At the end, if expectations are met, you continue. If not, we'll part ways."

**Section 5: Consequences**
"If expectations are not met by [end date], your employment will end."

**Why It Works:**
- Legal documentation
- Clear expectations (no ambiguity)
- Timeline (not indefinite)
- Gives rep a real chance (if they want it)

**Real Example:**
- Jamie: 60% of quota for 3 months, coached with no improvement
- Andrew put her on a 60-day PIP
- Clear metrics: 25 calls/week, 22% close rate
- Week 4: Jamie hit metrics
- Week 8: Still improving
- End of PIP: Jamie at 95% of quota; off PIP, continued employment

**Business Impact:**
- Clarity for everyone
- Legal protection
- Fair process
- Reps either improve or exit cleanly

**Tension to Address:**
- "Doesn't a PIP feel like I'm giving up on them?" (No, it's giving them a structured final chance.)
- "What if they improve during the PIP and then slip again?" (That's data. You gave them a chance; they didn't sustain it. Time to move on.)

### Sub-Module 5: Knowing When to Let Go (12 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers recognize when coaching isn't working
- Teach them to make the exit decision with confidence
- Show that letting someone go can be compassionate

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Some people won't improve, no matter how much you coach
- Holding on too long hurts everyone (you, them, the team)
- Letting someone go is sometimes the kindest thing
- Your responsibility is to the team and company, not to save everyone

**Signs It's Time to Let Go:**
- 90 days of coaching with no improvement
- PIP timeline ends with expectations not met
- Rep is consistently defensive or blaming external factors
- Team morale is suffering because you're not addressing it
- Leadership is questioning your judgment

**The Exit Conversation:**
"This isn't working. We've been coaching for 90 days. The metrics haven't improved. Your last day will be [date]. HR will explain next steps."

**Why It's Compassionate:**
- They're struggling in a role they can't succeed in
- You're freeing them to find a better fit
- You're being honest instead of dragging it out
- The team sees you address underperformance

**Real Example:**
- Rep on PIP for 60 days; no improvement
- Andrew: "We gave this a real shot. The metrics aren't there. This role isn't the right fit. Your last day is Friday. I'll support you in your transition."
- Rep: Upset but not surprised (they knew they were struggling)
- Team: Relieved (they'd been watching underperformance for months)

**Business Impact:**
- Team morale improves
- Leadership trusts your judgment
- You free up territory for someone who can perform
- You protect your own sanity

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if I'm wrong and they could have improved?" (You gave them 90 days of structured coaching. That's more than fair.)
- "Won't the team think I'm harsh?" (No, they'll think you have standards.)

- **Coachability Diagnostic:** Can you tell who's coachable? (scenario-based assessment)

## Key Tensions to Address

- **"How do I know if 90 days is enough?"** → If there's no improvement in 90 days of structured coaching, more time won't help.
- **"What if they improve right after I let them go?"** → That's their journey. You gave them a fair shot.
- **"Isn't using a PIP giving up on them?"** → No, it's giving them a structured final chance.
- **"What if the team thinks I'm too harsh?"** → They'll respect you for having standards.

## Tone & Voice Guidance

- **Direct:** Coaching works when it's structured. Holding on too long hurts everyone.
- **Compassionate:** Letting someone go can be the kindest thing.
- **Practical:** Give them templates and timelines they can use.
- **Honest:** Share times you held on too long; times you made the right call.
