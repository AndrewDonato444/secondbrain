## SUB-MODULE 1: THE COACHABILITY ASSESSMENT (13 minutes)
**Timecode: 0:00–13:00**

### OPENING HOOK (0:00–2:00)
[PAUSE: Let silence sit for 3 seconds]

"You have a rep who's underperforming. Low pipeline, missed quota, falling behind. Your first instinct—the natural one—is to coach. Give them more structure, more feedback, more of your time.

But here's what I've learned managing teams in SaaS and luxury real estate: coaching works brilliantly for some people. For others, you could coach until you're blue in the face and nothing changes.

The question isn't 'Can I coach this person?' The question is: 'Is this person coachable?'

That's the difference that changes everything. And that's what we're tackling in this module."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (2:00–12:00)

**POINT 1: What Is Coachability? (2:00–4:30)**

"Coachability isn't about intelligence or work ethic. It's not about IQ. It's about whether someone has five specific traits.

Let me read through them:

**1. Ownership.** They admit they're struggling. Not 'the leads are bad' or 'my manager doesn't support me'—but 'I'm not hitting my numbers and I want to fix it.'

**2. Openness.** They ask for help. They don't get defensive when you point out gaps. They say things like 'What am I missing here?' or 'How should I approach this differently?'

**3. Action.** They don't just nod and agree. They actually implement the feedback. You give them something specific to do, and they do it.

**4. Progress.** Within 2-3 weeks of trying new things, you see measurable improvement. Not perfection—improvement.

**5. Accountability.** When things don't work, they own it. They don't blame external circumstances. They ask 'What did I do wrong?' and adjust.

All five of these? That's a coachable person. Someone missing two or three? You've got a problem."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**POINT 2: Signals of Coachability (4:30–7:00)**

[TALKING POINTS BULLET FORMAT]

**When Someone IS Coachable:**
- In a 1-on-1, they ask you questions about their performance
- When you point out a gap, they don't argue—they listen
- They come back the next week and tell you what they tried differently
- They measure their own progress (not just you tracking it)
- When something doesn't work, they suggest alternatives
- They say things like: "I didn't hit my number this week. Here's what I think went wrong. Here's what I'm going to try."
- Their energy shifts when they realize they can fix something
- They have 'aha moments' where they connect the feedback to their results

**When Someone Is NOT Coachable:**
- They blame the market, the leads, the product, the economy
- When you give feedback, they get quiet or defensive
- When you follow up the next week, they haven't done anything different
- They don't ask questions; they just listen (passively)
- They make excuses: "I tried that, but it didn't work" (without trying harder)
- They say things like: "This won't work for my style" or "That's not how I sell"
- They repeat the same mistakes over and over
- Their defensiveness is consistent—same patterns in every 1-on-1
- They view coaching as criticism, not help
- The energy in the room feels heavy; you leave exhausted"

[PAUSE: 4 seconds]

**POINT 3: The Real Cost of Coaching the Wrong Person (7:00–9:00)**

"Here's what happens when you spend 90 days coaching someone who isn't coachable:

You invest massive amounts of time. Weekly 1-on-1s, role-plays, detailed feedback, customized training. You are *betting on them* with your time.

Meanwhile, your coachable reps—the ones who want to improve—are getting 20% of your attention. Your high performers are getting 10%.

The rep you're coaching? Nothing changes. Or it gets worse because now they're frustrated and so are you.

And the team? They see you holding on to someone who isn't pulling their weight. You're sending a message: *Underperformance is okay if you stick around long enough.*

That's how teams lose their standards.

Real example: I had a rep in luxury real estate—let's call him Tom. Tom blamed every lost deal on market conditions. In our 1-on-1s, he'd talk about how his territory was weak, how other agents had better luck, how buyers in his area were 'harder to work with.'

Never once did he say 'I think I could get better at X.'

I coached Tom for four months anyway. Hoping he'd have a breakthrough. Hoping the mindset shift would click.

It didn't. And you know what happened? My best reps watched me coddle Tom and started looking for other teams. One of them left.

That's expensive. And it was my fault for not making a decision sooner."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**POINT 4: The Coachability Assessment Conversation (9:00–11:30)**

"So how do you actually assess this? You have a conversation. A specific one.

Here's how it goes—and I'm going to give you the exact language:

**SETUP:**
Schedule 30 minutes. 1-on-1. Closed door. No distractions. You say:

'I want to talk about your performance because I care about you and your success here. I'm seeing some gaps. Before we go further, I need to understand: Do you see what I'm seeing? And do you want to fix it?'

[PAUSE for their response]

**Then you listen. Really listen.**

If they say 'Yes, I see the gap. I want to improve,' you've got a coachable person. Ask follow-ups:

- 'What do you think is causing this?'
- 'What have you tried so far?'
- 'What are you willing to do differently?'
- 'How will you know if you're improving?'

A coachable person will engage. They'll be honest about what they don't know. They'll ask for help.

If they say 'I don't really see a gap' or 'It's not my fault—it's [the market/the leads/the product],' you have a different situation. That's not coachable. Not yet, anyway.

That doesn't mean you give up. It means you're not in coaching mode. You're in performance management mode. That's a different conversation for later in the module.

The key: Don't assume. Ask. Listen. Decide based on what you hear, not what you hope."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

## SUB-MODULE 2: THE 60-90 DAY COACHING PLAN (14 minutes)
**Timecode: 13:00–27:00**

### OPENING HOOK (13:00–14:30)

"So you've assessed someone. They're coachable. They want to improve. Now what?

This is where most managers go vague.

They say something like: 'I'm going to coach you. We'll focus on sales skills.' Then they hope improvement happens.

It doesn't work that way. Hope isn't a strategy.

What *does* work is a structured timeline. A window. A beginning, a middle, and a decision point.

We call it the 60-90 Day Coaching Window. And it has four phases."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (14:30–25:00)

**PHASE 1: DIAGNOSIS (Week 1-2) (14:30–16:30)**

"The first two weeks, you diagnose. You're not coaching yet. You're understanding.

What are the actual gaps? Not vague ones like 'needs more confidence' or 'isn't strategic.' Specific ones.

**Example from Marcus:**

Marcus was a good guy. Smart. Personable. But his deals were falling apart in the negotiation phase. Buyers would get cold feet.

In my initial diagnosis, I sat in on three calls. I listened.

What I found: Marcus was apologizing. When a buyer raised an objection, Marcus would say things like:

- 'I'm sorry you feel that way.'
- 'Sorry, I should have explained that better.'
- 'My apologies, that's probably my fault.'

He wasn't being weak. He was being *deferential.* It was making buyers less confident.

That was the gap. Not 'needs more confidence.' Not 'needs better sales skills.' The specific behavior: *apologizing too much in negotiations.*

**DIAGNOSIS CHECKLIST:**

During this phase, you:
- Sit in on calls or observe actual work (don't rely on their story)
- Ask: What specific behaviors are causing the gap?
- List 1-3 concrete behaviors, not personality traits
- Get clear on the measurement (What will better look like? Be specific: close rate from 18% to 24%? Discovery questions from 2 to 5 per call?)
- Share the diagnosis with them: 'Here's what I'm seeing. Does this match your experience?'

They should recognize themselves in the diagnosis. If they don't, you're not deep enough yet. Keep digging."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**PHASE 2: STRUCTURED COACHING (Week 3-8) (16:30–22:00)**

"Now you have a diagnosis. You coach around it. Specifically.

This is six weeks. You see the rep once a week. Every single week. That's non-negotiable.

**THE WEEKLY RHYTHM:**

**Week 3-4 (Teaching & Modeling):**
- You show them what good looks like
- You role-play the new behavior
- You explain the *why*
- Example with Marcus: I demonstrated what it sounds like to name objections without apologizing. 'I understand that's a concern. Here's how we address it.' Confident. Not defensive.

**Week 5-6 (Practice & Feedback):**
- They try the new behavior in live calls
- You listen to calls or get detailed feedback
- You give specific feedback within 24 hours
- Example: Marcus tried it. First call, he still apologized once. I said: 'That's actually better—you caught yourself mid-sentence. But here's where you slipped: [specific moment on the call]. Try this instead: [specific language].' Not general feedback. Specific.

**Week 7-8 (Ownership & Iteration):**
- They start coaching themselves
- They identify their own patterns
- You're becoming less directive, more reflective
- Example: Marcus listened to his own call. He texted me: 'I did it again at 23 minutes—the sorry thing. I think it was when they mentioned budget.' I said: 'Exactly right. What will you try differently next time?' He had the answer.

**THE WEEKLY 1-ON-1 STRUCTURE:**

Each week, 30 minutes:
- **First 5 min:** What happened this week? (Review results)
- **Next 10 min:** Listen to calls or review work sample
- **Next 10 min:** Specific feedback (what worked, what didn't, what's next)
- **Last 5 min:** Commit to one thing for next week (not five things—one)

**MEASUREMENT DURING PHASE 2:**

Track the specific behavior weekly. If you said the goal is 'Ask 5 discovery questions per call,' track that. Not a feeling. Not 'seems better.' The actual count.

This is important because it keeps the coaching focused. And it shows the rep whether they're improving or not.

Marcus's measurement: How many times does he apologize in negotiations per call? Week 3: 4 times. Week 5: 2 times. Week 7: 1 time. Week 8: 0.5 times per call average.

That's a clear progression. He could see his own progress."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**PHASE 3: DECISION POINT (Week 9-12) (22:00–24:00)**

"After eight weeks of structured coaching, you have a decision point. Weeks 9-12 are evaluation weeks.

The question: Is this person improving or not?

You look at the data:
- Are the behaviors changing?
- Are the results shifting?
- Is the trajectory positive?

**THREE POSSIBLE OUTCOMES:**

1. **Improving Consistently:** They're trending the right direction. Results are better. Behaviors are in place. Decision: Continue coaching. Maybe shift to monthly check-ins instead of weekly. But they're on the right path.

2. **Plateaued:** They got better for 4-5 weeks, then stopped. The behaviors are inconsistent. Results are 10-15% better, but not where you need them. Decision: You need to diagnose what's blocking them. Is it lack of capability? Lack of commitment? Then you decide whether to go back to Phase 2 coaching with a different focus, or move to performance management.

3. **No Improvement:** It's Week 12 and they look the same as Week 1. Or worse. The behaviors aren't changing. The results are flat. Decision: This person is not coachable. You move to performance management or exit conversation. (We cover that in Sub-Module 5.)

**MAKING THE DECISION:**

Don't be wishy-washy. Don't say 'Let's do another 30 days and see.'

By Week 12, you have enough data. You've invested time. Now you make the call.

A coachable person with genuine skill gaps will show improvement by Week 8-10. If they're not showing it, something else is going on—and coaching isn't the answer.

Real example: Jamie, a SaaS AE. She had commission anxiety—scared of big deals because the stakes felt high. We diagnosed it. We coached specific behaviors: confidence scripts, objection handling, case studies.

By Week 6, she was closing bigger deals. By Week 10, she was at target. Decision: She's improving. Keep the coaching going but stretch it to bi-weekly. She's on track.

Compare that to another rep, Derek. Same company. Same coaching. Week 1-8, nothing changed. He wasn't implementing the feedback. In Week 12, I had the conversation: 'Derek, you're not improving. And that's not because the coaching is bad—it's because you're not ready.' We parted ways. Within two months, his replacement was crushing quota.

The decision—made clearly at Week 12—saved the team."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

## SUB-MODULE 3: BEHAVIOR-FOCUSED COACHING (14 minutes)
**Timecode: 27:00–41:00**

### OPENING HOOK (27:00–28:30)

"One of the biggest mistakes I see managers make: they give vague feedback.

'You need to be more strategic.'

'You need more confidence.'

'You're not closing enough.'

Reps hear that, and their brain basically shuts down. They don't know what to *do* with that.

The rep thinks: 'Okay, so... what does that mean? Be more strategic *how*? Ask more questions? Think longer? What?'

It's like saying 'Get better at basketball.' Get better *how*? Your footwork? Your shot? Your court vision? Which one?

Vague feedback doesn't change behavior. Specific feedback does.

That's what this sub-module is about: how to give feedback that actually sticks."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (28:30–39:30)

**PRINCIPLE 1: COACH BEHAVIORS, NOT PERSONALITY (28:30–31:00)**

"First principle: Coach *what people do,* not *who they are.*

**WRONG:**
- 'You're not confident enough.'
- 'You need to be more assertive.'
- 'You're too people-pleasing.'

Why this doesn't work: It's about their identity. They hear it as criticism of who they are. It triggers defensiveness. And it gives them nothing to actually *change.*

**RIGHT:**
- 'In that call, you asked the buyer what they wanted three times but never offered a recommendation. Let's work on when and how to make recommendations.'
- 'When the buyer pushed back on pricing, you immediately dropped your price by 10%. Let's practice holding the line and explaining value first.'
- 'You ended that call without confirming next steps. Let's make sure every call ends with a clear action item.'

Why this works: It's specific. It's observable. The rep can *see* the behavior. They can practice it. They can measure it.

**THE DISTINCTION:**

Personality (can't coach): Introversion, sensitivity, competitiveness, ambition

Behavior (can coach): Asking questions, listening, explaining value, confirming commitments, managing time, documenting decisions

If a rep is introverted, you don't try to make them an extrovert. But you *do* coach specific behaviors that introverts might avoid—like speaking up in meetings or introducing themselves to new contacts.

Does that distinction make sense?

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

The reason this matters: When you coach behavior, the rep feels capable of change. When you coach personality, they feel judged."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**PRINCIPLE 2: OBSERVABLE & MEASURABLE (31:00–34:00)**

"Second: Make the behavior observable and measurable.

Not 'better sales skills.' Observable: 'Ask three discovery questions before presenting.'

Not 'stronger objection handling.' Observable: 'When a buyer says no, pause for three seconds before responding. Then ask, Tell me more about that concern.'

Not 'more strategic.' Observable: 'Before each call, write down one strategic question you'll ask. Not questions about their needs—questions about their business goals.'

**WHY MEASURABLE?**

Because then the rep can track their own progress. They're not dependent on your assessment.

Example with Marcus again: I didn't say 'Stop apologizing.' I said 'Count how many times you apologize in each negotiation call. Week 1 baseline, then we'll reduce by 25% every two weeks.'

Suddenly, it's not a character flaw. It's a metric. He could track it. He could compete with his own baseline.

**THE BEHAVIOR-COACHING FORMULA:**

When you're coaching a behavior, use this structure:

1. **Describe the current behavior:** 'In that call, you [specific action].'
2. **Explain why it's a problem:** 'That signals [how it lands on the buyer], which usually leads to [outcome].'
3. **Give the specific new behavior:** 'Instead, I want you to [specific new action].'
4. **Show what it sounds like:** Demonstrate or give exact language
5. **Get them to commit:** 'How will you remember to do this?'
6. **Create accountability:** 'I'll listen for this on your next call.'

**EXAMPLE: VAGUE vs. SPECIFIC**

*VAGUE FEEDBACK:*
Manager: 'You need to be more consultative.'
Rep: [confused silence]
Manager: 'You know, less product-focused. More solution-oriented.'
Rep: [still confused]

*SPECIFIC FEEDBACK:*
Manager: 'I listened to your call with Acme. You asked about their current process. That's great. But then you jumped straight to your solution. Here's what I'd do: Ask one more question: Why that process? What's working about it, and what's not? That gets you into their *thinking,* not just their situation. Then your solution lands better because you've diagnosed their actual priority. Let me show you what that sounds like... [you demonstrate]. Next call with a similar prospect, I want you to ask that extra diagnostic question. Can you do that?'
Rep: 'Yes, I see it. One more question about their thinking.'

See the difference? One is abstract. One is actionable."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**PRINCIPLE 3: THE BEHAVIOR-CHANGE RHYTHM (34:00–39:00)**

"How often should you coach? And how many behaviors at once?

**Rule 1: One behavior at a time.**

Not five. Not three. One.

If you give someone five things to change, they change zero things. The cognitive load is too high.

Pick the one behavior that, if changed, would have the biggest impact on their results.

For Sarah, it was discovery questions. For Marcus, it was the apologizing. Not five things—one.

**Rule 2: Coach weekly until you see consistency.**

A behavior change takes about 3-4 weeks of practice to start sticking. Some people get it in 2. Some take 5.

So you check in weekly for the first month. By Week 4, if they're consistently doing the behavior, you can stretch to bi-weekly.

**Rule 3: Be specific in the feedback.**

Don't say 'Good job on discovery this week.'

Say: 'In the call on Wednesday with Tech Corp, you asked four discovery questions before presenting. That's exactly right. On Friday with Northern Inc., you asked two. On this call, what happened differently?'

This tells them what good looks like, and it helps them identify patterns in when they do it well vs. when they don't.

**Rule 4: Give feedback within 24 hours.**

If possible, same day. 'I just listened to your call. Here's what I heard...'

The longer you wait, the less the rep remembers their own thinking. The feedback lands better when it's fresh."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

```
"I was on a call with you yesterday. I noticed you did a lot of talking. You explained your solution pretty thoroughly. But I felt like you were in selling mode rather than discovery mode.

Keep listening until they've fully explained their situation. Then pitch.

You want to listen more than you talk—at least a 60/40 ratio. You talk 40%, they talk 60%. This week, I want you to track: Are you hitting that ratio?

One way to do this: At the end of each call, ask yourself: Did they talk more than I did? Yes or no? We're aiming for yes."

BEHAVIOR TO TRACK: Self-assessment after each call: Did I listen more than I talked? Target: Yes on 80% of calls."

```

**SARAH'S DISCOVERY COACHING:**

"Sarah was closing at 18%. I diagnosed the problem: She wasn't asking enough discovery questions.

**VAGUE:** 'You need to be more consultative and strategic.'

**SPECIFIC:** 'In your last call, you asked two questions about their current tools. Then you jumped to your solution. I want you to ask five questions before you pitch. Specifically: How are you using this tool now? What's working? What's not? What's your timeline? Who else needs to sign off?'

Sarah tracked it:
- Week 1: 3 discovery questions per call
- Week 2: 4 per call
- Week 3: 5 per call
- Week 4: 5-6 per call
- Close rate went from 18% to 22% to 26%

That's not a coincidence. That's coaching behavior specifically."

### TENSION POINT: "What If They Push Back and Say 'That's Not My Style'?" (39:45–40:15)

"I've heard this a lot: 'That's not how I sell.' Or 'That doesn't feel natural to me.'

Here's my response: 'I get that. It might feel uncomfortable at first. But if your current style is producing 18% closes and we need 22%, then we need to change something. This behavior is what will get you there. It might feel awkward for two weeks. But I need you to practice it anyway.'

Coachable reps will do it. They might not love it, but they'll try it.

Un-coachable reps will dig in: 'No, I tried that approach and it doesn't work for me. I'm just not a discovery person.'

That's when you know: This person is not coachable. They've decided who they are, and they're unwilling to change."

### CLOSING SUMMARY (40:15–41:00)

"Let's lock in the principles:

1. **Coach behaviors, not personality.** Observable, specific actions—not identity traits.

2. **Make it measurable.** So the rep can track their own progress.

3. **Coach one behavior at a time.** Not five. One.

4. **Coach weekly at first.** Until the behavior sticks (usually 3-4 weeks).

5. **Give feedback within 24 hours.** While it's fresh.

When you do this—specific, measurable, behavioral coaching—two things happen:

First, reps actually change. Because they know exactly what to do.

Second, you've created proof. You know whether coaching is working or not. Because you're measuring the actual behavior and the results tied to it.

Next: What happens when behavior coaching isn't enough. When someone needs a more formal intervention. That's the Performance Improvement Plan."

## SUB-MODULE 4: THE PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT PLAN (13 minutes)
**Timecode: 41:00–54:00**

### OPENING HOOK (41:00–42:15)

"Okay, so you've coached someone for 90 days. You've been specific. You've been consistent. And nothing's changed.

Or maybe you coached them, they got better, then they slipped back to old patterns.

At this point, coaching isn't enough. You need something more formal.

That's the Performance Improvement Plan. The PIP.

A lot of managers get nervous about PIPs. They think it's harsh. Mean.

But here's the truth: A PIP is actually the kindest thing you can do, because it's honest. It says: 'This is serious. We need a change, and here's exactly what that change needs to be. And here's the support we're giving you. And here's what happens if it doesn't improve.'

Ambiguity is cruel. Clarity is kind.

That's what a PIP does. It brings clarity."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (42:15–52:00)

**WHEN TO USE A PIP (42:15–43:30)**

"First: When *not* to use a PIP.

**Don't use a PIP if:**
- You haven't coached them yet. Coach first. 90 days minimum.
- You just hired them. They need a learning curve. Give them 180 days.
- The performance gap is tiny. They're at 85% of quota; you need them at 95%. That's coaching, not a PIP.
- They're coachable and improving. Even slowly.

**DO use a PIP if:**
- They've been coached for 90+ days, and there's no improvement
- Their performance is significantly below standard and impacting the team
- You've given clear feedback, and they haven't implemented it
- You're considering termination, and you want to give them one final, clear opportunity with a specific timeline

**Real example:** Jamie was on a PIP. But not because she was uncoachable. She was coachable, improving, and hitting targets. Once she hit them consistently, we took her off the PIP. A PIP isn't permanent. It's conditional.

Compare that to Derek. He was coached for 90 days. Nothing changed. He wasn't implementing feedback. He wasn't accountable. I put him on a 60-day PIP. By day 45, it was clear he wasn't going to make it. We accelerated the exit.

The PIP gave him clarity. And it gave the organization protection."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

**THE PIP STRUCTURE (43:30–48:00)**

"A PIP has five elements. Let me walk through each one.

**ELEMENT 1: THE PERFORMANCE GAP**

Describe, clearly and specifically, what's not working.

WRONG: 'Sales performance is below expectations. You need to improve.'

RIGHT:
- 'Close rate is 12%. Standard is 20%. That's an 8-point gap.
- 'Pipeline value is $85K. You're carrying $45K. That's not enough deals.
- 'Call volume is 8 calls per week. We need 15 per week to support quota.'

Specific. Measurable. No ambiguity.

**ELEMENT 2: THE CLEAR EXPECTATIONS**

What does success look like? In numbers.

'By the end of 60 days:
- Close rate: minimum 18% (we'll measure weekly)
- Pipeline: minimum $120K in active opportunities
- Call volume: minimum 12 calls per week (tracked daily)

These are the standards. This is what you need to hit.'

**ELEMENT 3: THE SUPPORT STRUCTURE**

What will you do to help them succeed? Be specific.

Examples:
- 'We will have weekly 1-on-1s focused on behavioral coaching.'
- 'I will listen to three calls per week and provide feedback within 24 hours.'
- 'You'll attend our top-performer ride-alongs on Tuesdays and Thursdays.'
- 'We'll enroll you in the sales skills workshop next month.'
- 'You have access to me for questions—I want you to win.'

This isn't fluff. This is real support. And it protects you, because if they say 'I didn't have support,' you have documentation of what you offered.

**ELEMENT 4: THE TIMELINE**

Usually 60 days. Sometimes 30 if they're egregiously behind and you're being generous. Rarely longer than 90 because at that point you've just re-done the coaching window.

'This PIP is effective immediately and runs through [date]. On [date], we will review your performance against the metrics above.'

**ELEMENT 5: THE CONSEQUENCES**

This is the hard part. And it's important.

'If at the end of 60 days you have not met the standards outlined above, your employment will be terminated.'

Or, if you want to be slightly gentler:

'If at the end of 60 days you have not met these standards, we will discuss whether continued employment is in your best interest. Continued poor performance may result in termination.'

The point: They need to know what's at stake. That's not cruelty. That's honesty.

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**THE PIP CONVERSATION (48:00–52:00)**

"Now, how do you *deliver* a PIP? Because that matters too.

Schedule 30 minutes. In person if possible. Closed door. Have HR in the room if your company requires it. You want witnesses.

**Here's the script:**

'I want to talk about your performance. You've been here for [time]. And I've given you feedback on [specific behaviors/metrics]. Over the past 90 days, I've coached you on [specific examples]. And we've had [number] conversations about improvement.

Here's where we are: You're not meeting the standards we need. Specifically:
- [Gap 1]
- [Gap 2]
- [Gap 3]

This is serious. And because it's serious, I'm putting you on a formal Performance Improvement Plan. That means, over the next 60 days, you need to meet these specific metrics:
- [Standard 1]
- [Standard 2]
- [Standard 3]

Here's what I'm committing to: [support 1, 2, 3].

And here's what happens if you don't hit these standards by [date]: Your employment will be terminated.

I'm giving you this Plan because I believe you can do this. But you need to understand: This is your last chance to show me you can meet our standards. Do you understand?'

[PAUSE for response]

'I want you to succeed. I'm going to invest heavily in helping you. But I need to see significant improvement. Weekly. Not in 60 days—starting this week.

Questions?'

[PAUSE for questions]

'Okay, let me give you a copy of this plan. Review it. Think about it. And let's schedule our first weekly check-in for [day/time].'

**WHY THIS WORKS:**

It's clear. It's not personal. It's about the metrics. It outlines support. And it outlines consequences. The rep can't say they didn't know what was expected.

**POST-PIP COMMUNICATION:**

Document everything. Every weekly check-in, every piece of feedback. The reason: If they don't improve and you terminate them, they might claim you didn't support them or changed the goalposts. Documentation proves otherwise.

Weekly email to them: 'Here's what I observed this week. Here's how it compares to the PIP standards. Here's what I want to see next week.'

This is not harassment. It's accountability. And it protects you."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

## SUB-MODULE 5: KNOWING WHEN TO LET GO (12 minutes)
**Timecode: 54:00–66:00**

### OPENING HOOK (54:00–55:30)

"This is the hard one.

Most managers I've worked with can do the coaching part. They can give feedback, structure a plan, support people through improvement.

The part that breaks them: Letting someone go.

They hold on too long. They convince themselves that one more month, one more conversation, one more chance will do it.

And meanwhile, the person stays stuck. The team is undermined. And the manager is exhausted.

Here's the truth I've learned: Sometimes the most compassionate thing you can do is let someone go.

Not as punishment. But as clarity. 'This job isn't the right fit. You deserve something better. And we need to move forward.'

That's what this sub-module is about. How to know when it's time. And how to do it with dignity."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

---

### KEY TEACHING POINTS (55:30–64:00)

**SIGN 1: THEY'RE NOT COACHABLE (55:30–57:00)**

"First sign: You've assessed them, and they're not coachable.

Remember the signals? Blame. Defensiveness. No action. No progress.

If someone is un-coachable, coaching won't save them. Neither will a PIP. Neither will more time.

**What this looks like:**
- You give feedback, and they argue with it or ignore it
- You ask them to change a behavior, and they say 'I tried that, it doesn't work for me'
- You show them data about their performance, and they blame external factors
- They don't ask questions; they're defensive
- They've been there 6+ months and still can't execute basic functions

**Real example:** That rep I mentioned, Derek. He was in SaaS sales. Not closing deals. I sat in on his calls.

He'd give a product demo for 45 minutes. Then ask: 'Any questions?'

When the prospect said 'Seems interesting but we need to think about it,' Derek would say: 'Okay, let me send you some information.' And he'd leave the call without asking about timing or concerns.

In our 1-on-1, I said: 'Derek, I noticed you're not asking for the close. After your demo, I'd like you to say: Based on what I've explained, here's what I recommend. Does that make sense?'

Derek said: 'Yeah, but that feels pushy to me. I don't like closing that hard.'

That's the signal. He identified it as a 'pushy' behavior. He wasn't willing to change his style to fit the role.

Coaching doesn't work on that. Because the person has decided who they are, and they're not open to change.

If they're not coachable, the timeline is short. Don't spend 90 days coaching. Spend 2-3 weeks assessing. Make the call. And move on."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

**SIGN 2: THEY'RE COACHABLE BUT NOT IMPROVING (57:00–58:30)**

"Second sign: They're coachable—good attitude, open to feedback—but they can't execute.

This usually means: They don't have the capability for the role. Their skill ceiling is below what the job requires.

That's different from un-coachable. They *want* to improve. They're just not able to.

**What this looks like:**
- They implement the feedback, but the results are still poor
- They understand the concepts, but execution is inconsistent
- They make the same mistakes despite your coaching
- They're working hard, but the outcomes aren't there

**Real example:** I had a rep in luxury real estate. Great attitude. Hungry to learn. She wanted to improve her prospecting. I gave her a structured prospecting plan. She executed it. She was consistent.

But her close rate never improved past 8%. She was working hard and doing what I asked, but she just didn't have the natural sales instinct for complex negotiations.

After 90 days, I realized: This is a her-limitation situation, not a coaching situation. She would never hit the standard for that role.

But she was coachable. So I said: 'Here's what I'm hearing. You're great at outreach and relationship-building. But complex negotiations aren't your strength. Have you thought about moving into a business development or account management role? Because I think you'd crush that.'

She moved into BD. She's fantastic at it. Making more money. Happier.

Sometimes letting someone go means letting them go from *this role,* not the organization.

If they're coachable but not improving despite structured coaching, the conversation is: 'This role isn't the right fit. Where else might you thrive?'

Or, if there's no other role: 'I don't think this is the right place for you long-term. I want to see you succeed, and I don't think you will here. Let's talk about what's next.'"

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

**SIGN 3: THEY'RE POISONING THE TEAM (58:30–60:00)**

"Third sign: Even if they're trainable, they're hurting the culture.

This is when someone has been underperforming for so long that other people are demoralized.

**What this looks like:**
- Your high performers are frustrated that the underperformer is still there
- People are asking 'When is management going to do something?'
- The underperformer is complaining to others, creating drama
- Your best people are considering leaving

I had this happen. A rep named Dave. He was there for 18 months. Never hit quota. I kept coaching. I kept hoping.

After six months, my top performer came to me and said: 'If Dave's still here in three months, I'm gone. We can't have someone underperforming for this long without consequences.'

That's when I realized: My compassion for Dave is costing me my best people.

If an underperformer has been there a long time and the team's morale is suffering, sometimes the kindest thing to the team is to let that person go.

It sounds harsh. But I've seen it countless times: You let someone go, and the team's energy improves immediately. People say 'Finally, standards matter here.'"

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

**SIGN 4: THE MATH DOESN'T WORK (60:00–61:30)**

"Fourth sign: The cost of keeping them is greater than the cost of replacing them.

This is utilitarian. But it's real.

If someone is taking 20% of a manager's time, is preventing two high performers from reaching their potential, and is blocking quota—the math probably says: Replace them.

**The calculation:**
- Time you're spending coaching: 5-10 hours/week
- Revenue they're generating: 60% of quota = $X
- Opportunity cost (what high performers could do with your attention): $Y
- Replacement cost and training (3 months): ~$30K

If they're not going to improve, and they're costing you more than they're worth—you have your answer.

I'm not saying it's heartless. I'm saying it's realistic.

One more thing: Holding onto someone too long has a cost you don't see initially.

It costs you high performers. It costs you culture. It costs you your own energy.

I had a manager who spent a full year trying to salvage one mediocre rep. By the end, that manager was burned out. Their whole team's results suffered. The underperformer still didn't improve.

If you'd made the call at Month 3, you'd have had 9 months to invest in your strong reps. Different outcome."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

**WHEN TO HAVE THE EXIT CONVERSATION (61:30–62:30)**

"Okay, so you've made the decision. They're not coachable, or they're not improving, or they're poisoning the team.

How do you know it's time to have the exit conversation?

**Checklist:**
- ☑ You've coached them for 60-90 days (or determined they're not coachable)
- ☑ There's no improvement in performance or behavior
- ☑ You've documented feedback and coaching
- ☑ You've considered PIP and decided against it (or they failed the PIP)
- ☑ You've talked to HR / legal (if your organization requires it)
- ☑ You have a plan for their backfill or transition

If you've checked all of those, it's time.

You don't wait for perfect timing. You don't wait for them to 'come around.' You have the conversation."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

### THE EXIT CONVERSATION SCRIPT (62:30–64:00)

"Here's how to do it.

**BEFORE THE CONVERSATION:**
- Schedule 30 minutes
- Do it early in the week (not Friday, when they have the weekend to spiral)
- Have HR present if required
- Have a severance/transition plan ready

**THE CONVERSATION:**

You: 'Thanks for meeting with me. I need to talk about your employment here.

[Pause.]

I've spent a lot of time thinking about this. Over the past [time period], your performance hasn't met the standards we need. I've coached you. I've given you feedback. I've put support in place.

And despite all of that, things haven't changed.

I want to be honest with you: I don't think this role is the right fit. And I don't think it's fair to you or to us to keep going.

So I've made a decision: We're going to end your employment here. Today is your last day. Here's your severance package [if applicable], your final paycheck, information about benefits continuation, and how we'll handle the transition.

I want you to know: This decision isn't about your worth as a person. You're capable. You're hardworking. This just isn't the right environment for you.

I hope you'll find something that's a better fit. And I wish you the best.

Do you have questions?'

[PAUSE for response]

**IMPORTANT:**
- Don't apologize excessively
- Don't over-explain or justify
- Don't make this a negotiation
- Keep it short (5 minutes of talking; 25 minutes for logistics)
- Have security present if you're worried about a reaction
- Have a plan for that person to collect their things and leave the same day

**AFTER THE CONVERSATION:**
- Tell the team quickly: 'We've decided to part ways with [person]. [New person] will take on their responsibilities starting Monday. Let's talk through the transition.'
- Don't badmouth them to the team
- Don't gossip about why they left
- Just move forward

**Real example:** Derek (SaaS sales). Un-coachable. I had the conversation. It took 7 minutes. He asked 'Why?' I explained the performance gaps and that coaching wasn't working.

He said 'Okay.' He packed his desk. He left.

And you know what? The energy on the team improved. His peers had respect for me because I'd finally made a decision."

[PAUSE: 3 seconds]

### REAL EXAMPLES/STORIES (64:00–64:45)

**THE UNCOACHABLE REP WHO GOT EXITED:**

"I had a rep—let's call her Lisa. She was in the role for eight months. Never hit quota. Always had an excuse.

'The product isn't competitive.' 'The market is down.' 'My territory is weak.' Never: 'I should try a different approach.'

After four months, I realized: Uncoachable. But I waited another four months, hoping.

When I finally let her go, her reaction was: 'Okay, this makes sense.'

Even she knew it wasn't working.

But here's the kicker: The day after she left, one of my strong performers came to me and said: 'Thank you. I was about to look for another job because I didn't think this company cared about standards.'

Letting Lisa go was the best thing I did for that team."

[PAUSE: 2 seconds]

### TENSION POINT: "What If They Need the Job?" (64:45–65:15)

"This is real. Some people are barely getting by. Losing their job is scary.

That's why, if you can, offer severance. Give them references. Help them find another role. Be decent about it.

But also understand: Keeping them in a job where they're failing isn't helping them. It's just prolonging the pain.

Sometimes the most compassionate thing is to set them free to find something where they can succeed."

"We've covered five sub-modules. Let's land this:

**60-90 Day Coaching Plan:** Structure your coaching with diagnosis, weekly coaching, and a clear decision point.
