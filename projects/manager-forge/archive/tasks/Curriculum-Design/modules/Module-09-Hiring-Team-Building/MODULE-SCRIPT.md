# MODULE 9: HIRING & TEAM BUILDING
## Video Instructor Script (65 minutes)

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE STRUCTURED HIRING PROCESS (14 minutes)

### [00:00-01:30] Opening Hook

"I want you to think about the last person you hired. Walk me through how you made that decision. Did you have a structured process, or did you... feel it out? Yeah. Most of you felt it out. And here's the thing: that's expensive. 

Every bad hire costs you 6-9 months of lost productivity, team friction, and your own mental energy. Every bad hire teaches you what you should have done differently. So today, we're going to give you a framework that takes the guesswork out of hiring. We're going to call it the **Capability-Coachability-Fit framework**, and it will change how you build your team."

**[Pause for 3 seconds]**

### [01:30-12:00] Key Teaching Points

#### Point 1: Why Gut Feel Fails

"Here's what happens: You meet a candidate. They're charismatic, easy to talk to, you'd grab coffee with them. And in your head, you think: *That's a good person. I want them on my team.* But likability is not a hiring criterion. It's a *bias*.

Real hiring is about three things, and only three things. Write these down:

1. **Capability** – Can they actually do the job?
2. **Coachability** – Will they take feedback and improve?
3. **Fit** – Do they fit your team's dynamic and values?

Most managers nail Fit (likability). Many get Capability (they lie on the resume and we believe them). Almost nobody assesses Coachability until it's too late.

Here's what happens: You hire a brilliant, charismatic person who can't take feedback. They're defensive in their one-on-ones. They blame external factors for failures. And by month 6, you realize: *I can't coach this person. They're not improvable. I should have known in the interview.*

The good news? You *could* have known. It's just a different set of questions."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

#### Point 2: The 4-Part Interview Structure

"Let's talk about how you actually assess these three things. You're going to use a 4-part interview. Here's the structure:

**PART 1: Experience Deep-Dive (25 minutes)**

You're not trying to verify their resume. You're trying to understand their growth trajectory and how they think about their work. Ask:

- "Walk me through your last 5 roles. Why did you make each move? What did you learn?"
- "Tell me about a time you didn't hit a goal. What happened? What would you do differently?"
- "What's the biggest mistake you made in your career? How did you recover?"

*Why this works:* You're looking for self-awareness. You're listening for external blame (red flag). You're understanding their growth mindset.

**PART 2: Skill Assessment – Show Me, Don't Tell Me (20 minutes)**

"I can talk myself into anything on a resume. But can you actually *do* the job? Find out.

For a sales role:
- "Here's a prospect description. Walk me through your first 48 hours: How would you research them? What would you say in the first call?"

For an operations role:
- "We just had a process breakdown. Process X failed because of Y. Walk me through how you'd redesign it."

For an analyst role:
- "Here's a dataset. What story does it tell you? What do you need to investigate further?"

*Why this works:* You see how they think. You see if they're surface-level or deep. You see if they ask clarifying questions or just run with assumptions.

**PART 3: Coachability Check (15 minutes)**

"This is the part most managers skip, and it's the most important. You're going to directly assess whether this person can take feedback.

Ask these:

- "Tell me about a piece of feedback you received that was hard to hear. How did you respond?"
- "Describe a time you were wrong about something. How did you find out? What did you do?"
- "In your last role, what's something your manager told you to do differently, and you didn't initially agree with? How did that play out?"

Listen for:
- Do they take ownership, or do they blame others?
- Do they acknowledge the feedback as valid, or do they defend their position?
- Do they show curiosity about how they could improve?

Red flags: Defensiveness. External blame. *'I knew I was right, but they just didn't get it.'* That's a sign they're not coachable.

**PART 4: Fit & Motivation (10 minutes)**

"This is about your team, not the job.

- "What kind of team environment brings out your best work?"
- "What matters to you most in a role – autonomy, structure, mentorship, learning?"
- "Why are you interested in joining *our* team, specifically?"

*Why this works:* You're checking if their work style matches your team. You're understanding if they're motivated by what your role offers. And you're assessing if they've done their homework about your company."

**[Pause for 3 seconds]**

#### Point 3: The Capability-Coachability-Fit Assessment

"After the 4-part interview, you score each dimension. Not a gut feeling – an actual assessment.

**CAPABILITY ASSESSMENT**
- Can they do the core job? (1-5 scale)
- Do they have relevant experience? (1-5 scale)
- Did they think through your scenario? (1-5 scale)
- *Threshold: You need a 4 or above to move forward.*

**COACHABILITY ASSESSMENT**
- Did they show self-awareness? (1-5 scale)
- Did they acknowledge feedback without defensiveness? (1-5 scale)
- Did they ask clarifying questions and show curiosity? (1-5 scale)
- *Threshold: You need a 4 or above to move forward. This is non-negotiable.*

**FIT ASSESSMENT**
- Do their work-style preferences match your team? (1-5 scale)
- Are they motivated by what your role offers? (1-5 scale)
- Are they genuinely interested in your company? (1-5 scale)
- *Threshold: You need a 3+ to move forward (Fit is more flexible).*

Here's the rule: **You must have a 4+ on both Capability and Coachability. If either is below 4, do not hire.**

Why? Because you can't fix incapability. And you *especially* can't fix uncoachability. Trying to coach an uncoachable person is like trying to teach someone who refuses to learn."

**[Pause for 4 seconds]**

#### Point 4: Objective Scoring vs. Gut Feel

"I know what you're thinking: *But my gut tells me this person is great, even though the scores are a 3.*

Here's what I want you to do: Write down why your gut says yes. Then show that reason to me.

Common ones:
- *'They remind me of my successful rep, Alex.'* – That's not a reason. That's bias.
- *'I just have a feeling they'll figure it out.'* – Feeling is not data.
- *'They're so likable. Everyone will want to work with them.'* – Likability doesn't hit quota.

Every single time you override your scoring system because of gut feel, you're making an emotional decision, not a strategic one. And emotional hires are expensive.

The managers who build great teams don't trust their gut. They trust their process."

### [12:00-13:00] Real Example: The Likability Trap

"Let me tell you about Jake. Jake interviewed for an SDR role with one of my managers. In the interview:

- **Capability:** Jake had done 4 SDR roles in 7 years. In the scenario, he asked smart questions and showed strong positioning skills. Score: 4.5/5.
- **Coachability:** When the manager asked about feedback, Jake said: *'My last manager didn't understand my style. I like to be autonomous.'* Red flag. When pushed: *'They wanted me to follow a script. I thought my way was better.'* Bigger red flag. Score: 2.5/5.
- **Fit:** Jake was excited. Score: 4/5.

The manager's gut said: *He's amazing. Hire him.*

I said: *His Coachability is a 2.5. Don't hire.*

Manager: *But he's so good at selling. I think I can coach him.*

He hired Jake anyway. Here's what happened:

- **Month 1:** Jake did great. Had great conversations.
- **Month 2:** The manager started coaching him on call structure and discovery questions. Jake pushed back. *'That's not how I do it.'*
- **Month 3:** Jake started missing one-on-ones. When they happened, he was defensive about feedback.
- **Month 4-5:** The team noticed Jake wasn't improving. The manager was frustrated.
- **Month 6:** The manager finally accepted: *This person isn't coachable.* He exited Jake.

That cost: 6 months of distraction, opportunity cost, and team energy.

If the manager had honored the Coachability score from day one, he would have hired someone else."

### [13:00-14:00] Tension Point: "What If They're a 3.5?"

"Here's the tension: *What if Capability is 4.2 but Coachability is 3.5?*

First instinct: *They're close. Let's give them a shot.*

My answer: **No. You don't give them a shot. You keep looking.**

Why? Because Coachability is not a threshold you can negotiate. An uncoachable person doesn't get better with time. They get more entrenched. You'll spend 6 months frustrated, trying to coach someone who doesn't want to be coached.

Capability at a 3.8? Sure, you can coach them up. Coachability at a 3.8? That's a fundamental mismatch. It's not about experience – it's about their willingness to change.

Your job is to build a team of coachable people, then invest in their capability. Not the other way around."

### [14:00-14:30] Closing Summary

"Okay, here's what you're taking away from this sub-module:

**1. Stop trusting gut feel. Start using the Capability-Coachability-Fit framework.**

**2. Use the 4-part interview structure:**
   - Experience Deep-Dive (understand their growth)
   - Skill Assessment (show me, don't tell me)
   - Coachability Check (can they take feedback?)
   - Fit & Motivation (do they match your team?)

**3. Score each dimension on a 1-5 scale. Honor the scores.**

**4. Non-negotiable threshold: 4+ on both Capability and Coachability.**

**5. If your gut says yes but the scores say no, trust the scores. Every time.**

In the next sub-module, we're going to go deep on Coachability – how to spot it, how to interview for it, and the red flags that tell you someone isn't coachable. Let's go."

## SUB-MODULE 2: ASSESSING COACHABILITY (12 minutes)

### [14:30-15:45] Opening Hook

"Here's a hard truth: In my experience, the biggest predictor of success isn't experience. It's not GPA. It's not even how smart someone is. It's whether they're coachable.

A coachable person with 2 years of experience will outrun an uncoachable person with 10 years. Every time.

So today, we're going to get surgical about what coachability actually is, how to spot it in an interview, and the red flags that tell you someone isn't coachable. Because recognizing this in the interview saves you 6 months down the line."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

### [15:45-26:00] Key Teaching Points

#### What is Coachability?

"Coachability is simple: It's the willingness and ability to receive feedback, acknowledge where you're wrong, and change behavior. That's it.

Here's what coachable people do:
- When you give them feedback, they ask clarifying questions instead of defending themselves.
- When they fail, they own it. They don't blame external factors.
- When you show them a better way, they try it, even if it's uncomfortable.
- They know they don't know everything. They're genuinely curious.

And here's what uncoachable people do:
- When you give them feedback, they explain why you're wrong.
- When they fail, they list the obstacles (the market, their team, the tools) – external blame.
- When you suggest a better way, they defend their current way.
- They're confident in their own approach. They don't question themselves.

Now, here's the thing: Uncoachable people aren't stupid. They're not lazy. They're just *stuck*. And you can't unstick them. Only they can."

#### The Five Coachability Red Flags

"Let me give you the five red flags you're listening for in the interview. If you hear more than one, seriously reconsider the hire.

**RED FLAG #1: External Blame**

When something goes wrong, coachable people own it. Uncoachable people blame circumstances.

*Coachable:* 
*"In Q3, I missed quota. I looked back, and I realized I wasn't asking the right discovery questions. I was trying to sell too fast. I changed my approach in Q4, and I hit 105%."*

*Uncoachable:*
*"In Q3, I missed quota. The market was slow, and my manager didn't support me with good leads. Also, our product was overpriced. Once I got to a team with better leadership, I hit 120%."*

See the difference? The first person owns the failure. The second person blames everyone else.

When you hire someone who blames externally, you become the excuse. *"I'm not ramping because my manager isn't teaching me. I'm not hitting quota because my territory is bad."*

**RED FLAG #2: Defensiveness to Feedback**

Ask them directly: *"Tell me about a time you got feedback you didn't initially agree with. How did you handle it?"*

*Coachable:*
*"My manager said I was rushing through discovery. I didn't think so at first. But I watched myself on a call, and she was right. It was uncomfortable to see, but I slowed down, asked better questions, and my close rate went up 20%."*

*Uncoachable:*
*"My manager said I was rushing discovery. But I've always moved fast, and it's worked. I didn't really change my approach because I know what works for me."*

Notice: Uncoachable person heard the feedback and decided it was wrong. They didn't test it. They didn't try it. They just defended their way.

When someone tells you, in the interview, that they've gotten feedback they didn't agree with and they didn't change – that's who they'll be on your team.

**RED FLAG #3: No Self-Awareness**

Ask: *"What would your last manager say is your biggest opportunity for growth?"*

*Coachable:*
*"She'd probably say I need to be more patient with long sales cycles. I like to close fast, and sometimes I'm impatient with nurturing deals. I'm working on that."*

*Uncoachable:*
*"Honestly, I don't think she'd have any major feedback. I was pretty strong in that role. Maybe she'd say I could delegate more, but that's more of a strength thing."*

Uncoachable people don't have a clear understanding of where they need to improve. They think they're already pretty good.

Coachable people can articulate their development areas specifically. They're self-aware.

**RED FLAG #4: Job-Hopping Without Growth**

"I had three jobs in four years" isn't necessarily a red flag. But *why* matters.

*Coachable:*
*"I was at Company A for 2 years. I hit my quota consistently and wanted to move into management, but the role wasn't available. So I moved to Company B, did that for 18 months, learned the SaaS space better, then moved here."*

*Uncoachable:*
*"I was at Company A, but the culture didn't work for me. Company B had bad product-market fit. I'm hoping this place is different."*

Coachable people move intentionally, for growth. Uncoachable people move because the environment wasn't good enough for them.

**RED FLAG #5: Certainty About Their Own Approach**

This is subtle, but listen for it.

*Coachable:*
*"I think my strength is relationship building. I know I need to get better at discovery and some of the technical aspects of the product. That's where I'm hoping to learn from the team."*

*Uncoachable:*
*"My approach has always been high-touch relationship building, and it works. I've never been a script-follower, and I'm not going to start now. I bring my own style."*

The second person just told you: *I won't change my approach. I'll do my style, regardless of what you teach.*

Do you want to hire that person?"

**[Pause for 4 seconds]**

#### How to Interview for Coachability

"So you know the red flags. Now, here's how to structure the questions in the interview.

**Question 1: Growth Mindset**

*"Tell me about the most significant way you've improved at your job over the last 5 years. What did you change, and why?"*

Listen for: Specific examples of change. Evidence of testing new approaches. Recognition of where they were weak and now they're strong.

**Question 2: Feedback Reception**

*"Describe the toughest feedback you've ever received. How did you feel about it at first? What did you do with it? What would you do differently now?"*

Listen for: Acknowledgment that the feedback was hard but valid. An example of actually changing behavior. Reflection on how the change helped.

**Question 3: Failure & Ownership**

*"Walk me through a significant failure. What was your responsibility in it? If you could redo it, what would you do differently?"*

Listen for: NO external blame. Clear ownership. Specific changes they'd make.

**Question 4: Curiosity**

*"What's something you don't know how to do well that you're working on learning?"*

Listen for: Genuine example, not fake humility. Evidence they're actively trying to learn.

**Question 5: Perspective Shift**

*"Is there anything your previous manager or colleague taught you that, initially, you weren't sure about, but now you see the value?"*

Listen for: Willingness to be wrong. Openness to different approaches."

#### The Coachability Scoring Rubric

"After the interview, score coachability on three dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Self-Awareness (1-5)**
- 5: Clearly articulates growth areas and recent improvements
- 3: Some self-awareness, but occasionally defensive
- 1: No awareness of opportunities for growth; thinks they're pretty solid

**Dimension 2: Feedback Reception (1-5)**
- 5: Describes hard feedback, acknowledges its validity, shows how it changed them
- 3: Takes feedback, but sometimes justifies past behavior
- 1: Gets feedback and explains why they were actually right

**Dimension 3: Growth Mindset (1-5)**
- 5: Multiple examples of trying new approaches and learning from them
- 3: Some willingness to learn; also some defensiveness about their strengths
- 1: Confident in their current approach; doesn't see the need to change much

Average all three. If the average is 4 or above, they're coachable. If it's below 3.5, they're not."

### [26:00-27:00] Real Example: Coachability vs. Experience

"Let me tell you about Sarah and Tom. Two candidates for the same sales role.

**Sarah's Background:**
- 1 year of sales experience
- At her first company, she ramped in 3 months and hit 87% quota by month 6
- No formal sales training (learned on the job)
- In the interview:
  - Coachability Questions: She talked about how her manager taught her discovery, and she was bad at it at first, but she watched recordings of herself, practiced, and now it's her strength. Score: 4.8/5
  - Capability: She didn't have as much experience, but she asked great questions and thought strategically. Score: 3.8/5

**Tom's Background:**
- 5 years of sales experience
- Consistent quota attainment across multiple companies
- Trained in the consultative sales methodology
- In the interview:
  - Capability Questions: He walked through his process, how he closes deals, his success rate. Score: 4.7/5
  - Coachability Questions: When asked about feedback, he said: *'I've always done pretty well with my own approach. My managers have been pretty happy with me.'* When pushed: *'I respect different methodologies, but I know what works for me.'* Score: 2.8/5

The manager looked at Tom's experience and wanted to hire him. But I said: *Tom has the experience, but he's not coachable. Sarah has the potential, and she's coachable.*

The manager hired Sarah.

Here's what happened:

**Sarah** ramped on your playbook. She took feedback on call structure and objection handling. By month 4, she was at 95% quota. By month 6, she was at 112%. By month 12, she was your top performer.

**Tom** (if we'd hired him) would have insisted on his own approach. He'd have friction with your coaching. By month 3, you'd be frustrated. By month 6, you'd want to exit him.

**Coachability > Experience.**"

### [27:00-28:00] Tension Point: Recognizing Your Own Bias

"Here's the challenge: When you interview someone, you might fall in love with their experience or their confidence.

Tom was *confident*. He knew his stuff. And confidence can look like competence.

But confidence without coachability is a liability. Uncoachable people are often very confident.

So in the interview, don't confuse confidence with competence. Don't confuse experience with coachability. And don't confuse likability with readiness.

Your job is to assess the three dimensions: Capability, Coachability, Fit. Stick to that."

### [28:00-29:00] Closing Summary

"Here's what you're taking from this sub-module:

**1. Coachability is the most important predictor of success.**

**2. The Five Red Flags:**
   - External Blame
   - Defensiveness to Feedback
   - No Self-Awareness
   - Job-Hopping Without Growth
   - Certainty About Their Own Approach

**3. Ask five targeted questions:**
   - Growth Mindset
   - Feedback Reception
   - Failure & Ownership
   - Curiosity
   - Perspective Shift

**4. Score Coachability on three dimensions: Self-Awareness, Feedback Reception, Growth Mindset. Target: 4 or above.**

**5. If Coachability is below 4, do not hire. The cost of coaching an uncoachable person is not worth it.**

Next, we're going to build the onboarding plan that turns new hires into productive team members in 90 days. Let's go."

## SUB-MODULE 3: THE 30-60-90 DAY ONBOARDING (14 minutes)

### [29:00-30:15] Opening Hook

"So you've hired someone great. They passed the Capability-Coachability-Fit test. Now the real work starts.

Here's what most managers do: They throw the new hire into the job, give them some training, and hope for the best. By day 90, the new hire is confused, the manager is frustrated, and the hire is behind.

Here's what the best managers do: They build a structured 30-60-90 onboarding plan, and they coach through every stage.

And here's what happens: The new hire ramps faster, hits quota sooner, and feels like they're part of a team that invested in them.

So today, we're going to build your 30-60-90 plan, stage by stage."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

### [30:15-42:00] Key Teaching Points

#### The Three Stages

"The 30-60-90 plan has three distinct stages:

**STAGE 1: LEARN (Days 1-30)**

Goal: The new hire understands the product, the process, and the environment. They're not productive yet. They're absorbing.

**STAGE 2: PRACTICE (Days 31-60)**

Goal: The new hire starts executing (calls, deals, tasks) with coaching. They're learning by doing. They're making mistakes with guardrails.

**STAGE 3: PERFORM (Days 61-90)**

Goal: The new hire is executing independently, with periodic coaching. They're ramping toward quota. They're hitting milestones.

Here's the critical insight: Most managers throw people into Stage 3 on Day 1. *'Here's your quota. Go hit it.'* That's a recipe for failure.

The managers who build great teams honor each stage. They don't rush Stage 1. They don't drop coaching in Stage 2. They don't assume independence in Stage 3."

#### Stage 1: Learn (Days 1-30)

"Days 1-7: Onboarding Week

*Objective:* New hire feels welcomed, understands the company, knows where things are, knows who to talk to.

**Day 1:**
- Welcome email from CEO (shows company cares)
- Meet with direct manager: Here's your role, here's what success looks like, here's how we'll work together
- Office tour, meet the team
- Set up computer, email, tools
- Evening: Team dinner (optional, but good for connection)

**Day 2-3:**
- Company history, mission, strategy (45 min training)
- Intro calls with 3-5 key people (sales leadership, product, operations, etc.)
- Product training part 1: Understand what you're selling (not how to sell it yet)

**Day 4-5:**
- Product training part 2: Go deeper
- Role-specific process training: Here's how we hire (for sales), here's how we scope (for operations), etc.
- Shadowing: Sit in on calls, meetings, customer interactions
- CRM/tool training: Here's how we track things

**Day 6-7:**
- Review: What are your biggest questions?
- One-on-one with manager: How's your first week? Do you feel oriented?
- No deliverables. No expectations. Just learning.

**Key Principle:** You're setting the tone that this company invests in onboarding. That's part of your culture.

---

Days 8-14: Core Competency Training

*Objective:* New hire knows the fundamentals of their job.

For a Sales Role:
- Call training: How do we position? How do we discover? How do we handle objections?
- Sales process walkthrough: Prospecting → Discovery → Demo → Negotiation → Close
- Role-play: Manager and peer play objection scenarios
- Listening to recorded calls (good and bad examples)
- Starting to listen to live calls (not participating yet)

For an Operations Role:
- Process walkthrough: Here's how hiring works end-to-end
- Tool training: How do we use spreadsheets, databases, Asana, etc.?
- Observing the process: Sitting in on interviews, check-ins, etc.
- Starting to handle small tasks (job posting setup, email templates)

For an Analytics Role:
- Data fundamentals: What data do we have? How is it structured?
- Dashboard walkthrough: Here's what we measure
- Tools training: SQL, Tableau, Python, Excel (whatever you use)
- Case studies: Here's how we solved problems with data

**Key Principle:** Depth over breadth. They don't need to know everything. They need to master the fundamentals.

---

Days 15-30: Integration & Preparation

*Objective:* New hire is ready to start practicing in Stage 2.

- Shadowing continues (more calls, more meetings)
- Start listening to customer calls independently and writing notes
- Attempt some low-stakes tasks (cold email templates, process documentation, etc.)
- Weekly manager one-on-ones: What's clicking? What's confusing? What do you need?
- Mock scenarios: If it's sales, do role-plays with peers. If it's operations, run through a hiring scenario.
- Week 4 checkpoint: Manager reviews – Ready to move to Stage 2? Or do we need more learning time?

**Key Principle:** By day 30, they should be ready to start executing. They won't be good yet, but they're prepared.

**End of Stage 1 Milestone:**
- New hire can articulate your value proposition
- New hire understands your process, top to bottom
- New hire knows who to ask for help
- New hire has done no billable work yet (that's fine)"

#### Stage 2: Practice (Days 31-60)

"Days 31-45: Supervised Execution

*Objective:* New hire starts executing with close coaching. Every action is reviewed before or after.

For a Sales Role:
- Make 10 dials per day (manager listens to 50%)
- Have 2 discovery calls per day (manager either listens or reviews notes)
- Follow sales process: If they're supposed to send a follow-up, they show manager before sending
- Manager reviews every call, every deal, every email
- Daily debrief (15 min): What went well? What didn't? What should you do differently tomorrow?
- No quota expectations yet (they're learning, not producing)

For an Operations Role:
- Post 2 jobs (with manager review)
- Screen 20 resumes (with manager feedback)
- Conduct 3 interviews (manager present or observer)
- Daily or every-other-day checkins: How did it go? What would you do differently?

For an Analytics Role:
- Build 2 simple dashboards (with manager QA)
- Query 5 datasets (manager reviews for correctness)
- Write 2 analyses (manager reviews the narrative)
- Every work product gets feedback before it goes live

**Key Principle:** Everything is supervised. Your job isn't to judge – it's to teach. You're saying: *Here's the right way. Let me show you.*

**Manager's Time Commitment:** This is expensive. You're spending 1-2 hours per day coaching this person. That's normal.

---

Days 46-60: Increasing Independence

*Objective:* New hire is executing more independently. Manager reviews actions (sometimes after, not just before).

For a Sales Role:
- Make 15 dials per day (manager listens to 20-30%)
- Have 3-4 discovery calls per day (manager reviews notes, not listening to every call)
- Manager reviews deals before the prospect meeting (not after)
- Weekly debrief instead of daily
- Start tracking activity and pipeline
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 50% of quota

For an Operations Role:
- Post 5 jobs per week (manager reviews posting strategy, not every detail)
- Conduct 8-10 interviews per week (manager joins some, but not all)
- Manager reviews final decisions before offers go out
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 50% of quota

For an Analytics Role:
- Build dashboards with less oversight
- Run analyses and present to team (manager reviews beforehand)
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 50% of quota

**Key Principle:** They're still learning, but you're gradually releasing control. By day 60, they should be able to execute a task 80% correctly, with periodic feedback.

**End of Stage 2 Milestone:**
- New hire has executed their job 50+ times
- New hire is hitting 50% of quota/goal
- New hire knows your process so well they could explain it to someone else
- New hire has a relationship with 3-5 peers who can help them
- Manager is confident about Stage 3 readiness (or you need to extend)"

#### Stage 3: Perform (Days 61-90)

"Days 61-75: Near-Full Responsibility

*Objective:* New hire is executing independently. Manager provides strategic coaching, not tactical reviews.

For a Sales Role:
- Run their own activity plan: 20+ dials, 4-5 discovery calls per day
- Manager listens to 1-2 calls per week (not every call)
- Pipeline reviews: Weekly look at deals, where they're stuck, what's next
- Coaching: More about strategy (how to position against competitor X) than tactics (how to dial)
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 75-80% of quota

For an Operations Role:
- Run hiring pipeline independently
- Post jobs, screen, conduct interviews, make offers without step-by-step oversight
- Manager reviews results (offer acceptance rate, time to hire)
- Coaching: Strategic (what hiring attributes are we undervaluing?) not tactical
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 75-80% of quota

For an Analytics Role:
- Identify their own questions and build analyses
- Present insights to leadership (manager reviews beforehand)
- Coaching: How to tell a story with data, how to influence decisions
- Quota expectation: Ramp to 75-80% of quota

**Coaching Shift:** You're no longer saying, *Do this, then tell me about it.* You're saying, *Here's the strategic challenge. How would you approach it?*

---

Days 76-90: Full Performance

*Objective:* New hire is performing like a team member. Manager provides coaching on growth, not onboarding.

- Activity and outcome metrics should match senior team members
- Manager involvement drops to monthly or quarterly strategic reviews
- One-on-ones shift from *How's ramp going?* to *What's your growth plan?*
- Quota expectation: Hitting 90%+ of quota (or equivalent metric)

**End of Stage 3 Milestone:**
- New hire is hitting 90%+ of quota
- New hire operates independently
- Manager is coaching them on growth, not execution
- New hire feels like a full team member (not a ramp)"

#### A Day-by-Day Example: Sarah's Onboarding (Sales Role)

"Let me walk you through an actual 30-60-90 plan for Sarah, an SDR we hired.

**WEEK 1 (Days 1-5)**
- Day 1: Welcome, company orientation, meet the team
- Days 2-3: Product training, tool setup
- Days 4-5: Sales process overview, listen to 5 recorded calls

By day 90, Sarah hit 95% quota. She felt supported. She knew the process. She was ready to perform."

#### The Weekly Checkpoint

"Every Friday, have a 30-minute checkpoint with your new hire. Ask:

*Week 1-4:*

This is not performance review. This is coaching checkpoint. It shows the new hire that you're invested in their success."

Week 9-12: Lisa ramped from 50% to 90% quota.

## SUB-MODULE 4: TEAM COMPOSITION STRATEGY (13 minutes)

### [45:30-46:45] Opening Hook

"Imagine you built a team where everyone was just like you. Same strengths, same weaknesses, same way of working. How would that team perform?

Well, you'd have a team that's good at the things you're good at. And terrible at the things you're bad at.

That's what most managers do. They hire clones. Then they wonder why their team gets blindsided by market changes, why they can't handle customer complexity, why they get burned out.

So today, we're going to talk about archetypes: the different types of people your team needs. And how to build a team that's balanced, not just comfortable."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

### [46:45-57:00] Key Teaching Points

#### The Team Archetypes Model

"There are six key archetypes on great teams. Most managers hire from 1-2 archetypes. Great managers hire across all six.

**ARCHETYPE 1: THE HUNTER**

The Hunter is the prospector. The rainmaker. The person who loves the chase and doesn't stop until they close.

Strengths:
- Driven by new business
- Doesn't back down from rejection
- Takes action fast
- Competitive

Weaknesses:
- Can be impatient with long deals
- Doesn't nurture relationships
- Sometimes oversells (overpromises the product)
- Can burn relationships in pursuit of quota

A team full of Hunters: Great at prospecting, terrible at farming and nurturing relationships. They'll close fast, but they'll lose deals in stage 2 of the pipeline.

**ARCHETYPE 2: THE FARMER**

The Farmer is the nurturer. The relationship builder. The person who owns the complexity and guides prospects through a multi-month decision.

Strengths:
- Patient with long sales cycles
- Builds trust with prospects
- Handles complex, political deals
- Retains customers

Weaknesses:
- Can be slow to prospect
- Doesn't like pressure or quotas
- Sometimes avoids the close
- Can get comfortable with prospects without pushing forward

A team full of Farmers: Great at nurturing, terrible at prospecting. They'll have amazing relationships, but they won't fill the pipeline.

**Why you need both:** Hunters fill the pipeline. Farmers nurture it. Together: growth.

**ARCHETYPE 3: THE ANALYTICAL**

The Analytical is the data person. The detail-oriented, structured thinker. They want to understand the system, not just execute it.

Strengths:
- Finds inefficiencies
- Builds repeatable processes
- Makes data-driven decisions
- Catches mistakes

Weaknesses:
- Can be paralyzed by analysis
- Wants perfection before execution
- Can slow down the team
- Doesn't like ambiguity

A team full of Analyticals: Perfect process, slow execution. You'll have ideal playbooks, but you won't move fast.

**ARCHETYPE 4: THE RELATIONAL**

The Relational is the people person. The emotional intelligence, intuition-driven, human-focused thinker. They care about how things *feel*.

Strengths:
- Reads people well
- Builds team cohesion
- Notices when something's off
- Great at mentoring

Weaknesses:
- Can avoid hard data
- Makes decisions based on gut feel
- Can be too soft on problems
- Doesn't like numbers or structure

A team full of Relationals: Great culture, unclear strategy. You'll have happy people, but you won't have direction.

**Why you need both:** Analyticals provide structure. Relationals provide humanity. Together: sustainable growth with team health.

**ARCHETYPE 5: THE FAST-MOVER**

The Fast-Mover is the action-oriented person. They see a problem, they fix it. They make decisions quickly and move on.

Strengths:
- Gets things done
- Energizes the team
- Doesn't overthink
- Adapts quickly

Weaknesses:
- Can make rushed decisions
- Doesn't always think through consequences
- Can create chaos
- Doesn't plan ahead

A team full of Fast-Movers: Lots of action, no strategy. You'll move fast, but you might move in the wrong direction.

**ARCHETYPE 6: THE DEEP-THINKER**

The Deep-Thinker is the strategic person. The person who sees patterns, thinks long-term, and doesn't move until they've thought it through.

Strengths:
- Sees around corners
- Plans strategically
- Prevents mistakes through foresight
- Thinks systemically

Weaknesses:
- Can be slow to act
- Overthinks opportunities
- Can get stuck in analysis
- Doesn't like being rushed

A team full of Deep-Thinkers: Perfect strategy, slow execution. You'll have a great plan, but you won't move.

**Why you need both:** Fast-Movers create action. Deep-Thinkers provide direction. Together: execution with strategy."

**[Pause for 3 seconds]**

#### How to Assess Your Team

"Here's what you're going to do: Take your current team (let's say you have 8 people). For each person, ask:

*Are they more of a Hunter or a Farmer?*
*Are they more Analytical or Relational?*
*Are they more of a Fast-Mover or a Deep-Thinker?*

Plot them on a simple map:

```
HUNTERS (prospecting energy)     |     FARMERS (nurturing energy)

ANALYTICAL (data-driven)         |     RELATIONAL (people-driven)

FAST-MOVERS (action bias)        |     DEEP-THINKERS (strategic)
```

Most teams look like this:

```
5 Hunters, 1 Farmer
5 Analyticals, 1 Relational  
5 Fast-Movers, 1 Deep-Thinker
```

Why? Because most managers hire people like themselves. If you're a Hunter, you hire Hunters. If you're Analytical, you hire Analyticals. And boom – homogenous team.

A balanced team looks like this:

```
3 Hunters, 3 Farmers, 1 manager who can do both
1 Analytical, 1 Relational, 1 flexible (can do both)
1-2 Fast-Movers, 1 Deep-Thinker, 1 flexible
```

The key: You have some of each. You're not optimized for one thing. You're resilient across all dimensions."

#### Example: The Clone Team That Failed

"Here's a real example. A manager built a team of 5 sales reps. All Hunters. All aggressive, quick-closing, competitive.

Strengths:
- Q1 and Q2 crushing it. New business pipeline was incredible.
- Team was energetic, competitive, fast-moving.
- Hit 120% quota in Q1.

Then Q3 hit:
- The Hunters had closed all the easy deals. Now they needed to nurture complex, 4-month deals.
- But Hunters don't nurture. They want the fast close.
- So they kept prospecting instead of nurturing their pipeline.
- Pipeline dried up.
- Q3: 85% quota.

Meanwhile, the customers they'd sold to (via aggressive closing) were complaining. The Hunters had overpromised. Customer satisfaction was bad. Churn was high.

By Q4, the manager realized: *We have a Hunter team. We need some Farmers.*

He hired two Farmers to nurture existing deals and accounts. But it took a quarter to realize the gap.

If he'd started with 3 Hunters and 2 Farmers, he would have crushed Q1-Q4, not just Q1-Q2."

#### Building Your Next Hire Plan

"When you plan your next hire, ask:

*What archetype gaps do I have?*

If your team is all Hunters, you need a Farmer.
If your team is all Analytical, you need a Relational.
If your team is all Fast-Movers, you need a Deep-Thinker.

And here's the key: When you interview, score them on archetypes too.

In the interview, ask:

For **Hunter vs. Farmer**: *'In your last role, what percentage of your time was spent on new business vs. managing existing accounts? Which did you enjoy more?'*

For **Analytical vs. Relational**: *'How do you make decisions – what data do you look at, or how do you feel about a situation?'*

For **Fast-Mover vs. Deep-Thinker**: *'Give me an example of when you moved quickly. And an example of when you took time to plan before acting. Which one felt more natural?'*

Their answers will tell you their archetype. Hire what you're missing, not what you already have."

### [57:00-58:15] Real Example: Building a Balanced Team

"Let me tell you about a sales manager who did this right.

She inherited a team of 5 Account Executives, all closing deals in 2-3 months (Hunters). No one was nurturing multi-quarter enterprise deals. No one was focused on customer success after the close.

She had a 12-month period to hire 2 more reps. Here's what she did:

*Month 3:* She hired Mike, a Farmer. Mike's background: 7 years managing complex enterprise deals. He took existing accounts and built them out.

*Month 9:* She hired Alex, also technically a sales rep, but really a Farmer with a sideline in customer success. Alex understood the post-sale, and he'd be the bridge to the customer success team.

By month 12:
- Her Hunters were crushing new business (staying at 120% quota on new bookings)
- Mike and Alex had taken 15 existing accounts and grown them 35% (expansion revenue)
- Customer satisfaction went from 6.8 to 8.2
- Churn went from 18% to 8%

The team went from optimized-for-new-business to balanced-for-total-growth. And her bonus reflected that."

### [58:15-59:15] Tension Point: Archetypes Are Flexible

"Here's the important caveat: No one is purely one archetype. Everyone has tendencies.

Sarah might be mostly a Hunter, but she has some Farmer skills. Tom might be mostly Analytical, but he's got some Relational instincts.

So when you're building your team, you're not looking for perfect archetypes. You're looking for **balance of tendencies.**

Also: You can develop archetype skills. A Hunter can learn to nurture. An Analytical can learn to read people.

But here's the truth: People are more comfortable with their archetype tendencies. So if you hire a Hunter and ask them to farm, they'll do it, but they'll be less energized. And over time, they'll drift back to what feels natural.

So the real insight is: **Hire people with complementary natural tendencies. Then you don't have to fight nature to get balance.**

A team of Hunters with one Farmer is still a Hunter team. A team with 3 Hunters and 2 Farmers and 1 flexible person – that's balanced."

### [59:15-60:00] Closing Summary

"Here's what you're taking from this sub-module:

**1. Most managers hire clones. Great managers hire across archetypes.**

**2. The Six Archetypes:**
   - Hunter (prospecting) vs. Farmer (nurturing)
   - Analytical (data) vs. Relational (people)
   - Fast-Mover (action) vs. Deep-Thinker (strategy)

**3. Audit your current team. Where are the gaps?**

**4. In your next hire, ask archetype questions. Hire what you're missing, not what you already have.**

**5. Balanced teams are resilient. Homogenous teams fail when conditions change.**

Next, we're going to talk about the most painful part of hiring: recognizing bad hires early and acting fast. Let's go."

## SUB-MODULE 5: HIRING MISTAKES & RECOVERY (12 minutes)

### [60:00-61:15] Opening Hook

"Here's the hard truth: You're going to hire someone who doesn't work out. Even with all the frameworks, all the scoring, all the process – you'll hire a 2.8 coachability person and hope they improve.

The question isn't *if* you'll make a bad hire. The question is *when*, and more importantly, *how fast you'll recognize it and fix it*.

Most managers waste 6-9 months trying to coach someone who isn't coachable. That's expensive. Great managers recognize bad hires by day 60 and make a decision by day 90.

So today, we're going to talk about the red flags, the day 60 assessment, and how to have the exit conversation if it's needed."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

### [61:15-69:00] Key Teaching Points

#### Bad Hire Recognition Checklist

"By day 60, you have enough data to know if someone's going to work out. Here's the checklist:

**Capability Checkpoint:**

*For a sales role:*
- [ ] Has the rep made 20+ dials? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Has the rep had 5+ discovery calls? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Did the rep understand the value prop? (If no, red flag)
- [ ] Did the rep ask smart questions in calls? (Or did they just pitch?)
- Score: Is capability improving, or declining? 1-5 scale.

*For an operations role:*
- [ ] Has the hire posted 3+ jobs? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Has the hire screened 25+ candidates? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Did the hire understand your hiring criteria? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Is the hire writing good job descriptions and feedback? (Or just going through motions?)
- Score: Is capability improving, or declining? 1-5 scale.

*For an analytics role:*
- [ ] Has the analyst built 2+ dashboards? (If not, red flag)
- [ ] Did the analyst query the data accurately? (Or are there errors?)
- [ ] Did the analyst write analyses that tell a story? (Or just dump data?)
- [ ] Is the analyst asking questions and digging deeper? (Or just executing tasks?)
- Score: Is capability improving, or declining? 1-5 scale.

**Coachability Checkpoint:**

- [ ] Have you given the hire feedback on 3+ occasions? (You should have by day 60)
- [ ] Did they acknowledge the feedback? (Or did they defend their approach?)
- [ ] Did they change behavior based on the feedback? (Or did they revert?)
- [ ] Did they ask clarifying questions? (Or did they explain why you were wrong?)
- Score: Is coachability improving, or declining? 1-5 scale.

**Engagement Checkpoint:**

- [ ] Is the hire participating in team meetings? (Or quiet/withdrawn?)
- [ ] Are they asking for help? (Or isolated?)
- [ ] Are they following the process? (Or doing their own thing?)
- [ ] Do they seem energized? (Or demoralized?)
- Score: Is engagement improving, or declining? 1-5 scale.

**The Day 60 Assessment:**

Average your three scores (Capability, Coachability, Engagement).

- **Score 4+:** Keep going. The hire is ramping. You're on track for success.
- **Score 3-3.5:** Caution. The hire is struggling on one dimension. Create a 30-day improvement plan (see below).
- **Score <3:** Serious risk. The hire is not working out. Prepare for exit or hard reset.

If your gut says *'This person might not work out'* – trust your gut. And pull the data. Most likely, the data backs you up."

**[Pause for 3 seconds]**

#### The 30-Day Improvement Plan

"If the day 60 assessment is 3-3.5, you create a 30-day improvement plan before day 90.

Here's the structure:

**Step 1: Be Honest**

In a 1-on-1, say: *'I want to be direct with you. At day 60, I'm seeing that you're struggling with [specific area – capability, coachability, engagement]. This is concerning because [why it matters]. I'm not ready to give up, but we need to be clear: between now and day 90, we need to see significant improvement in [specific behavior].'*

**Step 2: Be Specific**

Don't say: *'You need to improve your skills.'*

Say: *'By day 90, you need to be completing 20 dials per day, asking discovery questions in 80%+ of calls, and applying feedback from our one-on-ones.'*

Specific, measurable behaviors.

**Step 3: Provide Support**

*'Here's what I'm going to do to help: We're going to have daily check-ins instead of weekly. We're going to role-play objections twice a week. And I'm going to sit in on your calls every day and give you feedback immediately.'*

**Step 4: Set the Consequence**

*'If we don't see improvement by day 90, we'll have to make a change. That might be moving you to a different role, or us parting ways. I hope we don't get there, but you deserve to know the stakes.'*

This is not a threat. This is clarity. Clarity is kind. Ambiguity is cruel.

**Step 5: Coach Intensively**

Between day 60 and day 90, you're going to spend more time coaching this person, not less. You're giving them a real chance to improve.

But here's the key: If they're not coachable, all your coaching won't help. They'll make excuses. They'll defend their approach. They'll blame external factors.

If they are coachable, they'll ask for feedback. They'll try the new approach. They'll improve."

**[Pause for 2 seconds]**

#### The Day 90 Decision Point

"By day 90, you make one of three decisions:

**Decision 1: KEEP THEM**

The hire ramped. Capability is 4+. Coachability is 4+. They're hitting 90%+ quota. You're moving to standard coaching.

What to say: *'I'm really pleased with your progress. You came in as a learner, and you've become a full performer. Here's what I'm excited about for your growth over the next 6 months: [next-level goals]. You're part of this team.'*

**Decision 2: KEEP THEM IN A DIFFERENT ROLE**

The hire is coachable and engaged, but they don't have the capability for this specific role. Maybe they're a great operations person, but they're not a great sales person.

What to say: *'I've noticed that your strengths are [X, Y, Z], but the core of this sales role is [A, B], and that's not where you naturally excel. I think you'd be much happier, and more successful, in a role that plays to your strengths. Would you be open to moving to [different role]?'*

This is kind. It's not exile. It's alignment.

**Decision 3: EXIT THEM**

The hire is not making it. Capability is below 3.5. Or, coachability is below 3.5 (they're not taking feedback).

What to say: *'I've really appreciated working with you, and I respect your effort. But at day 90, I can see that this role isn't the right fit. We're both going to be frustrated if we drag this on further. I think it's best if we part ways. Here's what I'm going to do to support you: [severance, reference, etc.]'*

Be direct. Be kind. Don't let it drag."

#### Bad Hire Recovery: Real Examples

"**Example 1: The Experience-Without-Coachability Bad Hire**

Marcus was hired as a senior account manager. 8 years of experience. Impressive resume. Great interview.

Capability assessment: Strong. He knew the space.
Coachability assessment: 2.8. In feedback conversations, he was defensive. *'That's not how I do it.'*

By day 30, his manager was already frustrated. By day 60, it was clear: Marcus wasn't going to change his approach, and his approach didn't match the company's methodology.

His manager let it go until day 120. By then, Marcus had burned bridges with two prospects. The team was annoyed at his attitude.

When the manager finally said, *'This isn't working,'* Marcus was already job-searching.

**Cost:** 4 months of distraction + prospect damage.

What the manager should have done: At day 60, recognized low coachability, and exited at day 90.

---

**Example 2: The Coachable Learner in the Wrong Role**

Jennifer was hired as a sales rep. In the interview, she seemed capable. But she was young, and she'd only done account management (nurturing), not new business.

Day 30: She was struggling with prospecting. She's coachable, though – she asked for help.

Day 60: With coaching, she'd gotten better at prospecting, but she wasn't energized. She kept talking about account management.

Day 90: She'd hit 60% quota on new business, which was fine, but she wasn't excited.

Her manager said: *'I see your strength is in relationship management and account growth, not prospecting. Want to move to an account manager role?'*

Jennifer's eyes lit up. She moved roles. By month 4 in the new role, she was crushing it.

**Cost:** 90 days of the manager seeing the mismatch and coaching around it. But outcome: great hire, wrong role, solved.

---

**Example 3: The Coachable High-Performer**

Alex was hired as an operations manager. Day 60, he was hitting all his metrics. Day 90, he was exceeding them. He asked for feedback. He implemented it. He was coachable and capable.

Clear keep. By month 6, he was running a process that the team had needed for 2 years.

**Cost:** Structured onboarding worked. One great hire staying."

**[Pause for 3 seconds]**

### [69:00-70:15] Tension Point: Sunk Cost Bias

"Here's the hardest part of bad hire recovery: Sunk cost bias.

You've invested 4 months in this person. You've trained them. You've given them chances. And now you're thinking: *If I exit them, all that investment is wasted.*

That's sunk cost bias. The investment is already gone. The question isn't *Did I waste the investment?* The question is *Will keeping them cost me more than exiting them?*

If you keep an uncoachable person for 6 more months, hoping they'll change, you're not recovering the investment. You're compounding the loss.

So here's the rule: **If day 60 assessment says <3, plan for exit by day 90. Don't wait for 6 months.**

It's hard. It's emotional. But it's the right call."

### [70:15-71:00] Closing Summary

"Here's what you're taking from this sub-module:

**1. Bad hires happen. It's not a failure – it's a learning opportunity.**

**2. By day 60, you should know if someone's working out. Use the checklist: Capability, Coachability, Engagement.**

**3. Day 60 Score:**
   - 4+: Keep going
   - 3-3.5: 30-day improvement plan
   - <3: Plan for exit

**4. If you create a 30-day improvement plan, be specific about behaviors and consequences. Then coach intensively.**

**5. Day 90 Decision: Keep them, move them to a different role, or exit them. Be direct and kind.**

**6. Don't let sunk cost bias make you keep someone who isn't working. Exiting fast is kinder than dragging it out.**

That's Module 9: Hiring & Team Building. You now have the frameworks, the processes, and the real examples to hire great people, build balanced teams, and recover from mistakes.

The key insight: Hiring isn't luck. It's a system. Build the system, follow the system, and you'll build great teams."
