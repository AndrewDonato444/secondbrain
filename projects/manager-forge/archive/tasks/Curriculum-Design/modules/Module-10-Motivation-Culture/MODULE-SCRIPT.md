## SUB-MODULE 1: THE CULTURE AUDIT
**[00:45–13:00] — 12 minutes**

### Opening Hook (1:45 min)
**[00:45–02:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Every manager has a *stated* culture and an *actual* culture
- Stated culture: what's on your website, what you say in meetings
- Actual culture: what your team rewards, who stays, what's really celebrated
- The gap between these two is where toxicity lives
- Listen for signals: What do reps say in Slack when you're not there? What behavior gets promoted? Who leaves?

**SCRIPT:**

"I was sitting with a manager named Sarah last month. Her company's website says: 'We celebrate wins big and small. Everyone matters.'

Nice, right?

But when I asked Sarah, 'When's the last time you celebrated someone for closing a small deal?' She went quiet.

Then she said, 'Honestly? We only really celebrate big deals. Top deals. That's where the attention goes.'

So on her team, if you close $5K, nobody notices. Close $50K, you're the hero. That's her *actual* culture.

That gap—between what she said and what she does—is poison. Because everyone on her team gets the real message: *'Small wins don't matter. You only matter if you're closing big.'*

That's not stated anywhere. But every rep knows it.

Today, we're going to find the gaps in your culture. Because you can't fix what you don't see."

---

### Key Teaching Points (10 min)
**[02:30–12:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**

**PART 1: STATED VS. ACTUAL CULTURE (3 min)**
- Stated culture is what you *believe* should happen
- Actual culture is revealed through three signals: behavior you reward, who stays, who leaves
- Example: Manager says "we're collaborative" but promotes the person who closes the most deals solo
- People believe *actions*, not words
- Your actual culture is what new hires figure out in week one

**PART 2: THE SIGNALS (4 min)**
- **Behavior you reward:** Who gets promoted? Whose wins get celebrated? Whose failures get coaching vs. shame?
- **Who stays:** The people who fit your actual culture stay; mismatches leave
- **Who leaves:** Exit interviews are gold. People tell you why they're really leaving when they have nothing to lose
- **What's invisible:** Slack conversations. Side conversations. What people say when you're not there.

**PART 3: WHY THE GAP (3 min)**
- Managers don't intentionally create toxic gaps—they're usually blind to them
- You're so close to your team, you don't see what's broken
- You think you're rewarding "hustlers" but you're actually rewarding "people who never take days off"
- You think you're celebrating "wins" but you're only celebrating revenue, not growth or learning
- The gap grows when you're busy; no one's protecting culture, so default behavior (usually toxic) takes over

## SUB-MODULE 2: REMOVING DEMOTIVATORS
**[13:00–26:00] — 13 minutes**

### Opening Hook (1:45 min)
**[13:00–14:45]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- You can't motivate someone out of demotivation
- If the fundamentals are broken (no clarity, no fairness, no growth), adding a bonus won't help
- The five demotivators are universal: clarity, fairness, growth, connection, trust
- A team with broken fundamentals will resent rewards; a team with solid fundamentals will thrive on intrinsic motivation
- Your job as manager: remove the things that drain energy *before* you try to add things that inspire it

**SCRIPT:**

"I see a lot of managers try to motivate their way out of problems they should be *fixing*.

'Morale is low, let's do a contest. Let's offer a bonus. Let's have a team lunch.'

Meanwhile, the team is thinking: *'He's not listening. He doesn't see what's broken.'*

Here's the truth: You cannot motivate your way out of demotivation. 

If your team doesn't know if they're succeeding, a bonus won't help.
If fairness is broken—some people get flexible schedules, some don't—perks don't fix it.
If there's no growth path, no one sees a future, a team lunch feels insulting.

Before you try to inspire people, you have to remove what's draining them.

There are five things. Once you know them, you can fix them. And once the fundamentals are solid, people will *want* to show up."

---

### Key Teaching Points (10 min)
**[14:45–24:45]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**

**THE FIVE DEMOTIVATORS (in order of priority):**

**1. NO CLARITY (2 min)**
- Reps don't know if they're winning or losing
- They can't see what success looks like
- Nobody knows what the actual standards are (different for different people)
- Fix: Clear metrics, regular feedback, visible progress

**2. NO FAIRNESS (2 min)**
- Rules apply differently to different people
- Some get coaching, some get fired for the same miss
- Compensation isn't transparent or justifiable
- Fix: Consistent standards, visible rubrics, explanation of differences

**3. NO GROWTH (2 min)**
- There's no path forward
- They'll be doing the same thing in 2 years
- Development is lip service, not funded
- Fix: Quarterly career conversations, visible promotion path, skill investment

**4. NO CONNECTION (2 min)**
- They feel invisible and replaceable
- No one knows them; no one celebrates their wins
- They're part of a team only on the org chart
- Fix: 1:1s, recognition, belonging rituals

**5. NO TRUST (2 min)**
- They don't believe leadership
- They see inconsistency, favoritism, or hidden agendas
- Transparency is low; surprises are bad
- Fix: Radical honesty, predictable decision-making, follow through on promises

---

### FIVE DEMOTIVATORS CHECKLIST

**INSERT HERE: Diagnostic Checklist**

*For each demotivator, answer honestly:*

#### 1. CLARITY: Does your team know what success looks like?

- [ ] You have written metrics for each role (not just revenue)
- [ ] Every rep can tell you how they're progressing (weekly, not annual)
- [ ] Success looks the same for everyone (or you've clearly explained why it doesn't)
- [ ] When someone is underperforming, they heard it from you in a 1:1, not from a surprise PIP

**If <3 boxes checked: CLARITY IS BROKEN**

*Fix:*
- Create a simple one-page document: "What Success Looks Like for a [Role]"
- Include: metrics (revenue, deal count, customer health), behaviors (collaboration, learning), outcomes (retention, NPS)
- Share it with the team. Ask them to rate themselves weekly
- In 1:1s, show the gap between their rating and yours. No surprises.

---

#### 2. FAIRNESS: Do your rules apply equally?

- [ ] You've documented performance standards (not just gut feel)
- [ ] If someone got a second chance for missing quota, others would get the same second chance
- [ ] You don't have a "star" who operates under different rules than everyone else
- [ ] Compensation differences are explainable (not arbitrary)
- [ ] Feedback is consistent: same behavior gets the same response, regardless of who does it

**If <3 boxes checked: FAIRNESS IS BROKEN**

*Fix:*
- Write down your actual performance standards (what you *really* expect)
- Next time two people do something similar, treat them the same
- If you have "stars" who are exempt from rules, recognize that your team sees it—and it's toxic
- Document why comp is different (years, specialization, etc.). Be ready to explain.

---

#### 3. GROWTH: Is there a path forward?

- [ ] You've had a career conversation with each person in the past 6 months
- [ ] They know what the next role/level is and what it takes
- [ ] You're investing in their development (time, training, stretch projects)
- [ ] Someone has been promoted or developed into a new role in the past 12 months
- [ ] You're not the only one they learn from (mentors, external training, etc.)

**If <3 boxes checked: GROWTH IS BROKEN**

*Fix:*
- Schedule quarterly "career conversations" (non-negotiable)
- Ask: "What do you want to be able to do that you can't do now?"
- For each person, identify one stretch project or skill to build
- If there's no path up, be honest about lateral moves, external opportunities, or skill building
- Don't fake a growth path; it's insulting.

---

#### 4. CONNECTION: Do people feel like they belong?

- [ ] You do regular 1:1s (weekly, non-negotiable)
- [ ] You know something personal about each person (kid's name, hobby, what they're stressed about)
- [ ] You celebrate small wins publicly, not just big deals
- [ ] People feel seen and valued as individuals, not just as salespeople
- [ ] When someone has a rough week, they feel supported, not judged

**If <3 boxes checked: CONNECTION IS BROKEN**

*Fix:*
- Start a "wins wall" (Slack channel, whiteboard, etc.) where anything counts: small deal, customer feedback, skill mastered
- In 1:1s, ask personal questions. Remember the answers.
- Call out someone's win in the team channel. Make it specific. Make it visible.
- Host a weekly ritual where the team *together* celebrates. (See Module 4: Rituals)

---

#### 5. TRUST: Do they believe you?

- [ ] You communicate bad news directly, not through rumors
- [ ] You've admitted when you were wrong
- [ ] Your yes means yes; your no means no (you don't flip-flop)
- [ ] They don't find out about changes from someone other than you
- [ ] You do what you say you'll do (or you explain why you can't and propose an alternative)

**If <3 boxes checked: TRUST IS BROKEN**

*Fix:*
- Go to your team. Say: "I realize I haven't been transparent about [X]. Here's what's really going on."
- Make one commitment you'll keep: "I will tell you directly about any changes that affect you, before rumors start."
- Follow up with consistency. Trust is slow to build and fast to break.
- If you've broken trust, acknowledge it. "I said I'd [X], and I didn't. That was on me. Here's how I'll do better."

## SUB-MODULE 3: BUILDING INTRINSIC MOTIVATION
**[25:45–39:00] — 13 minutes**

### Opening Hook (1:45 min)
**[25:45–27:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Extrinsic motivation (money, prizes, bonuses) works fast but doesn't last
- Intrinsic motivation (mastery, autonomy, purpose) is slow to build but sticks
- The best teams aren't the highest-paid; they're the ones with purpose
- Intrinsic motivation reduces churn, increases discretionary effort, makes people *want* to show up
- Your job: Remove demotivators (Module 2) THEN build intrinsic motivation on top

**SCRIPT:**

"I'm going to tell you about two sales teams. Same company, same market, same compensation.

One team ran contests every month: 'Close the most in March, win $1,000.' 

The other team's manager said something different. She said: 'We're here to solve problems for our customers. Not just sell. Solve.'

One year later:
- Contest team: highest turnover, lowest customer retention, okay revenue
- Solve team: lowest turnover, highest customer retention, higher revenue per person

Why? Because the contest team was motivated by external rewards. The second they didn't win the contest, motivation dropped. External motivation is fragile.

The solve team was motivated by something deeper: *they believed in what they were doing*.

Here's the hard truth: if the only reason people show up is the paycheck, they'll leave the moment someone offers more money and less stress.

But if they're there for mastery—getting better at their craft—or autonomy—being trusted to make decisions—or purpose—changing something they believe in—they'll stay.

And they'll work harder, without you having to push."

---

### Key Teaching Points (10 min)
**[27:30–37:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**

**THE MOTIVATION SPECTRUM:**

**1. EXTRINSIC MOTIVATION (3 min)**
- What it is: external rewards (money, prizes, status, recognition)
- How it works: fast, immediate, easy to understand
- The problem: it wears off, it's expensive, it creates comparison (who won?), people optimize for the reward not the goal
- When to use: short-term push (close year-end), onboarding, crisis
- Warning: If extrinsic is your main lever, you're building dependency, not culture

**2. INTRINSIC MOTIVATION (4 min)**
- **Mastery:** "I'm getting better at this skill. I can see my growth."
  - Example: Rep learns to negotiate; starts saying no to bad deals; realizes their skill went up
  - How to build: skill shares, training, visible feedback on *how* they're improving
  
- **Autonomy:** "I get to make my own decisions. I'm trusted."
  - Example: Rep is allowed to customize their pitch; doesn't have to follow a script
  - How to build: less micromanagement, clear boundaries (here's what success looks like, you choose how)
  
- **Purpose:** "What I do matters. It's aligned with something I believe in."
  - Example: SaaS manager selling to nonprofits; knows they're helping mission-driven orgs
  - How to build: connect the work to a larger goal, celebrate the impact not just the revenue

**3. TOXIC MOTIVATION (2 min — warning)**
- What it is: fear, shame, competition, artificial scarcity
- How it works: short-term, burns people out, creates toxicity
- Example: "Only top 3 close this month keep their jobs" or constant public shaming of low performers
- The damage: people leave, burn out, or become jerks themselves

### Real Example / Story (1 min)
**[37:30–38:30]**

**SCRIPT:**

"I worked with a manager named David in luxury real estate. His team was solid, but churn was high—about 30%. Everyone was motivated by the commission.

David decided to shift the narrative. He started telling stories about the families, not the deals.

'This couple had been renting for 12 years. They thought homeownership was impossible. This month, your team found them a house. Now they have equity. They're building something.'

Purpose isn't about being cheesy or fake. It's about helping people see why their work matters."

### Tension Point / Address the Skeptic (1 min)
**[38:30–39:00]**

**SCRIPT:**

"I can hear some of you thinking: 'Purpose is nice, but people need to pay rent. If I don't pay them enough, all the purpose in the world won't matter.'

But once fairness is handled, once people know they can survive, intrinsic motivation *is* what sticks.

Fair pay alone = people who leave at the first better offer."

## SUB-MODULE 4: BUILDING RITUALS & BELONGING
**[39:00–52:00] — 13 minutes**

### Opening Hook (1:45 min)
**[39:00–40:45]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Rituals are what make culture *visible* and *repeatable*
- A ritual is anything you do consistently that reinforces belonging (standups, 1:1s, wins celebrations, all-hands)
- Without rituals, culture is abstract; with them, it's lived
- Rituals don't have to be big; they have to be intentional and protected
- Missing rituals is how good culture dies—not with a bang, but with a fade

**SCRIPT:**

"Culture lives in rituals.

Not in values on the website. Not in mission statements. Not in town halls you do once a year.

Culture lives in the small, repeated things: the weekly standup where people feel seen. The 1:1 where you actually listen. The Friday win celebration where tiny victories get acknowledged.

That's where people feel like they belong.

Without rituals, culture evaporates. You get busy, you skip the team meeting, you stop doing 1:1s, and suddenly the team feels disconnected. It's not that you did something wrong—it's that you stopped doing something right.

Your job is to *protect* rituals like you protect client meetings. Because they matter just as much."

---

### Key Teaching Points (9 min)
**[40:45–49:45]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**

**RITUAL FRAMEWORK:**

**WEEKLY RITUALS (4 min)**
- **Standup (or kickoff):** 15–30 min, whole team
  - What it is: Each person shares what they're working on, one win, one blocker
  - Why it matters: People are visible; blockers get surfaced; you hear what's really happening
  - Tone: Conversational, not reporting; "how can we help?" not "who's behind?"
  - Sample script: [See below]

- **1:1:** 30 min, one person at a time
  - What it is: You + one person, focused on them
  - Why it matters: They feel known; you catch problems early; trust builds
  - Tone: 50% them talking, 25% you listening, 25% you coaching
  - Sample script: [See below]

- **Closing huddle:** 10 min, whole team
  - What it is: Celebrate a win, acknowledge someone, close the week
  - Why it matters: People leave on a high note; belonging reinforced
  - Tone: Genuine, specific, not forced
  - Sample script: [See below]

**MONTHLY RITUALS (3 min)**
- **All-hands or team meeting:** 60 min, information + celebration
  - What it is: Company/team updates, wins celebration, skill share
  - Why it matters: Alignment, transparency, celebration at scale
  - Tone: Transparent (even bad news), celebratory (visible wins)

- **Wins celebration:** 30 min, dedicated time for stories
  - What it is: Each person shares a win (deal, customer feedback, learning)
  - Why it matters: People feel valued; culture is celebrated; positivity spreads
  - Tone: Safe, not competitive (celebrating variety of wins)

- **Skill share or learning:** 30 min, someone teaches something
  - What it is: One person teaches the team a skill (negotiation, new product, customer story)
  - Why it matters: Growth + belonging (we learn together)
  - Tone: Informal, collaborative

**QUARTERLY RITUALS (2 min)**
- **Offsite or culture check-in:** 4–8 hours
  - What it is: Away from desks, focused on connection and strategy
  - Why it matters: Resets culture, builds relationships, clears resentments
  - Topics: What's working? What's broken? What do we need to address?

### RITUAL TEMPLATES

**INSERT HERE: Copy-Paste Scripts**

#### WEEKLY STANDUP (15 minutes)

**Setup:** Monday morning or Friday afternoon, 30 min before standup, send Slack reminder: "Standup in 30 min—think about one win and one blocker"

**Script:**

"Good [morning/Friday]. This is our standup. Let's go around. Each person: one thing you're working on this week, one thing that went well, one thing that's in your way. Let's do 2 min each.

[Person 1], start us off."

*Listen. Write down blockers. Don't fix in standup—take to 1:1 or separate conversation.*

"Thanks. That's great. I'm hearing [summarize]. On the blocker, [Person X], do you have thoughts? No? Okay, let's solve that in a separate conversation.

[Person 2], you're up."

*Continue around the room.*

**Closing:**
"Great. I'm hearing momentum on [projects]. On the blockers, we have [list]. I'll take [this] on, and I'll follow up on [that] in 1:1s.

One thing before we go: I want to call out [specific win from someone]. You did [specific thing] and it moved [result]. That's the kind of thing that matters. Thanks."

**Timing:** 2 min per person, plus you talking = 15 min max.

---

#### WEEKLY 1:1 (30 minutes)

**Setup:** Same time every week (consistency matters). Calendar block. No cancellations.

**Script:**

"Hey, [Name]. Before we jump in—how are you doing? How was your [weekend/week]?"

*Listen for real answer, not just "good."*

"Got it. So here's what I want to focus on today: You tell me what's on your mind, then I share what I'm seeing. Sound good?

What's going on with you right now? How's the work feeling?"

*Listen 80% of this time. Let them talk.*

"I'm hearing [summarize]. On the [challenge], what would help? Have you thought about...? [Coach, ask questions, don't tell.]"

*Shift after 15 min:*

"Okay, here's what I'm seeing on my end. You're doing great with [specific thing]. I've noticed [specific behavior] and I want to make sure we're aligned. Here's what I'd like to see: [specific ask, not judgment]."

*Listen to their response.*

"Great. So to recap: [action item for them], [action item for me]. Any concerns before we wrap?"

**Timing:** First 15 min = them. Last 15 min = you.

**Red flags to listen for:**
- "I'm not sure if I'm doing well or not" → They need clarity (Module 2)
- "I don't see a path forward" → They need growth conversation (Module 3)
- "I feel like I'm invisible" → They need connection (Module 2)

---

#### FRIDAY CLOSING HUDDLE (10 minutes)

**Setup:** Friday at 4:30 PM (Friday close time). 10 min max.

**Script:**

"Hey team, quick huddle before the weekend. I want to call out some wins from this week:

[Person A]: You closed [deal/project]. That matters because [why].
[Person B]: You helped [Person C] with [thing]. That's collaboration.
[Person D]: You learned [skill] this week. That's growth.

I'm proud of how we handled [situation] this week. We [specific thing you did together].

Go have a good weekend. See you Monday."

**Tone:** Genuine, specific, brief. You're saying goodbye with gratitude.

---

#### MONTHLY ALL-HANDS (60 minutes)

**Structure:**
- 10 min: Company/team updates (what's happening, what changed)
- 20 min: Wins celebration (everyone shares)
- 15 min: Skills share or guest
- 10 min: Q&A or open discussion

## SUB-MODULE 5: REMOTE/HYBRID CULTURE
**[51:45–63:00] — 11 minutes**

### Opening Hook (1:45 min)
**[51:45–53:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**
- Remote culture doesn't happen by accident; it requires *more* intentionality than co-located culture

**SCRIPT:**

One: Rituals have to be rock-solid. People need to know when they'll see each other.

Three: Async-first mindset. Don't schedule meetings for everything. Write it down. Let people read on their time.

### Key Teaching Points (8 min)
**[53:30–61:30]**

**INSTRUCTOR TALKING POINTS:**

**PART 1: RITUALS (2 min)**
- Synchronous rituals are *more* important in remote, not less
- Standup still happens (maybe async Slack version, but still happens)
- 1:1s still happen, via video (not just Slack messages)
- Weekly team video call (not optional, not recorded—live connection)
- Monthly wins celebration (on video, live, people show up)

**Why:** In remote, rituals are the only time people see faces. Without them, relationships get thin.

**PART 2: ASYNC-FIRST (3 min)**
- Default to writing; live meetings are exception
- Decisions get written (why, what, what's next)
- Wins get posted (public, async, so async team sees them)
- Context gets documented (so someone joining at 3 AM isn't lost)
- Synchronous meetings are for discussion, debate, connection—not information dump
- Information dumping = "I'll send a doc. Skim it and we'll discuss live."

**Why:** Async respects timezones, family time, deep work. People don't feel forced into real-time.

**PART 3: OVER-COMMUNICATION (2 min)**
- You think you're over-communicating; you're actually at right level
- Say things three different ways (all-hands, Slack, written doc, 1:1s)
- Assume 30% of people didn't hear the first time
- Transparency is written, not assumed
- Bad news especially has to be communicated clearly (so rumors don't fill the gap)

**Why:** In remote, absence of information = assumption of bad news. Transparency kills rumors.

**PART 4: VIDEO CALLS (1 min)**
- Video calls require psychological safety (cameras on, faces visible)
- But not *all* calls need video (standup can be voice, doc, async Slack)
- Weekly team video call = non-negotiable (people need to see each other)
- Rotate who leads, who presents, who celebrates
- Keep video calls to 45 min max (Zoom fatigue is real)

### REMOTE CULTURE CHECKLIST

**Rate your remote culture on each dimension:**

#### RITUALS: Are people seeing each other?

- [ ] Async standup (Slack or doc, people update weekly)
- [ ] Synchronous team call (weekly, video, live)
- [ ] 1:1s happen on video (same person, same time weekly)
- [ ] Monthly wins celebration (async post + live discussion)
- [ ] Monthly all-hands (video, live)

**If <3 boxes checked: Your rituals are at risk**

*Fix:*
- Pick one ritual and protect it religiously (e.g., "Wednesday 9 AM team call, always on video")
- Make it recurring, same time, no moving
- In the invite, say: "Cameras on. This is where we see each other."

---

#### ASYNC-FIRST: Is information accessible?

- [ ] Decisions are written (why, what, who, when)
- [ ] Wins get posted publicly (Slack wins channel, weekly)
- [ ] Team learning/skills are documented (not just in meetings)
- [ ] Context for new hires exists (how we work, how decisions get made, FAQ)
- [ ] You're defaulting to async; synchronous is exception

**If <3 boxes checked: You're probably over-meeting**

*Fix:*
- Pick one decision you usually make in a meeting. Write a doc instead. Send it. Discuss async/live.
- Create a "Decisions" channel in Slack. Post everything there.
- For wins, create a weekly wins doc (Google Doc, Slack file, whatever). People post, you celebrate async and live.

---

#### OVER-COMMUNICATION: Is nothing ambiguous?

- [ ] Major changes are announced 3+ ways (all-hands, direct message, team email, docs)
- [ ] When something is uncertain, you say "here's what we know, here's what we don't"
- [ ] Bad news is communicated clearly (not hidden, not sugarcoated)
- [ ] Each person knows how their work matters (not assumed, stated)
- [ ] You've explained the "why" behind company/team decisions

**If <3 boxes checked: Rumors probably fill the gaps**

*Fix:*
- Next major decision: Write a doc explaining why, what's changing, what's not changing, what's uncertain
- Share it multiple ways
- In 1:1s, ask: "Did you understand the [change]? Do you have questions?"

---

#### VIDEO CALLS: Are they connecting, not exhausting?

- [ ] Cameras are expected to be on (not optional)
- [ ] But not every call is video (async standup, some meetings can be voice)
- [ ] No back-to-back Zooms in one day
- [ ] Team calls have breaks (stretch, off camera, breathing room)
- [ ] Hybrid attendees have equity (if some in-person, some remote, it's intentional)

**If <3 boxes checked: Zoom fatigue is probably real**

*Fix:*
- Look at your calendar. Count hours of video calls. If >4 hours a day, you're over-meeting.
- Default some meetings to async. "Can we use a doc instead of a call?"
- Make sure video calls have purpose (connection, discussion, not information dump)
- For hybrid: if one person is remote, everyone is remote (everyone on own device, not "in-person table + video call")

### REMOTE STANDUP EXAMPLE (Async Version)

**Setup:** Every Monday, 10 AM, people post to #standup channel (or Google Doc)

**Format:**
```
Name: ___________
This week I'm working on: ___________________________
One win last week: ___________________________
One blocker: ___________________________
Help needed from: ___________________________
```

**Example:**
```
Name: Marcus
This week I'm working on: Closing the TechCorp deal
One win last week: Customer gave referral to 3 friends
One blocker: Pricing negotiation stuck
Help needed from: Manager (pricing authority needed)
```

**Your job as manager:**
- React with 👀 or 🔥 (people see they were read)
- Reply to blockers: "Got it. I'll help with pricing. Let's sync Tuesday."
- Call out wins: "Love the referral, Marcus. That's business development."
- Pattern match: "I'm seeing three people blocked on pricing. Let's discuss Wednesday."

**Async, visible, and people feel seen.**
