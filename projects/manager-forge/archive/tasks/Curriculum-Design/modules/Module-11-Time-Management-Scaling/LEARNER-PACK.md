### Worksheet 4.2: Say-No Criteria & Language

**Instructions:** Decide in advance what you'll say yes and no to. Use this as a reference when someone asks.

**I WILL SAY YES TO:**
- [ ] Core manager work (coaching, hiring, strategy)
- [ ] High-impact delegatable work (training delivery, peer mentoring)
- [ ] Emergency issues (true urgent items, rare)
- [ ] My boss's direct requests (with priority conversation)
- [ ] My role's core meetings (team sync, leadership huddle)

**I WILL SAY NO TO:**
- [ ] Meetings I don't need to be in (politely: 'I'm confident you've got this')
- [ ] Interruptions during focus time (unless truly urgent)
- [ ] Non-urgent asks during my 1:1 blocks
- [ ] Meetings without a clear decision or outcome
- [ ] New projects without clarity on what I'm dropping

**Scripts (copy-paste ready):**

**Declining a meeting:**
> "I'm going to sit this one out. If you need me to weigh in on [specific decision], let me know by [day]. Otherwise, I'm confident you've got it."

**Declining during focus time:**
> "I'm in focus time right now. If it's urgent, I'll break out. If not, I'm free at [time]. What's up?"

**Declining scope creep:**
> "I'd like to help with that. What do I stop doing to add this? I can only take on new things if I'm clear on what I'm dropping."

**Declining a 'quick question' outside office hours:**
> "I'm not available right now, but I've got 15 min during office hours [day/time]. That work?"

# PART C: ROLE-PLAY SCENARIOS (5 detailed scenarios)

## Scenario 1: Saying No to a Meeting Request

**Setup:**
Your peer asks you to sit in on a client call. It's not your primary role, but she's worried about the conversation.

**Context:**
- Time: Monday 2 p.m. (during your 1:1 block)
- Your calendar is already tight
- She's capable, but nervous
- She hasn't asked you to coach her on this type of call before

**Dialogue:**

**Peer:** "Hey, can you hop on the client call at 2? I'm a bit nervous about this negotiation."

**You:** "I can't join live, but I can help you prep beforehand. What are the key issues?"

**Peer:** "She's pushing back on price. I want to make sure I don't give away too much."

**You:** "That's exactly what we should talk through. How about we spend 20 minutes before the call? I'll ask you questions, you tell me your position, and I'll coach you on negotiating stance. Then you're on your own. Sound good?"

**Peer:** "Yeah, that would actually be better than me just watching you."

**You:** "Great. Let's do 1:30–1:50, right before your call. That work?"

**Peer:** "Perfect. Thanks."

**Debrief Questions:**

1. Why did you decline the meeting but not the help?
   - *Answer:* You protected your focus time and her development. The meeting wasn't high-value delegation; coaching her to do it was.

2. What did you offer instead?
   - *Answer:* Focused coaching (TIME 1 SHOW equivalent). You're teaching her judgment, not doing the work for her.

3. How did this preserve both your time and her growth?
   - *Answer:* 20 min of coaching saves you 60 min of being in a meeting. And she learns how to negotiate, not just watches you negotiate.

4. What if she'd pushed back, saying she really needed you on the call?
   - *Answer:* "I understand. Tell me why me being there would help. What would I do differently than you?" (Often, she realizes she just needs confidence, not your presence.)

5. When would you accept being in the call?
   - *Answer:* True escalation (major client at risk) or first-time situation. But that's rare.

## Scenario 2: Delegating a High-Value Task

**Setup:**
You realize your top rep is ready to coach others on a critical skill (deal closing). You want to delegate this training.

**Context:**
- Your rep has 2 years in role, strong results, natural teacher
- You're currently coaching 8 reps on closing technique (5 hours/week)
- You've never delegated training before
- You're nervous about quality

**Dialogue:**

**You:** "I want to delegate something high-value to you. I'm spending 5 hours a week coaching reps on closing technique. I want you to own that."

**Rep:** "Really? I've never trained anyone before."

**You:** "That's the point. You're ready to grow into coaching. Here's how it'll work: I'll show you how I coach someone through a stalled deal—you watch and listen to my thinking, not just what I say. Then you'll coach a rep while I listen and give feedback. Then you run with it independently, and I spot-check every other week. Sound okay?"

**Rep:** "Yeah, I'm nervous, but yeah."

**You:** "Good. The nervousness means you care about doing it right. Here's the timeline: I'll show you this Friday for 30 minutes. You coach your first rep next Wednesday. I'll listen in. Then you own it. We'll review output every other Friday. Questions?"

**Rep:** "What if I mess up?"

**You:** "You won't mess up in a way that matters. I'll give you feedback. You'll adjust. That's learning. Deal?"

**Rep:** "Deal."

**Debrief Questions:**

1. Why did you frame this as a high-value delegation, not a task dump?
   - *Answer:* You explained the growth opportunity. You made clear that this is teaching her leadership skills, not offloading busywork.

2. What did you clarify about the timeline?
   - *Answer:* TIME 1 (Friday, you show), TIME 2 (Wednesday, she does with you listening), TIME 3 (ongoing, she's independent with spot-checks).

3. How did you address her worry about quality?
   - *Answer:* You normalized that she'll make adjustments. You're coaching her on coaching. You're not asking for perfection day 1.

4. What did you NOT do that's important?
   - *Answer:* You didn't say 'Give it a try whenever' or 'No pressure.' You set a specific timeline and made it clear this is expected.

5. When would you check in with her again?
   - *Answer:* After her first coaching session (get feedback). Then every other Friday for a month. Then monthly spot-checks.

## Scenario 3: Burnout Conversation with Your Manager

**Setup:**
You're at Month 6 of burnout (working 60 hours, exhausted, considering leaving). You decide to talk to your manager.

**Context:**
- Your manager is supportive but results-focused
- You don't want to seem weak or complaining
- You have a plan (delegation, automation, protecting time)
- You need her buy-in to make changes stick

**Dialogue:**

**You:** "I want to talk about something that's affecting my performance. I'm working unsustainable hours—60+ a week—and I'm not sustainable at this pace. I want to fix it."

**Manager:** "What's driving the hours?"

**You:** "I'm doing too much work that my team should be learning. I'm not delegating high-value work; I'm just offloading busywork. And I haven't automated repeatable tasks. I have a plan to fix it."

**Manager:** "What's the plan?"

**You:** "Three things. One: Delegate deal coaching to my top rep—she'll coach three others. Two: Automate forecasting with Zapier—saves 3 hours/week. Three: Block focus time and protect it. By the end of Month 3, I should be at 45–48 hours/week, my team should be stronger, and my coaching should be deeper."

**Manager:** "Will this affect results?"

**You:** "Actually, it'll improve them. Right now, I'm spreading myself thin on coaching. If I focus my coaching on the people ready to lead, and my rep coaches the rest, we'll have better-trained team and I'll be more strategic. Plus, I'll have time to think about our pipeline strategy, which I haven't been able to do."

**Manager:** "Okay. What do you need from me?"

**You:** "Just support when I start saying no to non-essential meetings. I'll keep you in the loop weekly."

**Manager:** "Fair. Let's check in in 4 weeks. See how it's going."

**Debrief Questions:**

1. Why did you start with 'This is affecting my performance'?
   - *Answer:* You framed it as a business issue, not a personal complaint. Managers respond to impact.

2. What did you NOT do?
   - *Answer:* You didn't apologize. You didn't ask for permission to delegate. You stated it as a plan.

3. How did you address the manager's concern about results?
   - *Answer:* You explained how delegation and focus would *improve* results (deeper coaching, strategic thinking).

4. What was your exit? (How did you end the conversation?)
   - *Answer:* You set a check-in. You made it clear you'd communicate. You didn't leave it open-ended.

5. What if your manager said, "We need you to work the 60 hours"?
   - *Answer:* "I understand. I'm concerned that won't be sustainable. Can we talk about what's critical and what we can defer or delegate?" [Then you have a real conversation about priorities.]

## Scenario 4: Protecting Your Focus Time

**Setup:**
It's Wednesday 10 a.m., your focus time block. A rep emails: "Can we sync? My deal just changed and I need coaching."

**Context:**
- Your rep is capable
- The deal is important but not an emergency (closes in 2 weeks)
- Your next availability is Thursday afternoon
- You've blocked this focus time and told your team not to interrupt unless urgent

**Dialogue:**

**Rep's Slack message:** "Hey, can we chat? Deal situation changed. Need your take."

**You (in focus time, on Do Not Disturb):** "I'm in focus time right now (out until 12). If it's critical, call me. If it can wait, I've got 30 min Thursday 4–4:30 p.m. Book that slot if you need it."

**Rep:** "Not critical. Thursday works."

**You:** "Perfect. I'll review your notes before we chat. Send me the update?"

**Rep:** "Will do."

*[Thursday 4 p.m., you chat]*

**You:** "So the client's pushing timeline up. What are you thinking?"

**Rep:** "I think we hold firm on the timeline, but I'm nervous about losing the deal."

**You:** "Okay. Walk me through your conversation with them. What's the business reason for their timeline push?"

**Rep:** "They said the implementation team is only available in Q2."

**You:** "Got it. So their constraint is implementation, not your delivery. How does that change your negotiation?"

**Rep:** "Oh. We could deliver early and have implementation happen later."

**You:** "Right. So how do you position that in your next call?"

**Rep:** "I say 'We can deliver [date], and your implementation team can start [later date]. Does that work?'"

**You:** "Exactly. And if they say no?"

**Rep:** "I ask why. And if it's still a blocker, I loop in [customer success]."

**You:** "Perfect. That's the move. Go handle it."

**Debrief Questions:**

1. Why didn't you break focus time?
   - *Answer:* Your rep's need was important but not urgent (deal doesn't close for 2 weeks). Protecting focus time isn't about being unavailable; it's about being available predictably.

2. How did you signal availability without breaking focus?
   - *Answer:* You offered a specific slot (Thursday 4 p.m.). That's way better than constant interruption.

3. What did you do in the coaching call?
   - *Answer:* You asked questions, not gave answers. You coached her thinking, not solved the problem. (That's high-value coaching, not busywork.)

4. How much time did you protect by deferring?
   - *Answer:* Your focus time was protected. You also only spent 30 min coaching instead of constantly being interrupted. Net: You probably saved 2–3 hours of focus time.

5. What would you do if your rep said, "This is actually urgent; we're losing the deal"?
   - *Answer:* "Okay. I'll hop off focus time. But tell me right now: Is there a decision only I can make, or do you need coaching?" [Often it's coaching; rarely it's decision-only.]

## Scenario 5: Automation Planning Walkthrough

**Setup:**
Your team's forecasting takes 3 hours per week. You're planning to automate it using Zapier. You're talking through the automation with a team member or your admin.

**Context:**
- Your forecast goes to your boss every Friday by noon
- You pull data from CRM, format in Google Sheet, email
- You're worried about accuracy and missed edge cases

**Dialogue:**

**You:** "I want to automate our weekly forecast. Here's the current process: Every Thursday night, I pull revenue data from our CRM, update the Google Sheet with calculations, and Friday morning I send it to my boss."

**Admin:** "Can that be automated?"

**You:** "Yeah. Here's what I'm thinking: Zapier can pull CRM data every Friday at 6 a.m., push it to our Google Sheet, and the Sheet will auto-calculate. Then Zapier sends a Slack message to me at 7 a.m. saying 'Forecast updated. Review and send.' I spend 15 minutes spot-checking, then I email it."

**Admin:** "What could go wrong?"

**You:** "Good question. If the CRM data is wrong, the forecast is wrong. So I'm keeping the manual spot-check. I'm not fully automating the decision—just the data collection and format. I'm still the filter."

**Admin:** "And if the Zapier doesn't run?"

**You:** "I set up a Slack reminder every Friday 8 a.m. to double-check. That's my safety net. And I probably run it manually the first 2 weeks to make sure it's working."

**Admin:** "How long to set up?"

**You:** "Probably 30–45 minutes. Do you want to do it, or should I?"

**Admin:** "I can do it. I'll need the CRM credentials and your Google Sheet link. And I'll test it Friday before going live."

**You:** "Perfect. Let's target Friday for a test run. If it works, it's live next Friday. If there are issues, we'll debug and go live the following Friday."

**Debrief Questions:**

1. Why did you keep the manual spot-check in the automation?
   - *Answer:* You're not blindly trusting the automation. You're automating the *mechanical* work (data pull, format), not the *judgment* work (validating the numbers).

2. What's the risk you identified?
   - *Answer:* CRM data being wrong + Zapier not running. You built in a safety net for both (manual review + Slack reminder).

3. How long is your first attempt timeline?
   - *Answer:* 2 weeks of testing (Friday 1: test, Friday 2: live, Friday 3: confident). Then it's hands-off.

4. What would you NOT automate?
   - *Answer:* The decision about whether the forecast looks right. The narrative explanation. The communication to stakeholders. You're automating *movement*, not *judgment*.

5. What if the test run fails on Friday?
   - *Answer:* You'd still manually forecast Friday (you're not worse off than today). You'd debug Saturday/early next week. You'd try again the following Friday.

# PART D: WEEKLY ZOOM DISCUSSION PROMPTS (5 total)

**Format:** Use these as discussion starters for cohort Zoom sessions. Copy-paste the prompt, give 2 minutes silent thinking, then open for discussion.

## Prompt 1 (After Sub-Module 1: Time Audit)

**"What's the biggest time-waster in your week? How could you eliminate it?"**

**Discussion guidelines:**
- Have folks identify one specific task (not 'meetings' generally; name the meeting)
- Ask: Is it automatable, delegatable, or eliminable?
- If automatable: 'What tool would you use?'
- If delegatable: 'Who's ready?'
- If eliminable: 'What would happen if you just stopped?'

**Sample responses to expect:**
- Weekly report I write manually (automatable)
- Status meetings with no agenda (eliminable)
- 1:1 prep I don't really do (unclear; probably keep but template)
- Email management (batching would help)

**What to watch for:**
- If everyone's biggest waste is different, that's good (people are in different situations)
- If multiple people share the same waste, it might be systemic (worth digging into)

---

## Prompt 2 (After Sub-Module 2: Delegation)

**"What could you delegate this week? Who's ready?"**

**Discussion guidelines:**
- Have folks name one person on their team who's capable
- Name one task that person could learn from
- Ask: 'How would you know they're ready?'
- Follow-up: 'What's holding you back from delegating this?'

**Sample responses to expect:**
- "My top rep could coach others on X" (high-value)
- "My ops person could own the dashboard" (high-value)
- "Someone could do admin work" (lower-value; steer toward higher-value)

**What to watch for:**
- Hesitation usually means manager worry about quality, not team readiness
- Ask: 'If they did it 90% as well as you, would that be okay?' (Usually yes.)
- If someone says 'No one's ready,' dig: 'What would make them ready?'

---

## Prompt 3 (After Sub-Module 3: Automation)

**"Share one automation that saved you time. Or, share one automation you're thinking about."**

**Discussion guidelines:**
- If they've already automated something, ask: 'How long to set up? What tool? How much do you save?'
- If they're thinking about it, ask: 'What's the first step?'
- If no one's automated yet, ask: 'What's one task >1 hour/week that's repeatable?'

**Sample responses to expect:**
- "Slack workflow sends team standup prompts" (saves 10 min/week)
- "Email-to-CRM automation logs calls" (saves 30 min/week)
- "Calendar booking link" (saves 30 min/week)
- "Zapier pulls forecast data" (saves 2+ hours/week)

**What to watch for:**
- Celebrate the small wins (Slack workflow is still automation!)
- Push on realistic ROI: 'That automation saves you 10 min. Is 20 min to set up worth it?' (Usually yes for recurring tasks)
- If someone's automating wrong (automating judgment, not mechanics), coach them

## Prompt 4 (After Sub-Module 4: Time Protection)

**"How many hours did you actually work this week? (Be honest.)"**

**Discussion guidelines:**
- This is vulnerable. Set a tone of non-judgment.
- Ask: 'Is that sustainable?'
- If >55 hours: 'What's one change you'd make to reduce hours?'
- If 40–50 hours: 'What's working? How can you protect it?'

**Sample responses to expect:**
- 'Honest? Sixty-plus.' (No shame; this is data.)
- 'I have no idea. I didn't track.' (Okay; time audit is homework.)
- 'Probably 48 hours, but it feels like 60.' (Good; perception and reality often differ)

**What to watch for:**
- Normalize that tracking hours is hard
- If someone's >60 hours, ask: 'Is delegation/automation starting?' (If no, they're not implementing the module)
- If someone's protected time successfully, ask them to share their system

## Prompt 5 (After Sub-Module 5: Burnout Prevention & Recovery)

**"What's one thing you're NOT doing because you don't have time? Should you be?"**

**Discussion guidelines:**
- This identifies burnout signals and misallocated priorities
- Ask: 'What would happen if you stopped not doing this?'
- Ask: 'How much time would it take?'
- Ask: 'Is this something you'd reclaim if you delegated/automated other work?'

**Sample responses to expect:**
- "I'm not coaching my struggling reps. I should be." (Burnout: doing busywork instead of core work)
- "I'm not planning the quarter. I should be." (Burnout: no time for strategy)
- "I'm not taking time off. I should be." (Burnout: not protecting recovery)
- "I'm not learning new skills. I don't think I should." (Okay; clarify priority)

**What to watch for:**
- If someone's not doing core manager work (coaching, strategy), they're in trouble
- Use this to point back to delegation/automation: 'If you automated that report, would you have 2 hours for coaching?'
- If someone's not taking time off and thinks they shouldn't, challenge it: 'Why? What would happen?'
