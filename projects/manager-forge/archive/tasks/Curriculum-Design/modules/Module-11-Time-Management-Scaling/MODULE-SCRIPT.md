## SUB-MODULE 1: THE TIME AUDIT
**[2:00–14:00 — Duration: 12 minutes]**

### Opening Hook (1:30)

> "Let me tell you about Sarah. She's a regional manager at a SaaS company, managing eight people. She told me, 'I don't have time to coach my people. I'm drowning in reports.' I asked her to track her time for one week.
>
> Sarah logged 40 hours. Here's how it broke down:
> - 15 hours writing forecasts and status reports
> - 10 hours in meetings that didn't need her
> - 8 hours in back-to-back 1:1s she didn't prepare for
> - 5 hours actually coaching her team
> - 2 hours on strategy
>
> She looked at that list and said, 'I could automate the reports. I could skip those meetings. I could cluster my 1:1s.'
>
> One month later, she was down to 45 hours, but 12 of those hours were deep coaching time. Her team's pipeline hit target for the first time. She's not burned out anymore.
>
> That's what a time audit does. It tells you the truth."

**Teaching Points (10 min)** — Refer to the accompanying **Time Audit Worksheet** below.

#### 1. **The Three Time Buckets**

**Core Manager Work (30–40% of your week)**
- 1:1 coaching sessions with your direct reports
- Strategic thinking and planning
- Hiring and onboarding
- Decision-making (approvals, prioritization, conflict resolution)
- Building culture and setting operating rhythm
- Pipeline reviews (if you're in sales/RE)

*Why it matters:* This is what you *must* do. No one else can coach your people. No one else has your strategic lens.

**Important But Delegatable (30–40% of your week)**
- Deal coaching (teaching a rep *how* to close, not closing for them)
- Training delivery (product launches, methodology, new processes)
- Performance feedback synthesis
- Forecasting (often can be delegated to a senior rep or analyst)
- Onboarding support
- Peer mentoring others' teams

*Why it matters:* These tasks develop your people AND your peers. Delegation here builds bench strength.

**Automatable/Eliminable (<10% of your week)**
- Weekly status reports (dashboard >> email)
- Scheduling (auto-scheduling, calendar blocks)
- Data entry (Zapier workflows, integrations)
- Recurring meeting prep (templates)
- Routine administrative work
- Repetitive communications (templated responses)

*Why it matters:* This is where you reclaim time. Most managers have 30%+ automatable, but they don't see it.

#### 2. **How to Track Your Time**

**Step 1: The One-Week Log (Worksheet provided)**
- Use 30-minute blocks
- Be ruthlessly honest (no optimistic logging)
- Include everything: meetings, email, thinking time, even scrolling

**Step 2: Categorize Each Block**
- Assign to bucket: Core, Delegatable, or Automatable
- Note: Some tasks span buckets (e.g., 1:1 is Core, but prep *could* be Automatable)

**Step 3: Calculate Your Percentages**
- Core: Total hours ÷ 40 = %
- Delegatable: Total hours ÷ 40 = %
- Automatable: Total hours ÷ 40 = %

**Step 4: Identify the Single Biggest Time-Waster**
- What's eating the most time that *doesn't* require you?
- Start there.

#### 3. **The Reality Check: Hidden Time**

Most managers underestimate automatable time because they don't see it:
- **Context switching:** How much time do you spend just *opening* emails, Slack, tools?
- **Unnecessary meetings:** How many meetings could be a 5-minute Slack message?
- **Preparation waste:** How much time do you spend preparing for things that didn't need prep?
- **Interruptions:** How many times do people pull you off focus time? (Cost: 23 min to regain focus per interrupt)

**Reality:** If you're at 60 hours, assume 5–10 hours are pure waste. Just reclaiming that takes you to 50–55 hours.

### Real Example (1:30)

**SaaS Regional Manager — One Week Audit**

| Task | Hours | Bucket | Automation Opportunity |
|------|-------|--------|----------------------|
| Weekly forecast email | 3 | Automatable | Zapier pulls data to dashboard |
| Status update presentations | 2.5 | Automatable | Slack workflow posts auto-summary |
| 1:1 coaching | 5 | Core | (Keep) |

| Weekly team meeting | 2 | Core | (Keep, but tighten to 30 min) |

| Email/Slack management | 5 | Automatable | Batch to 3x daily, 15 min each |
| Training/onboarding | 4 | Delegatable | Assign to senior rep; you audit |
| Admin/CRM data entry | 3 | Automatable | Zapier + integration |
| **Total** | **~40** | — | **~12 hours reclaimed** |

**Realistic outcome:** 40 hours → 35–38 hours (core + delegatable) within 2 weeks.

### Tension Point (1 min)

> "You're thinking: 'But if I don't do these tasks, who will? And will they do it as well as me?'
>
> Here's the hard truth: *Your ego doesn't scale.*
>
> If you spend 3 hours a week on a report that your top rep could do in 1.5 hours (after you teach them once), you're costing the company money. You're also blocking that rep from growing and taking on bigger work.
>
> The report doesn't need your genius. Your strategy does. Stop confusing the two."

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**Key Takeaway:**
- Your time audit is your baseline. Do it this week.
- You likely have 8–12 hours of automatable work hiding in plain sight.
- Core manager work (coaching, strategy) should be 30–40% of your week. Aim for that.
- Start with the single biggest time-waster. Automate or eliminate it this week.

## SUB-MODULE 2: DELEGATION THAT DEVELOPS PEOPLE
**[14:00–28:00 — Duration: 14 minutes]**

### Opening Hook (1:30)

> "Here's what most managers do: They delegate busywork.
>
> 'Hey, can you update this spreadsheet?' 'Can you send out the meeting notes?' 'Can you call these three people and log it?'
>
> And then they wonder why their team doesn't grow. Why retention is terrible. Why they can't scale.
>
> High-value delegation is the opposite. You delegate *decision-making*. You delegate *judgment*. You delegate *teaching opportunities*.
>
> That's how you build a team that can run without you. That's how *you* stop working 60 hours.
>
> One manager taught her top rep to coach three other reps on deal skills. That freed up 2 hours per week for her. But it also gave that rep the skills to become a team lead. Win-win. Actually—win-win-win, because the three reps got better coaching and leveled up faster.
>
> That's the goal."

**Teaching Points (11 min)**

#### 1. **High-Value Delegation vs. Busywork**

**Busywork (Don't delegate these):**
- Admin tasks that don't teach anything
- Data entry that doesn't require judgment
- Tasks where doing it yourself is faster than teaching it
- Work that isolates people from the business

**High-Value Delegation (Delegate ruthlessly):**
- **Deal coaching:** Teach a senior rep how to coach three others on specific deal scenarios (closing technique, objection handling, etc.). You watch, then they do it. You step back.
- **Training delivery:** 'You're running the new onboarding program for the next hire. I'll review the first session and the fifth session. You own it.'
- **Pipeline reviews:** 'You review the team's pipeline this week and bring me the top three issues. You own the diagnosis.'
- **Peer mentoring:** 'You're going to mentor Jane on her negotiation skills. I'll coach you on coaching.'
- **Process design:** 'We need to improve how we track leads. You design it, run a pilot, and report back.'

**The Difference:**
- Busywork: Removes you from work you shouldn't be doing. (Good.)
- High-value delegation: Develops your people AND removes you from work you shouldn't be doing. (Much better.)

#### 2. **The Three-Time Method**

This is your framework for high-value delegation. Use it every time.

**TIME 1: SHOW (You do it, they watch)**
- Pick a real, current task (not a training scenario)
- Talk through your thinking as you do it
- Narrate the *why*, not just the *what*
- 'Here's the objection I hear most often. Here's why it matters. Here's how I respond and why.'
- 20–40 min, depending on complexity
- They're listening, learning, absorbing your judgment

**TIME 2: WATCH (They do it, you watch)**
- They execute the task while you're present
- You give feedback in real time (gentle; focus on thinking, not execution)
- 'Why did you pause there?' vs. 'You should have paused.'
- Usually the same complexity as TIME 1
- 30–60 min
- They feel supported; you diagnose gaps

**TIME 3: STEP BACK (They do it, you don't watch)**
- They execute independently
- You might review the output after, or check in a week later
- If they struggle, go back to TIME 2 (never skip back to TIME 1)
- This is where the growth happens

**Timeline:**
- Simple task (deal coaching technique): TIME 1 (20 min) → TIME 2 (30 min) → TIME 3 (independent) — total, 50 min of your time
- Moderately complex (training delivery, pipeline review): TIME 1 (40 min) → TIME 2 (60 min) → TIME 3 (independent) — total, 100 min, spread over 2 weeks
- Complex (peer mentoring, process design): TIME 1 (60 min) → TIME 2 (60 min, multiple sessions) → TIME 3 (independent) — total, 120–180 min over 3–4 weeks, but frees 2–4 hours per week long-term

#### 3. **Delegation Conversation**

**How to initiate:**

> "I want to delegate something high-value to you. I'm doing X hours per week on [task]. After I teach you, you'll own it. I benefit because I get the time back. You benefit because you grow a critical skill. Our team wins because we scale faster.
>
> I'm going to show you how I do it. Then you'll do it with me watching. Then you'll do it alone. Questions?"

**How to set expectations:**

> "This is a judgment call, not a checklist. You'll make different decisions than I would. That's okay. I'll coach you on the thinking, not grade you on exact execution."

#### 4. **High-Value Delegation Examples by Role**

**SaaS Sales Manager:**
- Delegate: Deal coaching (teaching a rep to coach others on closing technique)
- TIME 1: 'Here's how I coach a rep through a stalled deal. Watch.'
- TIME 2: 'You coach that rep through their stalled deal while I listen.'
- TIME 3: 'You coach them independently. I'll spot-check the deals every two weeks.'
- Outcome: You go from 5 hours/week coaching to 1 hour/week coaching *others* coaching; team gets 2x the coaching.

**Luxury Real Estate Team Lead:**
- Delegate: Client negotiation coaching
- TIME 1: 'Here's how I prep a client for negotiation. Watch me do it with a real client.'
- TIME 2: 'You prep this client while I listen and give feedback.'
- TIME 3: 'You prep the next client. I review the notes.'
- Outcome: You spend 90 min teaching; you save 2 hours/week negotiation prep; your agent gets better and closes faster.

**Operations Manager:**
- Delegate: Process design and training
- TIME 1: 'Here's how I diagnosed our forecasting problem and designed the fix. Watch.'
- TIME 2: 'You diagnose the next process issue and present your solution to me.'
- TIME 3: 'You lead the next process improvement project with my weekly check-in.'
- Outcome: You move from doing process work to coaching your team to do process work; you scale without growing the team.

### Real Example (1:30)

**From Burned-Out to Strategic: How Delegation Changed a Manager's Week**

*Before delegation (SaaS manager, 8 direct reports):*
- 5 hours/week deal coaching (rep by rep)
- 8 hours/week forecasting reviews
- 6 hours/week training/onboarding
- 3 hours/week 1:1 prep/notes
- **Total: 22 hours** on work that wasn't strategic thinking or hiring

*Delegation plan (3-week cycle):*
- Identify top 2 reps capable of coaching
- Teach each rep to coach 3 others using 3-time method (40 min SHOW, 60 min WATCH)
- Teach a mid-level rep to own forecasting (40 min SHOW, 60 min WATCH)
- Assign onboarding training delivery to a senior rep (30 min SHOW, 60 min WATCH)

*After delegation (ongoing):*
- 2 hours/week deal coaching (only exceptional deals + coaching your coaches)
- 1 hour/week forecasting review (top-line only; your rep owns detail)
- 2 hours/week training/onboarding (audit, not delivery)
- 2 hours/week 1:1 prep/notes (focused, not reactive)
- **Total: 7 hours** on delegatable work; manager now has **15 hours/week** for strategy, hiring, and thinking

*Result:* Manager went from working 55 hours/week to 42 hours/week. Team got better. Retention improved. Pipeline hit target.

### Tension Point (1 min)

> "You're thinking: 'This takes time upfront. I don't have time to teach people.'
>
> I get it. You're drowning. But here's the math: Teaching someone to do a 2-hour/week task takes 2 hours once. Then you save 2 hours *every single week* for the next two years. That's 200 hours of your life, starting immediately.
>
> Stop thinking about 'I don't have time to teach.' Think about 'I can't afford *not* to teach.'
>
> You'll reclaim the teaching time within 4 weeks."

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**Key Takeaway:**
- Delegation isn't offloading. It's developing.
- Use the 3-time method: SHOW, WATCH, STEP BACK.
- Start with high-value tasks: deal coaching, training, pipeline reviews, process design.
- Teaching upfront (2–3 hours) saves you 2+ hours per week forever.
- Your job is to make your team bigger than you, not to be the bottleneck.

**Worksheet Needed:**
- 'What can you delegate?' (high-value task identification)
- 'Delegation plan: 3-time timeline' (show-watch-step back schedule)

## SUB-MODULE 3: AUTOMATION STRATEGY
**[28:00–41:00 — Duration: 13 minutes]**

### Opening Hook (1:30)

> "A regional manager in SaaS used to spend 3 hours every Friday on forecasting. She'd pull numbers from four different systems, format them into a deck, and send it to her boss.
>
> One day, she decided: 'This is a report. Reports are automatable.'
>
> She spent 30 minutes learning Zapier. She set up a workflow: automatic data pull from CRM + a Google Sheet dashboard update + a Slack message to her boss every Friday at 8 a.m.
>
> The next Friday, the forecast was ready before she got coffee. She saved 3 hours per week. The report was actually *better*—it updated in real time, so her boss could see live numbers, not Friday snapshots.
>
> That's automation. You don't hire for it. You don't work harder. You work smarter.
>
> And the best part? 90% of your repeatable work is automatable. You just haven't done it yet."

**Teaching Points (10:30)**

#### 1. **The Automation Mindset**

**Automation is not:**
- Hiring someone to do the work
- Buying expensive enterprise software
- Complex coding or technical wizardry

**Automation is:**
- Recognizing a repeatable task
- Using a tool (Zapier, Slack workflows, integrations, templates) to do it once, then never again
- Investing 20–40 minutes upfront to save 2+ hours per week forever

**Automation ROI:**
- Task taking >1 hour/week = worth automating
- Task taking 30–60 minutes/week = marginal (may be worth it; consider first)
- Task taking <30 minutes/week = probably not worth it (unless it's emotionally draining)

#### 2. **The Automation Candidates Checklist**

Use this to identify what to automate:

**HIGH PRIORITY (Do these first):**

☐ **Weekly status reports / forecasts**
- Time cost: 2–4 hours/week
- Automation: Zapier pull CRM data → Google Sheet/dashboard → auto-email/Slack post
- Tools: Zapier, Slack workflows, Google Sheets, Tableau
- Outcome: Saves 2–4 hours/week; reports are more accurate and real-time

☐ **Scheduling (calendar coordination)**
- Time cost: 30 min–1 hour/week (back-and-forth on meeting times)
- Automation: Calendly, auto-scheduling tools, calendar blocks
- Tools: Calendly, Google Calendar workflows, Slack integrations
- Outcome: Saves 30 min–1 hour/week; meetings fill asynchronously

☐ **Onboarding checklists / new hire workflows**
- Time cost: 2–3 hours per new hire (recurring; you hire ~4–6/year)
- Automation: Zapier trigger new hire → auto-email onboarding checklist → assign tasks to relevant people
- Tools: Zapier, Slack workflows, Airtable, BambooHR
- Outcome: Saves 8–18 hours/year; consistent onboarding; new hires get clearer path

☐ **Recurring meeting prep / agendas**
- Time cost: 30–60 min/week across all meetings
- Automation: Template agendas, auto-pull data, Slack reminders to bring data
- Tools: Meeting templates, Slack workflows, Google Docs
- Outcome: Saves 30–60 min/week; meetings are more focused

☐ **1:1 prep / note templates**
- Time cost: 20–40 min/week (if you prep; many don't, which is a problem)
- Automation: Templates for 1:1 agendas, Slack auto-reminder to review notes before meeting, template for follow-up
- Tools: Slack workflows, Google Docs templates, Notion
- Outcome: Saves 20 min/week; 1:1s are more focused and developmental

**MEDIUM PRIORITY (Tackle after high priority):**

☐ **Data entry / CRM logging**
- Time cost: 1–2 hours/week (if you do it; usually your team does)
- Automation: Email-to-CRM tools, SMS-to-CRM, Slack-to-CRM
- Tools: Zapier, HubSpot workflows, Pipedrive automations
- Outcome: Saves 1–2 hours/week; reps log more consistently; data is cleaner

☐ **Templated communications (responses to common questions)**
- Time cost: 20–30 min/week (answering same question 5x)
- Automation: Slack workflow shortcuts, email templates, canned responses
- Tools: Slack, Gmail templates, Help Scout
- Outcome: Saves 20 min/week; consistent communication; faster response time

☐ **Training content delivery (recorded, not live)**
- Time cost: 1–2 hours/week (live training + prep)
- Automation: Record training once, link in Slack, use Zapier to onboard new hires to recorded content
- Tools: Slack, Zapier, Loom, YouTube
- Outcome: Saves 1–2 hours/week after initial recording; better for async, distributed teams

**LOW PRIORITY (Automate only if <30 min investment):**

☐ **Routine notifications / check-ins**
- Automation: Slack workflow sends weekly pulse check ('How are you doing? Reply with emoji.')
- Outcome: Saves 15 min/week; real-time insights into team sentiment

☐ **Meeting follow-ups / action items**
- Automation: Slack workflow auto-posts meeting recap template; team fills in; Slack sends reminders for due actions
- Outcome: Saves 15 min/week; fewer dropped action items

#### 3. **The Automation Implementation Process**

**Step 1: Pick ONE task (Start small)**
- Choose a task >1 hour/week
- Choose something you do *every single week* (recurring)
- Choose something that doesn't require judgment (rules-based)

**Step 2: Map the Process**
- Write down exactly what you do, step by step
- Example: 'I log in to CRM, pull revenue data. I open Google Sheet. I manually copy numbers. I format. I send Slack message.'
- Identify the parts that are pure data movement (automatable) vs. judgment (not)

**Step 3: Find the Tool**
- **Zapier** (general; connects 5000+ apps): Slack → Google Sheet, CRM → Email, etc.
- **Slack Workflows** (free, within Slack): Scheduled messages, form triggers, reminders
- **Native integrations** (built-in): CRM native automation, Google Sheets scripts, Salesforce workflows
- **Templates** (simplest): Google Docs, Notion, email templates
- Cost: Usually free or $10–30/month for Zapier

**Step 4: Test Once**
- Set up the automation
- Run it once manually to confirm
- Fix errors
- Deploy

**Step 5: Monitor (First 2 weeks)**
- Check the output daily to make sure it's working
- Fix edge cases
- By week 3, it should be hands-off

**Realistic timeline:** 30–60 min setup, 10 min/week monitoring first month, then zero.

#### 4. **Real Automation Examples**

**Example 1: SaaS Forecast Automation**
- Manual process: 3 hours/week (pull data, format, send)
- Automation: Zapier pulls CRM data every Friday 7 a.m. → updates Google Sheet → auto-posts to Slack + emails boss
- Setup time: 30 min (first time; Zapier is very visual)
- Recurring time: 0 min (fully automated; you just check the sheet)
- Saves: 3 hours/week
- Bonus: Real-time forecast, not Friday snapshot

**Example 2: Luxury RE Lead Assignment Automation**
- Manual process: 30 min/week (leads come in via email; you assign to agents)
- Automation: Email → Zapier → looks up agent availability → auto-assigns → Slack notification to agent
- Setup time: 45 min (trickiest part: mapping availability; but one-time)
- Recurring time: 0 min
- Saves: 30 min/week
- Bonus: Instant assignment; agents see leads faster; no delays

**Example 3: 1:1 Agenda Automation**
- Manual process: 20 min/week (remind yourself to prep; write down talking points; email calendar invite with agenda)
- Automation: Slack workflow on Monday morning → remind you to prep; Slack auto-template for agenda; calendar invite includes template
- Setup time: 15 min
- Recurring time: 0 min (Slack handles it)
- Saves: 20 min/week
- Bonus: 1:1s are more consistent and focused

### Real Example (1 min)

**Forecast Dashboard: From 3 Hours to 15 Minutes**

A regional manager at a SaaS company was spending 3 hours every Friday on forecasting:
- 30 min pulling data from CRM
- 1 hour formatting into deck
- 30 min manually calculating variance

She set up Zapier:

By Friday 8 a.m., forecast was ready. She spent 15 minutes reviewing for accuracy. That's it.

**Time saved:** 2 hours 45 min per week = 143 hours per year.

### Tension Point (1 min)

> "You're thinking: 'I don't know Zapier. It sounds complicated.'
>
> It's not. Zapier is visual. You click 'if X happens, do Y.' That's it. Most automations are 5 minutes to set up.
>
> Your first automation will take 30 minutes because you're learning. Your second will take 15. Your third will take 5.
>
> And each one buys you back 2+ hours per week. That's a trade you take every single time."

### Closing Summary (1 min)

**Key Takeaway:**
- Automation is not technical. It's recognizing repeatable, rules-based work and letting tools do it.
- Start with forecasting, scheduling, onboarding, meeting prep, and templated communications.
- Use Zapier, Slack workflows, or native integrations. Usually free or very cheap.
- Each automation takes 20–60 min to set up and saves 1–4 hours per week.

**Worksheet Needed:**
- 'What takes >1 hour/week?' (task audit)

## SUB-MODULE 4: PROTECTING YOUR TIME
**[41:00–53:00 — Duration: 12 minutes]**

### Opening Hook (1:30)

> "You know what the biggest threat to your focus time is? It's not your workload. It's people.
>
> Someone needs a decision. Someone wants to add you to a meeting. Someone has 'just one quick question.'
>
> Each interruption costs you 23 minutes of refocus time. If you get interrupted 5 times a day, that's 115 minutes a day—almost 2 hours—just on context switching.
>
> Your job isn't to *have* time. Your job is to *protect* time.
>
> And you need to get comfortable saying no. Not 'maybe.' Not 'I'll see.' No. Just no.
>
> Here's how."

**Teaching Points (9:30)**

#### 1. **The Operating Rhythm: A Weekly Schedule Architecture**

You don't just block time. You build a rhythm. Your team should be able to see your calendar and understand your rhythm without asking.

**The Basic Template (40-hour week):**

| Time | Purpose | Details |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Monday 9–12** | Focus time | Strategy, hiring, projects; no meetings, no Slack |
| **Monday 1–3 p.m.** | 1:1s (staggered) | 3–4 of your direct reports |
| **Monday 3–4 p.m.** | Slack/email; regroup | Catch up on async; respond to urgent |
| **Tuesday 9–11 a.m.** | Office hours | Drop-in availability; reps can book 15 min slots |
| **Tuesday 11 a.m.–12 p.m.** | Team meeting | Weekly sync; 30 min agenda, decisions only |
| **Tuesday 1–3 p.m.** | 1:1s (staggered) | Remaining direct reports |
| **Tuesday 3–4 p.m.** | Email/Slack | Catch up; answer questions |
| **Wednesday 9–12** | Focus time | Projects, planning, writing |
| **Wednesday 1–3 p.m.** | Optional: peer meetings, coaching others' team members |
| **Wednesday 3–4 p.m.** | Flexible | Can flex to ad-hoc meetings if needed |
| **Thursday 9–11 a.m.** | 1:1s or coaching | Spot checks, development conversations |
| **Thursday 11 a.m.–12 p.m.** | Strategic meeting with leadership or peer lead |
| **Thursday 1–3 p.m.** | Deep work or training prep |
| **Thursday 3–4 p.m.** | Email/Slack |
| **Friday 9–11 a.m.** | 1:1s or coaching | Follow-ups from week |
| **Friday 11 a.m.–12 p.m.** | Weekly metrics review (your dashboard, not a meeting) |
| **Friday 1–3 p.m.** | Planning / next week prep |
| **Friday 3–4 p.m.** | Team wrap-up (optional; 15 min if culture requires) |

**Why this structure works:**
- **Focus time** (8–10 hours/week) is clustered early in the week and same time daily (habit-forming)
- **1:1s** are batched by day (Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, Friday), not scattered, so you're in coaching mode
- **Office hours** (1 slot per week, 1 hour) give reps predictable access without stealing your time
- **Meeting slots** are clustered (not spread throughout the week), so your other time is truly clear
- **Email/Slack** time is bounded (1 hour/day max), not constant
- **Admin** (forecasting, dashboard review) happens in a 1-hour block Friday morning, not scattered

**For Luxury Real Estate (higher interrupt culture):**
- Adjust office hours to 2–3 hours/week (higher intake)
- Reduce pure focus time to 6 hours/week (you'll have more client/agent pulls)
- Keep 1:1s clustered

**For Distributed/Async Teams:**
- Extend office hours to 2–3 hours
- Shift some 1:1s to async (Slack, voice note, video message) for async-first team members
- Keep focus time; just time-shift it (maybe 6–8 a.m. before team is active)

#### 2. **How to Block Time (Operationally)**

**Making it stick:**

1. **Put it on your calendar, recurring**
   - Every Monday 9–12, every Tuesday 9–11, etc.
   - Make it recurring (recurring blocks are harder to delete)
   - Set reminder: 15 min before
   - Title: 'Focus Time' or 'Strategy' (be specific, so people know not to ask)

2. **Set your calendar to 'Busy'**
   - In Google Calendar, set 'Busy' (not 'Free') so Calendly and scheduling tools won't book over it
   - In Slack, set status: 'In focus time' (visible to team; people will respect it more if you name it)

3. **Close Slack and email during focus time**
   - Seriously. Close them.
   - Use Do Not Disturb
   - Tell your team: 'I'm not available during focus time. If it's truly urgent, call me.' (Usually nothing is.)

4. **Protect office hours **
   - Publish: 'Tuesday 10–11 a.m. is my office hours. Book 15 min slots here.' (Use Calendly)
   - Hard stop. Even if someone wants an extra slot, say: 'Next week, same time?'

5. **Batch Slack/email time**
   - 9 a.m., 12 p.m., 4 p.m.
   - Spend 15 min each block
   - This is counterintuitive (you're not always on), but it's faster than being always-on
   - You process faster when you're not context-switching

#### 3. **How to Say No (Scripts)**

**No. 1 Meeting Request**

*Someone asks:* "Can you sit in on my meeting with [client/partner]?"

*Your response:*
> "I can't join live, but I can [review notes afterward / check in 15 min before to help you prep / give you feedback on the approach]. Which would be most helpful?"

*Translation:* You're not blowing them off; you're redirecting to something valuable and time-bound.

**No. 2 Unnecessary Meeting Invite**

*Someone sends:* Invite to a meeting that doesn't need you

*Your response:*
> "I'm going to decline this one. If you need me to weigh in on [specific decision], let me know by [day]. Otherwise, I'm confident you've got it."

*Translation:* You're respecting their ability to make decisions; you're not flaking; you're setting a boundary.

**No. 3 Urgent Ask During Focus Time**

*Someone messages:* "Can we chat about X right now?"

*Your response:*
> "I'm in focus time until [time]. How urgent? If it's critical, I'll hop off. If it can wait 2 hours, let's do [time]."

*Translation:* You're signaling that focus time is real, but you'll break it for true emergencies. (Turns out, very few things are emergencies.)

**No. 4 Scope Creep**

*Someone asks:* "Can you help with [new project] in addition to your regular stuff?"

*Your response:*
> "I'd like to help. What do I drop to add this? I can only take on new things if I'm clear on what I'm stopping."

*Translation:* You're not saying no to opportunity; you're being realistic about capacity. Makes you look smart and strategic.

**No. 5 'Just a quick question'**

*Someone:* "Do you have a minute?"

*Your response (if in focus time):*
> "I'm in focus time. If it's urgent, yes. If not, I'm free at [time]. What's up?"

*Your response (if in office hours):*
> "Yeah, what is it?" (And you give them 5–10 min, hard stop.)

*Translation:* You're not rude; you're clear about time boundaries.

#### 4. **The 'No' Decision Matrix**

When someone asks you to do something, ask yourself:

| Is it core manager work? (Coaching, hiring, strategy) | Is it urgent/critical to the business? | Your Decision |
|---|---|---|
| Yes | Yes | Do it. Protect time to do it well. |
| Yes | No | Block time later in the week and do it then. |
| No | Yes | Delegate or do it once; set boundary for future. |
| No | No | Decline. Cheerfully. 'This isn't in my focus areas right now.' |

### Real Example (1 min)

*Before:*

*Changes made:*

*After (8 weeks):*

## SUB-MODULE 5: BURNOUT PREVENTION & RECOVERY
**[53:00–62:00 — Duration: 11 minutes]**

### Opening Hook (1:30)

> "Burnout doesn't happen suddenly. It creeps.
>
> Month 1: You're excited. You're putting in 50–55 hours.
>
> Month 3: You're noticing you're tired. You're at 55–60 hours.
>
> Month 6: You're exhausted. You're at 60+ hours. You dread Monday. You snap at your team. You stop coaching because you don't have energy.
>
> Month 9: You're burned out. You're looking at other jobs.
>
> The fix isn't a vacation. The fix isn't 'trying harder.' The fix is systems.
>
> If you delegate, automate, and protect your time, you don't get burned out. And if you *are* burned out, we can walk you back from the cliff."

**Teaching Points (8 min)**

#### 1. **Burnout Recognition: The 10-Point Check**

**Score yourself (0 = not at all, 3 = very much):**

1. **Physical exhaustion:** Do you feel physically tired most days, even after sleep?
2. **Emotional exhaustion:** Do you feel emotionally drained or cynical?
3. **Reduced effectiveness:** Are you making worse decisions or missing things you usually wouldn't?
4. **Work-life bleed:** Are you working nights/weekends on non-critical stuff?
5. **Isolation:** Have you stopped socializing or connecting with colleagues?
6. **Cynicism:** Do you catch yourself being negative or dismissive?
7. **Lack of accomplishment:** Does your work feel pointless, even when you're being productive?
8. **Resentment:** Do you resent your boss, your team, or the work itself?
9. **Health issues:** Do you have new stress-related physical symptoms?
10. **Checked out:** Are you going through the motions, not really present?

**Your score:**
- 0–10: You're fine. Keep up prevention.
- 11–20: Early warning signs. Implement prevention now.
- 21–30: You're burned out. You need intervention (delegation, time off, or both).
- 30+: You're critically burned out. Talk to your boss; you may need a leave of absence.

#### 2. **Burnout Prevention (What You Should Be Doing Now)**

**Prevention = Delegate + Automate + Protect Time + Say No + Take Time Off**

These aren't separate. They're a system.

**Delegation (30% of your time on high-value work only):**
- Core manager work (coaching, hiring, strategy) = 30–40% of week
- Delegatable work (training, deal coaching, onboarding) = 30–40% of week (delegated)
- Automatable work = <10% of week (most automated)

If you're doing >40% delegatable and automatable work, you're in burnout territory.

**Automation (eliminate busywork):**
- If you're doing >2 hours/week of busywork (reports, data entry, scheduling), automate 1 task this month.
- If you're doing >4 hours/week of busywork, automate 2 tasks this month.

**Protect time (focus time + boundaries):**
- Non-negotiable focus time: 8 hours/week minimum
- Office hours: 1–2 hours/week (predictable availability)
- Email/Slack time: 3 hours/week max (batched)
- Everything else is meetings/coaching/1:1s

If you're in meetings >50% of the week, you don't have time to think. Fix it.

**Say no (ruthlessly):**
- Every meeting invite that's not core or delegatable development = decline
- Every email asking for 'just a quick thing' during focus time = defer
- Every meeting that doesn't need you = politely opt out

**Take time off (non-negotiable):**
- 1 week off every quarter (minimum)
- Truly off (not 'off but checking email')
- This isn't selfish; this is the reset button

**If you're doing all 5 of these, burnout is unlikely.**

#### 3. **Burnout Recovery Timeline**

**If you're already burned out, here's how you climb back:**

**Week 1–2: Recognition and Planning**
- Take the 10-point check (you'll probably score 25+)
- Talk to your boss: 'I'm working unsustainable hours. Here's my plan to fix it.'
- Identify your biggest time-waster (probably not delegating or automating enough)
- Commit to one delegation and one automation this week

*Workload:* Still 60+ hours (nothing changes overnight), but you have a plan.

**Week 3–4: First Delegation + Automation**
- Complete one TIME 1 + TIME 2 delegation (2–3 hours of teaching)
- Deploy one automation (forecasting or scheduling)
- Block focus time; protect it

*Workload:* 55–60 hours (1–2 hours reclaimed in first month)

**Week 5–8: Sustained Delegation + Second Automation**
- Complete TIME 3 (independent delegation; you step back)
- Deploy second automation (onboarding or meeting prep)
- Add office hours (1 hour/week predictable availability)
- Decline meetings aggressively

*Workload:* 50–55 hours (focus time increase; coaching depth increasing; busywork dropping)

**Week 9–12: Full System in Place**
- 2–3 delegations complete (you're getting 2–4 hours/week back)
- 2–3 automations live (another 2–4 hours/week)
- Focus time protected (8 hours/week minimum)
- Office hours routine (1 hour/week)
- Saying no as default

*Workload:* 45–50 hours (normal, sustainable)

**Month 4–6: The Reset**
- Take time off (1 week minimum, truly unplugged)
- Let your team run without you (they're ready now)
- Return to baseline: 40–45 hours, strategic focus, coaching depth

*Workload:* 40–45 hours (sustainable; you can think; team is stronger)

#### 4. **Red Flags: When to Escalate**

If any of these are true, talk to your manager or HR:

- You're consistently working >60 hours and it's not changing with delegation/automation
- You're having health issues (sleep, anxiety, physical symptoms) related to work
- You're considering leaving because you're burned out
- You're having thoughts of self-harm or severe depression
- You're seeing your mental health decline for >4 weeks despite preventive changes

**This is not weakness. This is information.**
