# Long-Term Leadership Growth — Module Brief

**Module Status:** Ready for Content Development  
**Module #:** 12 of 12  
**Position in Curriculum:** Long-term trajectory (final module; capstone for entire curriculum)

---

## Executive Overview

**Long-Term Leadership Growth** teaches first-time managers how to grow beyond their first role and build sustainable leadership. This module covers career trajectory, executive presence, navigating org politics, finding mentors, and how to stay a good leader as you move up.

The core insight: **Your first year is about learning to manage one team. Your next five are about learning to lead an organization. They require completely different skills. Start building them now.**

---

## Core Teaching Objective: THE AHA MOMENT

**What we want managers to realize:**

Most new managers think "success" is hitting quota and keeping their team happy. Those are table stakes. Real success is building capabilities that let you grow beyond the role—executive presence, strategic thinking, political savviness, influence without authority.

**More specifically:**

1. **Your first role is the foundation.** You're learning management fundamentals: how to coach, how to set rhythm, how to build culture. Master this first. But don't stay here forever.

2. **The next level requires new skills.** As you move up (from individual contributor manager to manager of managers to director to VP), the skills that got you here won't get you there. You need to learn: strategic thinking, cross-functional influence, exec communication, organizational navigation.

3. **Executive presence isn't charisma.** It's clarity, competence, follow-through, and showing up prepared. You can build it. It starts now.

4. **Politics is not dirty.** It's how decisions get made in organizations. Learning to navigate it (without compromising integrity) is a survival skill.

5. **Your mentors make or break you.** A good mentor accelerates your growth by 2 years. A bad one (or none) keeps you stuck. Finding and cultivating mentors is part of the job.

6. **Staying good as you grow is hard.** It's easy to become a tyrant when you have power. Staying grounded, staying curious, staying humble—that's the work.

**By the end of this module, managers should feel:**
- Clear on their growth trajectory (where do they want to go?)
- Equipped to build executive presence
- Understanding that politics is a skill, not a dirty word
- Intentional about finding mentors and sponsors
- Committed to growing without losing themselves

## The Problem We're Solving

### The Mistake Most New Managers Make

New managers typically do one of two things with growth—both problematic:

**Mistake 1: The Stuck Manager**
"I'm fine being a manager of reps. I don't need to grow."

What happens: You plateau. You get bored. You're doing the same thing for 5 years. Your learning stops. You become cynical or leave the company.

**Mistake 2: The Runaway Climber**
"I'm going to be VP by year 3. I need to move up fast."

What happens: You skip the fundamentals. You move up before you're ready. You struggle in the next role because you didn't master this one. You fail and have to start over.

### The Real Issue

**Growth takes intentionality AND patience.**

Most new managers don't think about growth (they're just trying to survive the first year). Some think about it but rush it. Neither works.

The way out: **Master your current role. Then intentionally build skills for the next one. Find mentors. Be patient. Move when you're ready.**

## Key Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. **The Leadership Growth Trajectory: Knowing Your Path**

Not everyone wants to be CEO. That's OK. Know what you want and build toward it.

**Possible Paths:**

**Path 1: The Individual Contributor**
- Start as AE or SDR
- Become a specialist (top performer)
- You're done. That's a great career.
- (This module isn't for you—no shame in this)

**Path 2: The Manager (One Level)**
- Start as individual contributor
- Become manager of team (6-10 people)
- Stay there for career
- Get really good at team management
- Become a trusted mentor
- Perfectly valid path

**Path 3: The Manager of Managers**
- Start as manager of team
- Grow to manager of 2-3 teams (20-30 people)
- Responsible for strategy, cross-team communication, budget, hiring
- Skills: Strategic thinking, delegating at scale, executive communication
- Takes 3-4 years minimum

**Path 4: The Director / VP**
- Start as manager of managers
- Grow to oversee entire function (60+ people)
- Responsible for function strategy, org design, board-level communication
- Skills: Org design, executive presence, political navigation, vision-setting
- Takes 5+ years minimum

**The Key:**
- Know which path appeals to you (now it might change)
- Build skills intentionally for the next step
- Don't skip steps (you'll fail)

**Reality Check:**
- Many great managers are at level 2 (manager of one team)
- It's OK to stay there
- You still need to grow (deeper skills, better coach, etc.)

### 2. **Executive Presence: The "What" & "How"**

Executive presence isn't charisma. It's a set of behaviors you can develop.

**What It Looks Like:**
- **Clarity:** You say what you mean. No rambling.
- **Confidence:** You don't apologize for your thinking. You invite challenge.
- **Competence:** You know your stuff. You're prepared.
- **Composure:** You stay calm under pressure. You don't panic.
- **Follow-through:** You do what you say. People trust you.

**How to Build It:**

**Clarity:**
- Before every meeting: What's the point? What do I want to say?
- Prepare 2-3 key messages (not 10)
- Practice your opening (know the first 30 seconds cold)
- Stop rambling (if you said it, you're done)

**Confidence:**
- Own your perspective (even if it's unpopular)
- Invite challenge ("Here's my thinking. What am I missing?")
- Don't apologize for having a viewpoint
- Back it up with data, not opinions

**Competence:**
- Know more than you're presenting (only share top 10% of your thinking)
- Prepare thoroughly
- Answer questions directly (don't dodge)
- If you don't know: "I don't know. I'll find out and follow up."

**Composure:**
- Don't react in the moment (especially if upset)
- Pause before responding
- Take a breath (literally, it helps)
- Calm people → calm you (feedback loop)

**Follow-through:**
- If you say you'll do it, do it
- If you're going to miss a deadline, tell people early
- Consistency builds trust
- One broken commitment = tons of doubt

**The Work:**
- These are learnable skills
- Fake it until you make it (try the behaviors; they become natural)
- Get feedback from mentors (ask them what you're missing)

### 3. **Navigation & Politics: Reading the Room**

Every organization has informal power structures. Learning to navigate them is a career skill.

**What Politics Isn't:**
- Backstabbing or lying
- Manipulation or deception
- Playing favorites or excluding people

**What Politics Is:**
- Understanding who has influence (not just titles)
- Knowing how decisions actually get made
- Building relationships across the org
- Aligning stakeholders before big decisions
- Managing your reputation

**How to Navigate:**

**Understand the actual power structure:**
- Who do execs listen to?
- Who gets their projects funded?
- Who has the ear of leadership?
- (Often not the person with the biggest title)

**Build relationships before you need them:**
- Coffee with peer managers (other departments)
- Coffee with your boss's boss (let them know you exist)
- Coffee with folks from other functions (sales, product, finance)
- These relationships = capital when you need it

**Know how decisions are made:**
- Official decision (what the org says)
- Real decision (what actually happened in the room before the meeting)
- Sometimes they're the same. Often they're not.
- Smart players understand both.

**Manage your reputation:**
- You're more powerful than you think (yes, even as a manager of one team)
- What people say about you behind your back matters
- Be competent, kind, and consistent
- That's 90% of reputation

**Align stakeholders before big asks:**
- Don't surprise leadership with a big decision
- Work the room first (understand objections, build buy-in)
- Then the official decision is just a formality

**The Rule:**
- Politics is not dirty. It's how organizations work.
- Naive people who ignore it get stuck.
- Smart people who navigate it get ahead.

### 4. **Finding Mentors & Sponsors**

You can't grow to the next level alone. You need people who've been there.

**Mentor vs. Sponsor:**

**Mentor:**
- Gives you advice and perspective
- Answers your questions
- Helps you think through problems
- You see them occasionally (coffee, lunch)
- No agenda for them (they just want to help)

**Sponsor:**
- Actively advocates for your growth
- Puts you in stretch roles
- Recommends you for opportunities
- Has power in the org
- Benefits if you succeed (reputation rubbing off)

**Why Both Matter:**
- Mentor = guidance (helps you avoid mistakes)
- Sponsor = opportunity (opens doors)
- You need both to grow fast

**How to Find a Mentor:**

**Step 1: Identify who to ask**
- Someone in a role you aspire to
- Someone who's done what you want to do
- Someone you respect
- Someone with time (very busy people are bad mentors)

**Step 2: Reach out directly**
- "I really respect what you do. Would you be open to getting coffee once a month?"
- Most people say yes (no one's asked before)
- If they say no, ask someone else

**Step 3: Come prepared**
- Have questions
- Don't waste their time
- Show you've thought about stuff
- End on time

**How to Find a Sponsor:**

**Step 1: Earn their attention**
- Do great work
- Show up in meetings (visible)
- Volunteer for stretch projects
- Be someone they notice

**Step 2: Be useful to them**
- If they're leading a big project, volunteer to help
- Make their life easier
- Deliver results

**Step 3: Make it easy for them to advocate**
- Managers want to recommend smart people (makes them look good)
- If you're reliable, competent, and visible—they'll recommend you

**Step 4: Ask for what you want**
- "I'd like to move into a manager of managers role in the next 2 years. Can you help me get there?"
- Most sponsors will actively help (if you're worth it)

**The Reality:**
- You might have 3-4 mentors
- You might have 1-2 sponsors
- These relationships compound your growth

### 5. **Staying Good as You Grow: The Ego Trap**

As you get more power, it's easy to become a jerk. Staying good is intentional work.

**The Trap:**
- You get promoted → people suddenly say yes to everything
- You like it → you start believing your own BS
- You become defensive when questioned
- You stop learning
- Your team fears you instead of respects you
- You fail on the next promotion and don't understand why

**How to Avoid It:**

**Stay curious:**
- Keep asking questions (don't assume you know)
- Read books, talk to people, learn from other managers
- The moment you think you know everything is when you stop growing

**Stay humble:**
- You don't have all the answers
- Your team members know things you don't
- Ask for their input (and actually listen)
- Admit when you're wrong

**Stay grounded:**
- Remember what it was like to be individual contributor
- Don't lose touch with the work
- Spend time with your team
- Understand their reality

**Stay self-aware:**
- Get 360 feedback (anonymous feedback from team, peers, boss)
- Don't get defensive about it
- Actually change based on it
- 360 feedback every 1-2 years as you grow

**Stay in touch with your values:**
- What matters to you? (this changes as you grow)
- Are you compromising on anything for advancement?
- If you are, is it worth it?
- Only you can answer that

**The Work:**
- Growth without integrity = success that doesn't feel good
- Stay aligned with your values as you move up
- Your team will forgive mistakes, not hypocrisy

## Sub-Module Breakdown

### Sub-Module 1: Knowing Your Growth Path (11 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers understand different growth paths
- Get them to think about what they want
- Eliminate the "I have to be CEO" mentality

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Not everyone wants to grow into VP
- That's OK; still requires intentional growth
- Know your path, build toward it
- Don't skip steps

**Paths:**
- Individual contributor (top performer, specialist)
- Manager of one team (good career endpoint)
- Manager of managers (bigger scope, bigger responsibility)
- Director/VP (org-wide impact, politics, strategy)

**Real Example:**
- Person A wants manager of managers (manager at scale, building multiple teams)
- Person B wants to stay manager of team (master that, become mentor to new managers)
- Both are valid; both require growth; just different directions

**Business Impact:**
- Retention (people know their path, willing to stay)
- Intentional development (build skills for next level)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't staying a manager of one team giving up?" (No. It's choosing what you want.)
- "How do I know what I want?" (Talk to people in different roles. You'll figure it out.)

### Sub-Module 2: Building Executive Presence (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach that exec presence is a skill, not a talent
- Show the components (clarity, confidence, competence, composure, follow-through)
- Give them actionable ways to build it

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Executive presence = clarity + confidence + competence + composure + follow-through
- Not charisma or personality
- Learnable; you can build it
- Starts now (in team meetings, with your boss, in 1:1s)

**Practice:**
- Every meeting: Have 2-3 key messages (not 10)
- Prepare opening (first 30 seconds)
- Don't ramble (if you said it, stop)
- Pause before answering (composure)
- Say what you'll do and do it (follow-through)

**Real Example:**
- Manager was rambling in meetings, lost people
- Focus: 3 key messages, practice opening, stop talking
- 3 months: People noticed the clarity; he was seen as more thoughtful

**Business Impact:**
- Better communication → better decisions
- People take you more seriously
- Opens doors for growth

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't this just acting?" (At first. Then it becomes real.)
- "Can I be myself?" (Yes. This IS being yourself, just more focused.)

### Sub-Module 3: Navigation & Politics (12 min)
**Objectives:**
- Reframe politics as a neutral skill
- Teach how to read rooms and build relationships
- Help managers understand power structures

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Politics is how organizations work
- Not dirty; just how decisions happen
- Smart people learn to navigate it
- Start building relationships now

**Three Skills:**
- Understand who has actual power (titles don't tell whole story)
- Build relationships early (before you need them)
- Know how decisions are made (official vs. real)

**Real Example:**
- Manager wanted to start new initiative
- Did homework first: Coffee with peers, boss's boss, finance
- Built buy-in before proposing
- Official decision was easy (everyone was already aligned)

**Business Impact:**
- Better odds on big initiatives (people are already on board)
- Faster decisions (less surprise)
- Better relationships (people know you before you need something)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Isn't this manipulative?" (No. It's transparent relationship-building.)
- "What if people see me as political?" (If you're competent and kind, they'll see you as smart.)

### Sub-Module 4: Finding Mentors & Sponsors (13 min)
**Objectives:**
- Teach the difference between mentors and sponsors
- Show how to find and cultivate both
- Help managers understand why both matter

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Mentor = advice/guidance (no power)
- Sponsor = advocate/opportunity (has power)
- You need both to grow to next level
- Most people are willing to help if you ask

**How to Find Mentor:**
- Identify someone in role you want
- Ask directly (most say yes)
- Come prepared, don't waste time
- Meet occasionally (coffee, lunch)

**How to Find Sponsor:**
- Do great work
- Be visible and useful
- Make it easy for them to advocate
- Ask for what you want (specifically)

**Real Example:**
- Manager wanted to grow to director
- Found mentor (director in another company, took coffee)
- Found sponsor (VP at current company, volunteered for her projects)
- 2 years later: Promoted, mentor had guided thinking, sponsor had advocated

**Business Impact:**
- Faster growth (mentors + sponsors accelerate it)
- Better decisions (mentor wisdom)
- More opportunities (sponsor opens doors)

**Tension to Address:**
- "What if they say no?" (Ask someone else. It's OK.)
- "How do I ask for help without being needy?" (Direct is better than needy. "Can we get coffee monthly?" is fine.)

### Sub-Module 5: Staying Good as You Grow (11 min)
**Objectives:**
- Help managers avoid the ego trap
- Teach that staying humble is a skill
- Help them stay aligned with their values

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Power changes people (easily)
- You have to stay intentional about staying good
- Curiosity, humility, groundedness, self-awareness = antidotes
- 360 feedback helps (listen to it)

**Staying Good Checklist:**
- [ ] Still asking questions (not just answering)
- [ ] Still learning (reading, talking to people)
- [ ] Getting feedback (360, informal)
- [ ] Staying in touch with team
- [ ] Remembering where I came from
- [ ] Acting aligned with my values

**The Trap:**
- People say yes to everything
- You start believing your own BS
- Stop learning
- Team fears instead of respects you
- Fail on next promotion; surprised why

**Real Example:**
- Manager promoted to director; suddenly everyone agreed with him
- Team was afraid to challenge him
- 360 feedback revealed it (team was walking on eggshells)
- He got it; stopped needing agreement; team relaxed
- Performance improved

**Business Impact:**
- Team performance stays high (trust remains)
- You stay coachable (keep growing)
- Relationships survive promotion (people still like you)

**Tension to Address:**
- "Doesn't power mean I finally get to be the expert?" (No. The more power, the more you need to listen.)
- "What if listening to feedback makes me look weak?" (It makes you look confident.)
