# SECTION C: ROLE-PLAY SCENARIOS (5 Detailed Scenarios)

## SCENARIO 1: ASKING FOR A MENTOR

**Context:**
You've identified someone you respect. Maybe it's someone in your company, maybe it's someone outside. Either way, they're impressive and you want their guidance.

**Your Challenge:** Ask them without being weird, desperate, or asking for too much.

---

### SCENARIO SETUP

**The Person:** Sarah, VP of Sales at a competitor company. She built a 20-person sales team from scratch. You've seen her speak at industry events. You admire how she handles objections and talks about team development.

**Your Situation:** You're a new manager, 6 months in. You want to understand how she built such a strong team. You don't know her well—you've met once at a conference.

**Your Goal:** Get her to agree to monthly coffee to give you guidance.

---

### THE RIGHT WAY TO DO IT

**Step 1: Find a Legitimate Connection**
Send her a message (email or LinkedIn) referencing something specific:

*"Hi Sarah, I saw your talk at [conference] about building high-performing sales teams. The part about hiring for coachability resonated with me—I'm trying to apply that with my team now. I've been following your work since, and I'd love to learn from your experience. Would you be open to coffee once a month? I have specific questions about how you approach team building. No pressure—just if you have space."*

**Why This Works:**
- Specific reference (not generic praise)
- Clear ask (once a month, not endless time commitment)
- Low pressure ("no pressure")
- About *her* and what she knows, not about you

---

### ROLE-PLAY: WHAT YOU'LL SAY

**Practice saying this out loud (even if it's awkward):**

*"Hi Sarah, I saw your talk about [specific thing]. I've been thinking about [your challenge]. I'd love to learn from you. Would you be open to grabbing coffee once a month? I have questions about [specific area]. I know you're busy—only if you have space."*

---

### THE CONVERSATION (IF SHE SAYS YES)

**First Coffee:**
- **You:** "Thanks for taking the time. I want to be respectful of your time, so I came with a few questions. Is that okay?"
- **Her:** "Sure."
- **You:** "I'm 6 months into managing. My challenge right now is [one thing]. I saw how you handled this in [example]. How did you approach it?"

(Then listen. Don't talk about yourself. Ask follow-ups.)

**After the coffee:**
- Send a thank-you message mentioning something she said that helped you
- Tell her the outcome or how you applied her advice
- Set the next coffee (30 days out)

---

### WHAT COULD GO WRONG

**Problem 1: You ask for too much time.**
- **Wrong:** "Would you be my mentor? Can we meet weekly?"
- **Right:** "Monthly coffee—would that work for you?"

**Problem 2: You're too needy.**
- **Wrong:** "I'm really struggling and I need help."
- **Right:** "I want to learn from your experience."

**Problem 3: You disappear after the first meeting.**
- **Wrong:** You get advice, say thanks, never reach out again.
- **Right:** You follow up with what you did with the advice. You keep showing up.

---

### DEBRIEF QUESTIONS

1. **Why did you choose this person as a mentor?** What made them the right fit?

2. **What was the hardest part of asking?** How will you get past that?

3. **If they'd said no, what would you have done?** (This should be your backup plan.)

4. **How will you show up in this relationship to make it worth their time?** (You have to give something, not just take.)

5. **What's one thing you hope they teach you?**

## SCENARIO 2: ASKING FOR A SPONSOR

**Context:**
You're not asking someone to sponsor you (that's weird and doesn't work). You're building visibility and demonstrating your capability so they *want* to sponsor you.

**But sometimes, you can nudge it. Here's how.**

---

### SCENARIO SETUP

**The Person:** Marcus, your VP. He's not your direct boss, but he oversees your department. He has a strong track record of developing people.

**Your Situation:** There's a new strategic initiative—market expansion into a new region. It's a high-visibility project. You think you could lead it (or be part of the team that leads it). You've seen hints that Marcus is looking for someone.

**Your Goal:** Get Marcus to think of you for this opportunity.

---

### THE WRONG WAY (DON'T DO THIS)

*"Hi Marcus, I want you to be my sponsor. Can you help me get the market expansion project?"*

(This doesn't work. Too direct. Puts him in an awkward position.)

---

### THE RIGHT WAY

**Step 1: Build Visibility (Before This Conversation)**

Over the last 3–6 months, you've:
- Asked thoughtful questions in all-hands meetings
- Shared wins (not bragging, just sharing)
- Been visible in your work
- Made sure Marcus knows you exist and respects you

**Step 2: Find a Legitimate Way to Connect**

Maybe there's a project Marcus is leading. Maybe you ask his advice on something. Maybe you run into him and the conversation naturally turns to the market expansion.

**Step 3: Signal Your Interest and Readiness**

If it comes up naturally:

*"I've been thinking about the market expansion. It's interesting—the challenges you're facing feel like [something you have experience with or have studied]. I'd be interested in being part of the team if there's room. What would it take?"*

**Why This Works:**
- You're expressing interest (not asking him to make you an opportunity)
- You're showing you've thought about it (not just hoping)
- You're asking what you need to do (giving him an easy answer)
- You're not asking him to advocate, just asking what's needed

---

### THE CONVERSATION

**You:** "I noticed the market expansion initiative. I've been thinking about it, and I think I could contribute. What would it take to be part of the team?"

**Marcus:** [Various responses possible]

**If he's interested:** "Great. What's the next step?"

**If he's hesitant:** "I understand. What's one thing I could do that would make me a fit for something like this?"

(Then listen. He might say "Develop this skill" or "Prove it on a smaller project first." Do that thing.)

**If he says no:** "Got it. Thanks for being straight with me. If another opportunity comes up, would you keep me in mind?"

(Then let it go, and keep building.)

---

### THE REAL MOMENT: WHEN HE THINKS OF YOU

This won't come from one conversation. It will come from 12–18 months of:
- Him seeing your capability
- You being easy to work with
- Him knowing you exist
- Him trusting you can execute

Then, one day, someone asks, "Who can lead this?" and he says, "I have someone."

*That's* the sponsorship moment. And you didn't ask for it.

---

### WHAT COULD GO WRONG

**Problem 1: You ask too directly.**
- Don't: "Will you sponsor me?"
- Do: Signal interest, demonstrate capability, let him initiate.

**Problem 2: You disappear after you ask.**
- Don't: Ask for the opportunity, get it, then never see him again.
- Do: Keep building the relationship. Keep being visible.

**Problem 3: You only talk to him when you need something.**
- Don't: Text him only when there's an opening.
- Do: See him regularly. Ask his advice. Be interested in his work.

---

### DEBRIEF QUESTIONS

1. **Who is your potential sponsor right now?** (Not who you want—who actually has power?)

2. **What's stopping you from building a relationship with them?**

3. **What would make you visible to them?** (What could you do in the next 3 months that they'd notice?)

4. **How will you stay in their orbit without being annoying?**

5. **What opportunity are you hoping they'll open the door for?**

---

## SCENARIO 3: NAVIGATION — WORK THE ROOM BEFORE A BIG PROPOSAL

**Context:**
You have a big proposal coming. It requires buy-in from multiple teams. You could present it and hope everyone agrees. Or you could work the room first.

---

### SCENARIO SETUP

**The Proposal:** You want to change how your team does quarterly planning. It involves shifting some responsibility to another team (Customer Success). It requires approval from three people: your boss, the VP of CS, and the CFO (who cares about process efficiency).

**The Temptation:** Send the proposal to all three, present it in a meeting, hope they say yes.

**The Smart Way:** Align each person *before* the meeting, so the meeting is just confirmation.

---

### THE STRATEGIC APPROACH

**Step 1: Map Stakeholders and Their Concerns**

| Stakeholder | Their Interest | Likely Concern | Position Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Your Boss | Team efficiency | Will this disrupt my team? | Champion |
| VP of CS | Team impact | Will this add work? | Supporter |
| CFO | Process | Will this improve efficiency? | Approver |

---

**Step 2: Talk to Each Before the Proposal**

**Conversation with Your Boss** (Your champion):
- *"I'm thinking about changing how we do quarterly planning. It would improve [benefit for your team]. I want to get you aligned before I propose it. Thoughts?"*
- (Listen. Incorporate feedback. Get her as your champion.)

**Conversation with VP of CS** (The potential resistance):
- *"I'm proposing a change that would involve your team a bit. I wanted to understand your perspective first. The challenge is [problem]. My idea is [solution]. Would this work for you? What would you need?"*
- (Listen. Adjust. Get her input so she feels heard, not surprised.)

**Conversation with CFO** (The approver):
- *"I'm thinking about a process change. It would [improve efficiency]. Here's the framework. Do you see any issues? What would you need to see to approve this?"*
- (Listen. Make her feel like she had input. Build consensus.)

---

**Step 3: The Proposal Meeting**

By now, all three people have *already agreed* (or you know exactly what adjustments they need).

The meeting is just formality.

*"Great. You've all seen the proposal. [Your Boss], you had some input—I incorporated that. [VP CS], you flagged [concern]—here's how we addressed it. [CFO], you wanted to see [thing]—we've included that. Any questions?"*

(Very smooth. Very high chance of approval.)

---

### WHAT COULD GO WRONG

**Problem 1: You skipped the alignment.**
- You presented cold. Now people are defensive. Pushback happens in the meeting.

**Problem 2: You aligned wrong stakeholders.**
- You got your boss and VP of CS but not the CFO. CFO tanks it in the meeting.

**Problem 3: You aligned but didn't listen.**
- You asked for input but didn't actually incorporate it. Now they feel ignored.

---

### DEBRIEF QUESTIONS

1. **What's your big proposal or initiative right now?** (Real one, not hypothetical.)

2. **Who are the three stakeholders you need alignment from?**

3. **What are their real concerns?** (Not what you think—what they actually care about.)

4. **Have you talked to each one individually yet?** If not, when will you?

5. **What could go wrong if you don't align first?**

## SCENARIO 4: RECEIVING FEEDBACK WITH GRACE

**Context:**
You get 360 feedback. It's hard to hear. It reveals something you didn't want to know.

Your challenge: Receive it, don't defend it, and actually change.

---

### SCENARIO SETUP

**The Feedback:** Your 360 feedback reveals: "You talk a lot in meetings. People don't feel heard. You seem defensive when people disagree with you."

**Your Reaction** (Internal, not out loud): *"That's not fair. I listen. I ask for input. This is wrong. Who said this?"*

**What You Actually Do:** You sit there. You nod. You say thank you.

---

### THE RIGHT RESPONSE IN THE MOMENT

**When your boss or mentor is giving you feedback:**

**They Say:** "Some people said you talk a lot and don't give others room to contribute."

**You Don't Say:** "That's not true. I do ask for input." (Defending = losing credibility)

**You Say:** "Thank you. That's hard to hear, but I appreciate it. Can you give me an example?" (Listening = gaining credibility)

**They Say:** "Sure. In the product meeting last week, you had strong opinions, and when [colleague] tried to offer a different view, you kind of shut it down."

**You Don't Say:** "I was building on his idea." (Explaining = defending)

**You Say:** "Okay. I didn't realize I did that. I'll think about this. I appreciate the honesty." (Accepting = building trust)

---

### AFTER THE MEETING

**What You Do:**
1. **Don't spiral.** It's one piece of feedback, not judgment on your worth.
2. **Don't dismiss it.** Even if 5% of it is wrong, 95% might be right.
3. **Do sit with it.** For 3–7 days, just let it land. Don't over-react.
4. **Do get more data.** Ask a trusted peer: "Someone said I talk too much in meetings. Do you see that?" (Listen to the answer.)
5. **Do make one change.** Don't try to change everything. Pick one thing. ("I'm going to talk less in meetings and ask more questions.")
6. **Do tell someone.** Accountability. "I got feedback that I talk too much. I'm going to work on asking more questions instead."
7. **Do follow up.** In 6 weeks, ask the person who gave feedback: "How am I doing with that thing?"

---

### WHAT COULD GO WRONG

**Problem 1: You get defensive.**
- "That's not fair" or "That person doesn't know me."
- (People lose trust. They stop being honest with you.)

**Problem 2: You promise to change and don't.**
- You say "Got it, I'll work on this." Then you do nothing.
- (People stop believing you. Feedback stops coming.)

**Problem 3: You change for a week, then revert.**
- You try to talk less, but it's hard. After two weeks, you're back to old habits.
- (This is normal. But you have to keep trying. Change takes 8–12 weeks to stick.)

---

### DEBRIEF QUESTIONS

1. **What feedback have you received that was hard to hear?**

2. **What was your first instinct? Did you defend, or did you listen?**

3. **Looking back, was there truth in it?**

4. **What would change if you actually acted on that feedback?**

5. **What's one piece of feedback you're going to act on this month?**

## SCENARIO 5: VALUES CONFLICT — PROMOTION VS. INTEGRITY

**Context:**
You're offered a promotion. It's exciting. But the new role requires something that conflicts with your values.

Your challenge: Decide consciously. Don't just say yes.

---

### SCENARIO SETUP

**The Opportunity:** You're offered a director-level role. 40% raise. Lead a whole department. Big title.

**The Catch:** The department has ethical corners being cut. Low cost, good for the company's profit margin. But not great for customers. You've seen the complaints.

**Your Values:** Integrity. Helping people. Doing the right thing.

**Your Conflict:** You want the promotion. But you're not sure you can run a department that operates this way.

---

### THE QUESTIONS TO ASK YOURSELF (Honest Ones)

**1. Can I change this if I take the role?**
- *"If I'm in charge, can I fix the ethical issues? Will I have the power and resources?"*
- If yes: Maybe take it.
- If no: You have a problem.

**2. Am I compromising temporarily or permanently?**
- *"Is this a 6-month adjustment while I fix things, or am I signing up for years of cutting corners?"*
- Temporary: Maybe acceptable.
- Permanent: Probably not.

**3. What happens if I don't take it?**
- *"Will I regret this? Will I resent the person who gets promoted instead?"*
- Maybe. But resentment will fade.
- Compromising values? That's permanent.

**4. Can I talk to the CEO or senior leader about changing this?**
- *"If I take the role, can I ask for the budget/permission to do this right?"*
- Yes, ask. If they agree, move forward.
- No, they say "it's not in the budget," then you know what you're walking into.

**5. What would I need to stay?**
- *"What would have to be true for me to take this role and feel good about it?"*
- Define it. Then see if it's possible.

---

### THE CONVERSATION (WITH YOUR BOSS/CEO)

**You:** "I'm really interested in this role. I have a question, though. I've noticed [ethical issue]. If I take this position, would I have the latitude to address that? Here's what I'd propose..."

**Their Response Options:**

**Option A: "Yes, we need someone to fix that. Go for it."**
- Great. Take the role. You have a mandate.

**Option B: "We can't afford to change that right now."**
- Okay. Now you know. Decision time.

**Option C: "It's not broken. It's industry standard."**
- Okay. Now you know they don't see it as a problem. Decision time.

---

### THE DECISION

**If It Aligns with Your Values:** Take it. Build a great department.

**If It Doesn't:** You have options.

**Option 1: Decline.** "Thanks for this opportunity. I'm not ready for it right now." (Honest. Clears your conscience.)

**Option 2: Counter-offer.** "I'd love this role if we can address [issue]. If not, I'm not the right fit."

**Option 3: Accept and Plan to Change.** "I'm taking this role. Year one, I'm going to fix [issue]. Here's my plan."

(Only do this if you genuinely believe you can change it and your values can survive the waiting period.)

---

### WHAT COULD GO WRONG

**Problem 1: You take the promotion and hate yourself.**
- You compromise every day. You resent your job. You burn out.
- (Not worth it.)

**Problem 2: You decline and regret it for years.**
- You wonder "what if." You resent that person who got it.
- (This fades. But it's painful.)

**Problem 3: You take it, try to change it, can't, and you're stuck.**
- Now you're director of a department you don't believe in. You can't leave—too big a step down.
- (This is bad. Better to not take it in the first place.)

---

### DEBRIEF QUESTIONS

1. **Are you facing a values conflict right now?** (Or something similar?)

2. **What's the promotion/opportunity?** What's the conflict?

3. **What's your gut telling you?** (Often right.)

4. **If you take it, what would you need to feel okay about it?**

5. **If you decline it, what's the worst that happens?** (Often, it's not as bad as you think.)

6. **What would the best version of you do?**

# SECTION D: WEEKLY ZOOM DISCUSSION PROMPTS (5 Prompts)

## DISCUSSION 1: YOUR GROWTH PATH
**For Sub-Module 1 | Duration: 20-25 minutes**

### Opening Question (Ask the group):

*"What's your 5-year goal? What skills do you need to get there?"*

---

### Breakout Format (Recommended):

**Pairs:** Have each person share their answer with one other person (5 min each = 10 min total).

**Then full group:** Ask 3–4 people to share with the whole group. (Note patterns. Are people clear? Confused? Drifting?)

---

### Facilitator Questions (To Deepen):

*If someone is unclear about their goal:*
- "What would make you happy in 5 years?" (Not ambitious—happy.)
- "Are you chasing this because you want it, or because you think you should?"
- "If you could stay in your current role forever, would you be okay with that?" (Answers reveal a lot.)

*If someone is clear:*
- "How are you building toward that right now?"
- "What's one skill you need that you don't have yet?"
- "Who's helping you with this?"

---

### Closing:

*"Write down one skill you're committing to develop this quarter. Tell someone about it. That's your accountability."*

---

---

## DISCUSSION 2: MENTORS & MENTORSHIP
**For Sub-Module 4 | Duration: 20-25 minutes**

### Opening Question:

*"Who could be a mentor for you right now? Why them? Will you ask?"*

---

### Breakout Format:

**Pairs:** Have each person name one potential mentor and explain why (5 min each).

**Then full group:** Ask volunteers: Did you already have a mentor? What's one thing they taught you? If you don't have one, how are you feeling about asking?

---

### Facilitator Questions:

*If someone already has a mentor:*
- "What's one piece of advice they gave you that was game-changing?"
- "How often do you see them?"
- "Are you actually applying what they tell you?"

*If someone is afraid to ask:*
- "What's the worst that could happen?" (Usually, they say no. Is that terrible? No.)
- "What would you need to ask them?"
- "When will you do it?"

*If someone is between mentors:*
- "Who do you want to replace the last mentor?"
- "What made that mentor valuable?"
- "Are you reaching out this week?"

---

### Group Dynamic:

Often, one person will offer to mentor another right in the call. That's beautiful. Let it happen.

---

### Closing:

*"If you don't have a mentor, send one email by next week asking. If you do have one, schedule your next coffee. That's your assignment."*

---

---

## DISCUSSION 3: POLITICAL WINS
**For Sub-Module 3 | Duration: 20-25 minutes**

### Opening Question:

*"Share a political win. When did you work the room, get buy-in, build relationships, and have an easy time getting approval?"*

---

### What You're Looking For:

- Stories of people *not* just relying on merit, but on relationships
- Stories of people aligning stakeholders *before* asking
- Stories of reputation management
- Stories of reading power dynamics and navigating them

---

### Breakout Format:

**Small groups (3–4 people):** Each person shares one story. (5 min each.)

**Then full group:** Ask 2–3 people to share with everyone. Ask the group: What made this work?

---

### Facilitator Questions:

*If someone shares a successful story:*
- "What did you do that made the difference?"
- "Did you plan this, or did it just happen?"
- "Who did you align with first?"

*If someone hasn't had a win:*
- "What's an upcoming initiative you could apply this to?"
- "Who's a stakeholder you need alignment from?"
- "When will you reach out?"

*To the group:*
- "What surprised you about their story?"
- "What's one thing you could steal for your own situation?"

---

### Learning Moment:

Often, people realize: *Most wins aren't luck. They're preparation.*

---

### Closing:

*"Pick one upcoming decision or initiative. Identify three stakeholders. Reach out to each one *before* you need approval. Report back next week."*

## DISCUSSION 4: FEEDBACK & GROWTH
**For Sub-Module 5 | Duration: 20-25 minutes**

### Opening Question:

*"What feedback from your team has surprised you? What will you do with it?"*

---

### Why This Question:

Surprise feedback often reveals blind spots. It's the most valuable.

---

### Breakout Format:

**Pairs:** Share one piece of feedback that surprised you. Why was it surprising? (5 min each.)

**Then full group:** Ask volunteers to share. Ask: What will you do with this?

---

### Facilitator Questions:

*If someone got feedback that stung:*
- "What hurt about it?" (Name the feeling.)
- "Was there truth in it?" (Usually, yes.)
- "What would change if you acted on it?"

*If someone hasn't asked for feedback:*
- "Who would tell you hard truth?" (Start with one person.)
- "What question would you ask them?"
- "When will you reach out?"

*To the group:*
- "How do you usually respond to feedback you don't like?" (Defend? Dismiss? Listen? Cry?)
- "What's the difference between dismissing feedback and learning from it?"

---

### Mini-Teaching Moment:

*"Feedback is free coaching. Your team is telling you where you can improve. If you listen, you change faster. If you defend, nothing changes. Your choice."*

---

### Closing:

*"Ask three people this week: 'What's one thing I could do better?' Then don't defend. Just listen. Report back what you learned."*

---

---

## DISCUSSION 5: VALUES & INTEGRITY
**For Sub-Module 5 | Duration: 20-25 minutes**

### Opening Question:

*"How are you staying true to yourself as you grow? What matters most to you?"*

---

### Why This Question:

This is the capstone. It's about legacy and who they want to become, not just what title they'll have.

---

### Breakout Format:

**Pairs:** Share your three core values and whether your job aligns with them. (5 min each.)

**Then full group:** Ask volunteers. Listen for patterns. Are people aligned? Drifting?

---

### Facilitator Questions:

*If someone is aligned:*
- "How do you protect that alignment?"
- "What's one decision you made based on your values?"
- "How did that turn out?"

*If someone is out of alignment:*
- "Which value are you compromising?"
- "Is it temporary or permanent?"
- "What would need to change?"

*If someone is lost:*
- "What did you care about before you became a manager?"
- "Do you still care about that?"
- "What changed?"

*To the group (deep):*
- "Have you watched someone get promoted and lose themselves?"
- "What happened?"
- "How are you going to avoid that?"

---

### Vulnerable Moment:

Often, someone will share something real. ("I'm not sure I like who I'm becoming." "I'm making decisions I'm not proud of.") Welcome it. This is the moment where real change happens.

---

### Closing:

*"This is a capstone module because your career is long. This is not just a job. This is 40+ years of your life. You get to decide who you're going to be in that time. Make it intentional. Make it true. That's the work."*

*"Before next week: Write down your three values. Share them with one person you trust. Ask them: 'Am I living these?' Then listen."*
