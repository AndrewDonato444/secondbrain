# Module 12: Long-Term Leadership Growth — CAPSTONE
## Video Instructor Script (60 minutes)

**Audience:** New managers in SaaS/luxury real estate  
**Duration:** ~60 minutes across 5 sub-modules  
**Core Message:** Success as a new manager ≠ long-term growth. Know your path. Build intentionally. Stay true to yourself as you climb.

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: Knowing Your Growth Path (11 minutes)
**[0:00–0:30] OPENING HOOK**

*[Instructor speaks directly to camera, calm and conversational]*

"You just got promoted to manager. That was the goal, right? You hit it. So now what?

Here's what most new managers tell me: 'I thought this was the dream. But now I'm wondering... is this really where I want to be in five years?' Or they think: 'Of course I'm going higher. Everyone goes higher.'

But that's not true. And that's actually okay.

This module is about the next 15 years of your career—not just the next quarter. And we're going to start with a question that sounds simple but changes everything: *Where do you actually want to go?*"

**[Talking Points for Instructors — Open with these]**
- Success doesn't auto-define your whole career
- Many valid paths forward; not one "up"
- Most managers don't ask this question until they're miserable
- This is the difference between drifting and deciding

---

**[0:30–11:00] KEY TEACHING: THE FOUR GROWTH PATHS**

*[Instructor presents the framework, using slides or visuals for each path]*

"Let me show you the four real paths that exist for managers. And I mean *real*—these are backed by research and thousands of manager interviews. Pick wrong, and you'll burn out. Pick right, and you'll thrive for decades.

### PATH 1: The Individual Contributor (IC) Specialist

This is not 'failure to advance.' This is a *choice* to go deep instead of wide.

**Who:** You love the craft. You love doing the work—writing code, closing deals, building products. Managing felt like a distraction.

**The Arc:** You manage for 1–3 years, figure out it's not your thing, and move back to IC with a higher title, more money, more respect. Think 'Principal Engineer' or 'Senior Account Executive.'

**Why It's Valid:** The best ICs make more than mid-level managers. You're not trapped. You're clarifying.

**What You Need to Build:**
- Deep expertise (stay current or you'll lose credibility)
- Teaching ability (mentor others without managing them)
- Strategic thinking (see how your work connects to business)
- Comfort saying 'no' to management (it's not a demotion)

**Timeline:** 3–4 years minimum as a manager before moving back to IC (and it's credible). If you do it in 1 year, you look indecisive.

**[PAUSE FOR REFLECTION]** *Instructor: "Raise your hand mentally: Is there any part of you that misses deep work? That's not weakness. That's data."*

---

### PATH 2: Manager of One Team (The Plateau—or The Perfect Fit)

**Who:** You love people. You love building and developing one team. You're excellent at 1:1s, creating culture, and making your people better. But you don't dream about 'scaling' or 'leading leaders.'

**The Arc:** You manage one team—8–15 people. You become exceptional at it. You might lead 2–3 teams max over your career. You're not pushing for the director role.

**Why It's Valid:** These managers create retention, psychological safety, and loyalty that companies depend on. They're not ambitious, but they're *intentional*. And they're rare.

**What You Need to Build:**
- Depth of coaching skills (your team is your legacy)
- Executive presence *within your span* (respected by peers, not competing for promotions)
- Strategic thinking about your function (own it end-to-end)
- Patience with politics and slower climb

**Timeline:** 3–5 years in one team role, then possibly move to another strong team (not "up" but sideways). This is a 20-year path, and it's fulfilling.

**Real Example:** A SaaS sales manager ran a 12-person team for 7 years. She knew every deal, every rep's family situation, coached through every slump. Her reps stayed 4+ years (industry average: 2). She made $140K + equity. Never went to director. Loved her job. That's this path.

**[PAUSE FOR REFLECTION]** *Instructor: "Imagine doing this job really, really well for the next 5 years. Not climbing. Just owning it. How does that feel? That feeling is important data."*

---

### PATH 3: Manager of Managers (The High-Growth Path)

**Who:** You're energized by *people's potential*. You don't just manage—you build managers. You think about org design, scalability, and creating leadership. You're willing to stop doing the work yourself.

**The Arc:** Manage a team for 3–4 years. Get promoted to 'Director' or 'Senior Manager' of multiple teams. You now manage 3–5 managers who manage 8–15 people each. You're responsible for 25–75 people's careers, not just your own team's output.

**Why It's Hard:** You stop shipping, stop being the hero, stop being close to the work. That's a real loss. You're now measured on org health, retention, promotability of your reports, and strategy—not on your own wins.

**What You Need to Build:**
- Executive presence (people have to believe in you without you proving it constantly)
- Delegation (you cannot do the work; you have to see through others)
- Strategic thinking (understand business, not just team)
- Ability to have hard conversations (firing, demoting, telling people 'no')
- Politics navigation (more stakeholders, more competing agendas)

**Timeline:** 3–4 years managing one team → 4–5 years managing managers → 5–7 years in director-level role. So 12–16 years to hit VP (if that's your goal).

**Real Example:** A luxury real estate team lead managed agents for 3 years. Promoted to Regional Manager (managing 3 other team leads). Scaled from 12 agents to 40. Now manages 4 managers. Makes $200K salary + commission. Path to VP exists if she wants it. But she's clear: she wants to build leaders, not run the whole region.

**[PAUSE FOR REFLECTION]** *Instructor: "Think about the managers above you. Are you excited about *being* them? Or just about what the title means? Those are different."*

---

### PATH 4: Director/VP (The Strategic Path)

**Who:** You think about the company, not just your organization. You're willing to sacrifice team closeness for strategic impact. You want to shape the business.

**The Arc:** 3–4 years on a team → 4–5 years managing managers → 5–7 years as director → 3–5 years as VP (or skip director and go SVP). You now own an entire function (Sales, CS, Ops, Product) or a big strategic initiative.

**Why It's Rare:** Most people don't make it here because they lose motivation somewhere in the middle. They got into management because they loved people, but by director level, it's all slides, board meetings, and politics. That's a real adjustment.

**What You Need to Build:**
- All of the above (clarity, confidence, competence, composure, follow-through)
- Plus: Business acumen (P&L, strategy, market dynamics)
- Plus: Political fluency (read boards, investors, other C-suite)
- Plus: Comfort with loneliness (fewer peers, different conversations)

**Timeline:** 12–20 years from IC to VP. And that assumes good companies, good luck, and clear intentionality.

**Real Example:** A SaaS sales rep became Sales Manager, then Director of Sales, then VP of Revenue at a $50M company. 18 years total. Made $500K+ at the end. But she's clear: the last 8 years were harder than the first 10, despite higher pay.

*[Instructor addresses the real concern]*

"Here's what I know: Most of you are thinking, 'Okay, but what if I choose wrong? What if I get to manager of one team and I want more? What if I go for director and realize I hate it?'

This module is about *deciding first.* Then building intentionally toward it.

Your five-year goal isn't 'get promoted.' It's 'end up in the role that makes me successful *and* fulfilled.'

That's the difference between a career and a job."

## SUB-MODULE 2: Building Executive Presence (13 minutes)
**[11:00–11:30] OPENING HOOK**

*[Instructor speaks with energy and clarity]*

"You've picked your path. Now here's the hard truth: **No one cares about your path if they don't believe in you first.**

Executive presence is the thing that makes people believe in you. It's not charisma. It's not personality. It's not even about being loud.

It's five things. And all five are *learnable skills* that you can practice starting today.

A manager I worked with was brilliant but quiet. In meetings, she said almost nothing. People didn't think she had conviction. So I told her to try something: In the next meeting, say *exactly three* things. No more. But say them with conviction.

She did. In a Q4 planning meeting, she made three clear points. Not a speech. Just three. By the end of the quarter, people were asking for her input in bigger meetings. Within a year, she was asked to lead a cross-functional initiative.

That's what executive presence does. It opens doors.

Let me show you the five components. If you build all five, people will follow you. Guaranteed."

---

**[11:30–13:30] KEY TEACHING: THE FIVE COMPONENTS**

*[Instructor presents each component with examples and practice]*

### COMPONENT 1: Clarity

**What It Is:** You say exactly what you mean. No hedging. No filler. People understand your point the first time.

**Why It Matters:** People are drowning in information. They need leaders who *reduce* complexity, not add to it.

**How It Looks:**
- **Fuzzy:** "We should probably think about maybe restructuring how we approach these account management discussions, depending on feedback."
- **Clear:** "We need to restructure account management. Here's why: [one reason]. Here's how we'll do it: [one way]. Here's the timeline: [dates]."

**How to Build It:**
- Before a meeting, write your point in one sentence. *Just one.* That's your message.
- In the meeting, lead with that sentence. Then explain it. Then stop.
- In 1:1s, practice this ruthlessly. Don't meander. Say what you want to say.

**Practice This Week:** Pick one meeting. Write one sentence before it. Lead with that sentence. Notice how people listen differently.

---

### COMPONENT 2: Confidence

**What It Is:** You own your perspective without needing everyone to agree. You're not defensive. You're not trying to convince. You're stating a position.

**Why It Matters:** People follow people who believe in themselves. It's magnetic.

**How It Looks:**
- **Seeking Approval:** "Does anyone else think we should try this approach? I kind of think it could work, but I'm not sure..."
- **Confident:** "I think we should try this approach. I could be wrong. If you see a flaw, tell me. But this is where I'm landing."

**How to Build It:**
- Make a decision. Then defend it only if asked—don't justify it preemptively.
- In meetings, speak to facts and your reasoning, not to what you think people want to hear.
- When someone disagrees, listen—but don't crumble. You can change your mind, but make it a choice, not a collapse.

**Practice This Week:** In your next meeting, make one recommendation without hedging language. Use 'I think' or 'I recommend,' not 'I think maybe we could consider...'

---

### COMPONENT 3: Competence

**What It Is:** People believe you know your stuff. You don't have to know *everything*, but people trust that you've done your homework and you'll find the answer if you don't know.

**Why It Matters:** Confidence without competence reads as arrogance. Competence without confidence reads as insecurity. Together, they're credible.

**How It Looks:**
- **Unconvincing:** "I'm not really sure about the numbers on this, but I think it's good?"
- **Competent:** "Here are the numbers I looked at. Here's my analysis. Here's what I don't know yet—I'll get back to you Thursday on that piece."

**How to Build It:**
- Before important meetings, prepare. Not overthink—prepare. Know your data.
- In conversations, admit what you don't know. That's competent. Pretending is not.
- Ask questions that show you understand the domain. Dumb questions are fine; lazy questions hurt you.

**Practice This Week:** In your next major meeting, come with one piece of data you researched yourself (not forwarded from someone else). Reference it. Let people see you've done work.

---

### COMPONENT 4: Composure

**What It Is:** Under pressure, you don't fall apart. You're not panicked. You're steady.

**Why It Matters:** People look to their leader in a crisis. If you're calm, they're calm. If you're rattled, panic spreads.

**How It Looks:**
- **Panicked:** "Oh no, we just lost a major deal. This is bad. What do we do? I'm not sure what we do..." [voice rising]
- **Composed:** "We lost a major deal. That's not great. Here's what I'm thinking we do next: [plan]. Questions?" [steady tone]

**How to Build It:**
- When something bad happens, pause before you react. Take three deep breaths. Then respond.
- In tense meetings, lower your voice slightly. Slower speech. Lean back a little. Your body language sets the tone.
- Have a bad-news protocol: Here's what happened, here's what we're doing about it, here's when you'll hear from me next. Repeat as needed.

**Practice This Week:** Next time something goes wrong, pause for 30 seconds before responding. Notice what changes.

---

### COMPONENT 5: Follow-Through

**What It Is:** You do what you say you're going to do. When you commit, it happens.

**Why It Matters:** Every broken promise erodes trust. Every kept promise builds it. This is the easiest component to nail, and the most impactful.

**How It Looks:**
- **Unreliable:** "I'll get you that analysis by Friday." [Friday comes, no analysis. Monday: "Sorry, I'll send it Tuesday." Tuesday: analysis arrives with caveats and apologies.]
- **Reliable:** "I'll get you that analysis by Friday." [Friday: analysis arrives, clean, on time. If you can't hit Friday, you tell them Thursday: "I'm going to need until Monday. Here's why." Then Monday comes and it arrives.]

**How to Build It:**
- Only commit to what you can actually do.
- If you're going to miss a deadline, tell people *before* the deadline, not after.
- Track your commitments in a list. Check them weekly. This is basic, but it matters.
- If you say it in a meeting, it's a promise. Act like it.

**Practice This Week:** Make three small commitments this week—to your team, to a peer, to your manager. Hit all three. Let people see you're reliable.

---

*Instructor: "Which of these five components is your weakest? Not your strongest—your weakest. That's where you start. You don't have to be perfect at all five. But you have to be good at all five. Pick one. This week, focus on that one."*

*[Instructor speaks with warmth and directness]*

Clarity: Stop meandering in meetings.

Composure: Pause before you panic.

These aren't personality traits. They're practices. And the moment you start practicing them, people notice. They notice *fast*.

That can be you. This week."

## SUB-MODULE 3: Navigation & Politics (12 minutes)
**[24:00–24:30] OPENING HOOK**

*[Instructor speaks matter-of-factly, with no judgment]*

"Let's talk about something people don't like to admit: **Politics matters.**

Not 'office gossip.' Not 'who's sleeping with whom.' Real politics: *understanding power and navigating it.*

Here's what I know: People who understand power and navigate it intentionally get promoted 3x faster than people who ignore it and hope merit speaks for itself.

Merit *does* matter. But merit + political awareness = acceleration.

I worked with a manager who was brilliant but politically naive. She built an amazing product. Six months later, a less brilliant manager—who had spent 18 months building relationships and aligning stakeholders—got promoted above her.

She was shocked. He was ready.

Politics isn't dirty. Politics is just 'knowing how decisions get made and making sure you're in the room when they do.'

Let me show you how."

---

**[24:30–35:30] KEY TEACHING: READING & NAVIGATING POWER STRUCTURES**

*[Instructor presents the framework with visual org chart examples]*

### LEVEL 1: THE FORMAL ORG CHART (What's on the website)

"This is what everyone sees. CEO → VP Sales → Director Sales → You. That's the official structure.

But it's not how decisions actually get made."

---

### LEVEL 2: THE ACTUAL POWER STRUCTURE (Who really decides)

*[Instructor draws or shows visual]*

"Here's what actually matters:

**Who has veto power?** Not who has approval authority—who can *block* a decision? Often it's not your boss. It's the CFO who controls budget. Or the CTO who controls engineering capacity.

**Who has the ear of the CEO?** This person might be a manager two levels below you, but because they talk to the CEO weekly, they shape decisions.

**Who are the power brokers?** These are people who know everyone. They're not the most senior, but everyone trusts them. Decisions don't move without their thumbs up.

**Who are the blocking coalitions?** Groups that vote together. Sales & Customer Success might align against each other on resource decisions. Product & Engineering might agree on everything.

*Example:* A SaaS company. Org chart says CEO → VP Sales → Sales Manager. But actual power: CEO → CFO (she controls deals), plus VP Sales (sales champion), plus one senior AE who's been there 10 years and has relationships with all major accounts.

When that senior AE says 'this deal is doable,' deals close. When she says 'this client is trouble,' even the CEO listens.

She has power that's not on the org chart.

---

### HOW TO MAP YOUR ORG'S ACTUAL POWER

**Step 1: Identify The Formal Decision-Makers**
- Who has to sign off on major decisions?
- Follow the approval chain.

**Step 2: Find The Gate-Keepers**
- Who *blocks* things even if they're not the decision-maker?
- Who can slow something down?
- Who do people ask before they ask their boss?

**Step 3: Find The Connectors**
- Who knows everyone?
- Who do people trust across departments?
- Who, when you ask them a question, actually knows the answer?

**Step 4: Find The Power Brokers**
- Who shapes opinion?
- Who, when they take a position, does everyone else align?

**Real Example (Luxury Real Estate):**
- Formal: Regional Manager → you
- Actual power: Regional Manager + Broker (the one who brings big clients) + Operations Manager (controls schedule, assignments) + One agent who's been there 15 years (everyone asks her advice)
- When the 15-year agent decided to switch regions, it was chaos. She had more power than anyone realized.

---

### HOW TO BUILD RELATIONSHIPS BEFORE YOU NEED THEM

**The Rule: Relationship Building = Insurance**

You don't build relationships *when* you need something. You build them *before.*

**How:**
- Spend time with people who aren't directly on your team or in your chain.
- Grab coffee with the CFO, the head of another team, the operations person. No agenda.
- Ask them questions about their work. Learn how they think.
- Help them with something without being asked.
- When you actually need them later, the relationship exists.

**Real Example (SaaS):**
- New sales manager spent 3 months building relationships: coffee with head of CS, dinner with head of Product, lunches with peer managers.
- When she wanted to run a pilot program that cut across teams, every stakeholder was already aligned because she'd already built the relationship.
- Same program, presented by someone with no relationships? Blocked for months in approvals.
- Relationship building = 3 months of work. But it saved her 6 months of political friction.

---

### MANAGING YOUR REPUTATION

**What's Your Reputation Right Now?**

Not what you think it is. What people actually say about you in hallway conversations.

Are you:
- "Brilliant but hard to work with"?
- "Reliable but slow"?
- "Fast but cuts corners"?
- "Helpful but people walk over you"?

You have one. Everyone does.

**How to Manage It:**

1. **Find out what it is.** Ask a trusted peer or mentor: "What do people say about me?" Be ready for the answer. It might sting.

2. **Be intentional about what you want it to be.** Not 'liked.' 'Trusted.' Not 'ambitious.' 'Strategic.' Pick what matters for your path.

3. **Align your actions with that reputation.** If you want to be 'trusted,' follow through obsessively. If you want to be 'strategic,' spend time on business, not just operations. If you want to be 'collaborative,' visibly solicit input.

4. **Change it slowly.** You can't wake up one day being a different person. But over 6 months, if you're consistent, reputation shifts.

**Real Example:**
- Manager had a rep as 'aggressive' (not in a good way—willing to throw people under the bus to win).
- She decided she wanted to be known as 'collaborative' instead.
- Over the next year: more cross-team projects, public credit to her peers, owned mistakes instead of blaming others.
- By year two, her rep had shifted. People wanted to work with her.

---

## SUB-MODULE 4: Finding Mentors & Sponsors (13 minutes)
**[36:00–36:30] OPENING HOOK**

*[Instructor speaks with honesty and urgency]*

"You cannot do this alone.

I don't care how smart you are, how hard you work, or how much potential you have. Without people who believe in you and advocate for you, your growth will be 10x slower.

But here's what most people get wrong: They think a 'mentor' is the person who believes in them and opens doors.

Actually, there are two people. And they're different.

One gives you advice. One gives you opportunity.

If you only have one, you're stuck. If you have both, you're unstoppable.

Let me show you the difference."

---

**[36:30–48:00] KEY TEACHING: MENTORS VS. SPONSORS**

*[Instructor uses visual comparison]*

### WHAT'S A MENTOR?

**Definition:** Someone who gives you advice, guidance, and perspective. They're like your smart friend who cares about your growth.

**What They Do:**
- Answer your questions (strategic, tactical, interpersonal)
- Help you think through decisions
- Tell you hard truths you need to hear
- Share their experience
- Warn you about pitfalls they've hit

**What They Don't Do:**
- Advocate for you in rooms you're not in
- Move mountains to get you opportunities
- Risk their political capital for you
- Use their power on your behalf

**Why You Need One:**
You will make mistakes. Mentors help you avoid some of them—or recover quickly from the rest.

**How Often Do You See Them?**
Monthly coffee. Maybe a Zoom every few weeks. Occasionally text. It's a *light* relationship.

**Real Example:**
- Manager at SaaS company has a mentor: CFO from her previous job.
- Every month they grab coffee. She asks about strategy, positioning, managing up, dealing with difficult team members.
- The CFO shares stories. Manager avoids three bad decisions because of these coffees.
- But when the manager was ready for promotion? The CFO didn't push her case. She didn't even know a promotion was being considered.
- That's a mentor relationship.

---

### WHAT'S A SPONSOR?

**Definition:** Someone with power and platform who believes in you and will *advocate* for you to other powerful people.

**What They Do:**
- Recommend you for opportunities
- Bring you into important meetings
- Reference you to peers and leaders
- Advocate for your promotion
- Risk their credibility for you

**What They Don't Do:**
- Necessarily give you advice (though they might)
- Be your friend (though you might like them)
- Have lots of time for you (they're busy)
- Explain what they're doing (they just do it)

**Why You Need One:**
Opportunities are often not posted. They're discussed in rooms you're not in. "We need someone to lead this initiative." Sponsor says, "I have someone. She's ready." Door opens. You never knew it was being considered.

**How Often Do You See Them?**
Less often than a mentor. Maybe quarterly. Often in group settings. They're sponsoring you *behind the scenes*, not hanging with you.

**Real Example:**
- Manager at luxury real estate company has a sponsor: the Regional Broker.
- They don't grab coffee. But the broker has said, publicly and privately, "This manager is going to be a director within three years."
- When the company was looking to restructure, the broker suggested the manager. No interview. Just recommendation.
- When the manager wanted to lead a new market, the broker opened doors. "Go talk to this person."
- The manager got the promotion. It wasn't because of hard work alone. It was because someone with power said, "You should trust this person."
- That's a sponsor relationship.

---

### MENTOR VS. SPONSOR: COMPARISON TABLE

| **Dimension** | **Mentor** | **Sponsor** |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Role** | Advisor, guide | Advocate, champion |
| **Frequency** | Monthly+, coffee/zoom | Quarterly, often in meetings |
| **Time Investment** | Light-to-moderate | Light (but visible) |
| **What They Give** | Wisdom, perspective, warnings | Opportunities, platform, credibility |
| **Risk to Them** | Low (advice is free) | Moderate-to-high (their rep is on the line) |
| **Why They Do It** | They like you; they want you to win | They believe in you; they want you to succeed *as part of their legacy* |
| **When You Need It** | Always. Before big decisions. | When you're ready for the next level. |

---

### HOW TO FIND A MENTOR

**Step 1: Identify Potential Mentors**

Who do you respect? Not because they're powerful—because *how* they operate is how you want to operate.

Questions to ask yourself:
- Who do I want to be more like?
- Who seems to navigate hard situations well?
- Who has a career arc I admire?
- Who seems genuinely happy in their role?

Then ask: What's the relationship? Are they approachable?

**Step 2: Approach Them (Sample Language)**

*"I've been following your work [specific example]. I respect how you [specific quality]. Would you be open to coffee once a month? I'd love your perspective on my career as I think about the next few years. No pressure—just if you have space."*

Why this works:
- Specific (not 'you're amazing')
- Time-bounded (monthly, so not a huge ask)
- Low pressure (no favor asking, just advice)
- Clear what you want (guidance, not sponsorship)

**Step 3: Actually Meet With Them**

- Come with 2–3 specific questions
- Don't complain. Ask.
- Thank them. Follow through on anything they suggest.
- Keep the meetings regular (consistency matters)

**Step 4: Keep It Going**

- If they suggest something, do it and tell them the outcome
- Every few months, give them a brief update on how things are going
- Make it clear their advice mattered

---

### HOW TO FIND A SPONSOR

This is harder. Sponsors choose you; you don't usually choose them.

**How It Actually Works:**

Step 1: **Do Great Work**
Sponsors don't sponsor people who can't execute. They need you to be solid, or you reflect poorly on them.

Step 2: **Make Sure They Know About It**
This isn't bragging. It's visibility.
- In meetings, contribute clearly
- Share wins, not constantly, but genuinely
- Let them see your potential

Step 3: **Build a Light Relationship**
You don't need to be buddies. But when you see them, be present. Be interested in their work. Ask good questions.

You're creating *familiarity*. "I know this person. I like how they operate."

Step 4: **Let Time & Opportunity Do The Work**
When they see an opportunity, they'll think of you. Not because you asked. Because you're *in their head* as someone capable.

Real Example (SaaS):
- New manager was excellent at her job. In all-hands meetings, she asked sharp questions.
- She made sure her VP saw her wins (not boasting, just sharing updates).
- She'd chat with the VP briefly when they crossed paths.
- One day, the VP was talking to the CEO: "We need someone to run a new pilot. I have someone ready."
- Manager didn't ask for this. Didn't interview. She was just in the VP's head as 'someone ready.'
- That became a promotion path.

---

### WHAT IF YOU DON'T HAVE EITHER?

**If you don't have a mentor:**
Ask someone this week. Pick one person. Send the email. Worst case: they say no. More likely: they say yes.

**If you don't have a sponsor:**
Focus on visibility and great work. Sponsor relationships take time to form. You can't force them. But you can make it likely by being excellent and present.

**How long does it take?**
- Mentor: Can be active within a month.
- Sponsor: Usually takes 18–24 months of regular visibility.

## SUB-MODULE 5: Staying Good as You Grow (11 minutes)
**[49:00–49:30] OPENING HOOK**

*[Instructor speaks with quiet intensity]*

"I've watched brilliant people get promoted and become terrible.

Not incompetent. Terrible. As in: people don't trust them, their teams fear them, they make decisions that hurt the company.

It's not because they became stupid. It's because power changed them.

When you're individual contributor, you're held accountable by outcomes. When you're a manager, you're held accountable by behavior. People watch how you treat people. How you make decisions. How you handle pressure.

Most people don't realize how much power they have until it's too late.

This sub-module is about staying good as you grow. Not 'staying humble.' 'Staying aware.' Not 'staying nice.' 'Staying honest.'

Here's how."

---

**[49:30–59:30] KEY TEACHING: THE EGO TRAP & HOW TO AVOID IT**

*[Instructor presents real warning signs and interventions]*

### THE EGO TRAP: What Happens

**The Setup:**
You get promoted. Suddenly, more people agree with you. You have power. People want to please you.

**What You Might Not Notice:**
Your team stops disagreeing with you. Your peer feedback gets nicer (because people want to stay on your good side). Your reports exaggerate your wins and hide your failures.

**Why This Is Dangerous:**
You lose real feedback. You start to believe your own press. You become isolated. You make worse decisions because you're getting incomplete information.

**Real Example (Director at SaaS):**
Manager promoted to Director of Sales. First 6 months: amazing reviews. Team was aligned. Proposals sailed through. She felt invincible.

Then: Her decisions started failing. She pushed into a market that wasn't ready. She hired people who didn't fit. She made strategic bets that lost money.

But she didn't *know* this. Her team wasn't telling her. They just nodded and executed.

It took 360 feedback (forced by her board) to reveal the truth: Her team was terrified of her. They disagreed with 80% of her decisions but didn't say so. She was isolated.

She was a good person. But power had changed how people treated her. And she hadn't noticed.

---

### THE FIVE ANTIDOTES TO THE EGO TRAP

**ANTIDOTE 1: Stay Curious**

**What It Means:** You keep learning. You're not the expert. You're the person asking the smartest questions.

**How It Looks:**
- In meetings, you ask *why* instead of assuming you know
- You read outside your domain
- You ask your team what they're seeing that you're missing
- You admit when you don't know something

**Practice:**
- This week, in three meetings, ask only questions. Don't make statements. Just questions.
- Notice what you learn.

**Real Example:**
VP at luxury real estate company made a point of asking frontline agents what clients wanted. Not telling them. Asking.

By listening, she realized the company was selling the wrong service. Her team knew it. They just weren't being asked.

---

**ANTIDOTE 2: Stay Humble**

**What It Means:** You remember you got lucky. Good timing. Good help. Good companies. If you'd been born somewhere else, been sick at the wrong time, worked for different people—you'd be different.

**How It Looks:**
- You give credit genuinely
- You talk about where you've failed
- You're not defensive when criticized
- You remember where you came from

**Practice:**
- In your next team meeting, share one recent failure. Not glossed over. Real failure.
- Name someone who helped you get to where you are.

**Real Example:**
Manager at SaaS company was promoted and got great reviews. But in her first team meeting, she said:

"I want to be honest. I made a terrible decision with a vendor last quarter. Cost us $50K. I realized I didn't ask the right questions. [Team member] probably would have caught it if I'd actually listened. I'm going to do better."

That one admission? Created a team that trusted her. Because she wasn't afraid to be fallible.

---

**ANTIDOTE 3: Stay Grounded**

**What It Means:** You remember who you were before the title. You keep relationships outside work. You don't live for the job.

**How It Looks:**
- You have a life outside of work
- You talk to friends who don't work at your company
- You remember what it felt like to be managed
- You don't confuse the title with your identity

**Practice:**
- This month, do one thing that has nothing to do with work
- Spend time with someone who doesn't care about your job title

**Real Example:**
Director at SaaS stayed close to her parents. Went home once a month. Her mom asked about *her*, not her job.

That grounding mattered. When she made decisions, she thought about people as humans, not just headcount. Her company's culture was noticeably different from peer companies—more humane, better retention.

---

**ANTIDOTE 4: Stay Self-Aware**

**What It Means:** You actively seek feedback. You know what people really think of you, not what they tell you.

**How to Do It: 360 Feedback**

Get feedback from your boss, your peers, your reports. Not in surveys. In conversations.

**Sample Questions:**
- "What do I do well that I should keep doing?"
- "What's one thing I could change that would make me more effective?"
- "When have I frustrated you? What happened?"
- "Am I easy to work with? What would make me easier?"

**How to Receive Feedback:**
- Don't defend yourself
- Don't explain
- Just listen and say "Thank you"
- Write it down
- Think about it for a week
- Then ask: "Is this true? Should I change?"

**Real Example (Director, Real Estate):**
Director got 360 feedback. Multiple people said: "You talk a lot. I don't get a chance to contribute."

She was surprised. She thought she was collaborative.

But she listened. She committed to changing.

In the next meeting: She spoke less. She asked others their opinion.

By six months later, her next 360 feedback: "Much better. People feel heard now."

Change is possible. But only if you're willing to see yourself clearly.

---

**ANTIDOTE 5: Stay Values-Aligned**

**What It Means:** You know what matters to you. You don't sacrifice it for the job.

**Why This Matters:**
I've watched managers compromise their values slowly. Not all at once. Slowly. A little ethical corner-cutting. A decision that hurts people but makes the quarter. Throwing a team member under the bus for a win.

By the time they realize they've changed, it's too late.

**Values Check (Exercise):**

Write down three values that matter to you: (family? integrity? growth? helping people?)

Now: Are your work decisions aligned with those values?
- Are you sacrificing family for the job? Should you be?
- Are you making decisions you're proud of? Or compromising?
- Are you helping people grow? Or just extracting output?

If you're not aligned, something's got to change. Either the values or the job.

**Real Example:**
Manager valued family. But her job required 60-hour weeks and constant travel.

She realized: Not aligned.

She had two choices:
1. Change the job (not possible in her company)
2. Leave

She left. Smaller role at a company that respected boundaries. Less pay. Much happier.

No shame in either choice. But making it *consciously*—that matters.

---

### WARNING SIGNS YOU'RE LOSING IT

Watch for these:
- ✗ People stop disagreeing with you
- ✗ Your team seems nervous around you
- ✗ You're surprised by problems your team knew about
- ✗ You feel attacked when someone disagrees
- ✗ You justify mistakes instead of acknowledging them
- ✗ You've stopped learning

If you see three or more, you need intervention. Get a mentor. Get 360 feedback. Get honest.
