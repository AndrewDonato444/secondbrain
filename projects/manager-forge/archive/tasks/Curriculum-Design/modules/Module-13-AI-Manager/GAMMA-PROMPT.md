# GAMMA PROMPT: Module 13 — The AI Manager

```
You are creating Module 13 (capstone) for Manager Forge: "The AI Manager."

This is NOT a "how to use ChatGPT" module. This is a theoretical deep-dive into what AI means for sales management, leadership authority, and the future of the job itself.

TARGET AUDIENCE: First-time sales managers (0-12 months in role) who understand that AI is reshaping their job and need a framework for navigating it.

CORE PHILOSOPHY (Critical — Embed This Throughout):
- AI is not a fad. It's the biggest disruption in sales technology in a decade.
- The question is not "Should we use AI?" It's "How do we use AI strategically while maintaining judgment and quality?"
- Managers who don't understand AI capabilities will become obsolete (like CRM-illiterate managers 10-15 years ago).
- Managers who understand AI but let reps abdicate responsibility will destroy team quality.
- Humans win where judgment, synthesis, and context matter. Grunt work is AI's domain.

## MODULE STRUCTURE: 5 SUB-MODULES

### SUB-MODULE 1: THE JOB IS CHANGING
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,500 words
**Learning Objective:** Establish stakes. Make clear this isn't about tools—it's about the job itself fundamentally shifting. Create urgency without panic.

**Key Points to Hit:**
1. **The Inevitability Framing** — AI is not coming. AI is here. It's in your company (or will be). It's in your reps' hands. The question is whether you understand it.

2. **What's Really At Stake** — This is not "nice-to-have" career development. This is table-stakes employment.
   - Compare to CRM literacy 10-15 years ago. If you didn't know Salesforce or HubSpot, you didn't get the job.
   - AI is that inflection point right now. It's not there yet—but it will be.
   - Managers who don't understand AI capabilities won't get hired. Full stop.

3. **Two Scenarios Your Reps Will Face** — And you need to be ready for both:
   - **Scenario A:** Your company leans into AI. They provide tools, workflows, guardrails. Your job = understand what's available and recommend how to use it.
   - **Scenario B:** Your company lags on AI. Your reps ask, "Can I use my own tools?" Your job = understand capabilities well enough to say "yes, do this" or "no, don't do that."

4. **The Real Shift** — It's not about convincing your company to buy tools. It's about YOU understanding capabilities so you can:
   - Recommend workflows that make your team more efficient, higher quality, faster
   - Tell your reps which AI solutions make sense for which work
   - NOT be surprised or confused when they're using AI to do their jobs better

5. **Personal Responsibility** — If your company doesn't provide AI access, that's not an excuse. There are free models, cheap subscriptions, ways to access this. The blocker isn't cost—it's whether you care enough to understand it.

**Tone:** Serious, direct, no sugar-coating. "This is real. This is happening. You need to be ready."

**Real Example Andrew Would Use:** Something like "I just hired a manager who didn't even know what prompt engineering was. She's smart, but she's playing with one hand tied behind her back. I'm going to have to teach her, or I'll have to replace her."

**Discussion Angle:** "What would happen to your job if every insight you pull from the system, an AI could pull first? What would you do differently?"

### SUB-MODULE 2: UNDERSTANDING AI CAPABILITIES
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,700 words
**Learning Objective:** Demystify what AI can and can't do. Map where human judgment is still essential. Create confidence ("I can evaluate this") not anxiety.

**Key Points to Hit:**

1. **What AI Is Actually Good At** (Be Specific)
   - Pattern recognition at scale (reading 100 emails, finding similar themes)
   - Template generation (cold email variations, follow-ups, structures)
   - Data synthesis (pulling info from transcripts, summarizing meetings)
   - Process automation (moving data, categorizing, organizing)
   - Forecast modeling (if given context, can predict outcomes)
   - Speed and consistency (does the same thing the same way, repeatedly)

2. **The Limitations That Matter Most**
   - **The Context Gap** — AI can see what's written; it misses what's not said. A prospect says "we want to close this month" but AI doesn't know if they *can* close this month. That's human judgment.
   - **The Hallucination Risk** — AI confidently generates plausible-sounding but wrong information. It doesn't flag uncertainty well.
   - **The Lazy Proxy Problem** — AI can do a task, but doing it via AI means you stop developing the skill yourself. A rep who only uses AI to cold email never gets good at cold outreach.
   - **The Tone & Authority Problem** — AI outputs can be robotic, generic, missing the personality or edge that makes communication land. Not always, but often.
   - **The "Unsaid Context" Blindness** — AI reads transcripts; it doesn't read the room. It doesn't catch hesitation, excitement, skepticism in tone of voice.

3. **Where Humans Still Win (The Spine of the Module)**
   - **Judgment on high-value communications** — Active negotiations, nuanced asks, relationship salvages. Here, you know the context AI doesn't. You write better than the tool.
   - **Synthesis** — AI gives you data/insights. You decide what to do with it. You connect dots across conversations, historical patterns, team dynamics.
   - **Prompt Engineering** — Some managers will be 10x better at asking AI the right questions, getting better outputs, iterating. This becomes a differentiator.
   - **Deal Quality Assessment** — Is this deal real? Will they actually close? AI can flag risk; humans read intent, credibility, organizational readiness.
   - **What Hasn't Been Said** — The most important context is often what's missing. "They didn't mention budget" or "They said Q3 but seemed hesitant." Humans catch this.

4. **Where AI Should Absolutely Own It**
   - Cold outreach (scale matters more than personalization)
   - Template generation (drafts, variations, structure)
   - Data entry and categorization
   - Meeting summaries and transcription
   - Administrative work
   - Forecasting (if you feed it context)

5. **The Judgment Call Framework**
   - Ask: "Does this communication require context only I have?"
   - Ask: "Am I trying to build a skill in someone, or are we trying to get work done?"
   - Ask: "Is the output quality better, worse, or the same as the human version?"
   - Ask: "What happens if I delegate this and my rep never develops this skill?"

**Tone:** Practical, honest. "AI is good at X, bad at Y. Here's how you tell the difference."

**Real Examples Andrew Should Reference:**
- Cold email: "AI can generate 100 variations in 5 minutes. A human rep takes an hour. Use AI. This is not a character test."
- Deal negotiation: "I negotiated a contract where the other side said X but their budget said Y. That contradiction was the entire leverage point. AI wouldn't catch that."
- Forecast: "If AI has the context and transcript, sure, let it forecast. But *I* still need to gut-check it against what I know about that buyer."

**Visual/Concept to Include:** Maybe a 2x2 grid:
- High-value, high-context work = Human wins
- High-value, low-context work = Judgment still matters
- Low-value, repetitive work = AI owns it
- Low-value, high-context work = AI can do it, but is it worth the cognitive load?

### SUB-MODULE 3: SPOT THE LAZY WORK
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,300 words
**Learning Objective:** Give managers specific frameworks for detecting when reps are abdicating vs. using AI as a multiplier. This is your quality control responsibility.

**Key Points to Hit:**

1. **The Abdication Risk Is Real**
   - Reps will over-rely on AI if you let them
   - They know more about the tools than you do—and they'll use that advantage to do less work
   - Your job (the hard part) is knowing when they've stopped doing meaningful work

2. **What Lazy Work Looks Like** (Specific Tells)
   - **Robotic language** — Generic phrasing, corporate jargon, lacks personality. "Synergize our solutions to drive mutual value" is a tell.
   - **Em dash overuse** — AI loves em dashes and exclamation points. Humans write differently.
   - **Perfect structure** — Too-perfect bullet points, symmetrical lists. Humans are messier.
   - **Lack of context** — The email doesn't reference specific conversation, doesn't personalize, doesn't show they listened.
   - **Speed red flag** — Your rep just sent 50 emails in 2 hours. Either they're using AI, or they're copy-pasting garbage.
   - **The "I was just about to say..." moment** — When you point out a flaw and the rep says "oh, the AI generated that," not "I wrote that and should have caught it."

3. **Your Quality Control Process** — What You Need to Do
   - **Review high-value communications** — Deal updates, prospect emails, performance feedback
   - **Ask for the thought process** — "Walk me through why you said it this way." If they stammer, they didn't write it.
   - **Check against past work** — Does this sound like them? Does it match their voice? Inconsistency is a tell.
   - **Spot-check for context** — Does this email reference the specific prospect, their industry, their pain point? Or is it generic?
   - **Look for the unsaid** — Good reps anticipate objections, pick up on hesitation, ask second-order questions. AI often misses these.

4. **The Conversation You Need to Have**
   - It's not "you're using AI, that's bad"
   - It's "when you use AI for X, that's great. When you use AI for Y instead of doing the work yourself, that's lazy."
   - **Example:** "AI can draft cold emails. Draft 10, pick the best one, send it. That's smart. AI can draft your deal updates, you send them verbatim without reading them. That's lazy. I can tell the difference."

5. **The Tricky Bit: Prompt Engineering**
   - Some reps will be *really* good at getting AI to produce great work
   - The output looks good, sounds human, has context
   - But they barely did the work—they just prompted well
   - You need to know: Is this person great at their job, or are they great at prompting?
   - **How to tell:** Ask them to do the work without AI. Can they? If not, they're good at prompting, not selling.

6. **Where You Should Encourage AI Use**
   - Don't be the manager who says "no AI ever." That's incompetent.
   - Be the manager who says "use AI for the work that doesn't matter. Do the work that does."
   - Template generation? Go for it. Cold outreach? Smart. Follow-up sequences? Sure. Personalization? Your job. Context gathering? Your job.

**Tone:** Pragmatic, slightly irreverent. "You're going to have reps who try to AI their way out of work. Here's how you catch it."

**Real Scenario:**
- Rep sends you a forecast update. It's too polished, too structured, lacks personality you've seen before.
- You ask: "Why did you structure it this way?"
- Rep: "Oh, I used... I mean, I just... [stammers]"
- You: "Let me see what you actually sent to the system vs. what you wrote."
- Turns out they copy-pasted an AI-generated forecast without checking it. Numbers are wrong, context is missing.
- Conversation: "I need you to do the thinking. Use AI to draft. But you check, you verify, you personalize. Understood?"

**Worksheet Idea:** Present 5 sample emails/communications. Reps rate them: "Is this lazy AI, smart AI, or human work?" Then debrief.

### SUB-MODULE 4: WHERE HUMANS WIN
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,500 words
**Learning Objective:** Reframe this as an opportunity, not a threat. Managers and reps who understand where humans still dominate will thrive.

**Key Points to Hit:**

1. **The Synthesis Opportunity** — The Real Value-Add
   - AI will increasingly give you data, insights, forecasts, analysis
   - The manager who just reports that wins nothing
   - The manager who *synthesizes* ("Here's what the data says, here's what it means for our strategy, here's what we do") wins everything
   - **Example:** "Our close rate is 22%." (AI can tell you this.) "Our close rate is 22%, but for enterprise deals it's 35%, and for SMB it's 12%. That means our sales process is broken for SMB. We should either fix the process or stop pursuing them." (This is you.)

2. **Judgment Calls on Deal Health** — The Irreplaceable Skill
   - AI reads transcripts, flags risk factors, predicts likelihood
   - **But:** A prospect says "we want to close this month" and AI thinks "strong signal." You listen to the call and think "they want to, but they can't approve by then."
   - That judgment call is the job. That's what keeps you employed.
   - **Other judgment calls:**
     - Is this deal real or is the buyer just exploring?
     - Can they actually afford this?
     - Will their CFO approve?
     - Is the champion losing power?
     - Are we going to get access to the real decision-maker?

3. **Prompt Engineering as Skill** — The New Differentiator
   - In 5 years, some managers will be 3x better at using AI than others
   - Not because of tools, but because they ask better questions
   - They know how to iterate prompts, give context, refine output
   - This is a learnable skill that's going to matter at hiring time
   - **Prompt engineering examples:**
     - Bad prompt: "Write a cold email"
     - Good prompt: "Write a cold email to a VP of Sales at a 50-person SaaS company who's likely managing a chaotic pipeline. Make them laugh. Reference something that would make them think we understand their problem."

4. **Reading the Unsaid Context** — The Hardest Part
   - AI is predictive but not omniscient
   - What's *not* said in a call is often the most important
   - Rep doesn't mention budget = dodging budget conversation
   - Prospect says "let me talk to my team" without committing to timeline = they're not sold
   - Silence on a specific feature = they don't care about it
   - A human who's been in sales knows what silence means. AI just sees data.

5. **High-Value Communication Authority**
   - Cold emails? AI can do it better at scale.
   - Deal status updates to the C-suite? That's you. You have context AI doesn't.
   - Contract negotiation? You. You read intent, you know what you'll accept, you have relationship credibility.
   - Firing or hard coaching conversation? You. Full context, nuance, humanity.
   - Strategic recommendation to leadership? You. You synthesize data, you add judgment.

6. **Staying Current as a Skill** — The Meta-Win
   - Managers who stay students of AI (free models, new features, use cases) will be better
   - Not because they're tech nerds, but because they understand what's possible
   - When reps ask, "Can we use X?" a manager who's played with these tools knows the answer
   - A manager who's on the sidelines just says, "I don't know, check with IT"
   - Guess who gets respect? Guess who gets to make the call?

7. **The Hiring Opportunity**
   - When you hire, you can now ask: "What's your relationship with AI?"
   - Great candidates will say: "I'm interested in it, I've experimented with it, I think about how it changes what I do"
   - Red flags: "Eh, it's not my thing" or "I don't really use it" in 2026
   - You want people engaged with the biggest disruption in sales tech. If they're sitting on the sidelines, they're a risk.

**Tone:** Inspirational but grounded. "This is where you win. Not because you're fighting AI. But because you're better than AI at the stuff that matters."

**Real Scenario:**
- AI flags a deal as "high confidence close, 80% probability"
- You read the transcript. Prospect said Q3 repeatedly, but when asked "Can you get approval by August?" they said "we're going to try"
- "Try" is not approval. That's a yellow flag.
- You rank it 50% probability, push them for a real timeline
- Turns out you were right. Deal slips to Q4.
- That judgment call is why you're the manager and AI is the tool.

### SUB-MODULE 5: THE MANAGER'S ROLE GOING FORWARD
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,300 words
**Learning Objective:** Clarify what the job looks like NOW and going forward. Actionable framework for hiring, leading, staying relevant.

**Key Points to Hit:**

1. **What Your Job Becomes**
   - **Before:** Close deals, move pipeline, hit forecast
   - **Now:** Understand capabilities, make judgment calls, synthesize insights, guide your team on what work matters
   - **What stays:** People leadership, coaching, accountability, culture
   - **What changes:** Grunt work is gone (AI's domain). Decision-making and synthesis are more important.

2. **Hiring for the AI Future** — Specific Signals to Look For
   - **Green flags:**
     - "I'm interested in AI" (shows they're thinking about the future)
     - "I've experimented with ChatGPT/Claude" (shows they're learning)
     - "I'm thinking about how this changes my job" (shows strategic mindset)
     - "I'm following [AI researchers/thought leaders]" (shows they're staying current)
   - **Red flags:**
     - "I don't really use AI" (sitting on sidelines)
     - "It's overhyped" (dismissive, not engaged)
     - "I prefer to do things manually" (good luck competing)
     - "What's prompt engineering?" (okay to not know, but not interested = problem)

3. **Your Responsibility as a Manager** — This Is New
   - **Understand capabilities.** You don't need to be an AI expert, but you need to know what it can do.
   - **Set boundaries.** High-value work done by humans. Grunt work can be AI. You draw the line.
   - **Spot lazy work.** You're the quality control. You catch when reps are abdicating vs. multiplying.
   - **Teach prompt engineering.** If your reps are going to use AI, teach them to ask better questions.
   - **Stay current.** Use the tools yourself. Try new features. Be a student.
   - **Make recommendations to your company.** When you see opportunities for AI (workflow, tool, process), flag it. This is proactive leadership.

4. **Building Culture Around AI** — Critical
   - **Not:** "We use AI here" or "We don't use AI here"
   - **But:** "We use AI to be smarter and faster at the work that doesn't matter. We do the work that does."
   - Make it clear: Using AI well is expected. Using AI to check out is not okay.
   - Celebrate reps who use AI strategically. Call out abdication.

5. **Skill Development for Your Team**
   - Teach prompt engineering (ask better questions, iterate, refine)
   - Teach judgment (how to gut-check AI outputs against reality)
   - Teach synthesis (how to take data and turn it into strategy)
   - Model these skills yourself
   - Don't just say "use AI." Say "use AI like this."

6. **The "Both/And" Framework** — Critical for Communication
   - AI is powerful AND it has limits
   - We should use AI extensively AND we should protect high-value work for humans
   - Reps should be fast AND they should be thoughtful
   - We should leverage technology AND maintain judgment
   - This is not "AI good" or "AI bad." It's "AI in service of better work."

7. **What You're Competing Against Now**
   - Not just other managers
   - Managers who understand AI and build teams that use it strategically
   - If you're not staying current, you're losing to the manager who is
   - This is the same pressure that made CRM literacy table-stakes

8. **The Personal Responsibility Piece**
   - You can't outsource this to the company's training team
   - You need to go learn this yourself
   - Try ChatGPT. Try Claude. Try Perplexity. Use them.
   - Spend 2 hours a month playing with new features
   - This is table-stakes for your job now. Treat it that way.

**Tone:** Direct, slightly urgent, but not panic. "This is your world now. Here's how you lead in it."

**Real Andrew Quote to Embed:** Something like "I'm going to hire people who are interested in this stuff. If you're not interested, if you're not learning, you're making yourself obsolete. I don't mean to be harsh, but that's the reality."

**Action Steps Section:**
1. Download ChatGPT or Claude and use it for one task this week
2. Ask your reps: "How are you using AI in your job right now?"
3. Ask them: "How is it helping? Where are you struggling with it?"
4. Play with prompt engineering—rewrite a prompt 3 times and see how output changes
5. In your next hiring interview, ask: "Tell me about your relationship with AI"
6. Pick one workflow in your team and explore how AI could improve it
```
