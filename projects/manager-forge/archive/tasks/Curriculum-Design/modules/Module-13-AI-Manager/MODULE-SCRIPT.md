# THE AI MANAGER — Module Script
## Instructor/Video Scripts for Asynchronous Delivery

**Module:** The AI Manager (Capstone)  
**Target Audience:** First-time SaaS sales managers (0–12 months in role)  
**Delivery:** 5 sub-modules | ~12-15 min each | Asynchronous video + weekly Zoom discussion  
**Tone:** Direct, battle-tested, conversational, serious  
**Core Teaching Goal:** AI is reshaping your job. Understand it. Lead through it. Stay relevant.

---

## SUB-MODULE 1: THE JOB IS CHANGING
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,500 words  
**Learning Objective:** Establish stakes. Make clear this is not hype—it's the real inflection point. Create urgency without panic.

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Direct, no sugar-coating]**

So let's talk about AI. And I'm not going to sugarcoat this: if you don't understand what AI can do, you're going to lose your job. Not tomorrow. But eventually.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

I know that sounds dramatic. But it's true. And here's why: this is the same inflection point that happened with CRM literacy 10-15 years ago.

**[PACING NOTE: Slow down here — this is the stakes moment]**

Think back. There was a time when Salesforce existed, but not every manager used it. Some managers thought it was dumb. They said, "I don't need a CRM. I just need a spreadsheet and my brain."

Then something shifted. CRM became table-stakes. You couldn't get a job at a real company without knowing how to use Salesforce or HubSpot. And the managers who hadn't learned? They got left behind. Some got fired. Some got demoted. Some just stopped competing.

That's happening right now with AI. And it's bigger than the CRM shift was.

**[TRANSITION — Shift to teaching mode]**

Here's what I mean. AI isn't a nice-to-have. It's not a tool you *could* use if you want to be fancy. It's becoming the baseline. It's going to be in your company—probably already is. It's going to be in your reps' hands whether your company sanctions it or not.

The question you're facing right now isn't "Should we use AI?" The question is "How do I understand what AI can do so I can guide my team and my company?"

**[KEY INSIGHT — Deliver clearly, with emphasis]**

Your job is changing. It's not changing because AI is taking over. It's changing because the work that used to take manual effort now takes two seconds.

Before, your rep would spend 2 hours writing 50 cold emails. Now they can generate 50 variations in 5 minutes using AI. Before, you'd sit and manually forecast your team's numbers. Now an AI can look at your pipeline and tell you what's going to close.

So what happens to you? What's your value-add if a machine can do that faster and sometimes better?

**[PAUSE — Reflection moment]**

That's the question every manager should be asking themselves right now. And the answer is: your value-add moves from "doing the work" to "understanding what the work means and what to do about it."

**[TEACHING POINT — Direct, practical]**

Let me break this down. There are three scenarios playing out right now:

**Scenario 1: Your company embraces AI.** They invest in tools. They build workflows. They say, "Use these tools. Become more efficient."

Your job: Understand what these tools can do. Recommend workflows that make your team better. Teach your reps how to use them. Catch lazy work.

**Scenario 2: Your company is slow on AI.** They haven't invested. They're not sure about it. Your reps ask, "Can I use my own ChatGPT account?"

Your job: Understand capabilities well enough to say yes or no. Guide what's okay and what's not. Not shut them down—guide them.

**Scenario 3: Your company doesn't care one way or the other.** They leave it to you.

Your job: Make recommendations. Show leadership how AI can make your team faster and better. Own the strategy.

**[IMPORTANT CLARIFICATION]**

Here's what your job is NOT. It's not convincing your company to buy an AI tool. It's not becoming an AI expert. It's not building AI applications.

Your job is understanding the capabilities. That's it. You need to know what AI can do so you can make smart decisions about your team's workflow.

**[PERSONAL STORY — Vulnerable, real]**

When ChatGPT first came out, I'll be honest, I didn't get it. I thought it was a gimmick. Some people were using it to write emails and I was like, "Why? I can write emails."

But then I started playing with it. I asked it to write a cold email. I asked it to write the same cold email three different ways. I asked it to write one that was funny. One that was serious. One that referenced a specific problem.

And I realized: this is not about replacing cold email writing. It's about doing it 10x faster. It's about scale.

That's when it clicked for me. This isn't a threat. This is a tool. But you have to understand it to use it right.

**[STAKES MOMENT — Drive this home]**

So here's what's at stake. If you don't understand AI:

1. **You can't guide your team.** Your reps will be using tools you don't understand, and you'll have no framework for it.

2. **You can't recommend to leadership.** When your CEO asks "Should we invest in AI tools?" you'll have no real answer.

3. **You can't hire well.** In a year or two, when companies start asking "Tell me about your relationship with AI," you won't know what to look for.

4. **You can't stay competitive.** Managers who understand AI will build teams that outpace you. Faster, better work. You'll fall behind.

And eventually, yeah, you might lose the job. Not because AI replaced you. But because you didn't evolve with it.

**[CLOSING REFRAME]**

But here's the good news: This isn't hard to fix. You don't need to be an AI expert. You don't need to spend 40 hours learning this.

You need to:
- Try the tools yourself (spend 2 hours playing with ChatGPT)
- Understand what they can do (cold email, templates, forecasting, analysis)
- Know where humans still win (judgment, context, high-value work)
- Set boundaries with your team (this is okay to automate, this you need to do)

That's it. That's the job.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Before we move on, think about this: What would happen to your job if your company gave every rep an AI tool? What would you do differently? How would you lead? What's your value-add?

Write that down. That's where your head needs to be for the rest of this module.

**[END SUB-MODULE 1]**

## SUB-MODULE 2: UNDERSTANDING AI CAPABILITIES
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes | ~2,700 words  
**Learning Objective:** Demystify what AI can and can't do. Map where human judgment is irreplaceable. Build confidence, not anxiety.

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Practical, demystifying]**

Okay, so you're sold on the fact that this matters. You're ready to understand what AI can actually do.

Let me start by saying: AI is not a magic box. It's a tool with real strengths and real limitations. And your job as a manager is knowing the difference.

**[PACING NOTE: Speed up slightly — this is where we get practical]**

**AI's Strengths:**

First, let's talk about what AI is *really* good at.

**Pattern Recognition at Scale.** AI can read 100 emails, 200 call transcripts, and find themes across all of them. A human would take days. AI takes seconds. This is powerful.

**Template Generation.** AI can generate 10 versions of a cold email in 5 minutes. It can create structures, frameworks, outlines. Humans create slower but sometimes more creatively. AI creates fast and consistently.

**Data Synthesis.** AI can pull information from a bunch of sources and summarize it. "Here are the top 10 objections from our prospects." "Here's what happened in all our calls this month." That's useful.

**Forecast Modeling.** If you give AI your pipeline data and context, it can model outcomes. "Based on deal size, stage, activity level, and historical patterns, here's what we'll close." It's not perfect, but it's fast and decent.

**Speed and Consistency.** AI does the same thing the same way, every time. No bad days. No shortcuts. That's valuable for certain work.

**[TRANSITION — Shift to limitations]**

Now, the limitations. And these matter more than you think.

**AI's Limitations:**

**The Context Gap.** AI reads what's written. It doesn't know what's not written. A prospect says "we want to close this month." AI thinks "strong signal." But you listened to the call. You know their approval process takes 6 weeks. Their timeline is impossible. That's a yellow flag, not green.

AI doesn't know the context you know. Ever.

**The Hallucination Problem.** AI makes things up confidently. It'll tell you something that sounds absolutely true and is totally wrong. And it doesn't flag uncertainty well. It doesn't say "I'm 40% sure about this." It just tells you.

**The Lazy Proxy Problem.** AI can do a task, but if your rep relies on it entirely, they stop developing the skill. They never get good at cold outreach because they've never done it. That's dangerous. When AI breaks or changes, they're stranded.

**The Tone Problem.** AI often sounds generic or robotic. It uses certain phrases, certain structures. Real people write differently. Sometimes this matters. Sometimes it doesn't.

**The Unsaid Context Blindness.** This is huge. The most important thing in a sales conversation is often what's NOT said.

Example: Your rep said to a prospect, "Are you ready to move forward?" The prospect said "maybe." But they didn't commit to a timeline. They didn't ask about pricing. They didn't introduce you to their boss.

AI reads the transcript and thinks "positive response." You read it and think "cold feet."

Humans catch what silence means. What hesitation sounds like. What's missing from the conversation. AI just sees data.

**[KEY FRAMEWORK — Deliver clearly]**

So where do humans still win? Let me be really clear about this, because this is hope-giving:

**Humans win on judgment calls.** Specifically:

**Deal Quality Assessment.** "Is this deal real? Will they actually close? Can they afford it? Will their CFO approve?"

AI can flag risk factors. It can model probability. But the judgment call—"I read that call and I think they're going to close"—that's you. Only you.

Example: A prospect says "Q3 is our target." AI tags it as Q3. But you know this prospect. You've worked with their company before. You know they always say the target but slip 30 days. You rank this Q4. And you're right.

**Synthesis.** Taking data and turning it into strategy.

AI tells you: "Your close rate is 22%."
You say: "Our enterprise close rate is 35%. Our SMB close rate is 12%. That tells me our SMB process is broken. Here's what we do."

That synthesis is the value. The data matters. But what you do with it matters more.

**Prompt Engineering.** Some managers will be way better at asking AI the right questions.

Bad prompt: "Write a cold email"
Good prompt: "Write a cold email to a VP of Sales at a 50-person SaaS company who's drowning in pipeline chaos. Make them laugh. Reference something that shows we understand their problem."

The second one gets a 3x better output. Not because the tool is different. Because the question is better.

This is a learnable skill. And it's going to differentiate managers.

**Reading the Unsaid.** What hasn't been said yet?

Your rep told you about a call where the prospect seemed interested but didn't ask about implementation, didn't ask about support, didn't mention ROI.

You say, "They're not sold on the product. They're curious. But they're not actually ready to buy." That's human judgment.

**[REAL EXAMPLE]**

Here's a real example. I was forecasting with a rep. The deal was $100K, in contract review, should close this month.

The AI model said 85% probability. The rep said "it's a lock."

But I'd worked with this customer before. The champion—the person who championed our solution—just got promoted. I didn't know the new stakeholder. The CFO had a reputation for being conservative.

I said, "This is 40% probability. I think it slips."

The rep pushed back. "No, it's good. The process has been clean."

And yeah, it slipped. Three weeks later, the deal went to next month.

That judgment call—reading what hadn't been said, knowing the historical pattern, understanding the stakeholder change—that's the job. AI can't do that.

**[TEACHING POINT]**

So here's the framework you need:

**Ask yourself: Does this communication require context only I have?**

If yes, you do it. Your rep writes it. You review it. You make sure the context is there.

If no—if it's generic, if it's scale, if it's process—then AI can do it faster.

**Ask yourself: Am I trying to build a skill in someone, or are we trying to get work done?**

If you're building a skill, the rep needs to do the work. You coach them. You help. But they do it.

If you're just trying to get work done, let AI do it. Move on.

**Ask yourself: Is the output quality better, worse, or the same as the human version?**

If AI is better, use it.
If it's the same, use it (faster).
If it's worse, use the human version.

**Ask yourself: What happens if I delegate this to AI and my rep never develops this skill?**

If the answer is "nothing bad," then it's fine.
If the answer is "they get stranded when they need this skill," then the rep does it.

**[CLOSING]**

Here's what I want you to take away from this section:

AI is powerful. Use it where it wins. But protect high-value work for humans—work that requires judgment, context, and synthesis. Because those are the skills that make you valuable.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Think about your job this week. What did you do that AI could have done? What did you do that only you could do? The stuff only you can do—that's your real job.

**[END SUB-MODULE 2]**

## SUB-MODULE 3: SPOT THE LAZY WORK
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,300 words  
**Learning Objective:** Give managers concrete tells for detecting when reps are abdicating vs. using AI strategically. Build quality control muscle.

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Practical, slightly irreverent]**

Okay, here's the reality: your reps are going to try to use AI to shortcut the work. And if you don't know what to look for, they'll get away with it.

Your reps know more about these tools than you do. They've spent more time with them. And they'll use that to their advantage if you let them.

Your job is simple: spot lazy work.

**[PACING NOTE: Direct, no fluff]**

**The Tells:**

Here's what lazy work looks like:

**Robotic Language.** You've read 10 emails from your rep. They have personality. They're conversational. They use their own voice.

Then you see one that sounds different. It's too polished. It uses corporate jargon. "Synergize our solutions to drive mutual value." That's not your rep. That's AI.

**Em Dash Overuse.** AI loves em dashes. And exclamation points. It's a tell.

You start reading an email and you see em dashes everywhere. "We're excited—truly excited—to help you." That's AI.

**Perfect Structure.** Humans are messy. We ramble. We backtrack. We add detail.

AI outputs are structured perfectly. Bullet points that are all the same length. Lists that are symmetrical. Paragraphs that are exactly 3 sentences.

When you see something *too* perfect, that's a tell.

**Lack of Context.** Good sales communication references the specific conversation. "You mentioned you're struggling with your approval timeline. That's exactly what we solve."

Lazy AI work is generic. "We help companies improve their sales process." Could be for anyone.

**Speed Red Flag.** Your rep sent 50 emails in 2 hours. Either they used AI, or they copy-pasted garbage.

Real work takes time. Not always a lot of time. But some time. If the speed is unrealistic, the quality is probably bad.

**The Stammer.** You ask your rep, "Why did you structure this email this way?" And they go, "Oh, um, I... the AI generated that part."

If they have to think about it, they didn't write it.

**[TEACHING POINT — This is critical]**

Here's what you need to understand: using AI is fine. Using AI *instead of thinking* is the problem.

Your rep can draft a cold email in AI. That's smart. Then they review it. They personalize it. They add their voice. They send it.

That's good AI usage.

Your rep can draft a deal update in AI. That's fine. But then they need to verify the info. They need to make sure the context is right. They need to review it before sending it to leadership.

That's good usage.

Your rep can use AI to generate a forecast. That's actually smart—faster, fewer mistakes. But *they* still need to gut-check it. They need to think about whether the assumptions are right.

Bad usage is: rep generates a deal update in AI, reads it once, sends it verbatim to leadership without checking if it's accurate.

Bad usage is: rep generates a forecast in AI and sends it without thinking about whether the context is right.

Bad usage is: rep uses AI to write their 1:1 preparation notes instead of actually thinking about their week.

**[REAL SCENARIO]**

Here's what happened in one of my teams.

I got a forecast update from my rep. It was too polished. Perfect structure. Sounded generic.

I asked her, "Walk me through how you got to these numbers."

She stammered. Couldn't explain it. Finally admitted she'd used AI to generate the forecast and just sent it.

I said, "Okay, here's what we're doing. You're going to take that AI output, and you're going to verify it against what you actually know about each deal. Is the stage right? Is the close probability right? Are we missing deals? Are we overweighting deals?"

She did that work. And you know what? The AI forecast was 70% right and 30% wrong. The gaps were important.

Conversation with her after: "AI is a starting point. Your thinking is the finish line. You don't get to skip the thinking."

**[YOUR QUALITY CONTROL PROCESS]**

So what do you do? Here's your framework:

**Review high-value communications.** Deal updates, prospect emails, performance feedback, offers.

**Ask for the thought process.** "Walk me through why you said this." If they pause or stammer, they didn't write it.

**Check against past work.** Does this sound like them? Does it match their voice from other emails? Inconsistency is a tell.

**Spot-check for context.** Is this personalized? Does it reference the actual conversation or is it generic?

**Look for the unsaid.** Does this anticipate objections? Does it pick up on hesitation from the prospect? Or is it a generic response?

**The conversation you need to have:**

"I'm cool with you using AI for drafting, templates, and brainstorming. I'm not cool with you using AI as an excuse to not think. You use AI to go faster. Then you do the work of verification, personalization, and context-adding. Understood?"

**[CLOSING]**

Lazy work will destroy your team's quality. Reps will get comfortable cutting corners. Your numbers might look okay short-term, but your deals will be shaky. Your forecasts will be wrong. Your comms will be generic.

Your job is stopping that. Not by banning AI. By making clear: lazy usage isn't allowed.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

Have you seen lazy work in your team? What did you miss? What would you do differently now?

**[END SUB-MODULE 3]**

## SUB-MODULE 4: WHERE HUMANS WIN
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes | ~2,500 words  
**Learning Objective:** Reframe as opportunity. Humans still dominate in the areas that matter most. End on hope, not threat.

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Inspiring but grounded]**

Alright, I want to shift gears here. Because so far this might feel a little threatening. Like AI is coming to take your job.

But here's the truth: if your job is judgment, synthesis, and context, you're going to be fine. Better than fine. You're going to be more valuable than ever.

**[PAUSE — Let that land]**

Let me explain why.

**[TEACHING POINT — Deliver with confidence]**

AI is going to commoditize grunt work. Data entry, cold outreach, template generation, administrative stuff—that all becomes cheap and fast.

The managers and reps who *only* do grunt work? Yeah, they're going to struggle.

But managers and reps who do judgment, who synthesize, who read context and make calls? You're going to be more valuable than ever. Because that's what's left when grunt work is automated.

**[REAL EXAMPLE]**

Here's what I mean.

Before AI: A manager spends 4 hours pulling forecast data from the CRM, organizing it, making charts, adding notes. That's the job. If you're fast and organized, you win.

After AI: AI pulls the data, organizes it, makes the charts in 30 seconds. Now what?

The manager who just reports "Here's our forecast" has no value-add.

The manager who says "Here's the forecast, here's what it tells us, here's what we need to change, here's my recommendation"—*that* manager is indispensable.

The synthesis is where the value lives.

**[KEY FRAMEWORK — Four Areas Where Humans Win]**

**1. Deal Quality Assessment**

This is where you apply judgment.

AI can read a transcript. It can flag that a deal is in contract review. It can see that activity is high.

But can it tell if the deal is *real*?

"This prospect says they want to close in Q3. But their approval process takes 6 weeks. Are they going to close in Q3?"

That's a judgment call. That requires context. That requires you having worked with similar companies, knowing their approval patterns, reading the conversation.

AI gives you data. You give it meaning.

Other judgment calls:
- Can they actually afford this?
- Is the champion losing power?
- Are we going to get access to the real decision-maker?
- Is the product actually a fit for them?

These are judgment calls. And they're more important than the data.

**2. Synthesis**

Taking multiple data points and turning them into strategy.

AI tells you: "Your close rate is 22%."

You look deeper and say: "Enterprise is 35%. SMB is 12%. Our SMB process is broken. We should either fix it or stop pursuing that segment."

That synthesis—connecting the dots, seeing the pattern, making a recommendation—that's the value.

Or: "Three of our last five lost deals mentioned timing. But when I dug in, it wasn't really timing. It was budget. So our messaging about budget is weak. Here's what we fix."

That synthesis comes from you thinking, not from AI analyzing.

**3. Prompt Engineering**

Here's a trend I'm predicting: some managers will be *way* better at using AI than others.

Not because they use different tools. Because they ask better questions.

Bad prompt: "Write a cold email"

Okay output.

Good prompt: "Write a cold email to a VP Sales at a 50-person SaaS company who's getting crushed by pipeline chaos. Make them laugh. Reference something that shows we understand their nightmare."

Great output.

Even better prompt: "Write three versions—one professional, one funny, one empathetic. Each should reference [specific problem we solve]. Use their voice, not corporate jargon. Make me want to respond."

Excellent output.

The difference is the question, not the tool.

Managers who learn to prompt well will get 3x better output than managers who just use the defaults.

This is a learnable skill. And it's going to differentiate managers in hiring.

**4. Reading What's Unsaid**

The most important context in a sales conversation is often what's *not* said.

Prospect said "we're interested." But they didn't ask about timeline. They didn't ask about pricing. They didn't ask about implementation.

AI reads the transcript and thinks "positive signal."

You listen and think, "They're curious but not sold."

Your rep left a voicemail for a prospect. Three days later, the prospect called back. But they called your rep's cell, not the main line.

AI notes "prospect called back."

You think, "Prospect was intentionally avoiding our office, wanted direct contact. Might have a sensitivity around this decision."

These are reads that come from experience and intuition. AI can't do this.

**[TEACHING POINT]**

Here's what I want to be clear about: you're not losing your job to AI. You're losing your job if you *don't* understand AI.

Because here's what happens:

Manager A doesn't understand AI. Reps are using it, he doesn't know what they're doing. He can't guide them. He can't spot lazy work. He's not using it himself to work smarter. He's slow, reactive, flying blind.

Manager B understands AI. She uses it to work faster. She guides her reps on smart usage. She spots lazy work. She focuses on judgment and synthesis—the stuff that actually matters. She's fast, proactive, intentional.

Manager B wins. Every time.

**[REAL SCENARIO]**

Here's an example that stuck with me.

I had two reps. Both capable. Both hitting quota.

Rep A used AI for everything she could. Drafts, templates, brainstorming. But she always did the thinking. She reviewed the output. She personalized. She made the call.

Rep B was skeptical of AI. Used it sparingly. Did most things manually.

Rep A was finishing her work by Thursday. Rep B was working until Friday.

Rep A's work quality? Just as good, sometimes better.

Why? Because Rep A was using a tool to go faster. She wasn't using it to check out.

Rep B was being stubborn, refusing a legitimate productivity tool.

Who do you think I promoted? Who do you think developed faster?

**[CLOSING — This is important]**

If you understand what AI is good at (grunt work, speed, consistency), you'll delegate it.

If you understand what AI is bad at (judgment, synthesis, context), you'll protect that work for humans.

And if you're the manager guiding that balance, you're valuable.

More valuable than you've ever been.

**[FINAL REFLECTION PROMPT]**

What's the judgment call in your job that only you can make? What context do you have that AI doesn't? Write that down. That's your real job. Protect it. Double down on it.

**[END SUB-MODULE 4]**

## SUB-MODULE 5: THE MANAGER'S ROLE GOING FORWARD
**Runtime:** ~12 minutes | ~2,300 words  
**Learning Objective:** Clarify what the job looks like now and going forward. Actionable framework for hiring, leading, and staying relevant.

---

### SCRIPT

**[OPEN — Tone: Direct, action-oriented]**

Okay, so here's where we are:

AI is reshaping the job. Grunt work is disappearing. Judgment and synthesis are becoming more valuable. Your job is changing.

So what do you actually *do* about this?

**[PACING NOTE: Practical, specific]**

**Your Responsibilities as a Manager**

You have four new responsibilities:

**1. Understand Capabilities**

You don't need to be an AI expert. But you need to know what it can do.

Spend 2 hours. Try ChatGPT. Ask it to write a cold email. Ask it to summarize a call transcript. Ask it to generate a forecast. Get a feel for what's possible.

That's it. Now you have a baseline.

**2. Set Boundaries**

High-value work is done by humans. Low-value work can be AI.

High-value:
- Deal negotiations
- Customer relationship building
- Strategic recommendations
- Performance coaching
- Complex problem-solving

Low-value:
- Cold outreach
- Template generation
- Administrative work
- Data categorization
- Follow-up sequences

You draw the line. You make that clear to your team.

**3. Spot Lazy Work**

You've already got the tools for this. Robotic language. Too-perfect structure. Generic context. Missing personalization.

You spot it. You call it out. Not harshly. But clearly.

"I see AI output here. That's fine for drafting. But you need to personalize this, verify this, think about this. Understood?"

**4. Stay Current**

This is the one most managers skip. Don't be that manager.

Spend 1-2 hours a month learning. Try new features. Read about what's changing. Experiment.

This isn't a hobby. This is your job now. You're a student of this field because it's your field.

**[TEACHING POINT]**

Here's where you should focus:

**Understand what exists now.** ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, other models. What can each one do?

**Understand what's coming.** New tools, new capabilities. Stay aware.

**Experiment yourself.** Don't just read about it. Try it.

**Teach your team.** If your reps are going to use this, teach them the right way.

**Recommend to leadership.** When you see opportunities, flag it. That's proactive management.

**[HIRING — This Is Critical]**

Your hiring approach is changing. You need new signals.

**Green Flags:**
- "I'm interested in AI" (shows they're thinking about the future)
- "I've experimented with ChatGPT" (shows they're learning)
- "I'm thinking about how this changes my job" (shows strategic mindset)
- "I follow [AI researchers]" (shows they're staying current)

**Red Flags:**
- "I don't really use AI" (sitting on sidelines)
- "It's overhyped" (dismissive, not engaged)
- "I prefer to do things manually" (going to be slow, stubborn)
- "What's prompt engineering?" (okay not to know, but not interested is a problem)

When you hire someone, you're hiring for 3-5 years. You need people who are going to evolve with the job, not resist it.

**[BUILDING CULTURE AROUND AI]**

Don't say: "We use AI here" or "We don't use AI here"

Say: "We use AI to be smarter and faster at work that doesn't matter. We do the work that does matter ourselves."

Make it clear:
- Using AI well is expected
- Using AI to shortcut thinking is not okay
- We celebrate reps who use AI strategically
- We call out lazy usage

**[STAYING RELEVANT]**

Here's the hard truth: if you're not staying current with this, you're making yourself obsolete.

Not because you'll get fired next month. But in 3-5 years, when AI literacy is table-stakes, you'll be behind.

What I'd do:

- Every Monday morning, spend 30 minutes trying something new with an AI tool
- Once a month, read something about AI trends in sales
- When a rep asks about an AI tool, learn about it. Don't just say yes or no.
- In your next interview, ask about AI. See how candidates respond.

That's it. 2-3 hours a month. That's table-stakes now.

**[REAL ANDREW QUOTE]**

I'm going to be real with you. I'm hiring people who are interested in AI. I'm hiring people who are experimenting with it, thinking about what it means, engaged in the conversation.

If you're not interested? If you're sitting on the sidelines?

I'm going to hire someone else. Because you're already behind.

This is the biggest disruption in sales technology we've ever seen. If you're not paying attention to it, you're missing the game.

**[TEACHING POINT — The Both/And]**

Here's the framework that separates good managers from great ones:

AI is powerful **AND** it has limits.

We should use AI extensively **AND** we should protect high-value work for humans.

Reps should be fast **AND** they should be thoughtful.

We should leverage technology **AND** maintain judgment.

This both/and is hard. It's easier to say "use AI for everything" or "never use AI." But the both/and is where real management lives.

**[SKILL DEVELOPMENT]**

Here's what you teach your team:

**Prompt Engineering:**
- How to ask better questions
- How to iterate and refine
- How to provide context

**Judgment:**
