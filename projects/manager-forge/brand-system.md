# Manager Forge — Brand System
*Designed: 2026-04-21*

---

## Positioning

### Options

**Option A: "The operating system for the AI-enabled sales manager"**
The working draft. Accurate, structured, positions AI as the operating context rather than a feature. The "OS" metaphor is smart — it says "runs underneath everything, not a bolt-on" — and it lands well for a technical-adjacent audience. The weakness: it's a little abstract and leans on "AI-enabled" which is already becoming noise. Does the phrase tell someone who isn't already in the frame what they're buying? Marginally.

**Option B (RECOMMENDED): "Six weeks to manage like the job actually is now"**
Direct. Implies the current state (the job has changed) without saying "AI-enabled" twice in the same sentence. The promise is tangible — six weeks, real shift. The word "actually" does the most work: it acknowledges the gap between what most managers are still doing and what the role has become. It also invites self-identification: if you've been wondering whether you're behind, this sentence answers yes without being confrontational about it. Shorter, more spoken, sounds like something Andrew would actually say.

**Option C: "The management operating system built for teams that use AI"**
More specific — shifts focus to the team rather than just the manager, which is accurate (the whole program is about managing a team navigating AI). "Built for" is more confident than "AI-enabled." But it's wordier and "built for teams that use AI" is closer to how a vendor would describe a software product than how Andrew would describe what he teaches. The "OS" metaphor is still doing good work here.

### Recommended Positioning Line

**"Six weeks to run your team like it's 2026."**

Reasoning: Punchy, works spoken and written, and the date does all the targeting work — any manager running a 2022 playbook on a 2026 team immediately self-identifies. Doesn't lean on "AI-enabled" (already noise). Slightly cheeky, which matches Andrew's voice. The OS metaphor works well in supporting copy as the second frame.

## Tagline / One-liner

### Options

**Option A: "Your team is already using AI. Now you need to know how to manage it."**
Strong clarity. Creates the before/after in one sentence. The weakness: it's a statement, not a hook. Works well in email body copy or landing page subtext, but doesn't have the punch needed for a LinkedIn bio or header. Also mildly accusatory — some managers will bristle.

**Option B (RECOMMENDED): "Built for managers who know the job changed — and are finally doing something about it."**
This is identity language, not product language. It tells the right person exactly who it's for and creates the self-selection "that's me" moment. "Finally" is earned here — this audience has been sitting with the tension. It's not insulting; it's honest. Works in a bio, works as a landing page header, works in an email subject line. It also implies scarcity and timing without being fake-urgent. The only risk: it's a bit long for a pure one-liner. Worth it.

**Option C: "Six sessions. One operating system. The manager your team needs right now."**
Punchy, structured, confident. Lands more like a pitch deck bullet than a conversation opener — which could be fine for certain uses. But "the manager your team needs right now" edges toward aspirational-generic, which is exactly what Andrew's voice avoids. Use as supporting copy, not the lead.

### Recommended

**"Built for managers who know the job changed — and are finally doing something about it."**

Why: It's self-selecting without being exclusive. The manager who reads this and thinks "yes, that's me" is the right buyer. The manager who reads it and thinks "wait, is the job changing?" is not. This does the targeting work so Andrew doesn't have to in every conversation. And it sounds like something Andrew would post on a Tuesday morning — not something a copywriter polished into blandness.

Secondary one-liner for contexts where shorter is needed: **"The job changed. Now your management does too."** — crisp, parallel structure, same message in nine words.

## Voice Guide

Read the BOL episodes before writing anything for Manager Forge. The voice there is the baseline: direct, point arrives late and lands hard, no hedging, honest about what Andrew doesn't know, treats the audience as peers who are also figuring things out. Manager Forge inherits all of that, with the additional constraint that the audience is spending $2,500 — which means they need to feel informed and respected, not sold to.

### 1. Write like you're talking to a peer, not a prospect.

**Do:** "If your pipeline review is still a status update, you're doing AI's job." Address the gap directly. Assume competence — this person knows what a pipeline review is.

**Don't:** "In today's rapidly evolving sales landscape, managers face unprecedented challenges in leveraging AI-driven insights to optimize team performance." Corporate gibberish that assumes the reader needs convincing about things they already know. Also: never "unprecedented."

### 2. Name the problem before you name the solution.

**Do:** Lead with the tension. "Most managers know their job changed. Very few have changed how they manage." The problem is the hook; the program is the answer. Let the problem breathe before reaching for the solution.

**Don't:** Open with features, benefits, or the program description. "Manager Forge is a 6-week cohort-based program that..." No. Start with the situation the person is actually in.

### 3. Handle AI with precision, not enthusiasm.

**Do:** Be specific about what AI does and doesn't do. "AI does the first pass on your pipeline review. You do the synthesis. The meeting focuses on the three deals that need human judgment." Concrete. Operational. Not hype.

**Don't:** Use "AI-powered," "cutting-edge," "next-gen," "unlock the power of," or any phrase that sounds like a vendor pitch. Also: don't romanticize AI. Andrew doesn't. He uses it because it works, and he's honest about where it falls short. That skepticism is a credibility asset — protect it.

**Don't:** Apologize for AI either. "Even if you're not a tech person..." is condescending. Assume this audience is at least AI-curious and probably AI-annoyed (by the hype).

### 4. Reference Andrew's credibility through evidence, not title.

**Do:** Let the specifics do the work. "Andrew built the pipeline review system his team uses. He ran the experiment. He knows where the AI forecast is wrong and why." Specific claims from inside the work.

**Don't:** "Andrew is a VP of Sales with 17 years of experience and a proven track record in..." Nobody believes a self-written bio. The credibility lives in the details: he built AutoLinkedIn, he runs ScrollProxy, he manages 10 reps with these systems in production. That's the thing to say.

**Don't:** Lean on the VP title. It's a credential, not a differentiator. The differentiator is that he builds the AI systems himself and teaches from inside the problem, not from a stage.

### 5. Keep sentences short. Let paragraphs breathe.

**Do:** One idea per sentence. Use periods where another writer would use commas. Fragments are fine if they land. "Six sessions. One concrete artifact per session. The operating system your team needs."

**Don't:** String three qualifying clauses together. If a sentence has more than one "which" or "that," it needs surgery. Andrew's voice breaks up. It comes back. It doesn't meander through a thought.

### 6. Say what the program is, not what it wants to be.

**Do:** "You'll leave each session with one concrete thing to do Monday morning. We've built it that way on purpose." Honest and specific.

**Don't:** "World-class content," "transformative experience," "industry-leading frameworks," "best-in-class curriculum." These phrases are indistinguishable from every other program on the market. The truth about Manager Forge is more compelling: real tools, real practitioner, real peer cohort, one artifact per session. Lead with what's true.

### 7. False urgency is the fastest way to lose this audience.

**Do:** "Cohort 1 is 10-15 seats. When it's done, it's done." That's real. Say it once, don't repeat it as a tactic.

**Don't:** "Doors close in 48 hours!" or "Only 3 spots left!" unless it's literally true in that moment. Sales managers sell for a living. They recognize a pressure play immediately — and they will not buy from someone who uses one on them.

## Template System Architecture

### Session Materials

**Template: Session Framework One-Pager**
- Where used: Displayed during live sessions as the primary visual (not a slide deck)
- Key elements: Session thesis (one sentence), the before/after shift, the 4-part arc (Opening / Teaching / Workshop / Close) with time allocations, the session's takeaway artifact named
- Tone/format notes: Clean, minimal. This is a working document the manager looks at during the session, not a designed graphic. Black on white or dark text on a muted background. No icons. No stock photos.

**Template: Takeaway Worksheet (Fillable PDF)**
- Where used: Posted in cohort community within 1 hour of session end; completed during the live session workshop portion
- Key elements: Session number + title, the core framework the worksheet applies, 2-4 structured exercises (fill-in, score, or draft), space for "one thing I'm doing differently this week," and a callback field to the prior week's artifact (to reinforce the through-line)
- Tone/format notes: Formatted for actual use, not decoration. Wide margins, readable type, fields that feel answerable (not open-ended essays). The artifact should be able to live on a manager's desk for a week without looking like homework.

**Template: Foundations Library Module Brief**
- Where used: Delivered Monday before each live session as async pre-work
- Key elements: Module name and number, which frameworks are covered, which 1-2 worksheets to complete before the live session, what to bring (e.g., "your real pipeline data"), preview of how this connects to the live session (one or two sentences, not a sales pitch)
- Tone/format notes: Conversational, short. Three to four paragraphs max. This is a warm-up, not a standalone course document.

**Template: Facilitator Notes (Andrew's Internal)**
- Where used: Andrew's reference doc for each session
- Key elements: Session arc with timestamps, exact language for opening frames (written out, not bullet points), live workshop instructions verbatim, role-play scenario scripts, transition language between sections, "watch for" signals (what it looks like when the group is stuck vs. engaged)
- Tone/format notes: Andrew's voice exactly. These aren't polished for external use — they're a script he can deviate from with confidence.

### Participant Communications

**Template: Welcome Email (Pre-Cohort)**
- Where used: Sent within 24 hours of enrollment confirmation
- Key elements: What to expect (honest, specific — not a hype list), how to access the platform and Foundations Library, the pre-Session 1 pre-work assignment, the live session link and time, one sentence about community norms ("this is a working cohort, not a support forum")
- Tone/format notes: Short. Andrew's voice. Reads like it was written personally, not auto-generated. No graphics, no hero image. Plain text or near-plain.

**Template: Pre-Session Reminder**
- Where used: Sent the Monday before each live session (may be bundled with the Foundations Brief)
- Key elements: This week's session topic in one sentence, the specific pre-work assignment (which module section, which worksheets), what to bring to the session (data, a real situation, a specific rep), the live session link and time
- Tone/format notes: Three to five sentences. Actionable. Not motivational.

**Template: Post-Session Follow-Up**
- Where used: Sent within 4 hours of session end
- Key elements: Link to session recording, link to the fillable takeaway worksheet PDF, the community thread prompt for the week's debrief (same as what Andrew posts in the community), one sentence previewing next week
- Tone/format notes: Short, clean. Functional. Not a recap of what just happened — the recording is the recap.

**Template: 30-Day Check-In Email**
- Where used: Sent 30 days after Session 6
- Key elements: Reference to the 30/90-Day AI Leadership Roadmap artifact from Week 6, the accountability call format and schedule link, one question: "What's one thing that changed?" — primes the accountability call without creating anxiety about it
- Tone/format notes: Warm but not soft. Andrew writes this personally, in his own voice. It should feel like a message from a peer, not a CRM drip.

### Community Posts

**Template: Weekly Debrief Thread**
- Where used: Posted in the cohort community by Andrew within 24 hours of the live session (Thursday or Friday)
- Key elements: One sentence naming the session's core idea. One question with two options ("one thing you're doing differently this week — reply with yours"). No instructions to "share your takeaways" — specific prompt only.
- Tone/format notes: Short. Conversational. Should look like Andrew posted it from his phone. Participation is expected — the structure creates social accountability. Not a formal assignment.

**Template: Office Hours Invite**
- Where used: Posted in community to fill the Week 3 midpoint check-in slots
- Key elements: The format (one situation, five minutes, group input), how many slots, how to claim a slot, one sentence managing expectations ("this isn't Q&A — bring a real situation")
- Tone/format notes: Direct. Anticlimactic on purpose — if it sounds optional, it becomes optional. "Bring a real situation. These are the conversations that don't happen in the full group."

**Template: Show Your Work Post (Prompt)**
- Where used: In the "Show Your Work" community space — Andrew posts an example or prompt to seed participation
- Key elements: Andrew's own completed version of the week's artifact (or a redacted example), an explicit prompt to share theirs
- Tone/format notes: Vulnerable, not polished. The value is in seeing real work, not perfect work. If Andrew posts his own, it should be the actual one, not an idealized version.

**Template: Milestone Celebration Post**
- Where used: End of Week 6, and whenever a cohort member shares a specific outcome
- Key elements: What happened (specific), why it matters (one sentence), the cohort member's name if they've shared publicly
- Tone/format notes: No cheerleading language. "Here's what [name] built after Week 2. The format they designed for their pipeline review. They ran it last Monday." Factual. The result speaks.

### Sales and Marketing Materials

**Template: Landing Page — Hero Section**
- Where used: Top of the Manager Forge landing page
- Key elements: Headline (positioning line or tagline), subheadline (one sentence on who this is for and what they get), program summary (three to four bullet points — dates, format, what's included), single CTA
- Tone/format notes: No images in the hero except possibly Andrew's photo. No carousel, no animation. The copy does the work. The hero should answer "is this for me?" in 10 seconds.

**Template: Landing Page — Credibility Section**
- Where used: Below the hero, before the program details
- Key elements: Andrew's actual role (VP of Sales), the specific AI systems he's built (named), the team size he manages, one or two specific outcomes or examples from his own practice. Not a bio — a proof point.
- Tone/format notes: No headshots with arms crossed. No "thought leader" language. Specific claims only. "He built the pipeline review system his team uses. He ran the AI fluency hiring screen before teaching it. He knows what over-reliance looks like because he managed through it."

**Template: Outreach Email (Cold or Warm)**
- Where used: Direct outreach to target managers — LinkedIn connection follow-up or cold email
- Key elements: One sentence on who Andrew is (specific, not title-first), one sentence on who the program is for, one specific reason it might be relevant to this person, the ask (a 15-minute call, not a buy now)
- Tone/format notes: Four to five sentences, max. Does not mention price. Does not list all six sessions. One reason this person specifically should care. Ask for a conversation, not a commitment.

**Template: LinkedIn Content — Program-Adjacent Episodes**
- Where used: BOL episodes 4-6 weeks before enrollment opens — sales management + AI content that builds the context without pitching
- Key elements: Real problem or framework from the program (without naming it as Manager Forge content), end with a question that resonates with the target audience, one mention of Manager Forge enrollment once it opens
- Tone/format notes: Exactly BOL voice. No difference. The episode doesn't know it's a marketing vehicle — it's just useful. If it sounds like a launch campaign, it's wrong.

## Visual Identity Direction

### Color Palette

**Primary — Slate (#1E2A3A)**
Near-black with blue undertone. Used for text, headings, worksheet backgrounds. Reads as serious and technical without being cold. Not startup blue, not corporate navy. Something a manager would trust.

**Secondary — Amber (#D4850A)**
Warm, grounded, not alarming. This is the accent color — used for call-to-action elements, highlights, the "Forge" mark if there's a wordmark. Amber reads as heat, craft, and precision: things that get forged. Avoids the tech-startup blue/green that saturates this space. Not orange (too aggressive), not gold (too premium/precious). Amber.

**Neutral — Warm Stone (#F4F0E8)**
Off-white with a very slight warm cast. Background for worksheets, slide decks, and community assets. Avoids the sterile feel of pure white. Makes content feel approachable without being casual.

**Supporting — Mid-Slate (#4A5568)**
For secondary text, labels, and supporting copy. Keeps hierarchy clear without introducing a fourth distinct color.

### Typography Direction

**Primary — Clean geometric sans-serif** (Inter, DM Sans, or equivalent)
Used for everything: headers, body, worksheets, slides. One typeface family throughout. Geometric sans-serifs read as modern, structured, and legible at any size. No serifs — this isn't a business book or a consulting firm. No display typefaces — this isn't a consumer brand.

**Mono — Fixed-width sans** (JetBrains Mono or equivalent) — used sparingly
Only for code examples, AI prompt examples, or tool demonstrations. This signals "actual technical work" when used with restraint.

No more than two typeface families in any one document. Weight variation (regular, medium, semibold) does the hierarchy work — not typeface switching.

### Visual Personality

**Precise.** Every element is there for a reason. No decorative elements, no pattern fills, no gradient mesh backgrounds. White space is not a failure to design — it's the design.

**Operational.** The visual language should feel like something built to be used, not admired. Worksheets that look like worksheets. Decks that look like working documents. Not polished into the uncanny valley of corporate design.

**Grounded.** Not startup-bro aesthetic (dark mode, neon accents, crypto energy). Not consulting-firm aesthetic (navy/gold, heavy serif, suits). Somewhere in between: the working practitioner who takes the craft seriously.

**Direct.** No stock photography. No abstract geometric shapes as visual filler. If there's an image, it should be functional — a diagram, a framework visual, or Andrew himself.

**Avoid:** Gradients, drop shadows on text, busy slide backgrounds, stock imagery of people pointing at screens, motivational typography, and anything that would look at home in a generic "Leadership Training" course catalog.

### Formatting Principles for Session Materials

**Slides (Framework Visuals):**
No traditional slide decks. Instead, each session has one to three single-page framework visuals — the kind you'd sketch on a whiteboard and then clean up. These are displayed as references while Andrew talks, not advanced through bullet by bullet. Each visual fits on one page and stands alone without Andrew's narration. If it needs him to explain every element, it isn't finished.

**Worksheets:**
Formatted for print and digital use equally. 8.5x11, portrait. Large answer fields — managers should be writing, not squeezing into a tiny text box. Two columns max for exercises with multiple parts. Generous white space. The Manager Forge mark in the corner, the session number and title in the header, the artifact name prominent. Nothing decorative. Everything readable.

**Community Posts and Email:**
No HTML templates with headers and branded banners. Plain text or near-plain. Andrew's name, not the program's. The email should look like it was written by a person because it was.

## Naming Conventions

### Sessions

**Format: "Week [N]: [The Shift in Plain Language]"**

The session title names the shift the manager is making — not the topic, not the framework, not the tool. It should be a complete thought that Andrew could open a live session with and the manager would understand immediately.

Examples (already set from program architecture):
- Week 1: Your Job Changed. Here's the New Job Description.
- Week 2: Pipeline Reviews That Don't Waste Everyone's Time
- Week 3: Coaching the Rep in the Age of AI Reps
- Week 4: The 1:1 and Team Meeting That Actually Works Now
- Week 5: Hiring for the Team That Doesn't Exist Yet
- Week 6: Building a Team That Doesn't Fear the Machine

**Rule:** No subtitles. No "Leveraging AI for..." No colon followed by a framework name. The title is the frame.

### Worksheets and Assets

**Format: [TYPE]-[WK]-[##]**

Types:
- `WS` — Worksheet (participant-facing, live or pre-work)
- `SA` — Self-Assessment
- `RP` — Role-Play scenario or script
- `FW` — Framework visual (one-pager)
- `BRIEF` — Foundations Library module brief (pre-work delivery)
- `SCRIPT` — Conversation scripts

Week codes:
- `W1` through `W6` — AI Spine sessions
- `FL` — Foundations Library (not tied to a specific live session)

Sequential number within type and week: `01`, `02`, etc.

**Examples:**
- `WS-W1-01` — Worksheet, Week 1, first worksheet ("My Irreplaceable Value-Add")
- `FW-W2-01` — Framework visual, Week 2, first one-pager ("AI-Augmented Pipeline Review")
- `RP-W3-01` — Role-play, Week 3, first scenario ("Your Rep Is Over-Relying on AI")
- `BRIEF-FL-07` — Foundations Library brief for Module 07 (Pipeline Review)
- `SA-W5-01` — Self-assessment, Week 5, first assessment ("Hiring Structure Audit")

**Display names** for participant-facing materials use the full human title alongside the code: `WS-W1-01: My Irreplaceable Value-Add`. The code is for the facilitator's file system. The title is what the manager sees.

**Existing archive assets** keep their original naming scheme (WS-13-03, RP-07-01, etc.) when referenced in facilitator notes and build documents. When they appear in participant-facing materials, they are relabeled to the cohort naming convention.

### Community Spaces (Circle)

| Space | Name | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| By week | `Week 1 — New Job Description` through `Week 6 — Culture` | Session-specific discussion, questions, debrief thread |
| Artifacts | `Show Your Work` | Post completed takeaway worksheets |
| Questions | `For Andrew` | Between-session questions, answered before each live session |
| Connection | `Introductions` | First week only — one post per member, stays as reference |
| Accountability | `30-Day Check-In` | Post-cohort only — unlocks after Week 6 |

**Naming rule:** Spaces use plain language, not branded program terminology. "Week 1 — New Job Description" not "AI Spine Module 1 Discussion." The week number plus the plain-English topic is enough.

### Email Sequences

**Format: [SEQUENCE]-[##]-[trigger]**

Sequences:
- `ENROLL` — Enrollment confirmation through pre-cohort setup
- `WEEKLY` — Weekly pre-session and post-session delivery emails
- `OFFBOARD` — Post-cohort accountability and follow-up
- `PRESELL` — Pre-enrollment outreach and nurture (for next cohort)

Examples:
- `ENROLL-01-immediate` — Welcome email, sent on enrollment confirmation
- `ENROLL-02-d-7` — Platform setup confirmation, sent 7 days before Session 1
- `WEEKLY-W1-pre` — Pre-session brief, sent the Monday before Week 1
- `WEEKLY-W1-post` — Post-session follow-up, sent within 4 hours of Week 1 session
- `OFFBOARD-01-d+7` — First post-cohort email, sent 7 days after Session 6
- `OFFBOARD-02-d+30` — 30-day check-in, sent 30 days after Session 6

### Cohort Iterations

**Cohort 1 naming:**

External (what participants and prospects see): **Founding Cohort**

Reasoning: "Founding Cohort" signals exclusivity and early-adopter status without being a transparency-hiding euphemism like "Cohort Alpha" or "Beta." It's honest — these are the managers who shape what the program becomes — and it carries a different connotation than "Cohort 1" (which sounds like the first attempt at something that might fail). Founding members get something: lower price, direct access to Andrew, their feedback visibly shapes the next version. The naming should reflect that.

Internal (file system, build tracking, planning): `C1`

**Subsequent cohorts:**

Externally: **Cohort 2**, **Cohort 3**, etc. — no need to maintain "founding" language past the first. By Cohort 2, the program has alumni case studies and the positioning shifts from "be a founding member" to "here's what Cohort 1 built."

Internal: `C2`, `C3`, etc.

**File system convention for cohort materials:**
`/curriculum/c1/week-1/` — all Cohort 1 build files by week
`/curriculum/shared/` — frameworks and assets used across cohorts
`/presell/c1/` — enrollment materials specific to Cohort 1
