# Module 02 — AI Layer
## What Changes About Managing Up When AI Is in the Room

*The core frameworks — Translator Role, Inform/Advocate/Execute, Trust Capital, the Forecasting Paradox — still hold. The content of the conversations is new. This document names what's new, what the new failure modes look like, and how the module's material adapts.*

---

## The Manager's Job in the AI Era

**Before:** Managing up meant translating between your reps and your leadership. Two worlds, different needs, you in the middle.

**Now:** You're translating between three worlds — your reps, your leadership, AND the AI tools that are already in the room. The AI tools are generating forecasts, flagging deal risk, summarizing calls, drafting outreach. Your leadership may trust those outputs more than they should. Your reps may be using tools your leadership hasn't approved. And you're the only person who sees all three layers at once.

Your translator job now includes:

- **Validating what AI generates before passing it up.** The AI forecast isn't your forecast until you've checked it. If you pass up unchecked AI outputs, the first time it's wrong, your credibility takes the hit — not the tool's.
- **Surfacing the AI reality on your team, even when it's uncomfortable.** Your reps are using AI. Some of it is sanctioned. Some isn't. Staying silent is a trust breaker now, not a neutral move.
- **Championing tools you don't fully trust yet.** Leadership is rolling out AI tools faster than you can evaluate them. You'll be asked to advocate before you've formed a real opinion. The old Inform/Advocate/Execute framework is the exact tool for this — but you have to use it honestly.
- **Holding a consistent line on judgment vs. tool output.** Becoming the "AI skeptic" in every conversation drains credibility. So does pretending to love every tool leadership picks. You need a coherent, defensible position: AI accelerates the work, it doesn't replace judgment, and here's how you know when each applies.

## New Failure Modes

These are the specific ways managers are losing trust capital in the AI era that didn't exist five years ago:

### 1. Passing up unchecked AI summaries

Your manager asks "how's the pipeline?" You forward the Gong summary. Two days later the VP cites a stat from your email and it's wrong because the AI misread a deal stage. You didn't catch it. Your credibility takes the hit — not Gong's.

**The old framework version:** Don't surprise your manager. Validate the data before you share it.

**The AI-era version:** The AI output is not your output until you've validated it. Same rule. Bigger exposure.

### 2. Becoming the AI skeptic

You push back on every AI initiative. You're right about some of them. But over time your manager stops bringing AI conversations to you because they know what you'll say. You're no longer at the table when the decisions get made.

**The old framework version:** Disagree AND commit. Be known as someone who engages, not as someone who blocks.

**The AI-era version:** Same. But the bar is higher because AI is moving faster than your ability to form a deep opinion on each tool. Pick your disagreements carefully and come with alternatives.

### 3. Pretending fluency you don't have

Your manager asks what you think of a tool. You give an answer that sounds informed. You haven't actually used it. Two weeks later, you're in a meeting where you have to demonstrate the thing. You can't. Your manager notices.

**The old framework version:** Bring real data, not estimates. Know what you know.

**The AI-era version:** The stakes are higher because AI fluency is now part of how your manager is evaluating you. Admitting you haven't formed an opinion yet is a trust deposit. Faking it is a withdrawal.

### 4. Staying silent on shadow tool use

Your reps are using [Tool X] without approval. You know. You haven't said anything because it's working. Then Legal finds out through an audit. Now you're in a meeting explaining why you didn't surface it.

**The old framework version:** Bring bad news early with a solve attached.

**The AI-era version:** Same principle, new category of bad news. Shadow tool use is now standard early-surface territory.

### 5. Letting the AI forecast become your forecast

Clari says $480K. You agree with Clari. Leadership asks you to defend the number on the exec call. You can't, because you didn't actually do the math — you just ratified the tool's output. Now you sound unprepared on something that was supposed to be your core competency.

**The old framework version:** Call the real number. Show your math.

**The AI-era version:** The tool does the first pass. You do the math anyway. You need to be able to explain a delta — up or down — in your own words.

## Concrete Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Shadow Tool Escalation

You've known for six weeks that three of your reps are using a personal-account ChatGPT for drafting customer-facing emails. They're getting results. Company policy is ambiguous — there's no formal ban, but there's no approval either. The reps haven't fed in PII, as far as you know.

**The old-framework move:** Bring it up cleanly in your next 1:1 with your manager: "Here's what's happening, here's what I recommend, here's what I'll do either way."

**The AI-era specifics:**
- Name the tools by name.
- Describe the actual use case — drafting outreach, not handling customer data.
- Propose a path: sanction it formally, ban it formally, or pilot a sanctioned alternative.
- Tell her what you'll do if she kicks it back: you'll establish your own team rule until policy catches up.

**Script:**

> "Three reps on my team are using personal ChatGPT accounts to draft outreach. It's producing better response rates than our templates. As far as I've seen, no customer data is going into it, but I can't be certain long-term. Our policy is ambiguous so I want to surface it before it becomes a risk. My recommendation is we either formally sanction a tool or formally ban it — the current ambiguous state is the worst of both. I'd like to pilot [sanctioned option] with those three reps for 30 days. If you want to hold policy-level, I'll set a team rule in the meantime: drafting only, no customer data ever, and all outputs get edited before they go out."

Notice what's happening: Inform (what's going on), Advocate (the recommendation), Execute (what you'll do regardless). Same framework, new content.

### Scenario 2: The AI Forecast You Don't Trust

Gong Forecast says your month is $480K. Your real math — qualified pipeline × historical conversion — puts you at $620K. You know why the AI is low: two big enterprise deals have low activity scores because the buyer is a committee that only engages in scheduled meetings, not email. Gong's model penalizes that.

**The old-framework move:** Call your real number. Explain the math. Name the risks.

**The AI-era specifics:**
- Open by acknowledging the AI number. Don't pretend it doesn't exist.
- Explain the specific failure mode for your pipeline. Not "AI is wrong" — "this model systematically underweights enterprise deals that move through committee."
- Commit to a checkpoint that would settle it: "If those two deals don't advance by the 15th, I'll come back to $480K."

**Script:**

> "Gong is calling $480K. I'm calling $620K. The delta is driven by two enterprise deals — [Company A] and [Company B] — that have low activity scores because the buyers are committees. They engage in scheduled touch points, not email threads, which Gong's model penalizes. I've checked in with both and they're tracking for signature by end of month. If they don't advance by the 15th, I'll revise down to Gong's number or below. I'm giving you the honest read, not the optimistic one."

### Scenario 3: Championing a Tool You Didn't Pick

Your CRO is rolling out [AI tool] company-wide. You've used it for two hours. You're not convinced it's better than what your team already has. She wants you on the all-hands next week to champion it.

**The old-framework move:** Advocate privately, execute publicly.

**The AI-era specifics:**
- Don't fake enthusiasm in the all-hands. Your team will see it and your credibility will drop faster than the tool's adoption.
- Do make the private advocate case before the all-hands — honestly, with data, with a proposed alternative path if you have one.
- If the decision holds, you execute cleanly. But you can execute cleanly while being honest: "This is the direction. Here's what I'm personally still figuring out. Here's what I'll ask of you in the meantime."

**Script to leadership (before the all-hands):**

> "Before I champion [Tool X] on Tuesday, I want to share where I actually am: I've spent two hours with it and I'm not yet sold. Here's what I'd need to see to be a genuine advocate: [specific]. If we're moving forward regardless, I'll execute it cleanly — but I'd rather tell my team honestly that I'm still evaluating it alongside them than oversell it and lose credibility when the rough edges show."

**Script to the team (at the all-hands):**

> "[Tool X] is what we're running with. I'm still figuring out what it does well and where it doesn't. I'd rather we do that together than I pretend I've already figured it out. Here's what we're committing to in the next 30 days: [specific adoption plan]. If you hit a wall, surface it — that's data, not failure."

This is the hardest move in the AI-era managing up set. The temptation to either fake enthusiasm or passive-aggressively undermine the rollout is strong. Neither is what the framework says to do.

## How Module 02's Material Adapts

**The Translator Role:** Expands to three-way translation — reps, leadership, AI tools. The tension isn't binary anymore. You're now responsible for validating AI outputs before passing them up, and for surfacing AI realities on the ground before leadership finds out from another source.

**Inform / Advocate / Execute:** Unchanged as a framework. The new applications are: (1) shadow tool surfacing as standard Inform territory, (2) AI forecast disagreement as standard Advocate territory, (3) championing tools you don't love as the hardest Execute scenario you'll face.

**Trust Capital:** Still the meta-lesson. The new deposits are: validating AI outputs before passing them up, surfacing AI realities early, admitting what you don't know about AI, holding a consistent judgment-first line. The new withdrawals are: passing unchecked AI summaries, faking fluency, becoming a default AI skeptic, letting shadow tools run without surfacing them.

**The Forecasting Paradox:** Now has a third party in the room — the AI forecast. Your job isn't to defer to it or dismiss it. It's to do the human math, compare, explain the delta, and commit to a checkpoint that would settle it. The AI forecast is an input, not an output.

**The Underperformer Communication Dilemma:** Gets a new dimension. AI-generated performance data — call analytics, activity scores, pipeline health — may surface performance issues faster and with harder-to-dispute evidence. Your "no surprises ever" principle becomes easier to honor AND the data gets richer. Use it. Don't let AI generate your conclusion for you — but let it accelerate the moment you can have the conversation.

---

*The through-line for this module doesn't change. Results are table stakes. Trust is the currency. What changes is the specific set of behaviors that now build or break trust with leadership. Name the new behaviors. Practice them. Don't wait until a bad moment to figure out what you believe about AI in front of your boss.*
