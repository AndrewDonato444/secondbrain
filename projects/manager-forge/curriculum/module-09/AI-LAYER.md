# Module 09 — AI Layer
## What Changes About Hiring When You're Building a Team for a Job That's Still Reshaping

*The Capability-Coachability-Fit framework still works. The 4-part interview structure still works. The 30-60-90 still works. This layer adds a fourth dimension — AI fluency — and explains how it joins the existing framework without replacing it.*

---

## The Manager's Job in the AI Era

**Before:** You hired for capability, coachability, and fit. Coachability dominated. The assumption was that the job itself was relatively stable — you were hiring for a known quantity, and the question was which candidate could ramp fastest and grow farthest.

**Now:** The job is reshaping in real time. The rep who makes quota in 2026 uses AI tools that didn't exist in 2024, and will use tools in 2028 that don't exist yet. You're not hiring for a static role — you're hiring for someone who can keep up with a moving one. That makes AI fluency a structural requirement, not a bonus.

But here's the subtlety: AI fluency is not the same as AI tool power-user. A rep who has used ChatGPT for two years but accepts every first output and sends generic AI-drafted emails is not AI-fluent. A rep who has used AI for six months but edits every output, iterates on prompts, and knows when to turn it off is AI-fluent.

Your hiring job in the AI era is to:

- **Screen for AI fluency as a fourth dimension alongside the existing three.** Not as a replacement. Coachability still dominates — and coachability on AI specifically is more predictive than current tool usage level.
- **Add specific AI fluency probes to the interview.** "Do you use AI?" is useless. "Tell me about the last time you used AI to prepare for a call — what did you prompt, what did you change, what did you ignore?" is diagnostic.
- **Screen out AI-resistant candidates who won't coach.** Skepticism is fine. Resistance is not. The difference is in whether they're curious under the skepticism.
- **Update the 30-60-90 with an AI ramp track.** Your new hire's onboarding has to include learning your team's AI workflow, not just your product and process.
- **Hire for team composition, not individual fit alone.** If half your team is AI-fluent and the other half is AI-avoidant, you have a performance gap that will compound. Your next hire shifts the balance deliberately.

---

## New Failure Modes

### 1. Hiring the traditional rep who "we can teach AI to later"

Strong capability, strong coachability, strong fit — and no AI fluency. You think: we can teach that. Six months in, they haven't adopted the team's AI tools. They're making quota with heroic manual effort while peers are 2x-ing their capacity. You're carrying a manual rep in an AI-augmented team.

**The tell:** You passed the AI fluency probe with "we'll coach them up on that."

**The cost:** Team composition drift. Over 18 months, the gap between AI-fluent peers and AI-avoidant reps becomes a retention issue (the AI-fluent reps feel dragged down) and a fairness issue (the AI-avoidant rep is working twice as hard for the same result).

### 2. Overweighting AI tool familiarity vs. AI judgment

A candidate has an impressive list of AI tools they've used. You hire them. In practice, they send first-draft AI outputs without editing, produce generic outreach at high volume, and have no judgment about when AI is the wrong tool. Volume looks great. Quality collapses.

**The tell:** You screened for AI usage, not AI judgment.

**The cost:** A rep who is a worse version of the AI tools they're using. And now you're coaching judgment, which is harder than coaching familiarity.

### 3. Dismissing the AI-skeptical candidate who could be coached

A candidate pushes back on AI in the interview: "I'm skeptical. I think a lot of my peers are using it as a crutch." You pass because you want AI-native hires. You miss a potentially high-quality rep who would have been one of your best AI users in 12 months.

**The tell:** You're confusing skepticism with resistance.

**The cost:** Under-hiring thoughtful reps who would develop into discerning AI users — exactly what your team needs.

### 4. Not updating the 30-60-90 with AI milestones

You hire. The rep onboards on product, ICP, process. No one tells them your team's AI workflow. By day 60, they've developed their own AI habits, half of which don't match what your team does. Retraining now is harder than training from scratch.

**The tell:** Your 30-60-90 doesn't mention AI.

**The cost:** Variance in how your reps use AI across the team. The team's quality control standards degrade.

### 5. Weighting AI fluency the same forever

AI fluency is a 30% weight in your rubric right now. In two years, when everyone's AI-fluent, it'll be a 10% weight, table stakes. Your rubric has to evolve. If you're still weighting AI fluency the same in 2028, you're over-indexing on what the market has caught up on.

**The tell:** You haven't revisited your rubric since you built it.

**The cost:** Under-hiring on the dimensions that will actually differentiate later (judgment, synthesis, relationship skill).

## Concrete Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Curious Skeptic

Jordan, 8 years of B2B sales experience, strong track record. In the AI probe, you ask: "Tell me how you're using AI in your current role."

Jordan: "Honestly, I'm skeptical. I've watched a lot of my peers use it as a crutch. I think the response rates they're getting are short-term — prospects will start recognizing AI outreach and it'll stop working. That said, I know your team uses it heavily, so I'd want to understand how you guys distinguish between good use and lazy use before I'd say I wouldn't use it."

**The wrong read:** "He's resistant. Pass."

**The right read:** This is exactly the profile you want. He's skeptical (shows judgment), curious (asked about your team's standards), not resistant (didn't refuse). Under coaching, he'll develop into a disciplined AI user because he starts from a judgment-first posture.

**Follow-up probe:** "Let's say you joined the team and our standard was 'AI drafts, human edits, never send first output.' How would you approach that?"

If Jordan engages thoughtfully — talks about how he'd want to see examples, experiment for a quarter, develop his own voice within the framework — hire. If he pivots to "I still don't think I'd use it much" — probable pass.

### Scenario 2: The Overconfident User

Priya, 4 years of sales experience, currently at a company with heavy AI tooling. AI probe:

Priya: "Oh I use AI for everything. I have 50+ prompts saved. I've built workflows. I barely write anything manually anymore."

**The wrong read:** "AI-native. Hire."

**The right read:** Promising, but probe further. Volume ≠ judgment.

**Follow-up probes:**
1. "Walk me through a specific AI-generated email you sent last week. What did you prompt, what did it produce, what did you change?"
2. "When is AI the wrong tool for a sales task?"
3. "What's an example of AI output you decided not to send?"

If Priya can answer all three with specifics — show you a real prompt, name a real scenario where AI is wrong, describe a real decision to throw something away — she's genuinely fluent. Hire.

If she gestures vaguely — "I always edit, of course" but can't produce examples; "it depends" on when AI is wrong but can't name a specific scenario; "I always use what I prompt" (nothing thrown away) — she's high-volume, low-judgment. The risk: she'll flood your team's outbound with generic AI outreach. Pass or coach-on-hire with explicit judgment-development milestones.

### Scenario 3: The Day-60 AI Gap

You hire Alex. Strong on all four dimensions at the interview — including AI fluency. Day 30 check-in goes well. Day 60 check-in: Alex is ramping on product and ICP but hasn't adopted the team's AI workflow. He's still drafting all his outreach manually.

**The wrong moves:**
- Ignore it because his numbers are fine so far.
- Escalate it as a performance issue.
- Add another training module.

**The right move:**
Raise it in the next 1:1 as a coaching conversation.

"I want to check in on something. At the interview, you were clear about how you use AI in your workflow. I'm not seeing that show up yet in how you're working here. What's going on?"

Possible answers:
- "Honestly, I'm still getting my footing with the product and I figured I'd layer AI in once I have the fundamentals." (Legitimate — set a specific checkpoint for when AI comes in.)
- "I've been experimenting but I don't think our team's tools are as good as what I used before." (Useful feedback — probe which tools specifically.)
- "I've been busy. I just haven't had time to set it up." (Resistance in polite clothing — escalate to explicit coaching.)

The point: the interview established a baseline. The 30-60-90 has to reinforce it or you lose what you hired for.

## How Module 09's Material Adapts

**Capability-Coachability-Fit:** Unchanged as a framework. AI fluency is added as a fourth dimension. Coachability still dominates the weighting — a candidate with moderate AI fluency but high coachability will outperform a high-AI-fluency, low-coachability candidate within a year. The rubric update in WS-09-15 makes this explicit.

**The 4-Part Interview Structure:** Unchanged as structure. The AI fluency probes (WS-09-12) add to Part 2 (Skill Assessment) and Part 3 (Coachability Check). You don't need a fifth interview segment.

**Coachability Red Flags:** Extended. The existing red flags (external blame, defensiveness, no self-awareness, job-hopping without growth) are unchanged. Two new AI-specific red flags are added:
- "Treats AI as cheating" — identity-level resistance that won't coach
- "No curiosity about tools" — signals rigidity that will become a liability as tools evolve

**The 30-60-90 Onboarding Plan:** Needs an AI ramp track added. WS-09-14 provides the milestones for day 30, 60, and 90 specifically for AI tool adoption. This runs in parallel with the existing product/process/skill milestones, not instead of them.

**Day 60 Decision Point:** Unchanged in structure. The addition: at day 60, you're now also checking AI workflow integration. A rep who's on track for product/process ramp but has stalled on AI adoption needs the same kind of coaching-plan-or-exit conversation you'd run for any other gap. Not to fire them — to surface the gap before day 90.

**Team Composition Strategy:** Now has an AI fluency dimension. Your team's archetype mix (hunters/farmers, analytical/relational, fast/deep) now also has an AI dimension — how many of your reps are AI-fluent, AI-skeptical-but-coachable, AI-avoidant? If the distribution is wrong, your next hire should shift it deliberately.

---

*Hiring is still the highest-leverage decision you make. That hasn't changed. What's changed is that one of the signals you're screening for didn't exist five years ago, and the strength of that signal is compounding in your team's performance every quarter you don't screen for it. You don't need to hire only AI-native reps. You need to stop hiring reps who won't coach on AI. That's a subtle but important distinction. Get it right and your team composition trends toward flexible, curious, and compounding. Get it wrong and you're carrying the same few reps through every new tool rollout for the next decade.*
