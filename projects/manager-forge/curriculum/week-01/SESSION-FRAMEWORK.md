# Week 1 — Session Framework
## "Your Job Changed. Here's the New Job Description."

**Program:** Manager Forge — AI Spine  
**Session:** Week 1 of 6  
**Format:** Live, 90 minutes via Zoom  
**Date:** July 13 (or 20), 2026 — Cohort 1  
**Facilitator:** Andrew Donato

---

## Session Goal

By the end of this session, every manager in the cohort has a written definition of their new value proposition — the specific judgment calls, synthesis moves, and human reads that AI cannot replicate. They leave with clarity about what the job actually is now, not anxiety about what it used to be.

**The shift:** Stop managing from anxiety. Start managing from a clear north star.

**Monday morning artifact:** WS-W1-01 completed — "My Irreplaceable Value-Add"  
*(3 judgment calls, biggest AI delegation opportunity, one thing they're stopping manually this week)*

## 90-Minute Run of Show

---

### BLOCK 1 — Opening Frame (15 min)

**[0:00 – 3:00] Welcome + poll read-back**

Andrew reads out 3-4 of the pre-session AI questions submitted. Does not answer them yet — names the pattern: "Almost everyone submitted a question that's actually the same question: 'Am I still going to be relevant?' That's what we're here to answer tonight."

---

**[3:00 – 10:00] The CRM literacy analogy**

*Source: Module 13 BRIEF opening frame — adapt in Andrew's voice*

Pull from the Module 13 opening: the moment in the early 2000s when CRM went from "exotic skill" to table stakes. Managers who learned it early got ahead. Managers who waited got left behind. Not because the job changed — but because the tools changed what excellence looked like.

The frame: "We're in that moment right now, but faster. And the managers who are here tonight? You're the ones who showed up early."

---

**[10:00 – 15:00] The anxiety audit — live show-of-hands**

Facilitator runs a rapid-fire hand raise (Zoom reactions: raise hand = yes):

1. "Raise your hand if you used AI for something work-related this week."
2. "Keep it up if AI touched something that used to take you an hour."
3. "Raise your hand if one of your reps uses AI more than you do."
4. "Raise your hand if you've ever wondered whether AI is going to make your role smaller."

Andrew names what just happened: "The anxiety is real. But I want to show you something. The thing making your job feel unstable — role compression — is actually the same force that's going to make the best managers more valuable than they've ever been. The question isn't whether AI changes things. It's whether you end up doing AI's job or your actual job."

---

### BLOCK 2 — Main Teaching (30 min)

**[15:00 – 25:00] The New Job Description — Both/And framework**

*Source: Module 13 Both/And framework, expanded. Use FW-W1-01 as visual.*

Screen share FW-W1-01 (New Job Description one-pager). Walk through both columns:

**What AI Should Own (your delegation list):**
- Cold outreach first drafts
- CRM data entry and stage updates
- Meeting summaries from Gong/Chorus
- Pipeline data compilation
- Rep activity tracking and alerts
- First-pass forecast modeling
- Onboarding content generation
- Email follow-up sequences

"If you're doing any of these manually in 2026, you're doing AI's job. And that's a problem — not because you're bad at it, but because every hour you spend on this is an hour you're not doing what only you can do."

**Where Humans Still Win (your new job):**
- Reading deal health from context the data doesn't capture
- Synthesizing AI outputs into judgment calls ("the AI says X, but here's what I actually think")
- Prompt engineering — knowing what questions to ask the AI and what to do with the answer
- Coaching rep behavior, not just rep output
- Reading what's unsaid in a conversation
- Managing team dynamics and morale
- Making the call when the data is ambiguous

---

**[25:00 – 35:00] The Abdication Risk — where this goes wrong**

"There's a failure mode on the other side of this that I want to name, because I see it. It's called abdication."

Andrew's real example from his team:
- How he uses Gong coaching summaries in his actual weekly routine
- The specific workflow: Gong generates the summary → Andrew reads it → Andrew notes what the summary got right and what it missed → Andrew goes into the 1:1 with his own read of the situation
- The specific judgment calls he always makes himself: deal health vs. rep confidence, coaching gap vs. motivation gap, when to push and when to protect

"The abdication version of this: you read the Gong summary and treat it as the coaching plan. You stop making the call yourself. The summary becomes the job. And when that happens, your value proposition to the organization disappears — because anyone can read a summary."

**The tell:** "You can spot abdication by asking yourself: when's the last time my read of a situation was different from what the data said? If the answer is 'never' — you're abdicating."

---

**[35:00 – 45:00] Managing rep AI anxiety — the new emotional category**

"Before we do the workshop, I want to add one thing to the New Job Description: managing your team through their own AI anxiety. Because your reps are having this conversation too."

Brief section on the three rep archetypes — expand on Module 01 emotional leadership content:

**The Over-Reliant Rep:** Using AI as a crutch. Output is generic. Quality is dropping. They think they're being efficient; you're seeing the signal. Management implication: coach on quality, not tool use. The question is "what did you add to this that AI couldn't?"

**The AI-Avoider:** Still doing everything manually. Slower than peers. Getting left behind on speed and coverage. Often the most experienced rep on the team — resistant because of identity. Management implication: don't shame the avoidance, find the one use case where AI makes their best skill better. Start there.

**The AI-Native with Other Gaps:** Comfortable with the tools. Faster than everyone else. But the gaps now show more clearly because AI has removed the easy excuses. Management implication: the data is now telling you things it couldn't before. Pay attention to what's surfacing.

"You need to know which archetype each of your reps is — because the coaching conversation is completely different. We'll go deep on this in Week 3."

---

### BLOCK 3 — Live Workshop (30 min)

**[45:00 – 60:00] WS-13-03 live — AI Capability Mapping**

"Okay. We're going to run this right now. Open WS-13-03."

*(Participants have it pre-loaded from Skool — or screen share if needed)*

Direct the cohort through Parts 2 and 3:
- List 5 tasks from this week
- For each: could AI help? What would it do? What would you still need to do?
- Time savings calculation

**4-minute solo work** → Andrew circulates with comments in Zoom chat *(or calls on 2-3 people to read their tasks aloud)*

"What I want you to notice: the tasks where AI can help most are the tasks you probably don't enjoy anyway. And the tasks where you're irreplaceable — those are the ones you should be protecting time for."

---

**[60:00 – 75:00] WS-13-04 + WS-W1-01 — Where's Your Judgment**

"Now we're going to do the harder exercise. Open WS-13-04."

Walk through the prompts:
- Think of 3 decisions you made this week that required judgment — not just data
- For each: why couldn't AI have made this decision? What context did you have that AI wouldn't?

**5-minute solo work** → verbal share-out: Andrew calls on 3-4 participants to name one judgment call

Andrew bridges to WS-W1-01: "What you just identified — those judgment calls — that's your new job description. That's what I want you to crystallize before you close your computer tonight."

Start WS-W1-01 together: name your 3 irreplaceable judgment calls from this exercise.

---

### BLOCK 4 — Close (15 min)

**[75:00 – 82:00] Lazy Work Detection — rapid fire**

"Quick last one before we close out. WS-13-05 — Lazy Work Detection. You've got 5 email examples. I want you to vote: lazy AI work, smart AI work, or human work?"

Run it as a live poll or rapid-fire Zoom chat reaction:
- Email 1: Lazy (vague corporate speak, template-obvious)
- Email 2: Human (specific, personal, researched)
- Email 3: Smart AI work (structured, but human judgment layered in on risks)
- Email 4: Lazy (generic language, "synergized")
- Email 5: Smart AI work (1:1 prep with AI-organized data + human coaching frame)

"The pattern: smart AI work has human fingerprints on it. Lazy AI work is output sent as-is. Your job as a manager is to be able to tell the difference — and to coach the rep toward the human fingerprint."

---

**[82:00 – 88:00] WS-W1-01 close — "My Irreplaceable Value-Add"**

"Before you leave: finish WS-W1-01. The last three fields. Your 3 judgment calls. Your biggest AI delegation opportunity. One thing you're going to stop doing manually this week."

Give 4 minutes. Ask 2 participants to read their "one thing I'm stopping manually."

"That's your homework. Not reading, not watching. Doing. You're going to delegate one thing to AI this week that you've been doing manually. And you're going to show up next week and tell us what happened."

## Facilitator Notes

**Things that tend to happen:**

*"What if my company doesn't use AI tools?"* → "The framework matters more than the tool. And you don't need company buy-in to start. Start with yourself."

*"I don't want to look like I'm replacing my reps with AI."* → "You're not. You're teaching your reps to be better at the parts of the job that matter. The managers who avoid this conversation are the ones who create the uncertainty they're trying to avoid."

*Quiet cohort during share-outs* → Run the judgment call exercise in breakout rooms (2-3 people) for 3 minutes, then come back and share. Smaller group = more willingness to go specific.

**Pacing check:** If you're behind by the time you hit the workshop block, cut the rep archetype section (brief it quickly, defer to Week 3 where we go deep on it). Don't cut the workshop.

**Energy management:** The first session sets the tone for all 6. The goal is that participants leave having done something real — not just listened. The WS-W1-01 artifact is the most important thing they take out of the room.
