# FW-W3-01 — Three AI Coaching Archetypes

**Manager Forge — AI Spine | Week 3**  
**Type:** Framework (diagnostic tool)  
**Use:** Facilitator reference + participant handout

---

## The Core Idea

When a rep's performance is connected to AI usage, there are three distinct failure modes — and they require completely different coaching approaches. Misdiagnosing the archetype means you coach the wrong thing, frustrate the rep, and don't move the number.

Diagnose before you coach.

---

## The Diagnostic Question

Before you open a coaching conversation about AI, ask yourself:

> *Is this rep using AI too much, not enough, or the right amount — and the problem is somewhere else entirely?*

The answer determines everything that comes next.

---

## Archetype 1: The Over-Reliant Rep

**The pattern:** High AI activity, below-quota results. The tool is running, but the human work that makes it effective has stopped.

---

### Observable Signals

- Outreach volume is normal or high; conversation and meeting-booked rates are low
- Work product is polished but generic — could have been sent to any prospect
- Enthusiastically describes AI usage but struggles to recall specific prospect conversations
- Prospecting "activity" is high; actual human-to-human conversations are rare
- Pipeline is full of deals with strong initial engagement and dead follow-through

### Root Cause

Not laziness. Usually a confidence problem wearing a technology costume.

Real conversations involve rejection. Sequences and AI-generated outreach feel safer — they're high-volume, low-exposure. The tool became a way to be busy without risking the discomfort of the actual job.

These are often thoughtful, motivated reps who found the tool, learned it well, and let it become a substitute for the hard part rather than a multiplier of it.

### What This Is NOT

Not a tool problem. Not an AI problem. The rep's AI usage is fine. The behavior the AI is enabling — avoidance of live conversations — is the problem.

### Coaching Entry Point

Don't coach the AI. Coach the conversion metric.

**Opening move:** "Your outreach volume looks solid. Your conversation rate is [X]. Walk me through a typical prospecting week — how many real conversations are you having?"

**Follow-up:** "What does a healthy conversation rate look like for this territory? Let's set a target there and track it."

The rep should leave with a specific, measurable behavior to track — not "use AI less."

### What to Avoid

- "Stop using AI for outreach" → You've become anti-tool
- "Your emails look too templated" → You're editing copy instead of coaching
- Coaching the tool rather than the conversion gap

### 30 / 60 / 90 Day Success

| Checkpoint | What you're looking for |
|---|---|
| 30 days | Conversation rate target set. Rep is tracking and reporting it. |
| 60 days | Trending toward target. At least one week at or above goal. |
| 90 days | Pipeline velocity improving. Quota moving. |

## Archetype 2: The AI-Avoider

**The pattern:** Manual processes where peers are using AI. Takes longer on tasks that could be accelerated. Falling behind on output volume or quality relative to peers, even when working hard.

---

### Observable Signals

- Writing outreach from scratch while peers use AI to draft and refine
- Manually completing tasks where AI has become standard on the team
- Expresses skepticism ("I don't think AI can write the way I do") or anxiety ("if the tool does it, what do they need me for?")
- May not know how to use the tools and hasn't said so
- Competitive disadvantage is compounding — working harder for the same output as peers doing less

### Root Cause

Almost always one of two things:

**Skill gap:** They don't know how to use the tools and are afraid to look incompetent. They've watched peers use AI fluently and don't want to start from zero in public.

**Belief gap:** A genuine values-based conviction that "real selling" is human-only and AI undermines the craft. This one is harder — it's not a knowledge problem, it's an identity problem.

Distinguish these early. They require different conversations.

### What This Is NOT

Not laziness. Not resistance for its own sake. The AI-avoider is often one of your grinders — the rep who has always outworked the problem. The AI era threatens that identity directly, and that's real.

### Coaching Entry Point

Start with curiosity. Do not open with a directive.

**Opening move:** "I noticed you're still writing your outreach manually. Walk me through why."

**If skill gap:** Lower the stakes. Find a peer who can show them the workflow in 20 minutes without judgment. Start with one task, not a full adoption ask. "What's one thing that eats your time that you'd be willing to try with AI for two weeks?"

**If belief gap:** Don't argue. Find one task where AI makes them better at the thing they already care about — not faster, but *better*. Let them experience it. "You told me you want your outreach to feel more specific. Here's how I'd use the tool to do that — you'd still be writing it, but the first draft would be yours to shape."

### What to Avoid

- "You need to start using AI" → Directive without diagnosis
- Framing it as "catching up to your peers" → Shame-based, triggers defensiveness
- Arguing with the belief gap → You will not win. Demonstrate instead.

### 30 / 60 / 90 Day Success

| Checkpoint | What you're looking for |
|---|---|
| 30 days | Identifies 1 specific workflow they'll experiment with. Tries it. |
| 60 days | Using AI consistently in that workflow. Not fighting it. |
| 90 days | Self-reporting time savings, quality gains, or both. Open to expanding. |

## Archetype 3: The AI-Native with Other Gaps

**The pattern:** Fluent AI user, comfortable with tools, visible in their usage — and still underperforming. The AI is not the problem.

---

### Observable Signals

- High AI fluency — comfortable with tools, often shows others how to use them
- Performance gap exists and doesn't map to AI tool activity
- May reference AI work when discussing their process ("I use AI for all my prep...")
- The gap is somewhere else: discovery, qualification, closing, pipeline discipline, time management
- The manager's instinct is to focus on the AI because it's the most visible variable

### Root Cause

There's a skill gap in a traditional sales behavior. The AI is working fine. The manager is being distracted by the tool.

This is the most common mis-diagnosis. The rep's AI fluency is visible and impressive, so it becomes the focal point — while the actual gap (weak closing, poor qualification, scattered pipeline management) goes uncoached.

### What This Is NOT

Not an AI problem. The AI is a decoy.

### Coaching Entry Point

Strip out the AI context entirely. Run a standard performance diagnosis.

**Opening move:** "Walk me through your last three deals that didn't close. At what point in the process did things stall?"

From there, diagnose exactly where in the sales process performance is breaking down. Coach that. Do not bring AI into the conversation unless it's directly relevant to the specific gap.

**The rule:** If the AI is working and the numbers aren't, look at everything except the AI.

### What to Avoid

- Coaching AI usage as a proxy for performance coaching
- "You're using AI, but the outputs aren't landing" → Usually wrong
- Letting the rep use their AI fluency as a deflection from the actual gap

### 30 / 60 / 90 Day Success

Standard performance coaching milestones. The AI is a non-factor once you've correctly identified and begun coaching the real gap.

---

## The Diagnostic Quick Reference

| If you see... | Likely archetype | Coaching entry point |
|---|---|---|
| High AI activity + low conversation rate | Over-Reliant | Coach the conversion metric, not the tool |
| Manual process where peers use AI + falling behind on volume | AI-Avoider | Curiosity first: "Walk me through why" |
| AI fluency + performance gap that doesn't map to AI | AI-Native with Other Gaps | Standard performance diagnosis — ignore the AI |

---

## One Rule Across All Three

You're not coaching AI behavior. You're coaching results behavior. The archetype tells you where to look. The results tell you what to fix.

*"I'm not evaluating whether the AI helped you. I'm evaluating whether you used it or whether it used you."*
