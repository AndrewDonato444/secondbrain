## 90-Minute Run of Show

---

### BLOCK 1 — Opening Frame (15 min)

**[0:00 – 5:00] The story: what the data showed vs. what the conversation revealed**

*Tell this in first person. This is Andrew's real situation from TrackForce.*

One of my reps — solid performer, been on the team 18 months, quota attainment historically in the 85-95% range — starts slipping. Q1 comes in at 71%. I pull up the activity data. Emails: normal. Sequences running: normal. On paper it looks like he's working.

I sit down with him for a pipeline review. Something's off. I ask him to walk me through his prospecting week. He talks for 90 seconds and then stops. I ask how many real conversations he'd had with prospects in the last two weeks — phone calls, discovery sessions, anything where a human talked back. He thinks about it. Three. In two weeks.

The AI was generating the outreach. The sequences were running. The emails were sending. But he had stopped having conversations. He'd automated the top of the funnel and convinced himself that was the job.

That's the over-reliance failure mode. And here's what made it tricky to coach: he wasn't slacking. He was actually working harder — managing sequences, personalizing templates, tracking open rates. He'd found a way to be busy that wasn't producing results.

The coaching conversation I had to have wasn't "stop using AI." It was: "The tool is sending emails. Your job is creating conversations. Those are different things. When did you last have five real conversations in a day?"

---

**[5:00 – 10:00] The "both/and" coaching trap**

There are two wrong responses to an AI-related performance problem:

**Wrong response 1:** "Stop using AI so much." 
→ You've become anti-tool. Now you're the manager who penalizes reps for using technology. You'll lose the AI-native reps first — which are often your best reps.

**Wrong response 2:** "At least they're using the tools — it'll work itself out."
→ You're using AI adoption as a proxy for performance. It isn't. A rep can be excellent at using AI and still be missing quota. The tool doesn't close deals.

The right response is both/and: *you're not anti-AI, and you're pro-results, and right now those are pulling apart — so let's figure out where the gap actually is.*

That requires diagnosing the archetype before you coach.

---

**[10:00 – 15:00] Preview: the three archetypes**

There are three AI-related coaching scenarios you will encounter on your team. They look similar on the surface — AI is involved, performance has a problem — but the root cause is completely different, which means the coaching entry point is completely different.

Tonight we're going to map all three. You're going to identify which archetype at least one of your reps fits. And you're going to leave with a first conversation drafted.

Pull up FW-W3-01. We're walking through this together.

### BLOCK 2 — The Three AI Coaching Archetypes (20 min)

**[15:00 – 35:00] Walk through FW-W3-01 live**

*Screen share FW-W3-01. Read each archetype aloud with commentary — don't just recite the doc, add the "I've seen this as" texture.*

---

**Archetype 1: The Over-Reliant Rep**

*Observable signals:*
- High AI-tool activity, below-quota results
- Outreach volume looks normal, conversation rate is low
- Their work product is polished but generic
- They talk about AI usage enthusiastically but struggle to recall specific prospect conversations

*Root cause:*
Not laziness. Usually a confidence problem wearing a technology costume. The tool feels safer than real conversations. Rejection is easier to handle when you're not the one making the call.

*Andrew's add:* "These reps are often some of your more thoughtful ones. They found the tool, they learned it, they believed in it. They just let it become a substitute for the hard part instead of a multiplier of the hard part."

*Coaching entry point:*
Don't coach the AI usage. Coach the conversion metric. "Your outreach volume is solid. Your conversation rate is low. Walk me through a week of prospecting — how many real conversations are you having?" Then: "What does a good conversation rate look like? Let's set a target there."

The AI isn't the problem. The avoidance the AI is enabling is the problem.

*30/60/90 success:*
30 days — conversation rate target set and being tracked. 60 days — trending toward target. 90 days — quota moving with it.

---

**Archetype 2: The AI-Avoider**

*Observable signals:*
- Manual processes where peers are using AI (writing their own outreach from scratch, manually logging everything)
- Takes significantly longer on tasks that AI could accelerate
- May express skepticism about AI quality ("it's not real selling") or anxiety about obsolescence ("if the tool does it, what do they need me for?")
- Competitive disadvantage compounds over time — they're working harder for the same output as peers doing less

*Root cause:*
Usually one of two things: fear of looking incompetent (they don't know how to use the tools and don't want to admit it) or a values-level belief that "real selling" is human only (this one is harder).

*Andrew's add:* "The avoider is rarely lazy. They're often your grinders — the ones who've always outworked the problem. The AI era is genuinely threatening to that identity. Your job isn't to tell them they're wrong. It's to show them what they're leaving on the table."

*Coaching entry point:*
Start with curiosity, not a directive. "I noticed you're still writing outreach manually. Walk me through why." Listen for whether it's a skill gap or a belief gap — those get coached differently. If it's a skill gap: find them a peer or resource and lower the stakes of learning. If it's a belief gap: you're not going to win an argument. Find one task where AI makes them better at the thing they care about, and let them experience it.

*30/60/90 success:*
30 days — identifies 1 workflow where they'll experiment with AI. 60 days — using it consistently in that workflow. 90 days — self-reporting time savings or quality gains.

---

**Archetype 3: The AI-Native with Other Gaps**

*Observable signals:*
- Fluent AI user, comfortable with the tools
- Performance gap exists but doesn't map to AI usage
- May be masking the real gap with tool sophistication ("look how good this prompt is")
- The diagnosis error: managers assume the tool is the problem because the tool is visible

*Root cause:*
There's a skill gap somewhere else — discovery, qualification, closing, time management, pipeline discipline. The AI is actually working fine. The manager just can't see past the shiny object.

*Andrew's add:* "This is the easiest one to mis-diagnose. The rep is the most impressive-looking person in the room when it comes to AI, and you spend six weeks coaching their tool usage when the actual problem is they can't hold a closing conversation. Don't let the tool distract you."

*Coaching entry point:*
Strip out the AI context entirely and run a standard performance diagnosis. Where in the sales process is performance breaking down? Coach that. The AI isn't your problem here — it's your decoy.

*30/60/90 success:*
Same as any standard coaching plan. The AI is a non-factor once you've correctly identified the gap.

### BLOCK 3 — Coaching Reps on Prompt Quality (10 min)

**[35:00 – 45:00] How to evaluate rep AI output without becoming a prompt editor**

*This is a brief but important teaching section — managers need a QC lens that doesn't require them to be AI experts.*

Here's the trap: a rep shows you an AI-generated email, and you immediately think "I could have written this better." You become a copy editor. That's not your job and it doesn't scale.

Your job is to evaluate whether the rep is using the tool as a thinking tool or as a shortcut. Three criteria:

**1. Context: Is the rep putting real information in?**

A good prompt includes: who the prospect is, what they care about, what the rep knows from prior conversations, what the specific goal of this touchpoint is. A lazy prompt is "write me a follow-up email for a sales prospect."

You can't read the prompt, but you can read the output. If the email could have been sent to anyone, the prompt had no real context. Ask: "Tell me what you told the AI about this prospect before it wrote this."

**2. Specificity: Does the output reflect what the rep actually knows?**

If the rep had a discovery call with a prospect two weeks ago and the follow-up email has zero reference to anything discussed on that call, something went wrong. The AI wrote a generic follow-up. The rep's job is to make it specific.

"What did this prospect say about their biggest challenge? Is that anywhere in this email?"

**3. Iteration: Did the rep accept the first output or refine it?**

Reps who use AI well treat the first output as a draft, not a deliverable. They read it, spot where it's off, and either edit it themselves or give the AI better instructions. Reps who are using AI as a shortcut send the first output.

You don't need to audit every output. You need to ask about the process occasionally: "Walk me through how you built this." If they describe a refine-and-iterate process, they're using the tool. If they describe "I typed in a prompt and it came out," they're shortcutting.

*The key line for managers:* "I'm not evaluating whether the AI helped you. I'm evaluating whether you used it or whether it used you."

### BLOCK 5 — QC Protocol + Close (15 min)

**[75:00 – 88:00] "Spot the Lazy Work" as an operating rhythm**

Pull from Module 13's framework. The goal is not to audit every rep's AI usage — it's to build a lightweight QC habit into the standard operating rhythm so lazy work doesn't compound.

Three practical spots to catch it without turning every 1:1 into an AI audit:

**1. Deal update reviews** — When a rep shares a prospect update, occasionally ask: "Tell me more about that." If they can't elaborate beyond what they wrote, they may not have written it.

**2. Outreach spot checks** — Once a week, pick one rep's outreach at random. Ask: "Tell me what you knew about this prospect before you wrote this." You're not looking for perfection — you're looking for whether they put real context in.

**3. Pipeline review prep** — When you run Step 2 of FW-W2-01, you'll naturally catch AI output quality issues as you review deal health. "That deal update doesn't match what I heard on the last call" is often a lazy AI output.

The rule: you're not the AI police. You're the results manager. Lazy AI work shows up in results before it shows up in the output. Build enough visibility that you catch the pattern early.

## Facilitator Notes

### Common objections:
- **"What if my rep is in multiple archetypes?"** → Rare, but it happens. If you see both over-reliance AND avoidance signals, you're probably looking at a more complex performance issue — maybe role fit. Start with coachability first. If they're not coachable, the archetype is secondary.
- **"What if my whole team is AI-avoiders? That's a culture problem."** → Yes. And that coaching conversation is with you, not them. How are you modeling AI usage? What do your 1:1s talk about? Culture follows behavior. Week 6 covers this.
- **"How do I know if it's really an AI problem or something else?"** → Strip out the AI context and run a standard pipeline / performance diagnosis. If the performance issue disappears, the AI was real. If it's still there, you've been solving the wrong problem.

### Energy management:
- The archetypes block is the heart of the session — give it the full 20 minutes. Don't rush Archetype 2 (Avoider); it's the most emotionally charged coaching conversation and managers need to sit with it.
- Role-plays often unlock the most honest conversations of the session. Don't cut them short. If the Avoider scenario is landing well, let it run an extra minute.
- WS-13-06 is a close, not a workshop — 2 minutes sketch, not a full build. The main output is WS-W3-01, done outside the session.
