# Week 4 — Session Framework
## "The 1:1 and Team Meeting That Actually Works Now"

**Program:** Manager Forge — AI Spine  
**Session:** Week 4 of 6  
**Format:** Live, 90 minutes via Zoom  
**Date:** August 3 (or 10), 2026 — Cohort 1  
**Facilitator:** Andrew Donato

---

## Session Goal

By the end of this session, every manager has a redesigned 1:1 format that uses AI-generated prep data without letting it run the conversation. They understand exactly what AI can prep for them, what it can't, and when to set the briefing aside entirely. They leave with their new 1:1 format written out and a commitment to run it next week.

**The shift:** Stop walking into 1:1s with either no prep or a data summary that turns the conversation into "here's what the AI said, do you agree?" Start walking in with AI-generated context you've already interpreted — so the meeting can be about coaching, not information transfer.

**Monday morning artifact:** WS-W4-01 completed — "My AI-Integrated 1:1 Format"  
*(AI prep section filled in, manual safety check complete, opening question written, coaching topic identified before the conversation)*

## 90-Minute Run of Show

---

### BLOCK 1 — Opening Frame (15 min)

**[0:00 – 5:00] What Andrew's pre-1:1 prep actually looks like**

*Show, don't tell. Screen share or describe in concrete detail.*

Before a 1:1, here's what I do. I ask my AI tool to pull together: this rep's pipeline health from the last two weeks (deal movement, activity volume, any sudden drops), their quota attainment trend over the last three months, any Gong call flags from the week (calls where the rep did more talking than listening, calls that ended without a clear next step), and any deal-specific signals I should know going in (deals that moved unexpectedly, deals that are stalled).

That takes the AI two minutes to generate. It takes me five minutes to read and highlight two or three things I want to understand better.

Then I close the briefing. I don't bring it into the meeting. I walk in with questions — not data.

Here's what the briefing told me before a recent 1:1 with one of my reps: activity was down 30% week over week. Two deals had slipped to an earlier stage. Gong flagged a discovery call where my rep talked for 18 minutes before asking the first question.

What I opened with: "How are you feeling about the quarter?"

Not "your activity is down 30%." Not "the AI says your deals are slipping." Not even a reference to what I'd read. Because if I'd led with data, I'd have gotten a defense. I got something much more useful.

She told me she'd had a rough week at home. Told me she'd been distracted. Told me she knew the pipeline was light and she was worried about it.

The AI gave me context. The open question gave me the actual conversation.

---

**[5:00 – 10:00] The failure mode: when the briefing runs the meeting**

The trap is seductive. You've got better pre-meeting data than any manager had five years ago. Pipeline trends, call analytics, sentiment flags. It feels like being prepared. And then the 1:1 becomes: "The AI flagged this. What happened?" "Your activity is down — why?" "This deal slipped — talk me through it."

You've turned a coaching conversation into a data interrogation. And the rep stops bringing the real stuff, because every 1:1 feels like they're being reviewed against a report card.

The Safety Pyramid from Module 06 is the lens here. AI doesn't know where your reps are on the pyramid. It can't tell you whether a rep is at Layer 1 (consistent, trusts the basics) or Layer 4 (willing to be genuinely vulnerable with you). And if you let the briefing drive your 1:1, you will never find out — because you'll never get far enough into the conversation to reach it.

*Andrew's line:* "The briefing tells you what questions to ask. It doesn't tell you how to ask them — or whether this is the moment to ask at all."

---

**[10:00 – 15:00] The specific skill this session teaches**

There are three skills packed into effective AI-integrated 1:1s:

1. **What to ask AI to generate** — and what the AI will consistently get wrong (it can't read the room, and it doesn't know your history with this person)

2. **What to verify manually before the meeting** — the human signals that no tool surfaces: energy, mood, something you noticed from last week that doesn't show up in data

3. **What questions to never let AI generate** — specifically the opening question, and any question where the answer depends on emotional safety rather than information

We're going to walk through FW-W4-01 — the protocol — and then run the workshop with your actual reps.

### BLOCK 2 — The Protocol + Coaching AI Habits in 1:1s (20 min)

**[15:00 – 28:00] FW-W4-01 walkthrough**

Screen share FW-W4-01. Walk through each section with commentary.

*Key emphasis points to hit:*

**On "what AI gets right":** Pipeline trends and activity patterns. These are data-level inputs. AI is good at surfacing them. Use them as context.

**On "what AI gets wrong":** Recency bias (same as in pipeline reviews — a bad week can obscure a strong trend), any inference about why something happened ("this rep seems disengaged" is not a data output, it's a hallucination), and anything that requires knowing the rep's personal situation.

**On the "never let AI generate" list:** The opening question is sacred. The opening question is the one that sets the tone for the entire conversation — and it should be based on what you know about this specific person, this specific moment, what you noticed last week. "How are you feeling about the quarter?" is human. "Your pipeline is down 15% — what happened?" is a briefing talking.

**The rule for the opening question:** Ask yourself: "Could this question have been generated without knowing this person?" If yes, don't ask it.

---

**[28:00 – 35:00] Coaching Reps on AI Habits in the 1:1**

*New teaching section — this is a coaching topic that doesn't exist in the archive.*

Your 1:1 is the right place to coach AI behavior — not the team meeting, not a Slack message, not a separate "AI check-in." The 1:1 is where behavior coaching happens. AI habits are behavior.

**When to bring it up:**
Not every week. Not as a regular agenda item. You bring it up when you've seen a pattern in the data (from your pipeline review prep or from direct observation) or when the rep surfaces it themselves.

The signal that makes it worth addressing: AI usage is creating a performance gap — either because the rep is over-using it (Week 3 archetype work applies here) or because peers are capturing time savings the rep is leaving on the table.

**How to open the conversation:**
Not: "I want to talk about how you're using AI."  
Yes: "I noticed [specific thing]. Help me understand what's going on there."

If a rep's outreach response rate has dropped 20% while their volume went up 40%, that's an observable pattern. You can name the pattern without naming the AI. "Your volume is up but your response rate is down. Tell me what's changed in your outreach approach."

If the rep explains they've been using AI to generate sequences and scaling up volume, you're now in a productive conversation — and the coaching is about response rate, not AI policy.

**What a productive outcome looks like:**
The rep leaves with a specific behavior to try or track — not a judgment about their AI usage. "Let's track your response rate alongside your volume for the next two weeks" is a coaching outcome. "Use AI less" is not.

**Coaching reps who are leaving time savings on the table:**
This is the AI-Avoider from Week 3 territory, but the 1:1 version is lower-stakes. You're not confronting avoidance — you're having a conversation about capacity.

"You're doing a lot manually. I've noticed [peers] getting their outreach done faster using AI tools. Have you experimented with any of them?"

If they say no: "What's getting in the way?"  
If they say they've tried and don't like the results: "Show me. Let's look at what you generated and figure out what's off."

Keep it curious. Keep it specific. Keep it about the result, not the tool.

### BLOCK 3 — Workshop (30 min)

**[35:00 – 65:00]**

**Round 1 — WS-06-02: Where's My Safety Gap (12 min)**

Pull up WS-06-02. Run it live — managers score each of their reps on all five Safety Pyramid levels.

*Facilitator prompt:* "For each rep, score 1-5 on each layer. Layer 1: Are they confident I'm consistent and reliable? Layer 2: Do they feel listened to? Layer 3: Do I acknowledge the human stuff? Layer 4: Are they genuinely vulnerable with me about what's hard? Layer 5: Do they feel safe enough to disagree with me?"

After scoring: "Which rep has the lowest overall safety score? That's the rep whose 1:1 you most need to redesign — and the one whose AI coaching conversation is most at risk of going sideways."

Brief share-out (2-3 volunteers): What layer is the gap? What's driving it?

*Andrew's add:* "Here's the AI wrinkle. If you walk into a low-safety 1:1 with a data briefing and start asking data questions, you will drive safety lower — not higher. The briefing is a tool for prepping questions. If the rep doesn't feel safe, lead with safety, not data."

---

**Round 2 — WS-06-05: Pick One Performance Gap + Coach Through It (10 min)**

Using the rep they identified with the lowest safety score (or a different rep if they prefer), managers run WS-06-05.

*Prompt:* "Pick one performance gap — AI-related or not. Write out: What's the specific behavior gap? What's the coaching question you'd ask? What does success look like at 30 days?"

If the performance gap is AI-related: apply the archetype diagnosis from Week 3. Which archetype? What's the coaching entry point?

If the performance gap is not AI-related: strip out the AI context entirely (per Week 3 Archetype 3 guidance) and coach the actual gap.

---

**Round 3 — WS-05-07 + WS-05-08: AI-Specific Signals to Watch (8 min)**

*New AI-specific signals to add to the standard WS-05-07 tracker:*

**Signal 1 — Drop in response rate on AI-assisted sequences**
A rep's outreach volume is stable or up, but their response and meeting-booked rates are declining. Usually means the AI is generating generic outreach and the rep isn't editing for specificity.
→ Coaching response: "Let's look at your open rate vs. response rate. What's changed in your outreach?"

**Signal 2 — Inconsistency between AI-generated meeting summaries and rep-reported deal status**
AI tool generates a deal summary that says "exploring options, no timeline established." Rep tells you in pipeline review: "This one's closing next month." One of those is wrong.
→ Coaching response: "I saw the AI summary on that deal. Walk me through where you actually are — I want to understand the discrepancy."

**Signal 3 — Sudden reversal to fully manual outreach**
A rep who was using AI tools has stopped completely — activity is still there, but volume is down and they're writing everything from scratch. Often signals a values shift or a frustrating experience with the tool they didn't raise.
→ Coaching response: "I noticed your outreach approach changed in the last few weeks. What's going on there?"

*Prompt for WS-05-07 update:* Add at least one of these three signals to your tracker. Which one is most relevant to your current team?

### BLOCK 4 — Role-Play + Close (25 min)

**[65:00 – 85:00] RP-06-01: The Guarded Rep**

Run in pairs. Andrew reads the setup:

> *You're walking into a 1:1 with a rep who used to be open with you but has been surface-level for the last three weeks. Performance is fine — 91% of quota. But something's off. She's giving you the "everything's fine" answers. She used to bring real problems to you. Your AI briefing shows no red flags: activity is normal, pipeline is healthy. But your gut says something's wrong.*
>
> *Your job: Open the conversation in a way that creates an opening for what's real — without interrogating her and without pretending you don't notice.*

Pairs run for 8 minutes. Rest of cohort observes and notes: What did the manager's opening create? Did the rep (playing the other person) feel an opening to be real?

Debrief (5 min): Where did AI data matter in that scenario? Where didn't it? The point: when a rep is guarded, no briefing helps. The only thing that helps is the opening question — and the only thing that generates the right opening question is knowing the person.

---

**[85:00 – 88:00] RP-06-04 quick scenario (optional if time permits)**

> *A rep left something unsaid two weeks ago. They started to say something real and stopped. You let it go. You've been thinking about it since. You're walking into this week's 1:1.*

Facilitator demonstrates: what does following up on an unsaid thing sound like? "Two weeks ago you started to say something about [topic] and stopped. I've been thinking about it. What was that?"

*The point:* the AI doesn't know about that moment. Your notes from last week do — if you kept them. The 1:1 format they're building tonight has a place for those notes.

## Facilitator Notes

### Common objections:
- **"My company doesn't use Gong or Clari. I don't have AI-generated briefings."** → The protocol works with any data. You're learning to separate data review (which any tool provides) from question generation (which is always human). If you're pulling a Salesforce report before 1:1s, you're already doing Step 1. This session teaches you how to use it better.
- **"My reps don't trust me enough for this kind of 1:1. Safety is low."** → That's the right diagnosis. The Safety Pyramid work in Round 1 is for you. The 1:1 format you're building in WS-W4-01 should reflect where your actual rep is on the pyramid — not where you wish they were.
- **"I feel like bringing up AI habits in the 1:1 makes it a surveillance conversation."** → Only if you open with a policy angle. "I want to talk about how you're using AI" is surveillance. "Your response rate is down — walk me through your outreach approach" is coaching. The framing is everything.

### Energy management:
- The Safety Pyramid round tends to generate the most honest conversation of the session — managers often haven't thought about safety in per-rep terms. Give it space.
- The Guarded Rep role-play is emotionally rich. If pairs are still in it at the 8-minute mark, let it go one more minute before pulling them out. The debrief is more important than strict timing.
- WS-W4-01 is light — managers should be able to draft their opening question in 60 seconds. Don't over-explain it; they know enough to fill it in.
