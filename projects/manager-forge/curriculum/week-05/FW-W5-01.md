# FW-W5-01 — AI Fluency as a Hiring Dimension

**Manager Forge — AI Spine | Week 5**  
**Type:** Framework (hiring diagnostic)  
**Use:** Facilitator reference + participant handout

---

## The Core Idea

AI fluency is not a resume line item. It's a set of habits — and habits are what you're screening for. The candidate who lists five AI tools they've used tells you almost nothing. The candidate who can explain when they override the AI output tells you everything.

Add AI fluency to your hiring framework as a **fourth dimension**, alongside capability, coachability, and fit. Not a replacement — an addition.

---

## What You're NOT Screening For

- Whether they've used specific tools (Gong, Clari, ChatGPT, Claude)
- Whether they can talk about AI convincingly
- Whether they have "AI" anywhere on their resume
- Current fluency level — this changes too fast to be a reliable hiring filter

**Why tool knowledge is insufficient:** The tools your team uses today will look different in 18 months. You're not hiring someone for the current toolset — you're hiring someone who can adapt as the toolset changes. Adaptability is a habit. Current knowledge is a credential.

---

## What You ARE Screening For: Three Habits

### Habit 1: Curiosity

Does this candidate experiment with AI — trying new workflows, pushing the edges of what the tool can do, failing and adjusting?

**What it looks like in a candidate:**
- Has tried things that didn't work and can tell you what they learned
- Gets interested in the *why* behind AI outputs, not just the output
- Has explored the tool beyond what they were shown in training
- Asks questions about your team's AI workflow during the interview

**What it doesn't look like:**
- Uses the same two workflows they were shown six months ago
- Describes AI usage as "I use it for email drafts" with no elaboration
- Has never had a failure with the tool (means they're not pushing it)

**Coachability lens:** A curious rep who's currently low-fluency will build strong habits. A non-curious rep who's currently fluent will plateau and resist change when the tools evolve.

---

### Habit 2: Judgment

Does this candidate think critically about AI output — editing it, questioning it, knowing when it's wrong?

**What it looks like in a candidate:**
- Describes making specific changes to AI-generated outputs before using them
- Can name something the AI consistently gets wrong in their workflow
- Has a point of view about where AI is unreliable
- Treats first output as a draft, not a deliverable

**What it doesn't look like:**
- "I use whatever it generates" — AI is using them
- Can't name anything they've edited or rejected
- Describes AI output as "usually pretty good" without specifics
- Hasn't thought about what the tool can't do

**The interview tell:** Ask them what they changed the last time they used AI for outreach. If they can't answer specifically, they accepted first output. That's a judgment gap.

---

### Habit 3: Discipline

Has this candidate decided *when not to use AI* — and can they explain why?

**What it looks like in a candidate:**
- Has a conscious process for when to go human vs. when to use AI
- Can name at least one workflow where they've decided AI doesn't belong
- The boundary is theirs, not just their company's policy
- They've thought about it, not defaulted

**What it doesn't look like:**
- "I use it for everything" — no discipline; high crutch risk
- "I never use it" — potential avoider; probe further
- "It depends on [vague factor]" — no real framework

**Why this matters more than fluency:** A rep with discipline can adapt to new tools without losing themselves in them. A rep without it will either over-rely on every new tool or reject every new tool. Both are management problems.

---

## Section 3: The AI Fluency Interview Probe

Add these three questions to the **skill assessment section** (Part 2) of your interview. They replace one of your existing skill probes or follow naturally after your standard role-play.

---

### Question 1 — Curiosity Probe

**"Walk me through how you currently use AI in your sales work. What have you tried that didn't work?"**

*Why the second half matters:* Every candidate will tell you what works. Only curious candidates can tell you what failed. No failures = not experimenting.

| Response | Score |
|---|---|
| Lists tools; can't name a failure | ⚠️ Yellow — uses AI but not experimenting |
| Names specific failures; describes what they learned | ✅ Green — curious, growth-oriented |
| "I don't really use AI yet" | Neutral — probe coachability separately: "What would it take to start?" |
| "I use it for everything, no real failures" | 🔴 Red — lacks candor or critical judgment |

**Follow-up if yellow or unclear:**  
*"If you were going to experiment with one new AI workflow in your first 90 days here, what would it be and why?"*

---

### Question 2 — Judgment Probe

**"Tell me about a time you used AI to prepare for a call. What did you ask it? What did you change before you used it? What did you ignore?"**

*Three-part design:* What they asked = whether they gave real context. What they changed = whether they critically evaluate output. What they ignored = whether they have opinions about the tool's limits.

| Response | Score |
|---|---|
| Describes generic prompt; used output as-is | ⚠️ Yellow — low judgment, high crutch risk |
| Describes specific context, specific edits, one thing discarded | ✅ Green — applies judgment |
| Prepared it themselves; didn't use AI | Neutral — probe coachability on adoption |
| "I just use whatever it generates" | 🔴 Red — AI is doing their job |

**Follow-up if yellow:**  
*"What's something AI consistently gets wrong when you use it for [specific task]?"*  
A candidate with judgment can always name this. A candidate without it says "it's pretty accurate."

---

### Question 3 — Discipline Probe

**"When do you NOT use AI in your sales process? Where have you decided it doesn't belong?"**

*The filter question:* Screens for whether they've made a conscious decision or just defaulted. The reasoning matters as much as the boundary.

| Response | Score |
|---|---|
| Can't name a boundary; "I use it everywhere" | 🔴 Red — no discipline; over-reliance risk |
| Names one specific boundary with clear reasoning | ✅ Green — deliberate process |
| "I don't really use AI" | Revisit — avoidance or values position? Ask: "What would change your mind?" |
| Names a boundary, then asks what you think | ✅✅ Strong green — curious AND self-aware |

**The one question that cuts through everything:**  
*"What would change your mind about using AI [more / differently] in your sales process?"*

If they can answer it specifically, they're coachable on AI — regardless of their current fluency level. If they can't, that's your answer.

## Section 4: AI Ramp Track for 30-60-90

Onboarding should include AI habit milestones — not mandates, structure for developing the habits you just screened for.

### Day 30 — Orientation and First Use

**Milestone:** Rep has been oriented to the team's AI toolset and has completed at least one end-to-end AI workflow (outreach drafting, pipeline prep, or call summary prep).

**Manager's role:** Do a 20-minute AI workflow walkthrough with the rep. Don't make them figure it out alone. Show them what you do, why you do it, what you've stopped doing.

**Watch for early signals:**
- Are they curious and experimenting? (green)
- Are they doing exactly what you showed them and nothing more? (neutral, watch)
- Are they not engaging with the tools at all? (early avoidance signal — name it)

### Day 60 — Consistent Process

**Milestone:** Rep has a consistent personal process for at least two workflows. Manager has reviewed one AI output with them — not to audit, to understand their process.

**Manager's role:** "Show me how you prepped for [recent call] using the AI. Walk me through what you asked, what you changed, what you sent." This is a coaching conversation, not an inspection.

**Apply the Week 3 archetypes:** If you're seeing over-reliance or avoidance signals, name them now. At 60 days, the habit window is still open. At 90 days, it's setting.

### Day 90 — Personal Framework

**Milestone:** Rep can articulate their personal AI framework: what they use it for, what they don't, why. They've experimented beyond what they were shown. No AI-related performance gap is going uncoached.

**Manager's role:** Ask directly in a 1:1: "How has your AI process evolved since you started? What are you doing differently now than day 30?" A rep who can answer that is building habits. A rep who can't may have defaulted.

---

## The Four-Dimension Hiring Summary

| Dimension | What you're assessing | AI-era addition |
|---|---|---|
| **Capability** | Can they do the job? | Can they do the job with AI as part of their toolkit? |
| **Coachability** | Do they take feedback and improve? | Are they coachable on AI habits specifically? |
| **Fit** | Will they make the team better? | Do their AI habits set a standard or lower one? |
| **AI Fluency** | *[new]* Curiosity, judgment, discipline — not tool knowledge | Screen with the three probe questions |

---

## One-Line Summary

*You're not hiring for current AI fluency. You're hiring for the three habits that predict whether they'll build and maintain it: curiosity, judgment, and discipline. Screen accordingly.*
