## Session Goal

By the end of this session, every manager has updated their interview process to screen for AI fluency as a fourth hiring dimension — alongside capability, coachability, and fit. They know what good-with-AI and using-AI-as-a-crutch look like before the interview happens, and they have an AI ramp track to add to their 30-60-90 onboarding plan.

**The shift:** Stop treating AI fluency as a resume line item ("has experience with AI tools") and start screening for the three actual habits that predict whether a rep will thrive in an AI-enabled environment: curiosity, judgment, and discipline.

## 90-Minute Run of Show

---

### BLOCK 1 — Opening Frame (15 min)

**[0:00 – 5:00] The specific mistake managers are making right now**

Here's the version of AI hiring that's happening everywhere, and it's producing bad results in both directions.

**Version 1 — The AI Checkbox:**  
Manager sees "ChatGPT" and "AI tools" on a resume. Thinks: this candidate is AI-fluent. Moves them forward. Doesn't probe it further. Hires them. Three months in, discovers the candidate used AI to write their resume and their cover letter and their demo prep — and has no ability to apply judgment to AI outputs, no discipline around when to edit and when to send, and no curiosity about what else the tools can do. They're an AI consumer, not an AI thinker.

**Version 2 — The AI Skeptic:**  
Manager doesn't screen for AI at all. Hires exactly the same way they did three years ago. Brings in a rep who is talented on the fundamentals but has never had to build habits around AI tools — no curiosity, no experimentation. Three months in, that rep is watching peers generate twice the outreach volume in half the time while they write every email from scratch. By month six, they're behind and frustrated and not sure what to do about it.

Both of these managers hired for 2022. You're hiring for 2026.

---

**[5:00 – 10:00] The reverse mistake: making AI fluency the only filter**

Before we get to the framework, a warning.

AI fluency is the fourth dimension, not the first. A candidate who can run impressive AI workflows but can't hold a discovery conversation is not a rep you want on your team. A candidate who mentions Gong and Claude and has built outreach templates in HubSpot but doesn't understand why prospects buy — that's an AI-fluent rep with a ceiling.

The frame for today: AI fluency predicts adaptability in a role that's changing. It doesn't replace the fundamentals of the role. You're adding a screen, not swapping one.

*Andrew's line:* "I've hired people who mentioned AI in every answer. I've hired people who had never thought about it. The difference that mattered most wasn't the tool knowledge — it was who got curious when the tools didn't work, who thought critically about the output, and who had the discipline to edit before they sent."

---

**[10:00 – 15:00] What you're actually screening for (preview)**

Pull up FW-W5-01. Three things. Not tool knowledge — habits.

1. **Curiosity** — Do they try things, experiment, get interested in how the tool works?
2. **Judgment** — Do they think critically about AI output, or do they accept what they're given?
3. **Discipline** — Do they have a process for when to use AI vs. when to go human? Or do they swing between all-in and all-out?

Current fluency level is almost irrelevant. What you're hiring for is whether they can develop and maintain AI habits — because the tools are changing faster than any hiring rubric we could write tonight. Coachability on AI is more predictive than their current toolset.

We're going to build the probe questions together in the workshop.

### BLOCK 2 — FW-W5-01 Walkthrough (15 min)

**[15:00 – 30:00] AI Fluency as a Hiring Dimension**

Screen share FW-W5-01. Walk through each section with commentary.

*Key emphasis points:*

**On the three habits vs. tool knowledge:**  
"If I ask a candidate 'what AI tools do you use?' and they list five of them, I've learned almost nothing. If I ask 'tell me about a time the AI gave you something that felt off — what did you do?' and they can't answer, I've learned a lot."

**On the coachability lens:**  
The highest-value AI hire is not the candidate who already knows everything. It's the candidate who is genuinely curious, questions the output, and is willing to try and fail with a new tool. That's the same coachability you're screening for everywhere else — it just has a specific expression in the AI context.

**On the "using AI as a crutch" signal in interviews:**  
Some candidates will tell you everything they do with AI. The signal isn't volume — it's whether they can articulate the human judgment they're applying on top of it. "I use AI for all my prospecting emails" is a yellow flag. "I use AI to generate a first draft and then I rewrite the opening every time because that's where personalization matters most" is a green flag.

**On AI ramp as onboarding infrastructure:**  
The 30-60-90 doesn't end at "close 3 deals by day 90." It now includes: "is using AI for X workflows by day 30, has experimented with Y by day 60, has a consistent personal process by day 90." That's not a mandate — it's structure for developing the habits you just screened for.

---

### BLOCK 3 — The AI Fluency Interview Probe (10 min)

**[30:00 – 40:00] Three questions, one rubric**

*Walk through the probe questions from FW-W5-01 Section 3. Read each question aloud, then score the sample answers.*

**Question 1 — Curiosity:**  
*"Walk me through how you currently use AI in your sales work. What have you tried that didn't work?"*

Why the second half matters: the first half gives you current usage. The second half tells you whether they experiment. A candidate who has never tried something that failed hasn't explored the edges of the tool. They're using it safely, not curiously.

| Response type | Score |
|---|---|
| Lists tools; can't name a failure | ⚠️ Yellow — uses AI but not experimenting |
| Names specific failures; describes what they learned | ✅ Green — curious, growth-oriented |
| "I don't really use AI that much" | 🔴 Red — may be an avoider; probe further |
| "I use it for everything, no failures" | 🔴 Red — lacks critical judgment or candor |

**Question 2 — Judgment:**  
*"Tell me about a time you used AI to prepare for a call. What did you ask it? What did you change before you used it? What did you ignore?"*

This question has three parts by design. What they asked tests whether they gave the AI real context. What they changed tests whether they critically evaluate output. What they ignored tests whether they have opinions about the tool's limits.

| Response type | Score |
|---|---|
| Describes generic prompt; used it as-is | ⚠️ Yellow — low judgment |
| Describes specific context, edits made, one thing they discarded | ✅ Green — applying judgment |
| "I prepared it all myself; didn't use AI" | Neutral — probe coachability on AI adoption separately |
| "I just use whatever it generates" | 🔴 Red — AI is using them |

**Question 3 — Discipline:**  
*"When do you NOT use AI in your sales process? Where have you decided it doesn't belong?"*

This is the filter question. A candidate who has thought about when not to use AI has a framework. A candidate who uses it for everything — or nothing — doesn't. What you're looking for: they've actually made the decision, not just defaulted.

| Response type | Score |
|---|---|
| Can't name a boundary; "I use it everywhere" | 🔴 Red — no discipline; high crutch risk |
| Names one specific boundary with clear reasoning | ✅ Green — deliberate process |
| "I don't really use AI" | Revisit — is this avoidance or a genuine values position? |
| Names a boundary, then asks what you think about it | ✅✅ Strong green — curious AND self-aware |

*Andrew's close on this block:* "You're not looking for the candidate who gets every answer right. You're looking for the candidate who has thought about these questions. Thinking about them is the signal."

### BLOCK 4 — Workshop (30 min)

**[40:00 – 70:00]**

**Round 1 — RP-09-01: The Sarah Interview — AI Section Added (12 min)**

Andrew plays Sarah. Cohort members rotate through running the AI probe questions from FW-W5-01 Section 3.

Setup: "Sarah is a strong candidate — 3 years in SaaS sales, solid quota attainment, great energy. She mentioned she uses 'a lot of AI tools' in her opening. You're now in the skill assessment section of the interview. Run the AI probe questions."

Rotate in 2-3 cohort members. Andrew responds as Sarah would — sometimes confidently (green), sometimes with the kind of vague enthusiasm that sounds good but reveals nothing. After each rotation, debrief: what did you learn? What would you probe differently?

*Key coaching moment:* When a candidate gives a green-sounding answer, follow up: "Can you give me a specific example?" Specificity separates genuine habit from interview performance.

**Round 3 — WS-09-05: AI Ramp Track for 30-60-90 (8 min)**

Managers open WS-09-05 (Build 30-60-90 Day Onboarding Plan).

Task: Add AI ramp milestones to their existing onboarding structure.

*Facilitator prompt — suggested milestones to adapt:*

**Day 30 milestone:**  
Rep has completed orientation to the team's AI toolset (Gong, Clari, or equivalent). Has used AI for at least one end-to-end workflow: outreach drafting, pipeline prep, or call summary. Has not been left to figure it out alone — manager has done a 20-minute AI workflow walkthrough.

**Day 60 milestone:**  
Rep has a consistent personal process for at least two workflows. Manager has reviewed one AI output with them — not to audit, to understand their process. Any early signals of over-reliance or avoidance (from Week 3 archetypes) have been named, not filed away.

**Day 90 milestone:**  
Rep can articulate what they use AI for, what they don't, and why. They've experimented with at least one workflow beyond what they were shown. No performance gap traced to AI habits is going uncoached.

Managers write in their specific milestones for their role/stack. 2-3 volunteers share one milestone they're adding.

### BLOCK 5 — Red Flag Marcus + Close (20 min)

**[70:00 – 88:00] RP-09-02: Red Flag Marcus — New AI Flag Added**

Run as a group. Andrew reads the setup:

> *Marcus is interviewing for a rep role on your team. He has 4 years of experience, strong close rates, and a portfolio of big-name logos. In the first 10 minutes, he's impressive.*
>
> *Then:*
>
> *Flag 1 — When asked about a deal he lost, he blames a product gap. No ownership.*
>
> *Flag 2 — When role-playing a discovery call, he presents the product before asking the third question.*
>
> *Flag 3 — When asked about his current manager, he describes them as "not very strategic."*
>
> *Flag 4 — When asked about AI in his sales process: "Honestly, I think it's kind of overhyped. I prefer to do real selling. Relationships close deals, not tools."*
>
> *Which flags are hard stops? Which are worth a follow-up conversation?*

Group discussion (8 min): Go through each flag. Andrew facilitates.

The AI flag specifically: this one's nuanced. "AI is overhyped" from a confident, experienced rep is different from "I've tried it and it doesn't help me." The first is a values position that may evolve. The second is actually worth probing: what did they try, what didn't work, what would change their mind?

The hard stops in this interview: Flag 1 (no ownership on lost deals — uncoachable signal) and Flag 2 (presenting before qualifying — fundamental sales process gap). Flags 3 and 4 are conversations, not exits — but they require follow-up.

*Andrew's take:* "Marcus is actually a useful interview. The AI response is the most interesting flag because it tells you the most about coachability. Someone who says 'I prefer real selling' might mean 'I haven't learned yet' — coachable. Or it might mean 'I've decided and I'm not changing' — not coachable. One more question tells you which."

What's the one more question? "What would change your mind about using AI in your process?"

If they can't answer it, that's your answer.

## Facilitator Notes

### Common objections:
- **"My company doesn't have AI tools yet. This feels premature."** → You're hiring for a role that's going to require these habits in 12-18 months whether the company has deployed the tools or not. You're screening for curiosity and coachability — those matter regardless of the current toolset.
- **"What if the best candidate has no AI experience at all?"** → That's probably fine — if they're curious, coachable, and can articulate genuine interest in learning. Curiosity is the signal, not current fluency. The ramp track handles the rest.
- **"I feel like I'm being asked to penalize candidates who haven't had AI in their previous role."** → You're not penalizing the absence of fluency. You're rewarding the presence of curiosity and judgment. Those can show up in someone who has zero AI tool experience. "I've been curious about it but haven't had a chance to use it — here's what I'd try" is a green.

### Energy management:
- The Sarah interview role-play (Round 1) is where the session comes alive — give it the full 12 minutes. Cohort members need multiple reps to feel the difference between a green and a yellow answer.
- Red Flag Marcus is the best closer in the program — the room always has opinions. The AI flag is deliberately ambiguous so it generates real debate. Let it run.
- WS-W5-01 is lighter than the previous worksheets — managers only need 90 seconds to identify their one concrete change. Don't over-explain it in the room; the Skool post is where the reflection happens.
