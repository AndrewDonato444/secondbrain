# FW-W6-01 — AI Communication Framework for Your Team

**Manager Forge — AI Spine | Week 6**  
**Type:** Framework (process document)  
**Use:** Facilitator reference + participant handout

---

## The Core Idea

The fastest way to lose your team to AI anxiety is to say nothing about it. The fastest way to lose your credibility is to give one speech and move on as if that was enough.

What works is a process — three phases over 4-8 weeks that build clarity, set expectations, and create feedback loops. Not a one-time conversation. Not a policy. A process your team can feel.

---

## What This Is Not

- A company-wide AI policy (that's not yours to write)
- A one-time team meeting speech ("AI is an opportunity, not a threat")
- A mandate for how reps use tools
- A performance review category called "AI adoption"

What this is: how you talk to your team about AI change over time, so the anxiety is named, the expectations are clear, and the feedback loops exist.

---

## Phase 1 — Acknowledge What You Know and Don't Know

**When:** Week 1 of your communication process. One-on-one or small group setting — not a big announcement.

**What you're doing:** Naming the elephant before it gets bigger. Not with certainty — with honesty.

**The language:**

> "I want to be upfront about where we are with AI on our team.
>
> Here's what I know: [specific tools the team is using, what they do, how we're using them right now].
>
> Here's what I genuinely don't know: [how the role will evolve over the next year, what leadership is planning at the company level, what the timeline looks like for any bigger changes].
>
> Here's my commitment: I'm going to keep you informed as I learn more — not just when things are settled, but as I'm figuring it out. And I want to know what questions you're carrying. I'd rather work through this together than have you fill in the blanks with the worst-case version."

**Why this works:**

- It positions you as honest, not omniscient
- It draws a clear line between what's real and what's speculation
- It invites dialogue — the rep can now bring their actual question instead of guessing whether it's safe to ask
- It signals that you're not hiding anything

**What you're listening for in their response:**

If a rep asks a specific question ("are they going to automate our outreach?"), that's the real anxiety. Answer it directly and honestly, including "I don't know" if that's true.

If a rep goes quiet, follow up in the next 1:1: "After what I shared last week — anything still sitting with you?"

**What NOT to do:**

- "Everything is fine, don't worry about it" — shuts down the conversation
- "AI will never replace what you do" — you can't promise that, and they know it
- Calling a big team meeting for this phase — you want dialogue, not an audience

---

## Phase 2 — Set Clear Expectations About What "Good AI Use" Looks Like on Your Team

**When:** Week 2-3. A team meeting, or woven into your regular 1:1 rhythm over several conversations.

**What you're doing:** Defining the standards so reps don't have to guess. Removing the ambiguity that makes AI feel like a minefield.

**The language:**

> "I want to be clear about how I think about AI on our team — what good looks like, and what I'll coach when I see the alternative.
>
> Good AI use looks like: using the tool for the first pass, adding your judgment to the output, personalizing it to the actual person and situation, and tracking whether it's working. The AI helps you move faster and think better. You're still the one deciding.
>
> Lazy AI use looks like: sending the first output without reading it, using sequences to avoid real conversations, or letting the tool make the judgment call you're supposed to make. I'll coach that when I see it — not as a punishment, as a conversation.
>
> Here's what's off-limits on our team: [any specific use cases your company or team has defined — e.g., no AI-generated deal updates that the rep hasn't verified, no AI-generated cold calls without human review, etc.].
>
> The standard isn't about how much you use it. It's about whether you're using it or whether it's using you."

**Why this works:**

- Removes ambiguity — reps no longer have to guess what you'll reward or penalize
- Makes the standard about judgment, not compliance
- Gives reps a framework they can self-assess against
- Positions the manager as a coaching partner, not a monitor

**What to do if a rep pushes back:**

If a rep says "I don't think AI belongs in real selling" — that's the AI-Avoider position from Week 3. You're not going to win a debate. Instead: "Tell me more about what concerns you. What specifically feels like it doesn't belong?" Then find one workflow where the tool serves the thing they care about. Let them experience it.

---

## Phase 3 — Build Feedback Loops

**When:** Ongoing, starting in week 3-4 and continuing through the end of the quarter.

**What you're doing:** Creating a mechanism for the team to raise concerns, surface what's working, and share experiments — without having to wait for you to bring it up.

**The mechanisms:**

**1. A standing 1:1 question (2 minutes):**  
"What's working in your AI workflow right now? What's not?"  
Ask it every 2-3 weeks. Not every week — that's surveillance. Every 2-3 weeks — that's a feedback loop.

**2. A "show your work" moment in team meetings (5 minutes):**  
Rotate through the team: one rep per meeting shares something they tried with AI — works, didn't work, weird result, unexpected use case. Not a performance. A learning moment. The first time you do this, demonstrate it yourself. "Here's something I tried this week that didn't work the way I expected."

**3. An explicit invitation for the edge cases:**  
"If AI creates a situation you're not sure how to handle — a tool doing something weird, a question about whether something crosses a line, something you're seeing on another team — bring it to me. I'd rather hear about it early."

**Why this works:**

- The manager is no longer the only one responsible for surfacing AI problems — the team is participating
- It normalizes failure and experimentation, not just successful AI use
- It gives you ongoing intelligence about what's actually happening on the team, not just what the data shows
- It builds the culture around AI rather than implementing a policy about it

**What to do when something surfaces:**

When a rep brings you an edge case (a peer misusing a tool, a sequence that generated a complaint, a tool output that was factually wrong), treat it as a coaching moment, not a compliance event. Ask questions. Figure out what happened. Share what you learned with the team.

## The Fairness Question — Three Scenarios

When AI creates a visible performance gap between reps, you'll face questions about fairness. The right response depends entirely on which scenario you're in.

---

### Scenario 1: Gap Due to Effort

*One rep invested in learning the tools. Others didn't. The gap is driven by effort.*

**This is a performance issue, not a fairness issue.**

The move: coach the reps who haven't adapted, set expectations with timeline, give support to close the gap. Celebrate the rep who invested — but make the behavior (learning the tool, building the habits) the thing you celebrate, not the person.

**Do not:** Cap the high-fluency rep's performance to make others feel better. That's penalizing exactly the behavior you want. It will not go unnoticed by that rep, and it will not go unnoticed by the team.

---

### Scenario 2: Gap Due to Access

*One rep has a better tool, faster hardware, or a background that gave them a head start. Others are working with less.*

**This is a systems issue, not a coaching issue.**

The move: advocate upward using the Inform-Advocate-Execute framework.

"I'm seeing a tools access gap on my team that's creating a performance differential I can't coach my way out of. Here's what the data shows. Here's what I think we need. Here's what I'd recommend and the timeline."

Then execute within the constraints while you advocate. Document what you raised and when. If leadership doesn't change it, you've created a record that you named it — and you protect yourself from accountability for a gap that isn't yours to own.

---

### Scenario 3: Gap Due to Natural Talent Variance

*Some reps are genuinely better at using AI tools — more curious, faster to adapt, better at iteration. The gap reflects real variance in this skill.*

**This is the hardest scenario — and it has no clean answer.**

The move: coach reps who can close the gap (most of them can, with support and time), accept that some variance will persist (it always does with any skill), and adjust expectations proportionately for reps who have strong fundamentals but lower AI aptitude — the same way you'd handle any skill gap that's real but not disqualifying.

The move you avoid: treating all three scenarios as the same problem. They need different responses.

**When a rep asks "is this fair?":**

They're almost never asking about the tool. They're asking whether you see them. Whether you're going to help them close the gap or let them fall behind. Answer that question: "I'm not letting you fall behind. Here's what I'm going to do."

## Managing Up About AI

Three situations you'll face. One framework for all three.

**The Inform-Advocate-Execute Framework (from Module 02):**
1. **Inform** — Give leadership your honest read on what you're seeing, with data
2. **Advocate** — Share your recommendation, clearly and specifically
3. **Execute** — Once a decision is made, execute it fully — including decisions you disagreed with

---

**Situation 1: Leadership mandates an AI tool you think is wrong.**

Inform: "Here's what I'm observing with my team that makes me concerned about this rollout — [specific data point, not vague resistance]."  
Advocate: "Here's what I'd recommend instead: [alternative, timeline, or modified version]."  
Execute: If they proceed, roll it out without undermining it. Your credibility depends on your team seeing you execute with integrity, not broadcast your disagreement.

---

**Situation 2: Leadership asks you to report on AI adoption.**

Don't say "we're doing great." Give them specifics: what's been adopted, where the gaps are, what you're coaching, what you'd need to go faster. That's the answer of a manager who has data and is managing intentionally — which is exactly how you want to be positioned.

---

**Situation 3: No clear AI policy exists at your company.**

Name it upward: "We don't have clear standards around AI use on our team. I'm operating from my own framework, which I'm happy to share. I want to make sure I'm aligned with where we're heading."

That conversation puts you on record as thinking about it — ahead of the curve. Which is where you want to be.

---

## One-Line Summary

*Not a speech. A three-phase process: acknowledge what you know and don't know, set clear expectations, build feedback loops. Then handle the fairness question and advocate upward with precision.*
