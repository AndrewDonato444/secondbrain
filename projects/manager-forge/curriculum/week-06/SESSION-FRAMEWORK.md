# Week 6 — Session Framework
## "Building a Team That Doesn't Fear the Machine"

**Program:** Manager Forge — AI Spine  
**Session:** Week 6 of 6 — Capstone  
**Format:** Live, 90 minutes via Zoom  
**Date:** August 17 (or 24), 2026 — Cohort 1  
**Facilitator:** Andrew Donato

---

## Session Goal

By the end of this session, every manager has a concrete plan — not a speech — for leading their team through AI change without creating anxiety or false certainty. They understand the fairness question (what to do when AI creates a performance gap between reps), how to advocate for or against AI decisions above them, and they leave with their 30/90-day AI Leadership Roadmap completed. That roadmap is what they're accountable to at the check-in call.

**The capstone shift:** Everything from Weeks 1-5 has been about doing specific things differently — reviewing pipelines, running 1:1s, coaching archetypes, hiring. This session is about leading through change as a manager. The operating system is installed. Now we talk about culture.

**Takeaway artifact:** WS-W6-01 completed — "My 30/90-Day AI Leadership Roadmap"  
*(Week-by-week actions for month 1 + monthly themes for months 2-3 — this is the accountability document for the 30-day check-in call)*

## 90-Minute Run of Show

---

### BLOCK 1 — Opening Frame (15 min)

**[0:00 – 5:00] The poll read-back — and what it reveals**

Read the poll results. The pattern is almost always the same: most managers have sensed AI anxiety on their team even if no one has named it directly. A few have had explicit conversations. Almost no one has a structured approach to those conversations.

"Here's what I see: the anxiety is real, most of it is quiet, and most managers are doing one of two things — neither of which is working."

---

**[5:00 – 12:00] The two failure modes**

**Failure Mode 1: The Silence**

Manager says nothing about AI to the team. Doesn't bring it up. Assumes people will figure it out or that raising it will create more anxiety than it resolves.

What the team hears: "Our manager hasn't said anything about how AI will change our role. Either they don't know, or it's bad." Silence doesn't reduce anxiety — it fills with speculation. And the speculation is almost always worse than the reality.

The rep who is quietly disengaging from their tools? They're asking a question you're not answering. The rep who is over-relying on AI? They're trying to solve a problem you haven't named. The rep who pushes back in team meetings? They want to know if there's a plan.

**Failure Mode 2: The Speech**

Manager calls a team meeting. Gives a rallying talk: "AI is the future, we're embracing it, here's why it's an opportunity not a threat." Very convincing. Very sincere.

Two weeks later, nothing has changed. The speech didn't install anything. The team appreciated the enthusiasm, but they still don't know what "good AI use" looks like on this team, they still don't know what the manager will do if their AI-assisted output drops in quality, and they still don't know what to do with the anxiety they feel when the tool doesn't work the way they expected.

Speeches create a moment. Process creates change.

---

**[12:00 – 15:00] The third way: communication as a process, not an event**

FW-W6-01 is a three-phase process. Not a one-time conversation — a cadence over 4-8 weeks that builds clarity, sets expectations, and creates feedback loops.

"What you're building tonight is not a speech. It's a plan for how you talk to your team about AI change over time — what you say in week one, what you set up in week three, what you ask for in week six."

Pull up FW-W6-01.

---

### BLOCK 2 — AI Communication Framework (15 min)

**[15:00 – 30:00] FW-W6-01 walkthrough**

Screen share FW-W6-01. Walk through each phase with specific language.

**Phase 1 — Acknowledge what you know and don't know**

*When:* Week 1 of your communication process. One-on-one or small team setting, not a big announcement.

*What you're doing:* Naming the elephant in the room before it gets bigger. Not with certainty — with honesty.

*The language:*
"I want to be upfront about where we are with AI on our team. Here's what I know: [specific tools we're using, what they do, how we're using them]. Here's what I genuinely don't know: [how the role will evolve, what leadership is planning at the company level, what the timeline is]. I'm going to keep you informed as I learn more. And I want to know what questions you're carrying — because I'd rather we work through this together than have you fill in the blanks with the worst-case version."

*What this does:* It positions you as honest, not omniscient. It invites dialogue. It signals that you're not hiding anything. It's the opposite of the speech — it doesn't claim to have all the answers.

*Andrew's note:* "I have had this conversation with my team. The thing I didn't expect: half the questions I was afraid of answering weren't actually the questions they were asking. The anxiety was about something more specific — usually about one workflow that was changing or one tool they didn't understand yet. Asking beats guessing."

---

**Phase 2 — Set clear expectations about what "good AI use" looks like on your team**

*When:* Week 2-3. A team meeting, or added to your regular 1:1 rhythm.

*What you're doing:* Defining the standards so reps don't have to guess. What does using AI well look like here? What does using it badly look like? What's off-limits?

*The language:*
"I want to be clear about how I think about AI on our team. Good AI use looks like: [using tools to do the first pass, adding your judgment, personalizing the output, tracking whether it's working]. Lazy AI use looks like: [sending first output without editing, replacing real conversations with sequences, using it for things that require your judgment]. I'm going to coach the first type, and I'm going to name the second when I see it — not as a punishment, as a coaching conversation."

*What this does:* Removes ambiguity. Reps no longer have to guess whether you're going to reward their AI experiments or penalize them for "cheating." The standard is named.

---

**Phase 3 — Build feedback loops**

*When:* Ongoing. Built into 1:1s and team meetings.

*What you're doing:* Creating a mechanism for the team to raise concerns, questions, and experiments without having to wait for you to bring it up.

*The mechanisms:*
- A standing question in 1:1s: "What's working in your AI workflow right now? What's not?" (Takes 2 minutes. Creates information.)
- A "show your work" moment in team meetings: one rep per week shares something they tried with AI — works, didn't work, weird result. Not a performance — a learning.
- An explicit invitation: "If AI creates a situation you're not sure how to handle — a rep on another team doing something that feels like it should be a problem, a tool your company deployed that you think is being misused — bring it to me. I'd rather hear it early."

*What this does:* The manager is no longer the only one responsible for surfacing AI problems. The team is actively participating in building the culture around AI use. That's culture, not compliance.

### BLOCK 3 — The Fairness Question + Managing Up (15 min)

**[30:00 – 45:00]**

**[30:00 – 40:00] The Fairness Question**

*This is new territory — it's not in the archive, and it's where managers get stuck.*

You've done everything right. You've installed the AI-augmented pipeline review. You've coached the Over-Reliant rep. You've screened for AI fluency in your last hire. And now you have a rep who is genuinely exceptional at using AI tools — and their numbers are running 30% ahead of peers. The gap is real, it's visible, and the peers are noticing.

This is the fairness question: when AI creates a performance gap between reps, what do you do?

**Three scenarios — different diagnoses, different moves:**

*Scenario 1: Gap due to effort*
One rep invested in learning the tools. Others didn't. The gap is real and it's driven by effort differential.

This is a performance issue, not a fairness issue. Treat it as you would any other effort gap: coach the reps who haven't adapted, set expectations, give them time and support to close the gap. Celebrate the rep who invested — but make the behavior, not the person, the thing you're celebrating.

The wrong move: capping the high-AI-fluency rep to make others feel better. That's penalizing the behavior you want. Don't do it.

*Scenario 2: Gap due to access*
One rep has a better AI tool, a faster computer, a tech-forward company background. Others are working with less.

This is a systems issue. Your move is upward: advocate for equal access. "Our team has a tools access problem that's creating a performance gap I can't coach my way out of. Here's what I'm seeing and what I'm recommending."

Use the Inform-Advocate-Execute framework from Module 02. You inform leadership of what you're seeing. You advocate for what you think should change. If they don't change it, you execute within the constraints — but you've named the issue and created a record.

*Scenario 3: Gap due to natural talent variance*
Some reps are naturally better at using AI tools — more curious, faster to adapt, better at iteration. This is a talent dimension, not an effort or access problem.

This is the hardest scenario. The right answer is not one answer: you coach the reps who can close the gap (most of them can, with time and support), you accept that some gap will persist (it always does with any skill), and you adjust expectations for reps who have the fundamentals but not the AI aptitude — the same way you would for any skill gap that's real but manageable.

The wrong move: treating all three scenarios as the same problem. They're not.

*Andrew's note:* "The rep who asks 'is this fair?' is usually not asking about the tool. They're asking whether you see them. They're asking whether you're going to help them close the gap or just let them fall behind. That's a conversation about your relationship with them — and it belongs in a 1:1, not a team meeting."

---

**[40:00 – 45:00] Managing Up About AI**

*Quick section — based on the Inform-Advocate-Execute framework from Module 02.*

You will face one of three situations with AI and your leadership:

**Situation 1: Leadership wants you to implement an AI tool you think is wrong.**
Use Inform-Advocate-Execute. You execute — but not before you've informed leadership of your concerns and advocated for what you think is right. "I want to flag something before we roll this out. Here's what I'm seeing with my team that makes me think the timing is off / the tool is wrong for this use case / we need more ramp time. I'm not saying don't do it. I'm saying here's what I think we're missing."

Then — if they go forward — you execute without undermining it to your team. Your job is to make the decision work, not to be right.

**Situation 2: Leadership asks you to report on your team's AI usage.**
You have data now — pipeline reviews, coaching notes, 1:1 observations, the ramp track milestones from Week 5. Give them specific, honest information. Not "my team is doing great with AI" — that's a speech. Give them: "Here's what we've adopted, here's where the gaps are, here's what I'm coaching, here's what I'd need to go faster."

**Situation 3: Your company's AI policy is unclear or nonexistent.**
This is the most common situation right now. Name it up. "We don't have clear standards around AI use on the team. I'm operating from my own framework. I want to make sure that's aligned with where we're heading." That conversation puts you on record as thinking about it — which is exactly where you want to be.

### BLOCK 4 — Workshop (30 min)

**[45:00 – 75:00]**

**Round 1 — WS-10-01 + SA-10-02 Debrief: What's Your Actual Culture (8 min)**

Managers completed WS-10-01 (What's Your Actual Culture) and SA-10-02 (Demotivator Audit) in pre-work. Quick debrief:

*Prompt:* "What was the gap between your stated culture and your actual culture? What demotivator surprised you most?"

2-3 volunteers share. Andrew notes the pattern: AI anxiety almost always amplifies existing demotivators. Unfairness concerns get louder when AI creates visible performance gaps. Disconnection deepens when remote reps are excluded from AI tool rollouts. Lack of clarity about the future reads as "leadership doesn't have a plan."

**The sixth demotivator — AI uncertainty:**

Add this to WS-10-03 (Identify Demotivators):

*AI Uncertainty:* Reps don't know how AI changes their role, whether their job is at risk, or what "good AI use" looks like on this team. They're working with unresolved questions that affect their engagement, their willingness to experiment, and their tenure consideration.

*Behavioral indicators:* Reps avoiding AI tools without explaining why. Reps asking sideways questions about company direction ("what do you think happens when these tools get better?"). Reps who are normally candid going quiet in conversations about AI.

*The fix:* Phase 1 of FW-W6-01. Acknowledge what you know and don't know. Then set expectations (Phase 2) and build feedback loops (Phase 3). Not a speech — the three-phase process.

---

**Round 2 — SCRIPTS-02: Conversation Scripts for the Hard Moments (12 min)**

Pull Scripts 5 and 10 from the Module 02 script archive.

**Script 5 — Advocating against a decision:**  
Practice this specific framing for an AI mandate you'd push back on. Managers write out the key sentences before role-playing with a partner: "Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what I think it means. Here's what I'd recommend instead. I'm ready to execute either way — I just want to make sure you have the full picture."

**Script 10 — Executing a leadership decision your team won't like:**  
Practice this for rolling out an AI tool the team is skeptical of. Managers write out how they'd present it: not "leadership decided this and we're doing it" — that's a cop-out. "Here's what we're doing and why. Here's what I think is right about it. Here's the legitimate concern I raised with leadership and what they said. Here's what I need from you."

*Andrew's note:* "The rep who trusts you is the rep who knows you're honest about things you disagree with. If you just transmit every leadership decision without your own perspective, you're not leading — you're dispatching. Script 10 is how you execute with integrity."

Partners run each script for 3 minutes. Debrief: What felt authentic? What felt like a cop-out?

---

**Round 3 — RP-10-01 or RP-10-02 (10 min)**

Facilitator choice based on energy in the room:

**Option A — RP-10-01: Rep Thinking of Leaving**  
The rep who comes to you and says "I'm not sure this is the right place for me anymore." AI anxiety is the subtext — but you don't know that yet. Run the conversation. Where does AI come up? Where doesn't it?

**Option B — RP-13-04: Leadership Asks About Your AI Investment**  
Leadership calls you to ask how your team is using AI and what the ROI looks like. You've been coaching it, building the protocols, running the frameworks. Can you articulate it in a way that builds your credibility instead of underselling it?

Run in pairs, 5 minutes, then full group debrief.

### BLOCK 5 — Capstone: WS-W6-01 + Program Through-Line (15 min)

**[75:00 – 90:00]**

**[75:00 – 83:00] WS-W6-01: The 30/90-Day AI Leadership Roadmap**

Managers open WS-W6-01. Fill in live — this is not homework.

*Facilitator prompt for the 30-day section:*  
"Week 1 of your roadmap: what specifically changes? Not 'I'll use AI more' — what meeting, what 1:1, what pipeline review looks like different because of what you built in this program?"

*Facilitator prompt for months 2-3:*  
"Month 2 theme: pick one. Culture (Phase 1 communication process). Coaching (one archetype you're going to close the gap on). Hiring (the update to your next interview). What's the one place you're going to invest attention?"

*Note:* This document is what they're accountable to at the 30-day check-in call. Andrew will read them. Be specific enough that you can actually check against them.

2-3 managers share one thing from their week-1 actions. Andrew gives brief feedback: specific enough, or needs sharpening?

---

**[83:00 – 90:00] The through-line — from Week 1 to tonight**

*Do not skip this. It's the emotional close of the program.*

"Six weeks ago, we started with a question: what's actually in your job description now? You wrote out three irreplaceable judgment calls. You named what AI owns and what you own.

Since then you've:
- Redesigned your pipeline review so the AI does the first pass and you do the synthesis
- Built a coaching framework for three specific AI-related failure modes you're going to see in your reps
- Redesigned your 1:1 so the briefing closes before the conversation opens
- Updated your interview process with four hiring dimensions and an AI ramp track
- And tonight — you have a communication plan for leading your team through the change that's already happening

That's not AI fluency. That's an operating system. The operating system for the AI-enabled sales manager.

The job description you wrote in Week 1 is more true now than when you wrote it. You're the person in the room who knows what the tools can do and knows what they can't. You're the one who synthesizes the data into coaching. You're the one who reads what's not in the report.

You're still the manager. You're just operating from 2026 now."

Pause. Ask for 1-2 final reflections: what's different about how you see your job compared to six weeks ago?

Close: "I'll see you at the 30-day check-in. Bring your roadmap and tell me what happened."

## Facilitator Notes

### Tone guidance for the capstone:
- This session should feel different from Weeks 1-5. Less teaching-heavy, more reflective. More space for the cohort to speak.
- The workshop blocks can breathe a little. If SCRIPTS-02 is generating great conversation, let it run past 12 minutes.
- The through-line close is the most important 7 minutes of the program. Don't rush it. Don't let it become a list of accomplishments. Let it land.

### Common final-session moments:
- Managers will have things they wish they'd done differently in the program (a 1:1 they ran wrong, a coaching conversation they avoided). Acknowledge it: "That's the point of the 30-day window. You now know what to go fix."
- Some managers will want to talk about what comes after — "what's next after this program?" Plant the seed for Manager Forge alumni community and any future cohorts, but keep the focus on the roadmap.
- The "Rep Thinking of Leaving" role-play almost always generates the most emotional conversation in the room. Let it.

### Energy management:
- Block 2 is conceptually dense (three scenarios for the fairness question + managing up). Keep it moving with concrete examples rather than framework recitation.
- The 30/90-day roadmap fill-in should be done in the session — don't leave it as homework. Managers who don't fill it in during the session rarely submit it before the check-in call.
- Save 7 full minutes for the through-line close. It's the close that makes the program feel complete.
