## Post 1 — "The AE role is quietly splitting in two"

**Target post date:** Mid-May

---

Something I've been chewing on and don't fully have my arms around yet.

The AE role is quietly splitting in two and nobody's renamed either half.

On one side there's a rep who's basically an orchestrator — runs five AI tools, moves three times as many deals through the top of the funnel as they used to, optimizes for activity. The numbers look great in weeks one through six.

On the other side there's a rep who does fewer things but can actually hold a conversation a buyer didn't expect. Pushes back. Reads a room. Names a thing the buyer hadn't named yet. Those deals close at a different rate, but they're slower to ramp and harder to count.

For a while I thought those were two stages of one rep — like the orchestrator matures into the second one. I'm less sure now. I think they might be different jobs, and we're running the same comp plan and the same coaching on both, and that's why half the coaching conversations I've watched this quarter have felt off.

I don't know what the org chart fix is. Probably not a clean one. Maybe the fix is that managers have to run two different playbooks on the same team and stop pretending it's one role.

What are you seeing? Am I reading this wrong?

## Post 2 — "Pipeline review as a management trust exercise"

**Target post date:** Late May

---

The pipeline review is one of the most honest management tests I know of, and almost nobody treats it that way.

Here's what I mean. If your reps are using AI for anything real, they're showing up to pipeline review with deal health scores, probability ratings, summaries of every recent interaction. Good — that's the job. But the manager then has two options.

Option one: the manager reads the summary, accepts the rating, nods, moves on. Meeting takes 40 minutes, everyone feels productive, and literally nothing got interrogated.

Option two: the manager reads the summary and asks one specific version of "why" that the AI can't answer — why is this champion actually championing, what's the political situation we're not seeing, what's the thing this rep hasn't said out loud yet.

Option one is a status meeting dressed up in AI. Option two is coaching.

The uncomfortable part: I've done option one more than I'd like to admit. Usually when I'm tired or behind on something else. The AI summary is the easiest thing in the world to nod at. It looks like rigor. It's not.

I don't think the answer is "don't use AI summaries" — that's a pointless fight. I think the answer is that the question at the end of the review got more important, not less. And most of us didn't update our question.

## Post 3 — "Hiring for the thing you can't list in the JD"

**Target post date:** Early-to-mid June

---

I rewrote one of my interview questions this quarter and I'm still not sure it's right.

The old question was some version of "tell me about a time you figured something out without a playbook." Fine question. Answers were getting generic — people have learned how to tell that story.

The new question is more specific: "Walk me through the last time you used an AI tool for something at work and it gave you the wrong answer. What did you do next?"

What I learned — and I wasn't expecting this — is that the question isn't really about AI. It's about whether the person treats a tool's output as input or as conclusion. That's a completely different human than the one who shows up on the resume. And you can't screen for it with a skills list, because the people who can't operate in this environment often have the skills. They just trust the wrong things.

I don't have a clever framework for this yet. I'm just watching my own pattern and noticing that the candidates who've actually fought with an AI tool and have a specific story about it are the ones I end up wanting on the team. The ones who talk about AI as "super helpful" without a specific story are almost always going to be a problem in month three.

Maybe this generalizes, maybe it doesn't. I'm running it on every interview this quarter and seeing what comes out.
