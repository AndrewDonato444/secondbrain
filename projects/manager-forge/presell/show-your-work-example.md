## Part 1: My Three Irreplaceable Judgment Calls

**Judgment Call 1:**

What was the decision?
> Pulled one of my reps off a $180k deal three weeks into the cycle and moved the account to another rep. Original rep pushed back — his deal scoring and AI deal-health tool both said the deal was tracking fine.

Why couldn't AI have made it? (What did you know that the data didn't show?)
> The champion on the buyer side had gone quiet in a specific way I'd seen twice before — they were still responsive on email but had stopped forwarding us to anyone new. Deal health was reading the email cadence and scoring it green. I'd watched the same pattern kill two deals last year. The data didn't have the pattern; I did.

---

**Judgment Call 2:**

What was the decision?
> Told a senior rep I wasn't going to approve the discount structure he'd prepped for a QBR renewal, even though the AI-generated pricing recommendation landed inside our historical discount band.

Why couldn't AI have made it?
> The customer had been in an acquisition rumor cycle for six weeks. The pricing tool didn't know that. If we locked in a three-year at a steep discount right before they got acquired, we'd be eating that margin through the new entity's renegotiation. The judgment call was about timing context the tool couldn't see.

---

**Judgment Call 3:**

What was the decision?
> Kept a rep on my team who'd had two bad quarters in a row, against the recommendation of a performance dashboard that flagged him as at-risk.

Why couldn't AI have made it?
> The rep had been carrying a territory transition plus a personal situation he'd told me about in confidence. The dashboard saw quota attainment. I saw a rep who'd kept showing up, kept his forecast accuracy high, and was going to bounce once the transition closed. He did — put up the biggest quarter of anyone on the team the next cycle.

---

## Part 2: My Biggest AI Delegation Opportunity

**The task I'm going to stop doing manually:**
> Drafting the first version of the weekly forecast commentary to leadership. I've been writing a two-paragraph summary from scratch every Friday — reading the deal movement, looking for the trend, writing the narrative. Roughly 45 minutes, sometimes more.

**How much time it takes me per week:** ~45 minutes, sometimes 70 if a deal moved weirdly

**What I'll use instead (tool or approach):**
> Claude, fed the week's pipeline delta export plus the last three weeks of my own commentary as context. Goal is a first draft in my voice that I can edit in 10 minutes instead of write from scratch in 45.

**What I'll do with the time I get back:**
> I'm going to spend the extra 30 minutes a week listening to one additional call per rep. Not the whole call — the 10 minutes around the moment the rep got pushed back on. That's the piece of coaching I've been skipping because forecast commentary was eating the time.

---

## Part 3: One Thing I'm Stopping Manually This Week

**The specific thing:**
> Writing the weekly forecast commentary from scratch. Starting the coming Friday (04-17).

**What the AI-enabled version looks like:**
> Friday morning: I export the pipeline delta report, drop it into a Claude prompt that already has my tone and the last three weeks of commentary loaded. I get a draft in 60 seconds. I read it, mark what's wrong or what's missing, rewrite the weak parts. Send it to leadership by 11am instead of 3pm.

**How I'll know it worked:**
> Three tests. One, total time under 15 minutes. Two, leadership doesn't ask clarifying questions the old version would've pre-empted (means the draft held the judgment, not just the data). Three, I actually use the time I got back to listen to the calls instead of letting the day absorb it. Honest measure on that third one is the hardest — I'll set a recurring Friday afternoon block on the calendar before I know for sure.

## My New Job Description (One Sentence)

*In your own words. What does "being a good manager" mean now?*

> Being a good manager now means spending less time gathering the picture and more time interrogating it — fewer status updates, more pattern recognition, and a real read on each person on my team that no dashboard is going to have.

## Judgment notes on this example (not for participants — internal)

- The three judgment calls are real-shape examples drawn from Andrew's TrackForce context (VP of Sales, ~10 reps, SaaS). Names and deal sizes generalized. If any of them are too close to a specific live deal to post publicly, swap the specifics before going live — the structure and honesty level is what matters, not the exact details.
- Part 2 is deliberately small (forecast commentary, not something showy). The modeling intent is "your delegation opportunity should be a real weekly task, not an ambitious rebuild." If Andrew's actual Part 2 is bigger, keep this smaller version for the example and use the real one in his own work.
- The "How I'll know it worked" answer is intentionally three-part because the worksheet field rewards specificity and participants need to see what that looks like. Two-line answers won't teach them.
