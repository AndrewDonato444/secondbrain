# Manager Forge — Program Architecture
*Designed: 2026-04-21*

---

## Program Overview

Manager Forge is a 6-week cohort for AI-enabled sales managers — SaaS and software managers who know their job is changing but haven't built the operating system for it yet. The program runs on two tracks: a live AI Spine (6 sessions, 90 min each) where AI is the operating context, not a feature or a bolt-on, and a Foundations Library of the best async content from the existing 13-module archive, available as pre-work and reference throughout. Andrew's credibility is that he builds the AI systems himself — AutoLinkedIn, ScrollProxy, MSA platform — and manages a sales team with them in production. Every session is taught from inside the work, not from the sidelines.

### Week 1: Your Job Changed. Here's the New Job Description.

**Thesis:** Most sales managers are still running a manual operating system inside an AI-shaped world — the anxiety comes from the gap, and the cure is clarity about what the job actually is now.

**The shift:** The manager leaves with a written definition of their new value proposition — the specific judgment calls, synthesis moves, and human reads that AI cannot do — and has articulated it out loud to peers. They stop managing from anxiety and start managing from a clear north star.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew opens with the CRM literacy analogy from Module 13 — the shift from "exotic skill" to table stakes. He names what's actually happening: not replacement, but role compression. Quick show-of-hands audit: what did you do this week that AI could have done? What did you do that AI couldn't?
- Main teaching (30 min): The Both/And framework from Module 13 as the operating philosophy. "Where Humans Still Win" (judgment on deal health, synthesis, prompt engineering, reading the unsaid) expanded into a full framework. "What AI Should Absolutely Own" (cold outreach, data entry, meeting summaries, admin, first-draft forecasting) as the starting delegation list. The Abdication Risk — what it looks like when a manager delegates judgment, not just execution. Andrew demonstrates with a real example: how he uses AI-generated Gong coaching summaries without letting the summary replace his read of the rep.
- Workshop (30 min): WS-13-03 (AI Capability Mapping + time savings calculation) run live — managers work through their actual weekly tasks, categorize them, calculate real time savings. WS-13-04 (Where's Your Judgment) as the second half — identify 3 judgment calls from last week that AI could not have made.
- Close (15 min): WS-13-05 (Lazy Work Detection Practice — 5 email examples) as a rapid-fire team exercise. Debrief: what's the tell, what's the coaching conversation. Preview of Weeks 2-6 as the operating system they're building, one layer at a time.

### Week 2: Pipeline Reviews That Don't Waste Everyone's Time

**Thesis:** If your weekly pipeline review is still a data-gathering exercise, you're doing AI's job — the manager's job in an AI-assisted team is interpretation, not compilation.

**The shift:** The manager redesigns their pipeline review from a status update to a coaching conversation. They have a concrete format where AI does the first pass, they do the synthesis, and the meeting focuses on deals that need judgment — not deals that need explaining.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew frames the "status update vs. coaching conversation" distinction. Opens with a concrete example: what a pipeline review looks like when the manager is doing AI's job (asking "what's in your pipeline?") vs. when AI has done the first pass and the manager opens with "the AI flagged three deals stuck at proposal — let's talk about why." He shows his actual Gong/Clari-style pipeline summary format.
- Main teaching (30 min): The Pipeline Stage Framework and health diagnostics from Module 07 as vocabulary every manager needs. The Sandbagger vs. Hyper-caller distinction still matters, but the source of the number is changing. Then the new layer: how to interrogate an AI forecast — where do AI tools systematically miss (recency bias, rep-reported stage, deal size distortions), and what the manager's job is in that gap. The "pipeline is a story" teaching from Module 07 reframed: AI tells you the shape, you read the meaning.
- Workshop (30 min): WS-07-01 (Map Team Pipeline by Stage) and WS-07-02 (What Does the Shape Tell You) run live with managers' real pipeline data. WS-07-08 (Coaching Opportunities from Pipeline) — for each health pattern, what coaching question does the manager now ask? WS-07-10 (Coach the Conversation Script) — managers write out the actual questions for one rep.
- Close (15 min): RP-07-01 (Reading a Pipeline) run as a group scenario — Andrew plays the rep, cohort members play the manager, calling out what the AI would have flagged and what human read they're adding. Review: what's the format they're taking back to Monday's pipeline review?

### Week 3: Coaching the Rep in the Age of AI Reps

**Thesis:** AI didn't eliminate the need to coach reps — it created two new failure modes (over-reliance and avoidance) and gave you better diagnostic data than you've ever had, if you know how to use it.

**The shift:** The manager has a specific coaching framework for AI-related performance gaps and can distinguish between three coaching scenarios: rep using AI as a crutch (quality problem), rep avoiding AI entirely (competitive disadvantage problem), and rep using AI well but other gaps showing up in the data. They coach differently in each case.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew opens with a real coaching scenario from his own team — a rep who replaced prospecting calls with AI sequences and slipped below quota. What the AI data showed vs. what the conversation revealed. The "both/and" coaching challenge: you're not anti-AI, but you're pro-results, and right now those are pulling apart.
- Main teaching (30 min): The Behavior-Focused Coaching Framework from Module 08 applied to AI-era performance gaps — the principle is the same (coach observable behavior, not personality or tool choice), but the specific behaviors are new. Three coaching archetypes: the Over-Reliant Rep, the AI-Avoider, the AI-Native Who Has Other Gaps. For each: what the diagnostic data looks like, what the coaching conversation opens with, what behavior change looks like. The Coachability Assessment from Module 08 reframed: which reps will thrive with AI, which will resist — and what the manager's job is in each case.
- Workshop (30 min): WS-08-05 (Translate Vague Feedback to Specific Behavior) — practiced with AI-era examples ("you need to stop letting AI write your outreach" → specific observable behaviors). WS-08-01 (Assess Your Underperformers Coachability Matrix) run as a real-time team audit — managers score their actual reps. RP-13-05 run in pairs: "Your Rep Is Over-Relying on AI" — one manager, one rep, the rest observe and debrief.
- Close (15 min): "Spot the Lazy Work" from Module 13 as a QC protocol — how to build this into your standard operating rhythm without turning every 1:1 into an AI audit. WS-13-06 (Your Quality Control Process) as the closing output — managers draft their QC framework before they leave.

### Week 4: The 1:1 and Team Meeting That Actually Works Now

**Thesis:** AI gives you better pre-meeting data than you've ever had and worse meeting instincts than you've ever needed — the manager who knows how to use the briefing without letting it flatten the conversation wins.

**The shift:** The manager has a redesigned 1:1 format that incorporates AI-generated prep data without reducing the conversation to a data review. They have a protocol for when to use the AI briefing, when to set it aside, and how to use the 1:1 for coaching on AI adoption itself.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew demonstrates his actual pre-1:1 prep process using AI — what he asks it to pull together, what it gets right, what he ignores. The specific failure mode: letting the AI briefing flatten the conversation into "here's what the data says, do you agree?" rather than opening a real coaching conversation. The Safety Pyramid from Module 06 as the lens: AI doesn't know where your reps are on the pyramid, and if you let it run your meeting it will never find out.
- Main teaching (30 min): The Safety Pyramid framework from Module 06 (5 layers: expectation clarity, performance clarity, surface problems, challenge manager, be vulnerable) — updated for AI-era 1:1s. At each layer, what does AI provide, and what does it miss? The Listening Framework from Module 06 (what's said vs. unsaid vs. different from last week) — this is the layer AI cannot access. How AI-generated call analysis or rep performance data changes the "diagnosis" step of a coaching conversation. The new 1:1 coaching topic: coaching reps on their AI habits as a standard category of conversation, not a special circumstance.
- Workshop (30 min): WS-06-02 (Where's My Safety Gap) run live — managers score each rep on all 5 pyramid levels. WS-06-05 (Pick One Performance Gap + Coach Through It) applied to an AI-specific gap if one exists. WS-05-07 and WS-05-08 (Signals to Watch For + How to Respond) updated with AI-specific signals — what does a rep who is quietly disengaging from their AI tools look like in the data?
- Close (15 min): RP-06-01 (Guarded Rep) — the hardest 1:1 scenario; run in pairs. Debrief: where in that conversation did the rep's AI usage matter? Where didn't it? What's the 1:1 format they're committing to for next week?

### Week 5: Hiring for the Team That Doesn't Exist Yet

**Thesis:** You're about to hire a rep into a role that AI is reshaping in real time — the manager who doesn't screen for AI fluency is building a team that's already behind.

**The shift:** The manager has an updated interview process that screens for AI fluency alongside coachability, and a 30-60-90 onboarding plan that includes an AI tool ramp track. They know what "good with AI" and "using AI as a crutch" look like in an interview before they're in one.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew opens with the hiring decision he wishes he'd made differently — the rep who looked great in every traditional signal but had zero AI curiosity and is now struggling against peers who use AI to multiply their output. The real cost: not just one rep, but the team composition problem it creates when some reps are 2x-ing their capacity and others are working manually. The through-line from Week 1: the "Where Humans Still Win" framework applies to hiring too — you're now screening for judgment + AI fluency, not just coachability.
- Main teaching (30 min): The Capability-Coachability-Fit framework from Module 09 updated with a fourth dimension: AI Fluency. What AI fluency actually means in a sales rep context (not: are they a power user? But: do they have the curiosity to learn, the judgment to know when AI is helping vs. hurting, and the discipline to maintain quality control on AI outputs?). What "AI-curious and coachable" looks like in an interview vs. "AI-avoidant" vs. "AI-overconfident." The Coachability Red Flags from Module 09 plus two new red flags: "treats AI as cheating" and "no curiosity about tools." How to add a specific AI fluency probe to the existing 4-Part Interview Structure without extending the interview. The 30-60-90 onboarding plan updated with an AI tool ramp track: what the rep should be able to do with AI at day 30, 60, and 90.
- Workshop (30 min): RP-09-01 (Full 90-Min Sarah Interview Walkthrough) with the AI fluency section added — Andrew plays Sarah, cohort members rotate through running the AI probe questions. WS-09-03 (Coachability Interview Questions) paired with WS-13-09 (Your Hiring Rubric) — managers build their AI fluency probe question set. WS-09-05 (Build 30-60-90 Day Onboarding Plan) adapted with AI ramp milestones.
- Close (15 min): RP-09-02 (Red Flag Marcus Interview) run as a group — Marcus presents 3 of his original red flags plus one new one (dismisses AI with "I prefer to do real selling"). Debrief: which is a hard stop, which is a conversation to have? Review: one change each manager is making to their interview process before their next hire.

### Week 6: Building a Team That Doesn't Fear the Machine

**Thesis:** The fastest way to lose your team to AI anxiety is to say nothing about it — and the fastest way to lose your credibility with leadership is to say too much without a plan behind it.

**The shift:** The manager has a concrete communication plan for their team on what AI means for their roles (not a speech, a process), and a framework for advocating to leadership on AI adoption without overstepping. They leave knowing how to lead through ambiguity without either false certainty or avoidance.

**Session arc:**
- Opening frame (15 min): Andrew opens with the culture question nobody talks about: what do you tell your team when the company is rolling out an AI tool and you don't fully understand it yet? His real experience: being asked to champion a tool he was skeptical about, managing the team's fear while building his own fluency simultaneously. The Inform-Advocate-Execute framework from Module 02 applied to AI adoption: what's your job as the translator between leadership's AI strategy and your team's reality?
- Main teaching (30 min): The Five Demotivators framework from Module 10 updated with the sixth: AI-related uncertainty — reps who don't know if their role is changing, what's expected of them regarding AI, or whether they'll be left behind. This is currently the most common disengagement driver on SaaS sales teams. The "stated vs. actual" culture gap from Module 10 applied to AI: what does your team actually believe about AI in your org vs. what you've officially said? The fairness question — what do you do when AI creates a performance gap between reps who use it effectively and reps who don't? Building a culture where AI experimentation is encouraged vs. where it's theater: what the difference looks like in observable behaviors.
- Workshop (30 min): WS-10-01 (What's Your Actual Culture) run with an AI lens — managers map what their team actually believes about AI vs. what they've communicated. WS-10-03 (Identify Demotivators) with the sixth demotivator added. WS-01-10 (Transparent Communication Script Builder) applied to AI change communication — managers draft the specific message they're delivering to their team this week. RP-13-04 (Leadership Asks About AI Investment) — run in pairs; the "you'll own implementation" turn is the pressure point.
- Close (15 min): WS-13-10 (AI Leadership Roadmap — 30/90 Day) completed — this is the capstone artifact. Cohort share-out: what's one thing changing in the first 30 days? What's the 90-day version of yourself as a manager? Review: the program through-line from Week 1's "new job description" to the full operating system now installed.

**3. Module 08: Coaching Reps to Results** — Drop-in ready. The Coachability Assessment, 60-90 Day Coaching Window, and Behavior-Focused Coaching Framework are the performance management playbook. Pre-work anchor for Week 3.

**4. Module 03: Difficult Conversation Choreography** — Drop-in ready (note: this refers to the content-audit verdict; the materials audit shows Module 03 was never built beyond an outline. **This module must be constructed from adjacent assets before release.** The Data-First Framework referenced in the content audit is actually housed in WS-06-08 and RP-06-05. ## Cohort Cadence

### Weekly Rhythm

**Monday (Pre-work delivery):**
Foundations Library module for the week unlocks at 8am, delivered via course platform (Kajabi, Circle, or equivalent). Includes the specific worksheets to complete before the live session. Email reminder with the 2-3 things to bring to the session (e.g., "bring your real pipeline data," "bring the rep you're finding hardest to coach").

**Wednesday or Thursday (Live Session — 90 minutes):**
Andrew runs live on Zoom. Format:
- No slide decks as primary format — frameworks displayed as one-page visuals, not slide bullets. Andrew talks, demonstrates, coaches.
- 15 min open / 30 min teach / 30 min workshop / 15 min close is the standard arc; sessions 2, 3, and 5 run slightly workshop-heavy because the exercises require live data.
- Workshop portions use breakout rooms (pairs or trios) for role-plays and worksheet work. Andrew floats between breakouts, coaches in real time.
- Session recording posted within 24 hours in the cohort community.
- One shareable artifact per session — the takeaway worksheet — posted as a fillable PDF in the community within 1 hour of session end.

**Thursday or Friday (Community debrief):**
A structured async thread posted by Andrew in the cohort community (Circle or Slack): "One thing you're doing differently this week based on [session topic]. Reply with yours." Participation is expected but not graded. This is the accountability mechanism — public commitment creates follow-through.

**Ongoing (Between sessions):**
The Foundations Library is self-paced access throughout the 6 weeks. Modules unlock in recommended consumption order but can be accessed non-linearly. No gating — cohort members who want to go deep can; those who want pre-work-only coverage can.

### Office Hours / Accountability Calls

**Week 3 midpoint check-in (30 min, optional, small group):**
A single 30-minute open office hours call between Sessions 3 and 4. Not a Q&A — structured as a "what's actually happening" check-in: one manager, one situation, five minutes, group input. Max 8 participants per call; first-come slots. This is where the real stuff surfaces (situations they won't raise in the full cohort).

**30-Day Accountability Call (Post-cohort, 60 min):**
One month after Session 6, Andrew runs a small-group accountability call (max 12 per call) structured around the 30-day AI Leadership Roadmap artifact from Week 6. Each manager shares: what they committed to, what happened, what's stuck. Not a coaching call — Andrew facilitates, the group coaches each other. This is a retention and referral driver, not just a nice-to-have.

### Community Platform

Circle is the recommended platform over Slack for a 6-week cohort:
- Structured spaces by week (Week 1 Discussion, Week 2 Discussion, etc.) that stay organized across the cohort timeline
- A "Show Your Work" space where managers post completed artifacts — seeing others' real takeaway documents is the most powerful learning reinforcement in a peer cohort
- A "Questions for Andrew" space that gets answered before each live session (feeds the opening frame)
- DM-capable for peer connection between cohort members

Andrew's presence in the community: one substantive response per day on weekdays, not more. Quality over volume. The community should feel like a working cohort, not a support ticket queue.

## Pricing Recommendation

### What the Inventory Justifies

The archive is 308 cataloged assets, 11 fully developed modules, and production-quality role-plays and worksheets that most training programs at 3x this price don't have. Module 13 alone is a $500 standalone product if sold individually. The Foundations Library as a standalone async course would price at $800-1,200 in the current market. That's the floor before you add the live sessions.

The 6-week live cohort with Andrew is the premium layer — not because it's the most content, but because it's the specific credibility of a VP of Sales who runs these AI systems himself, teaching from inside the problem, with a live peer cohort. That combination is genuinely scarce. There are hundreds of sales training programs. There are very few run by someone building the AI stack while managing the team.

### Recommended Pricing

**Cohort 1 target price: $2,500 per seat**

Floor: $1,800 (the point where it's priced as a mid-market training course and loses the premium positioning)
Target: $2,500 (positions it correctly — between a $1,200 async course and a $5,000+ in-person program)
Premium anchor: $3,500 (for teams of 3+ from the same company — the premium isn't per seat, it's organizational alignment)

**Cohort 1 justification:**
- 6 live 90-minute sessions with a practitioner who builds the systems ($900 in consulting time per seat at $150/hr, and Andrew's rate is higher than that)
- Full Foundations Library access (11 modules, 300+ assets, ongoing reference)
- 30-day accountability call post-cohort
- Community with 6 weeks of facilitated peer interaction
- One concrete artifact per session that managers take back Monday morning

The "Cohort 1" framing matters: price it honestly as beta pricing with the understanding that feedback shapes the program. This is not a discount — it's a co-development premium for the managers who shape what it becomes. Offer 10-15 seats max at Cohort 1 pricing. Cohort 2 price goes to $3,200 with expanded case studies from Cohort 1 alumni.

**The number Andrew can defend:** $2,500 is one month of a mid-level sales manager's training budget at a 100-person SaaS company. It's less than one bad hire. It's less than one lost deal. The ROI case writes itself if the manager improves team performance by even 5% — at $1M team quota, that's $50,000 in incremental revenue. **Group pricing:**

**What would justify $3,500+:**
